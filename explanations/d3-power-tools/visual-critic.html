Visual Critic

The gap between "it renders" and "it communicates" is about harmony, hierarchy, whitespace, and restraint.

A chart that runs without errors is not a well-designed chart. Most AI-generated visualizations land around score 3 on a 10-point scale: technically correct, visually generic. Default colors, flat typographic hierarchy, cramped layouts, no sense of restraint. The visual critic skill scores how designed a visualization looks, not whether it works. The standard is publication-quality work from graphics desks and dedicated data journalists.

Design quality is evaluable on a scale. The dimensions that separate a score-3 chart from a score-8 chart are concrete: color harmony, typographic hierarchy, confident whitespace, data-ink ratio, and overall feel. These are not subjective hand-waving. They are observable properties that anyone can learn to see.

The scoring is deliberately honest. "Generic but working" is a 5, not a 7. A chart with perfect typography but clashing colors is not a 7. A chart with default styling but excellent proportions and restraint might be. The critic trusts its overall impression first, then uses the individual dimensions to explain that impression. This matters because design quality is holistic: you cannot score each dimension in isolation and average them.

Score 3 charts are everywhere in AI-generated output. They render. They are not broken. But nobody made a design choice. Score 9 is rare and requires restraint: fewer colors, fewer gridlines, fewer labels, and every remaining element earning its place. The visual critic's job is to name the gap honestly, then give specific, actionable feedback so the next version can close it.

The Full 1-10 Scale

The scale is not arbitrary. Each level describes an observable quality that distinguishes it from the levels above and below. The question at every level is: "How many design decisions were made, and how many of them were good?"

ScoreWhat it meansYou would see this in...
1 Visually broken. Content renders but is unreadable. Overlapping text, data hidden behind chrome, elements clipped off-screen. A violin plot where the violins are two pixels wide. A map where states are indistinguishable from the ocean background.
2 Technically renders but fundamentally fails at its job. You can tell what it is supposed to be, but you cannot extract information from it. A bar chart where all bars are the same height due to a scale bug, but axes and labels are present. A treemap where labels overflow every cell.
3 Works but undesigned. Default fill colors, zero margin thought, everything the same visual weight. D3's defaults with no overrides. The "SteelBlue bars" that every first-draft AI chart produces. A line chart with schemeCategory10 and no title.
4 Minimal effort toward design. Someone picked a color, added a title, and stopped. Margins exist but are cramped. Hierarchy is flat. A grouped bar chart with a title but no subtitle, adequate but tight margins, and category colors that were chosen but do not harmonize.
5 Functional and readable. The chart communicates its data. Nothing is broken, nothing is ugly, and nothing is intentional. This is the median of competence. A dashboard panel with readable axes, acceptable spacing, and a sensible color scheme that was not obviously picked for the specific data.
6 Above average. One or two design choices stand out: a restrained palette, a well-placed annotation, direct labels instead of a legend. But the whole is not yet cohesive. A scatter plot with a good color scheme and direct axis labels, but gridlines that are too heavy and a title that competes with the data for attention.
7 Deliberately designed. Every element feels considered. Color harmony, clear typographic hierarchy, whitespace that breathes. The chart has a visual argument. A linked scatterplot matrix with an intentional palette, labels sized to their importance, and brushing that feels responsive and satisfying.
8 Strong design with personality. The chart has a recognizable style beyond "competent." Interaction feels polished. The visual hierarchy guides the eye without effort. A small multiples layout with a warm neutral base, one accent color for the key finding, generous margins between panels, and a headline that tells you the story before you read any axis.

10 Exceptional. Not just well-designed but original. The form itself is a contribution. You study it not just for what it says but for how it says it. Nadieh Bremer's data art, Giorgia Lupi's Dear Data series, Maarten Lambrechts' best interactive work. This score is vanishingly rare and should be reserved for work that advances the craft.

Two things to notice about this scale. First, the midpoint is 5, not 7. Academic grading has trained people to treat 7/10 as average, but in this system 5 means "functional, readable, and generic." That is a real accomplishment for generated code, and being honest about it avoids the grade inflation that makes the scale useless. Second, the jumps are not uniform. Moving from 3 to 5 requires learning the basics (margins, legible fonts, a non-default color). Moving from 5 to 7 requires learning to see (hierarchy, harmony, intentional whitespace). Moving from 7 to 9 requires learning what to remove.

Data-Ink Ratio in Practice

Edward Tufte coined "data-ink ratio" as the share of a chart's ink that represents data, as opposed to the ink spent on borders, gridlines, tick marks, backgrounds, decorations, and other chrome. The principle is simple: maximize it. In practice, it is a useful lens but not a religion.

A gridline that helps the reader compare values across a wide chart is data-supporting ink. The same gridline on a narrow chart where the bars are easy to compare by eye is wasted ink. A light border around a chart panel that visually separates it from surrounding text is structural ink that costs almost nothing. A heavy dark border with a drop shadow is decoration that competes with the data for attention. The question is never "does this element exist?" but "does this element earn its place?"

Here is a practical checklist for applying data-ink thinking:

Gridlines. Start with none. Add them only if the reader needs to compare values that are not adjacent. When you add them, make them the lightest element in the chart: thin, dashed or dotted, in a color barely distinguishable from the background. If the reader does not notice them consciously, they are doing their job.

Axis lines. The bottom axis line (the baseline) usually earns its place because it grounds the data. The left axis line often does not, since the gridlines or tick labels already define the scale. Try removing it and see if anything is lost.

Borders and boxes. A chart that stands alone on a page needs no border. A chart embedded in a dashboard with adjacent panels may need a subtle one for separation. Never add a border "because charts have borders."

Legends. A legend forces the reader to look away from the data, hold a color-to-label mapping in memory, then look back. Direct labels on the data eliminate that round trip. When you have two or three series, label them directly. Legends earn their place only when direct labeling would create clutter worse than the cognitive cost of cross-referencing.

The highest data-ink ratio is not the goal. A chart with zero chrome is unreadable. The goal is that every non-data element justifies the attention it costs. If you remove an element and the chart gets harder to read, put it back. If you remove it and nothing changes, leave it out.

Typographic Hierarchy Communicates Importance

Typography in a chart is not about choosing a pretty font. It is about creating a visual order that tells the reader where to look first, second, and third. When every piece of text in a chart is the same size, weight, and color, the reader's eye has no entry point. It bounces between title, axis labels, tick values, and annotations with equal probability. That is not neutral design. It is a failure to communicate priority.

A well-designed chart has at least three levels of typographic hierarchy:

Level 1: The headline. The largest and heaviest text. This is the entry point. In a data visualization, this is the chart title, and it should often be a finding, not a description. "Q4 closes strong at $8.3M" tells the reader what to look for. "Quarterly Revenue" tells them nothing they would not already know from the axes.

Level 2: Supporting context. Smaller than the headline, lighter in weight or color. This includes subtitles, axis titles, annotation text, and direct data labels. These elements are important, but the reader should encounter them after the headline has established the frame.

Level 3: Reference detail. The quietest text. Tick values, source attributions, footnotes. These exist for the reader who needs them but should never compete with the data or the headline for attention. Use smaller sizes, lighter colors (a medium gray rather than black), and lighter font weights.

Look at the score 9 version in Figure 1 above. The headline is 17px, bold, dark, with tight letter-spacing. The subtitle is 11px, gray, regular weight. The tick label is 9.5px in a near-invisible gray. The quarter labels under the bars vary in weight to draw the eye to Q4. None of this is accidental. Each text element has been assigned a loudness that matches its importance.

The most common typographic failure in generated charts is uniformity. Everything at 12px, everything black, everything the same font weight. The fix is not to make things bigger. It is to make the unimportant things quieter. Push tick labels to 10px and light gray. Push the subtitle to 11px and medium gray. The headline does not need to get louder if everything else gets softer.

Whitespace Is a Design Element

Whitespace is not leftover space. It is not the part of the chart you have not filled yet. It is the silence between the notes. A chart with generous margins, open padding around its data area, and breathing room between labels and axes communicates confidence. A chart with everything packed edge-to-edge communicates "I ran out of room," even when the viewport is 1200 pixels wide.

There are three places where whitespace decisions matter most in a chart:

Margins. The space between the data area and the edge of the container. Cramped margins are the single most common visual defect in generated charts. The default D3 margin convention ({top: 20, right: 20, bottom: 30, left: 40}) is a starting point, not a destination. Publication-quality charts often use margins that feel luxuriously large: 50 or 60 pixels on top to give the title room to breathe, 30 or 40 on the right so the last data point does not feel pressed against a wall.

Padding within the data area. For bar charts, this is scaleBand().padding(). A padding of 0.05 crams bars together. A padding of 0.35 to 0.45 gives each bar its own visual presence. The bars are thinner, which feels counterintuitive, but the chart becomes easier to read because each bar is a distinct object rather than a continuous wall of color.

Distance between labels and the things they label. Tick labels that sit right against the axis line feel anxious. Pushing them 8 to 10 pixels away (using tickPadding) gives the eye room to separate the label from the structure. The same applies to value labels above bars: 8 to 12 pixels of clearance above the bar top is usually right. Less than 5 feels cramped. More than 20 feels disconnected.

The test for confident whitespace: if you could remove a margin, reduce a padding, or tighten a gap without making the chart harder to read, the space is not confident. It is accidental. Confident whitespace is space you would defend if asked to justify it.

The Difference Between "Looks Good" and "Communicates Well"

A chart can look good and say nothing. A dark background, a monochrome palette, ample whitespace, and a single elegant line. You glance at it and feel satisfied. But if the title says "Revenue Over Time" and there is no annotation, no context, no indication of what the line means, looking good is not enough. The chart is a decoration that happens to contain data.

The visual critic scores design quality, not communication quality. These overlap substantially but are not identical. A chart that communicates well almost always looks deliberately designed: the hierarchy guides the eye, the annotation draws attention to the finding, the restrained palette keeps focus on the data. But the reverse is not guaranteed. A chart can have perfect typography, a beautiful palette, and generous whitespace while saying absolutely nothing because nobody decided what it should say.

The practical difference shows up in two ways. First, a communicating chart has a headline that is a finding, not a description. "Q4 closes strong" versus "Quarterly Revenue." Second, a communicating chart has at least one annotation, reference line, or visual emphasis that tells the reader where the story is. The visual critic can see whether those elements exist and whether they are well-designed. It cannot judge whether the finding is correct or the annotation is pointed at the right thing. That is the job of the person who knows the data.

This means a perfect visual critic score is necessary but not sufficient for a great chart. A chart with a score of 9 and no editorial judgment is a beautifully framed empty picture. A chart with a score of 6 and a clear, annotated finding is more useful to its audience, even if the gridlines are too heavy.

When This Skill Hurts

Aesthetics can conflict with clarity

The critic rewards visual restraint, which means a minimalist chart scores well on feel and data-ink ratio. But minimalism taken too far removes elements the reader actually needs. A chart with no axis labels, no title, and no gridlines might score high on "confident whitespace" while being genuinely unreadable. When an LLM optimizes for critic score, it may strip away labels and annotations that look like clutter but are actually doing work. A clean chart is not automatically a clear chart.

Score optimization replaces communication

When an LLM has access to the visual critic, it can start treating the score as a loss function. It learns that muted palettes, generous margins, and direct labels reliably push the score up, then applies those patterns regardless of whether they serve the data. A chart about an emergency outbreak might warrant red and urgency, not the calm teal-and-gray palette that scores well on harmony. The critic rewards design quality in isolation. Whether the design choices are appropriate to the content is a judgment the critic cannot make.

False precision in the 1-10 scale

Is a 7.2 meaningfully different from a 7.4? Almost certainly not. The scale has about five real levels (broken, undesigned, functional, designed, publication), mapped onto ten numbers for finer communication. But the finer gradations are not reliably reproducible. Run the same screenshot through the critic twice and you may get a 7 and then an 8. Treat the score as a band, not a decimal. A chart at "about 7" is different from a chart at "about 5," but a chart at 7.2 is not meaningfully different from one at 7.4. Do not optimize for tenths of a point.

Screenshots miss interactive quality

The critic evaluates a screenshot: a single frozen frame. It cannot feel whether a tooltip appears at the right speed, whether brushing is responsive, whether hover feedback is satisfying, or whether the animation timing creates a sense of flow. A chart that looks mediocre in a screenshot but feels wonderful to use will be underscored. A chart that looks beautiful but has sluggish, buggy interaction will be overscored. Interactive quality is a real dimension of design, and the critic is structurally blind to it.
