Four Views of One Network

The same graph looks completely different depending on how you lay it out. Each representation reveals different structure and hides the rest.

A network is a set of nodes connected by edges. That sounds like one thing, but there are many ways to draw it, and the choice of layout determines what a viewer can actually see. Force-directed node-link diagrams are the default, but they are not always the best option. Adjacency matrices, arc diagrams, and chord diagrams each foreground different structural features of the same data.

Below is a small social network: 20 people in 3 communities. The edges and groups are identical in all four representations. Toggle between them and notice what jumps out in each one. Clusters are obvious in the force layout but invisible in the arc diagram (unless you sort by group). The matrix reveals exact connection density. The chord diagram shows how much traffic flows between communities.

The node-link diagram answers "who is near whom?" The matrix answers "how dense are the connections?" The arc diagram answers "how far do connections reach?" The chord diagram answers "how much flows between groups?" Same data, four different questions.

When Each Layout Works Best

Node-link diagrams excel at sparse graphs under 500 nodes where topology matters: finding paths, spotting bridges between clusters, or identifying outliers at the periphery. They become unreadable past about 2,000 edges. Adjacency matrices handle any density because every possible edge has a dedicated cell. They are faster and more accurate for cluster detection and weight comparison in dense graphs (Ghoniem et al. 2004), but tracing a multi-hop path requires sequential row-column lookups. Arc diagrams shine when nodes have a natural ordering (time, sequence, rank) because arc height directly encodes connection distance. Chord diagrams are best for 5 to 20 groups with asymmetric flows, showing both volume and direction at a glance.

The decision is not aesthetic. It is about which question you need answered. Flow volume between departments calls for a chord diagram, not a force layout. A sparse dependency graph calls for node-link, not a matrix. Picking the wrong layout buries the answer in visual noise.

What Each Representation Reveals

Node-link diagrams foreground topology. The viewer perceives clusters as spatial clumps, bridges as lone edges stretching between groups, and outliers as isolated dots at the periphery. The layout answers "who is near whom?" but hides exact density -- two tightly connected clusters look the same whether they have 60% or 95% internal edge density.

Adjacency matrices reveal density patterns. A filled diagonal block means a dense community. A sparse off-diagonal block means two groups barely talk. The viewer can compare connection density between any pair of groups at a glance, something that is nearly impossible in a node-link diagram. But a multi-hop path -- A connects to B connects to C -- requires tracing rows and columns sequentially, which is slow and error-prone.

Chord diagrams show flow volumes between groups. The ribbon width encodes how much traffic passes between two categories, and the arc length encodes each group's total volume. They answer "how much flows from X to Y?" with immediate visual clarity. But they require a square flow matrix and break down past about 20 groups, when ribbons overlap and become unreadable.

Sankey diagrams show quantities through a pipeline. Each link's width is proportional to the quantity flowing from source to target, and the conservation law -- flow in equals flow out at every node -- is visible by construction. They answer "where does the volume go?" and "where does it leak?" They require directed acyclic data: source/target/value triplets with no cycles.

Arc diagrams expose ordering effects. When nodes are sorted meaningfully (by time, rank, or cluster membership), the height and reach of arcs directly encode connection distance. Short arcs cluster together for nearby connections; tall arcs spanning the full width reveal long-range bridges. The tradeoff is that clusters are invisible unless the sort order groups them.

Sankey: Flow Through a Pipeline

A Sankey diagram makes conservation visible. Every unit that enters must exit or be accounted for at each stage. This is why Sankey is the right choice for funnels, budgets, energy flows, and any data where "where does it go?" is the central question. The width of each link is proportional to the quantity flowing through it, so the viewer can see at a glance where the biggest losses occur.

Below is a website conversion funnel: 10,000 visitors arrive, some sign up, fewer start a trial, fewer still convert to paid, and some of those churn. Hover any link to see the exact flow. Notice how the visual width makes the biggest drop (visit to signup) immediately obvious without reading any numbers.

Bipartite Graphs: Two Types Connected Across

A bipartite graph has two distinct node types, and edges only connect nodes of different types. Reviewers and papers, students and courses, suppliers and products. Force-directed layouts obscure this structure because they intermingle the two types. A bipartite layout pins each type to its own column and draws edges across the gap, making the two-type structure explicit.

The layout below shows six authors on the left and five papers on the right. Each edge means the author contributed to that paper. Hover an author to see which papers they wrote; hover a paper to see its author list. The visual pattern immediately reveals who collaborates broadly (many edges fanning out) versus who works in isolation (a single edge).

Community Detection in Practice

Communities are groups of nodes more densely connected to each other than to the rest of the network. In the four-view figure above, the three communities are assigned in the data. In real networks, you discover them algorithmically.

The visual signature of community structure depends on the layout. In a force-directed diagram, communities appear as spatial clusters separated by whitespace, with a few bridge edges spanning the gaps. In an adjacency matrix sorted by community assignment, they appear as dense blocks along the diagonal -- the off-diagonal blocks are sparse by comparison. In a chord diagram, communities become arcs, and inter-community flow becomes the ribbons connecting them. The same structure, three different visual patterns.

D3 has no built-in graph algorithms. For community detection in the browser, use graphology with the Louvain algorithm: load your graph, run graphology-communities-louvain to assign community IDs, then feed those IDs into D3 color scales. A slider controlling Louvain's resolution parameter lets the viewer adjust granularity in real time -- low resolution produces a few large communities, high resolution produces many small ones.

Directed vs. Undirected Conventions

Undirected graphs have symmetric relationships: Alice knows Bob means Bob knows Alice. Directed graphs have asymmetric relationships: Alice follows Bob does not mean Bob follows Alice. The visual convention matters because the wrong one misleads.

For undirected graphs, edges are plain lines with no markers. The adjacency matrix must be symmetric -- if cell (A, B) is filled, cell (B, A) must be too. An asymmetric matrix for undirected data is a bug: it means half the edges are missing.

For directed graphs, edges get arrowheads. In D3, this means SVG markers with orient="auto" and a refX offset that accounts for the target node's radius, otherwise the arrowhead hides inside the circle. The adjacency matrix is not necessarily symmetric -- cell (A, B) and cell (B, A) can differ. Chord diagrams handle direction naturally through asymmetric ribbon widths: the ribbon is wider at the source side and narrower at the target side when flow is unequal.

When This Skill Hurts

  • Node-link default bias. LLMs default to force-directed node-link because it is the most familiar network layout in training data. The skill lists alternatives -- matrix, chord, Sankey, arc, hive plot -- but LLMs rarely pick them even when they would communicate better. A dense 200-node collaboration network with the question "which departments interact most?" calls for an adjacency matrix or chord diagram, not a force layout. But force is what gets generated, because it is what the model has seen most often.
  • Chord diagram complexity. Chord diagrams require a square flow matrix and careful ribbon ordering. LLMs often produce broken chord diagrams with overlapping ribbons, incorrect flow directions, or asymmetric matrices for undirected data. The d3.chord() API is unforgiving: a non-square matrix, negative values, or mismatched indices produce silent rendering failures where ribbons render at wrong angles or disappear entirely. Debugging requires understanding the radial geometry, which LLMs frequently get wrong.
  • Sankey data requirements. Sankey needs source/target/value triplets with no cycles. LLMs sometimes generate cyclic data -- A flows to B flows to C flows back to A -- that d3-sankey cannot handle. The result is a silent failure: nodes stack at position zero, links collapse to zero width, or the layout throws during the relaxation step. The skill mentions cycle removal via DFS, but LLMs often skip this validation step and produce a Sankey that renders incorrectly without any visible error message.
  • Scale mismatch. Every representation breaks at a different scale, and the skill does not state these limits bluntly enough. Node-link becomes a hairball past roughly 500 nodes. Adjacency matrices become unreadable past about 100 nodes because the cells are too small to distinguish. Chord diagrams fail past 20 groups because ribbons overlap into an indecipherable tangle. Arc diagrams lose clarity past 200 nodes because labels collide and arcs stack. An LLM that picks a layout without considering the node count will produce a visualization that technically renders but communicates nothing.
