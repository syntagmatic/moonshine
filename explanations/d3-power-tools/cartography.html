Cartography

Every map projection is a lie about the Earth's shape. The projection you choose determines which lie you tell.

The Earth is roughly a sphere. Paper and screens are flat. There is no way to flatten a sphere without tearing, stretching, or compressing it. That is the fundamental problem of cartography, and it means every flat map sacrifices something. Mercator preserves angles but inflates areas near the poles, making Greenland appear the size of Africa. Equal Earth preserves area but bends shapes. Orthographic shows the planet as a globe, hiding half the world. These are not flaws to fix. They are tradeoffs to choose deliberately.

The projection you pick should depend on what question the map answers. Showing a choropleth of population density? Use an equal-area projection, because area distortion would lie about magnitude. Making a navigation chart? Mercator's angle preservation matters. Showing the whole planet for general reference? A compromise projection like Natural Earth or Equirectangular trades a little of everything for nothing terrible.

D3 makes this choice visible. The same GeoJSON data rendered through d3.geoMercator() versus d3.geoEqualEarth() tells a different story. Toggle between the projections below and watch the Tissot indicatrices: small circles placed at regular intervals across the globe. On a perfect, distortion-free map they would all be identical circles. On a real projection, they stretch into ellipses, revealing exactly where and how the map lies.

Countries colored by continent. Tissot indicatrices are circles of equal radius (5 degrees) placed every 30 degrees of longitude and latitude. Where they stretch into tall ellipses, the projection exaggerates north-south distances. Where they widen, it exaggerates east-west. Where they grow, it inflates area.

The pipeline behind this map follows a standard D3 pattern. TopoJSON geometry loads from a CDN atlas (pre-simplified, topologically correct, cut at the antimeridian). topojson.feature() converts it to GeoJSON. A projection maps [longitude, latitude] to [x, y] screen coordinates. d3.geoPath() converts projected geometry to SVG path strings. Layer order matters: water and graticule first, then country fills, then borders as a single mesh (drawn once for shared edges, courtesy of topology), then the indicatrices on top.

TopoJSON: Topology as Infrastructure

TopoJSON is not just a compression format. It encodes the topological relationships between geographic features -- which boundaries are shared, which regions are adjacent, how shapes nest. This topology enables three operations that GeoJSON cannot support.

topojson.mesh() extracts boundaries as a single MultiLineString. The critical detail is the filter function. With (a, b) => a !== b, you get only internal borders -- the lines where two distinct features share an edge. With (a, b) => a === b, you get the outer boundary. Without a filter, you get everything. This matters visually: internal borders should be thin and light (white or near-white), while the outer boundary should be heavier. Drawing borders as a mesh rather than stroking each polygon individually means shared edges render once instead of twice, avoiding the double-stroke artifacts that make borders appear darker where three countries meet.

topojson.merge() dissolves multiple features into one. Give it a filtered subset of geometries and it produces a single polygon with only the outer boundary, erasing all internal divisions. This is how you build a national outline from state geometries, or Census divisions from counties. The filter is just an array filter on topology.objects.states.geometries. The resulting shape is topologically correct -- no self-intersections, no gaps.

topojson.neighbors() returns an adjacency list: for each geometry, an array of indices of geometries that share a boundary arc. This powers four-color map algorithms (assign colors so no two neighbors share one), region grouping, and spatial analysis. The adjacency is exact, derived from shared arcs, not proximity heuristics.

Think of these three operations as a toolkit: mesh() draws lines, merge() erases lines, and neighbors() tells you who touches whom. Together they handle most of the geometric manipulation that mapmakers need without ever touching raw coordinates.

Classification Changes the Story

A choropleth maps a numeric value to a color for each geographic region. The part that gets the least attention and matters the most is classification: how you divide the continuous range of values into discrete color bins. The same data, mapped through different classification methods, can tell opposite stories.

Quantile breaks divide the data so each bin contains roughly the same number of features. This maximizes visual contrast -- you will always see a spread of colors on the map. But it can be misleading: if values cluster tightly, quantile breaks will exaggerate small differences. A country at the 49th percentile and one at the 51st may look like they are in different worlds when the actual difference is trivial.

Equal interval (also called quantize) divides the data range into bins of equal width. This is honest about the numeric scale: a bin from 0-20 covers the same range as 20-40. But on skewed data, most features pile into one or two bins. A map of GDP per capita with equal intervals will color most of Africa and South Asia the same shade while reserving several distinct shades for the handful of wealthy nations.

Natural breaks (Jenks) finds class boundaries that minimize within-class variance, placing breaks at the gaps in the data. This often produces the most readable map because the bins follow the data's structure rather than imposing a mathematical rule. The cost is computational complexity and the fact that the breaks are data-dependent -- change the dataset slightly and the bins shift.

Threshold breaks are set manually at meaningful values. For a poverty rate map, you might place breaks at 5%, 10%, 20%, 40% because those are the policy thresholds that matter. This is the most honest method when domain-meaningful cutoffs exist, because it lets the reader compare each region against a known standard rather than against the distribution of the dataset.

Bubble Maps vs. Choropleth

Choropleths encode values as color fill over the geographic area of each region. This works well for rates and densities -- values that are already normalized by area. But for absolute quantities (total population, total GDP, number of cases), choropleths have a structural problem: large countries dominate the visual field regardless of their data values. Russia and Canada take up enormous map area, so their color dominates perception even when their values are middling. Meanwhile, Singapore and Luxembourg are invisible dots no matter how extreme their values are.

Bubble maps solve this by encoding values as circle area positioned at each region's centroid. The geographic shapes remain as a neutral basemap, and the data lives in the proportional symbols. Area perception is less precise than position along an axis, but it decouples the data encoding from the geographic area, which is the whole point. Use d3.scaleSqrt() for the radius -- a linear radius scale would make the largest values appear quadratically larger than they are.

The practical rule: if your value is already a rate or density, use a choropleth. If it is a count or total, consider a bubble map. If you must show a count as a choropleth, normalize it by area or population first. A raw-count choropleth is almost always a proxy for a population density map, which tells the reader nothing they do not already know.

Zoom to Feature

A world map at static zoom shows every country but lets you examine none of them. Zoom-to-feature fixes this: click a country and the projection smoothly re-centers and re-scales to frame just that feature. Click again (or click the background) to zoom back out to the full view.

The technique uses projection.fitSize() to compute the scale and translate that would frame a given GeoJSON feature within the viewport, then interpolates from the current projection parameters to the new ones over a transition. For equal-area projections, interpolating scale and translate is sufficient. For the orthographic globe, you also need to interpolate the rotation to center the target country.

Globe Rotation and Versor Drag

An orthographic projection shows the Earth as a globe, which is visually compelling but hides half the world. Drag rotation lets the viewer spin the globe to see any part. The naive approach -- mapping horizontal drag to longitude and vertical drag to latitude -- works near the equator but breaks at the poles. Dragging left at the north pole should rotate the globe clockwise, not shift it laterally. The two-axis approximation cannot express this.

The correct solution uses quaternion rotation via the versor library. On drag start, record the current projection rotation as a quaternion and the geographic coordinates under the pointer. On each drag event, compute the geographic coordinates under the new pointer position (using the original rotation, not the updated one), then compute the quaternion that rotates from the start point to the current point. Multiply the quaternions and convert back to Euler angles for projection.rotate(). This handles poles, dateline crossings, and roll correctly.

For the back hemisphere -- the half of the globe hidden behind the front -- a second orthographic projection with clipAngle(180) can render the land at low opacity, giving the viewer spatial context even for the hidden side. This is a visual hint, not a precision tool: the severe distortion at the edges of the back hemisphere means shapes are unreliable, but it helps the viewer understand what is about to rotate into view.

Canvas Multi-Layer Architecture

SVG maps hit a performance ceiling around 2,000 to 5,000 path elements. County-level US maps have 3,143 features; world maps at 10m resolution have thousands more. At this scale, Canvas is the practical rendering target. But Canvas lacks the DOM -- no event delegation, no CSS selectors, no built-in hover states. The solution is a multi-layer stack.

The standard architecture uses four layers, bottom to top. A tile canvas for raster basemap tiles (streets, satellite, terrain). A data canvas for the choropleth fills and border strokes -- this is where the heavy geometry lives. An interaction canvas (hidden, never displayed) that renders each feature with a unique RGB color for hit detection: read the pixel under the mouse, decode the color to a feature index, and you have O(1) hover detection for arbitrarily complex geometries. Finally, an SVG overlay for tooltips, legends, labels, and any interactive controls that benefit from DOM events.

The color-pick trick deserves emphasis. Assign each feature an index, encode it as RGB (r = (i+1) >> 16 & 255, g = (i+1) >> 8 & 255, b = (i+1) & 255), and fill the feature on the hidden canvas with that color. Disable anti-aliasing (imageSmoothingEnabled = false) and do not stroke, because edge blending would create ambiguous intermediate colors. On mousemove, getImageData the single pixel under the cursor, decode the RGB back to an index, and look up the feature. This supports 16.7 million features and is frame-rate independent.

Geographic Labels

Placing text labels on a map is deceptively hard. The obvious approach -- position the label at the feature's centroid -- fails for concave shapes. Florida's centroid falls in the Gulf of Mexico. Chile's centroid lands in the Pacific. Michigan's is in Lake Huron.

The better approach uses the pole of inaccessibility: the point inside the polygon that is farthest from any edge. The polylabel algorithm computes this efficiently. It guarantees the label anchor is inside the polygon and maximally distant from boundaries, which usually means it lands in the widest part of the shape -- exactly where a human cartographer would place it. For MultiPolygons, compute polylabel on the largest polygon by projected area.

Even with good anchor points, labels overlap. At world scale, small European countries are impossible to label without collision. Cartographers handle this with leader lines (a thin line connecting a label outside the polygon to a point inside it), selective labeling (only label the features that matter for the current story), and adaptive font sizing (larger countries get larger text). In D3, force simulation can push labels apart while anchoring them near their geographic targets -- the same technique used for bubble maps, applied to text bounding boxes instead of circles.

Large Geometry and Level of Detail

The world-atlas and us-atlas packages come in two resolutions: 110m (coarse, fast, good for world-scale views) and 10m (detailed, large, good for regional views). At world zoom, 10m resolution draws thousands of coastline vertices that collapse to sub-pixel noise. At country zoom, 110m resolution shows jagged borders and missing islands. The solution is level-of-detail switching: load the coarse geometry first for fast initial render, then lazy-load the detailed geometry when the viewer zooms in past a threshold.

Client-side simplification via topojson-simplify offers more granularity. The presimplify function annotates each coordinate with an area weight (using Visvalingam's algorithm), and simplify removes coordinates below a threshold. Higher thresholds remove more detail. You can precompute multiple simplification levels and swap between them as zoom changes, trading geometry size for rendering speed.

The general strategy: at zoom level 1-2 (full world), use 110m. At zoom 3-5 (continent), use 10m or lightly simplified full-resolution. At zoom 6+ (country or region), use full-resolution, lazy-loaded for just the visible area if the full dataset is too large to hold in memory. Viewport culling -- skipping features whose bounding box falls entirely outside the visible area -- is essential at high zoom. Without it, you re-render thousands of features that no pixel of the screen will ever show.

When This Skill Hurts

  • Projection misuse. LLMs default to Mercator because it is the most familiar projection in their training data, even when the skill explicitly recommends Equal Earth for choropleths. Worse, they sometimes use an orthographic (globe) projection for a choropleth where the viewer needs to see all countries at once -- hiding half the data behind the planet. The skill documents projection selection criteria, but LLMs tend to reach for what they have seen most often rather than what the criteria recommend.
  • Choropleth color lies. The skill covers classification methods, but LLMs frequently default to linear color scales on highly skewed data. A linear scale on population data, for example, will color 90% of the map the lightest shade while a handful of outliers (China, India, the US) get the darkest. The map looks like it has almost no variation. Quantile breaks would spread the colors evenly, but choosing between quantile and linear requires understanding the data distribution -- a judgment call that LLMs often skip.
  • TopoJSON complexity. The topology operations -- merge(), mesh(), neighbors() -- are powerful but conceptually dense. LLMs frequently produce broken boundaries from incorrect merge() calls: passing the wrong geometry collection, forgetting to filter, or confusing topojson.feature() output (GeoJSON) with topology input (TopoJSON). A bad merge produces an empty shape or a shape that covers the entire globe minus the target, because the winding order inverted.
  • Data join failures. Matching geographic features to data requires exact ID matching. The world-atlas uses numeric country IDs (ISO 3166-1 numeric); the us-atlas uses FIPS codes. LLMs often generate synthetic data with IDs that do not match the TopoJSON features -- using ISO alpha-2 codes ("US", "FR") when the atlas expects numeric codes ("840", "250"), or generating county FIPS codes that are 4 digits instead of 5. The result is a perfectly rendered map with no data on it: all features colored the fallback gray. The map looks correct at a glance, which makes the bug hard to catch.
