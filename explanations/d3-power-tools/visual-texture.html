Visual Texture

A second encoding channel beyond color.

Color is the first visual channel most chart-builders reach for. It works well on screen, but it has limits. About 8% of men have some form of color vision deficiency. Printers flatten hue to grayscale. And once you have used color for one categorical variable, you have spent that channel. Texture gives you a second voice: hatching, stippling, crosshatching, dots. Each is distinguishable by shape, orientation, or density, without requiring any color perception at all.

The bar chart below encodes the same six categories three different ways. "Color only" is the default most charts ship with. "Texture only" strips away color entirely and relies on SVG <pattern> fills to distinguish categories. "Color + texture" layers both channels together, so each category is redundantly encoded. Toggle between them and notice which version survives the grayscale simulation.

Texture alone can carry categorical information as effectively as color. When you combine both channels, the encoding survives screen, print, and color vision deficiency. The cost is visual noise, so use texture only when it adds information the viewer cannot get otherwise.

Why Five or Six Patterns, Not More

Julesz's texton theory (1981) explains the limit. Texture discrimination is preattentive, driven by three feature classes: elongated blobs (line segments varying in orientation, width, length), terminators (endpoints, crossings), and color/density. Two patterns pop out from each other instantly only if they differ along one of these texton dimensions. The six patterns used above each occupy a different slot: diagonal hatch (orientation at 45 degrees), horizontal lines (0 degrees), dots (blob shape vs. line), crosshatch (crossing terminators), stipple (random density), and vertical lines (90 degrees). A seventh pattern would reuse a dimension and force the viewer to consult the legend. Ware's synthesis adds that orientations need at least 30 degrees of separation to be preattentive, which is why the set uses 0, 45, and 90 rather than finer increments.

Texton theory in more detail

Julesz arrived at textons through a series of experiments where viewers were shown two halves of a texture field and asked whether they could instantly tell the halves apart. The findings were consistent: if the two regions differed in first-order statistics of their textons (the density of oriented line segments, blobs, or terminators), the boundary popped out in under 150 milliseconds, before focal attention could engage. If the regions differed only in second-order statistics (the spatial arrangement of identical textons), discrimination required slow, serial scanning.

This asymmetry has a direct consequence for chart design. Two patterns built from the same texton at the same density but with different spatial arrangements (say, a regular grid of dots versus a jittered grid of dots at the same average spacing) will not pop out from each other in a legend or on a map. The viewer will see them as "the same texture" unless they stare. Conversely, a pattern of short horizontal line segments and a pattern of small circles will separate instantly, because they activate different texton detectors (elongated blob versus compact blob).

Ware's later synthesis incorporated this into a practical hierarchy: the strongest preattentive texture channel is pattern type (lines versus dots versus shapes), followed by orientation (with that 30-degree minimum separation), followed by density. Within a single dimension, you get roughly three to four distinguishable levels before confusions mount. Across dimensions, the levels multiply. This is why a well-chosen six-pattern set works: it draws from three dimensions rather than trying to cram six levels into one.

The SVG Pattern Element

An SVG <pattern> defines a rectangular tile. The browser stamps it out repeatedly to fill any shape that references it via fill="url(#id)". The tile itself can contain any SVG content: lines, circles, paths, even nested groups with transforms.

patternUnits

The patternUnits attribute controls how the tile's coordinate system relates to the filled shape. It takes two values:

userSpaceOnUse fixes the tile to the SVG canvas coordinate system. A tile that is 8 by 8 user units stays 8 by 8 regardless of the shape it fills. Every bar, every polygon, every path gets the same visual density. This is almost always what you want for data visualization. The pattern reads as a consistent texture property, not as a geometric artifact.

objectBoundingBox (the default) maps the tile to the bounding box of the filled shape, where width and height range from 0 to 1. A 200-pixel-wide bar and a 50-pixel-wide bar both get the same number of pattern repetitions, which means the narrow bar's lines are four times closer together. The viewer reads density as data. This is the single most common SVG pattern mistake in generated code, and it produces charts where the texture looks wrong without an obvious reason.

patternTransform

The patternTransform attribute applies an SVG transform to the pattern tile before stamping. The most common use is rotation: patternTransform="rotate(45)" turns a vertical-line tile into a diagonal-hatch tile without changing the geometry inside the <pattern> element. You can also use scale() to adjust density at runtime or translate() to phase-shift the pattern for layered effects.

One subtlety: rotation angles other than 0, 45, and 90 degrees can produce visible seams at tile boundaries, because the rotated tile does not neatly tessellate. If you need a 30-degree hatch, increase the tile size (16 or 20 pixels instead of 8) so seam artifacts are pushed further apart and become less noticeable.

patternContentUnits and viewBox

For most visualization work, you will not need patternContentUnits or a viewBox on the pattern. But they exist for cases where you want to define patterns in a normalized coordinate space and scale them. Setting patternContentUnits="objectBoundingBox" means the marks inside the tile use 0-to-1 coordinates relative to the pattern tile itself. Combined with a fixed patternUnits="userSpaceOnUse" tile size, this lets you define resolution-independent pattern geometry. In practice, it is simpler to just work in user-space pixels.

Canvas Pattern Fills

Canvas 2D has its own pattern primitive: ctx.createPattern(image, repetition). The image argument accepts an HTMLCanvasElement, an HTMLImageElement, an SVGImageElement, or an ImageBitmap. The most flexible approach for data visualization is to draw the tile onto an offscreen canvas, then create a pattern from it.

The tile canvas must be DPR-aware. On a 2x retina display, an 8-by-8 CSS-pixel tile needs a 16-by-16 backing store with ctx.scale(dpr, dpr) applied before drawing. Without this, the pattern looks blurry on high-density screens while everything else in the chart is crisp. The mismatch is subtle but immediately noticeable to designers.

Once created, a CanvasPattern object is GPU-resident in most browser implementations. Creating it is expensive (it involves rasterization and texture upload), but using it as a fillStyle is cheap. This means you should create all your patterns at initialization and reuse them. Changing fillStyle between different CanvasPattern objects flushes the GPU draw buffer, so sort your draw calls by pattern to minimize state changes. If you have 500 shapes across 6 pattern types, draw all shapes of type 1, then all shapes of type 2, rather than alternating.

Canvas patterns support transforms via pattern.setTransform(DOMMatrix). This is the Canvas equivalent of patternTransform in SVG. You can rotate, scale, or translate the tiling origin. One practical use: rotating a horizontal-line tile by 45 degrees to create diagonal hatch without redrawing the tile geometry.

Pattern Density and Discriminability

The spacing between repeated elements in a pattern controls its perceived density, which viewers interpret as an ordered variable: tighter spacing reads as "more" or "heavier." This makes spacing a viable channel for encoding sequential data, but only within a single pattern type. Mixing pattern types (dots at 10px spacing versus lines at 5px spacing) creates a categorical contrast that overrides the density signal.

The figure below shows four pattern types at varying line spacings. Drag the slider to change the spacing and watch how discriminability between patterns changes. At very tight spacings (3 to 4 pixels), all four patterns start to converge into undifferentiated gray. At wide spacings (12 pixels or more), individual lines and dots become visible as discrete marks rather than a texture. The sweet spot for most screen work is 5 to 10 pixels.

Combining Texture with Color for Dual Encoding

The strongest case for texture is not as a replacement for color but as a redundant second channel layered on top of it. Each category gets both a distinct hue and a distinct pattern. On a color display, the viewer uses whichever channel their visual system processes faster (usually color). On a grayscale printout, the patterns carry the entire encoding. For a viewer with deuteranopia, the patterns disambiguate the red-green pairs that color alone cannot separate.

The implementation is straightforward: draw each shape twice. The first layer fills with the category color. The second layer overlays the pattern at reduced opacity (typically 0.4 to 0.6 on light backgrounds). The pattern marks should be dark enough to survive the overlay but not so dark that they muddy the color. The table in Figure 1's color+texture mode demonstrates this: toggling to grayscale shows that the encoding is fully preserved.

When combining texture with a sequential color scale (for example, a choropleth where both color darkness and pattern density increase together), the two channels should reinforce the same ordering. Sparse dots on a light background for the low end; dense crosshatch on a dark background for the high end. If color and texture tell different stories (light color but dense pattern, or dark color but sparse dots), the viewer gets a conflicting signal and has to resolve the contradiction consciously.

Dual encoding is not about doubling the number of variables you can show. It is about making one variable readable across more viewing conditions. Use color and texture for the same variable, not for two different ones.

In SVG, dual encoding means two overlapping <rect> elements (or <path> elements for areas and maps). In Canvas, you draw the color fill first, switch globalCompositeOperation to "multiply" or "source-atop", draw the pattern fill, then reset to "source-over". The multiply blend mode darkens the color fill where the pattern strokes land, which preserves hue while adding texture.

Print Considerations

Screen rendering is forgiving. Printers are not. A 1-pixel line on screen maps to roughly 0.08mm at 300 DPI, which is below the reliable threshold for most inkjet and laser printers. Pattern strokes need a minimum of about 1.5 CSS pixels (0.12mm at 300 DPI) to survive print reliably. At 600 DPI, you can go thinner, but 1.5 pixels is a safe floor for mixed-output work.

Transparency is another print hazard. rgba(0,0,0,0.2) looks great on screen, where the browser composites it against the background in real time. Printers handle transparency inconsistently: some flatten it to a muddy gray, some ignore the alpha entirely, and PDF renderers may rasterize transparent layers at their own chosen resolution. For print targets, use opaque pattern marks. If you need a light pattern on a colored background, compute the composited color in advance and set it as a solid fill.

Tile sizes between 6 and 10 CSS pixels work across both screen and 300 DPI print. Below 6 pixels, the pattern becomes too dense on screen and may create Moire artifacts on print. Above 10 pixels, individual marks become visible as discrete shapes rather than a texture, especially on large printed areas.

Consider adding a @media print rule that boosts pattern contrast or switches entirely to texture-only mode. A chart that uses subtle gray patterns on screen for aesthetic restraint may need bolder marks to survive the print pipeline.

When This Skill Hurts

Failure modes to watch for

Moire patterns. When a repeating SVG or Canvas pattern interacts with the pixel grid of the display, interference fringes appear as shimmering bands or unexpected waves across the fill. This is most visible with fine-pitched patterns (spacing below 5 pixels) at non-integer zoom levels, and it gets worse on low-DPI screens. The pattern looks correct at 100% zoom but develops visible artifacts at 90% or 110%. On print, Moire appears when the pattern frequency conflicts with the halftone screen. The only reliable fix is testing at multiple zoom levels and print scales, and keeping tile sizes large enough that sub-pixel aliasing does not create new visual frequencies.

Texture fighting the data encoding. Diagonal hatching on a line chart with diagonal trend lines creates visual interference: the hatch marks and the data lines run at similar angles, and the viewer cannot separate the two. Horizontal lines in an area fill under a horizontal axis make the fill merge with the axis grid. The pattern's orientation should contrast with the dominant orientation of the data geometry, not echo it. LLMs generating textured charts rarely check this because they select patterns from a fixed list without considering the chart's visual structure.

Performance at scale. Each SVG <pattern> element is cheap to define, but the browser must composite the tiled pattern for every shape that references it. On a choropleth with 3,000 counties, six pattern types mean 3,000 overlapping pattern fills rendering in the DOM. Pan and zoom become sluggish. Firefox is particularly sensitive to this: complex pattern fills on many paths can drop frame rates below 10 FPS. For large-element-count charts, pre-render patterns onto a Canvas layer and use SVG only for interaction targets.

LLM-generated textures that look busy rather than informative. When asked to add texture to a chart, language models tend to apply patterns to every surface at high contrast, producing a chart that looks like a swatch book rather than a data display. The patterns become the dominant visual feature, and the data encoding (bar length, area size, position) recedes into the background. The skill's purpose is to create a second channel that the viewer can use when they need it, not a decorative layer that competes with the primary encoding for attention. The test is simple: cover the legend and look at the chart. If the first thing you notice is the texture rather than the data pattern, the texture is too loud.
