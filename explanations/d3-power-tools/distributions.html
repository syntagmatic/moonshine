The Shape of Data

Why summaries lie and distribution charts tell the truth.

The mean of a dataset is a single number. So is the median. Both pretend to represent the data, but neither describes its shape. A group with values clustered tightly around 50 and a group with two peaks at 30 and 70 can share the same mean and the same median. The summary statistics are identical. The data is not.

Distribution charts exist to solve this problem. Instead of compressing data into one or two numbers, they show you where the values actually fall. But different chart types reveal different things, and some conceal the very features you need to see. A box plot, for example, shows quartiles cleanly but hides bimodality entirely. A violin plot shows the full density shape but obscures individual points.

Figure 1. Four groups with different distribution shapes (normal, bimodal, right-skewed, uniform), each containing 50 data points. Toggle between representations to see what each chart type reveals or conceals. Pay attention to the bimodal group.

The box plot is the most common and the most dangerous. It reduces every distribution to five numbers (minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum) plus outliers. For the bimodal group, it shows a box centered between the two peaks, implying a single cluster where none exists. The violin plot and bee swarm both expose the two peaks clearly. The strip plot hints at it if you look carefully, but random jitter can obscure the gap between peaks.

The ridgeline view stacks the groups vertically, which makes it easy to compare shapes across many groups at once. It is strongest when there are six or more ordered groups, but even with four it shows the contrast between a smooth normal curve and a double-humped bimodal one. The tradeoff is that overlapping rows can occlude each other, and there is no shared y-axis baseline for precise comparison.

Choosing the Right View

Each distribution chart type has a natural habitat. The box plot excels at quick comparison across many groups when the question is about medians and spread. It is the fastest to read when you have ten or twenty groups side by side, because the visual encoding (position of median line, width of box) is simple and consistent. But it answers "where is the center?" and never "what is the shape?" Use it when you already know the distributions are unimodal, or when shape genuinely does not matter for the decision at hand.

The violin plot is the right choice when you suspect the shape carries meaning: bimodality, skewness, heavy tails. A violin shows the full estimated density, so it reveals features that a box plot flattens into a single box. The cost is that it requires more vertical or horizontal space per group, and it relies on KDE, which means bandwidth becomes an editorial decision (more on that below).

The bee swarm is strongest when every individual point matters and the sample is small enough to see them all. With 20 to 200 points per group, a bee swarm shows the actual data without any summary abstraction. You can see gaps, clusters, and outliers without trusting a smoothing algorithm. Beyond about 500 points per group, the dodge layout either takes too long to compute or the points become too small to see individually. At that point, switch to a violin or density plot.

Ridgeline plots work best for comparing shapes across five to eight ordered groups. They use vertical stacking with overlap, which creates a strong gestalt for seeing how a distribution shifts or changes shape across conditions. But the overlap is also their limit: past about fifteen groups, the lower rows become partially occluded by the rows above, and at twenty-plus groups, the chart becomes unreadable.

In practice, the best displays are often layered. A violin with an inner box plot overlay gives you shape and summary in one view. A bee swarm with a median line gives you every point plus a reference for the center. Raincloud plots (half-violin, box, and strip in one) combine all three representations. The key is to layer views that answer different questions rather than repeating the same information.

Bandwidth Shapes What You See

Kernel density estimation turns a collection of raw values into a smooth curve. The bandwidth parameter controls how much smoothing to apply. It is the single most consequential choice in any violin plot, ridgeline, or density chart, and it is almost always set to a default without thought.

A bandwidth that is too narrow treats every data point as its own peak. The curve becomes spiky, suggesting modes and structure that do not exist in the population. A bandwidth that is too wide smooths away real features. A bimodal distribution becomes a single hump. The "truth" is somewhere in between, but the right value depends on the data and the question.

Silverman's rule of thumb provides a reasonable starting point for roughly normal data. It uses the standard deviation and interquartile range to set a bandwidth that balances bias and variance. But for bimodal or heavily skewed data, Silverman over-smooths. In those cases, you need to halve the bandwidth (or use a more adaptive method) to preserve the features you care about.

Figure 2. KDE bandwidth explorer for the bimodal group. At low bandwidth (~1), every point is a spike. At high bandwidth (~12), the two modes merge into one. The Silverman default for this data is around 3.5.

The lesson is that bandwidth is not a technical detail to leave at its default. It is an editorial choice about which features of the data to show the viewer. A chart-maker who uses the default without checking is making an implicit claim that the default reveals the right features. Often it does not.

QQ Plots: Checking the Assumption

Many statistical methods assume the data is normally distributed. A QQ (quantile-quantile) plot tests this assumption visually. It plots the data's quantiles against the quantiles you would expect if the data were perfectly normal. Points that fall on the diagonal line are consistent with normality. Points that curve away from it show how the data departs.

The four groups from Figure 1 make this concrete. The normal group falls roughly along the diagonal. The bimodal group shows a characteristic S-shape, lingering at the bottom-left and top-right where the two modes pull values away from the center. The skewed group curves upward at the right end, indicating a long right tail. The uniform group shows the opposite of heavy tails: it bends inward, because a uniform distribution has less spread in the tails than a normal distribution.

The gray confidence band in the figure above shows where points would fall 95% of the time if the data really were normal. Points inside the band are consistent with normality. Points outside it are evidence of departure. The normal group stays mostly inside. The other three stray well outside.

When This Skill Hurts

Failure modes to watch for

Violin plot fetishism. LLMs love violins because they look sophisticated. But for three groups with ten data points each, a strip plot shows every point while a violin hallucinates a smooth density from almost nothing. KDE with fewer than about 30 points fabricates curves that imply structure the data cannot support. The skill's chart-selection table says this, but in practice the minimum sample size requirement gets ignored because the violin "looks better."

KDE bandwidth as a lie. The bandwidth choice is an editorial decision that can smooth away real bimodality or create phantom peaks from noise. LLMs use default bandwidths without considering whether they are appropriate for the data. Figure 2 above makes this visible: drag the slider to 12 and the bimodal group looks unimodal. Drag it to 1 and a smooth group looks like it has five modes. Both are lies told by the bandwidth parameter, and the default happens to tell one of them.

Ridgeline overload. Ridgelines look great for five to eight groups. The overlapping densities create a compelling visual rhythm. But the skill does not emphasize that at twenty-plus groups, the overlap becomes unreadable. The lower rows are occluded. The y-axis labels stack so densely they cannot be read. LLMs scale up linearly without checking legibility, producing ridgeline charts with thirty or forty rows that communicate nothing.

Bee swarm performance. The dodge algorithm for bee swarm placement is fast for small datasets, but forceSimulation-based alternatives are O(n log n) per tick. At 500+ points per group, a force-based bee swarm visibly stutters on initial load. The skill recommends the deterministic dodge algorithm and pre-computed layouts for static displays, but LLMs often reach for forceSimulation by default because it appears in more tutorials.
