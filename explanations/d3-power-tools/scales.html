Scales

The scale you choose determines what relationships the viewer perceives.

A scale is a function that maps data values to pixel positions. That sounds mechanical, but the choice of mapping is editorial. Linear, logarithmic, and square-root scales applied to the same numbers tell three different stories. The viewer trusts the axis, so the scale author is responsible for what it reveals and what it hides.

The chart below shows populations of 20 countries spanning three orders of magnitude, from roughly 100,000 to over 1 billion. Toggle between scale types and watch how the bars reshape. On a linear scale, small countries vanish. A log scale spreads them across the full width, revealing structure at every magnitude. Square root lands in between.

Every scale choice is a claim about what matters. Linear says "absolute size is the story." Log says "relative order of magnitude is the story." If you haven't decided what the story is, you haven't finished the design.

Why the Scale Matters

On the linear scale above, India and China consume almost the entire chart. Countries below 10 million are nearly invisible, their bars a few pixels wide at most. The viewer gets one fact: two countries are enormous. On the log scale, every country gets a readable bar because the axis treats each tenfold increase as an equal step. You can now see that Iceland is roughly ten times smaller than Estonia, which is ten times smaller than Sweden. On the sqrt scale, small values get more room than linear gives them, but large values still dominate more than log allows. It is a useful middle ground when you want to preserve some sense of absolute size while not crushing the bottom of the range.

Tick formatting is a separate editorial choice. Compact labels like "100M" reduce clutter and let the viewer scan quickly. Full labels like "100,000,000" are precise but dense. The right pick depends on your audience. The width slider demonstrates responsive ticks: as the chart shrinks, D3 reduces tick count automatically so labels never overlap. The code divides available pixels by about 80 to decide how many ticks fit, then lets d3.axis pick "nice" round values near that count.

Broken Axes

Sometimes one value is so extreme that a linear scale crushes everything else into a sliver. A company's revenue chart where one quarter spiked 10x due to an acquisition. A sensor dataset with one reading at 9000 and everything else between 20 and 80. The temptation is to break the axis: split it into two segments with a zigzag gap symbol between them, so the outlier gets its own compressed region while the rest of the data breathes.

The technique works by building a piecewise scale. Each segment maps a portion of the data range to a portion of the pixel range, and the break symbol tells the viewer that the space between segments is not proportional. D3 does not provide a built-in broken scale, but you can compose one from two scaleLinear instances that share the same output axis.

The danger is real. A broken axis makes 900 look visually close to 50, which it is not. If the outlier is the story ("one quarter was ten times larger"), breaking the axis minimizes the shock. In that case, a log scale or a simple annotation calling out the extreme value does the job without deception. Reserve broken axes for when the outlier is context, not content: the reader needs to see fine structure in the normal range, and the outlier must appear only so they know it exists.

The Dual-Y-Axis Controversy

Two datasets, two units, one chart. Temperature on the left axis, rainfall on the right. Revenue on the left, headcount on the right. Dual-y-axis charts are everywhere in business dashboards and weather reports, and they are one of the most reliably misleading chart types in common use.

The problem is not technical. D3 makes it easy to put two scales on opposite sides of a chart. The problem is perceptual. Viewers naturally assume that when two lines cross, something meaningful happened. But the crossing point is an artifact of where you set each scale's domain. Shift the right axis up by 10% and the lines cross in a different month. The chart author controls where the "correlation" appears, and the viewer has no way to detect the manipulation.

The dual-y chart's fundamental flaw is that the relationship between the two scales is arbitrary. You can make any two series appear to correlate or diverge by adjusting one axis range. If the goal is to show correlation, use a scatter plot. If the goal is to show two trends in context, use small multiples with a shared time axis.

There are defensible uses. When both series share the same conceptual baseline (zero) and the audience understands they are looking at different units, a dual-y chart can save space. Color-code each axis to match its series. Force both domains to start at zero. And never adjust one scale to "line up" with the other. The moment you find yourself tuning a scale range to make two lines converge, you are fabricating a visual argument.

Time Gaps

A scaleUtc maps time uniformly. Every hour gets the same number of pixels whether anything happened during that hour or not. For financial data, this means weekends and holidays consume roughly 28% of the x-axis showing empty space. The viewer sees a cliff where Friday afternoon meets Monday morning, and the visual gap implies something dramatic happened over the weekend when nothing traded at all.

The standard fix is to treat each trading day as a categorical position using scaleBand. No gaps, equal spacing, and the axis only shows days where data exists. For line charts where you do not need band width, an index-based scaleLinear mapping data index to pixels achieves the same effect. Both approaches sacrifice proportional time spacing. A three-day holiday looks the same as a regular weekend. If that matters (interest accrual, decay calculations), keep scaleUtc and mark the non-trading periods with shaded regions so the viewer knows the gap is intentional.

The deeper question is whether the gap itself carries meaning. Sensor outages, market halts, and communication blackouts are all gaps where the absence of data is the story. Hiding those gaps hides the story. Before removing time gaps, ask what the viewer loses by not seeing them.

Label Collision

Labels overlap when there are too many of them for the available space. This is the most common axis legibility problem, and it gets worse as charts become responsive. A bar chart with 20 categories looks fine at 700 pixels wide and becomes unreadable at 350. The labels do not just overlap; they become a dark smear of ink that the viewer's eye skips entirely.

The fix is not a single technique but a hierarchy of strategies, applied in order from lightest touch to most invasive. Fewer ticks is always the first move. If a numeric axis has 12 ticks and 8 would be legible, reduce the count. If a categorical axis has 30 labels and every third one would suffice, filter them. After that comes truncation with tooltips for the full text. Then staggering, where alternating labels drop to a second line. And finally rotation, the last resort, because rotated text is measurably slower to read than horizontal text.

If you find yourself rotating labels on every chart, the real problem is usually too many categories for the chart width, and a horizontal bar chart or a scrollable container would serve the reader better.

When This Skill Hurts

Failure modes to watch for

Log scale on data with zeros. scaleLog maps through the logarithm function, and log(0) is negative infinity. D3 does not throw an error. It silently returns NaN, and your data points vanish from the chart without any visible warning. The fix is scaleSymlog, which handles zeros and negatives through a sinh-based transform. But LLMs reach for scaleLog by default because it appears more often in examples, and they rarely check whether the dataset contains zeros before choosing it. The result is charts with mysteriously missing data that pass code review because the code looks correct.

LLMs generating dual-y-axis charts that imply false correlation. When a prompt asks to "show revenue and headcount together," the easiest answer is a dual-y chart. An LLM will produce one without hesitation. But the scale ranges it picks are arbitrary, and the visual relationship between the two lines is meaningless. Worse, the LLM will often tune the right axis range so the two lines track each other, because that "looks right." The result is a chart that manufactures a visual correlation between two variables that may have no relationship at all. A scatter plot or small multiples with shared time axis would answer the same question honestly.

Scale type chosen for aesthetics, not data properties. Log scales spread data out and make charts look more "interesting" because values at different magnitudes become visible. But a log scale on data that spans a single order of magnitude distorts proportional comparisons without adding information. Similarly, sqrt scales appear as a sophisticated middle ground when in fact they warp distance perception in ways that make bar length non-proportional. Every scale transform should be justified by a property of the data (range spans multiple orders of magnitude, values cluster near zero), not by how the chart looks.

Responsive tick counts that produce 1 or 0 ticks at narrow widths. The standard formula divides available pixels by a per-tick budget (60 to 100 pixels). At narrow container widths, the math bottoms out. A 200-pixel-wide chart with 100 pixels per tick gets two ticks. At 150 pixels, one tick. At smaller widths, zero. A single tick is useless because it provides no reference for comparison. Zero ticks leaves the viewer with an axis line and no labels at all. The fix is a floor: Math.max(2, Math.floor(innerWidth / 80)). But LLMs often omit the floor, and the chart looks fine during development at desktop width. The problem only surfaces when someone opens it on a phone.
