AI-Assisted Annotation

LLM reads the chart, identifies notable features, and generates annotation callouts automatically. The author curates rather than creates from scratch.

The skipped step

Annotation is where data becomes story. A bare line chart says "here is the data." An annotated one says "here is what matters about the data, and why." The annotation layer is what separates a chart that gets glanced at from one that actually teaches something. It is the interpretive bridge between visual pattern and human understanding.

It is also the step most often skipped.

Not because it is hard to draw an arrow pointing at a spike, but because the cognitive work comes before the drawing: you have to look at the chart, decide what is notable, figure out what to say about it, and judge what your audience will find helpful versus obvious. That deliberation takes real effort. The actual mechanics of placing a callout at pixel coordinate (342, 87) with a text label and a connector line are tedious but straightforward. The editorial judgment that precedes it is the bottleneck.

What if an LLM could handle the first pass? Not replacing the editorial judgment, but giving you a draft of it. The model reads the chart, identifies candidates for annotation (the spike, the trend change, the curious plateau), and produces a set of suggestions. The author reviews, adjusts, and publishes. Curation instead of creation.

The pipeline

The automated annotation pipeline has five stages. The chart exists first as a normal D3 visualization. Then we capture it, analyze it, generate structured specs, and render the annotations back onto the original.

Figure 1. The full annotation pipeline. The LLM sits in the middle, receiving both the visual (screenshot) and the structured (data) representations. It returns structured annotation specs, not pixel drawings.

The dual-input design matters. A screenshot alone gives the LLM the same information a human viewer has: the shape of the chart, the visual salience of features, the spatial relationships between elements. But screenshots are lossy. The model cannot reliably read axis labels at small font sizes, or distinguish a value of 47.3 from 48.1. Sending the underlying data alongside the image lets the LLM ground its visual observations in exact values.

The output is not an image with arrows drawn on it. It is structured annotation specs: objects with types, positions, text, and priorities. These get fed back to D3, which renders them as proper SVG elements on the existing chart. This keeps the annotations resolution-independent, stylistically consistent, and editable.

What the LLM can see

Given a chart screenshot and its backing data, an LLM can reliably identify several categories of notable features:

Outliers. Points that sit far from their neighbors. A spike in a time series, a dot isolated from a cluster in a scatterplot. These are visually salient and statistically identifiable from the data alone, so the model gets strong signal from both channels.

Trend changes. Moments where the direction shifts. A series that was climbing and starts to fall. A growth rate that suddenly accelerates. The visual shape of the line makes these easy to spot, and the data confirms the inflection point.

Local extrema. Peaks and troughs. The highest point in a series, the lowest month of the year. These are among the most useful annotations because readers often want to know "when was it highest?" and the chart does not answer that question until you label the peak.

Crossover points. In multi-series charts, the moment where one line overtakes another. These often correspond to meaningful transitions: the quarter when mobile traffic surpassed desktop, or when renewable energy generation exceeded coal.

Flat periods. Sustained plateaus or periods of low variance. These are interesting precisely because they contrast with movement elsewhere in the data. "Nothing happened for six months" can be just as notable as a spike, depending on context.

Gaps and discontinuities. Missing data ranges, sudden jumps between adjacent points. These often signal real-world events (a sensor going offline, a policy change, a pandemic) and benefit from explicit labels.

The annotation spec

The LLM returns a JSON array of annotation objects. Each one has a type, a target (where on the chart it points), text (what it says), and a priority (how important the model thinks it is). The priority field is critical for filtering: the LLM might identify a dozen features, but a well-annotated chart usually has three or four callouts at most.

[
  {
    "type": "callout",       // callout | reference | region
    "target": { "x": "2024-08", "y": 312 },
    "text": "Peak in August after product launch",
    "priority": 1
  },
  {
    "type": "reference",
    "target": { "y": 150 },         // horizontal reference line
    "text": "Industry average",
    "priority": 3
  },
  {
    "type": "region",
    "target": { "x1": "2024-03", "x2": "2024-06" },
    "text": "Flat period before launch",
    "priority": 2
  }
]

Three annotation types cover most needs:

TypeTargetRenders as
callout{x, y}Point marker + connector line + text label
reference{y} or {x}Dashed line across the chart + label
region{x1, x2}Shaded vertical band + label

Priorities run from 1 (most important) to 5 (marginal). A simple filtering rule: show everything at priority 1 and 2, let the user opt in to 3+. This prevents the common failure mode where the model annotates everything it can see.

What LLMs struggle with

The list of things LLMs can identify is encouraging. The list of things they do poorly is where the design gets interesting.

Pixel precision. When an LLM looks at a screenshot and says "the spike is at approximately x=340, y=85," that coordinate is approximate. It might be off by 20 or 30 pixels. This is why the annotation spec uses data coordinates ("2024-08") rather than pixel positions. The LLM identifies the feature in data space, and D3's scales handle the pixel mapping. Never ask an LLM to return pixel coordinates.

Distinguishing signal from artifact. Anti-aliasing at chart edges, slight wobble in a line due to many close data points, a gap in rendering that is not a gap in data. These visual artifacts can look like features to a model working from a screenshot. The data channel helps here: if the model thinks it sees a dip in the line but the data shows no corresponding decrease, the data should win.

Stating the obvious. "The line goes up in 2024" is technically an observation about the chart. It is not a useful annotation. LLMs tend to describe what they see without filtering for whether the observation adds value. A good annotation says something the reader would not get from simply looking at the chart. "Revenue grew 34% after the pricing change in March" is useful. "Revenue increased" is not. Prompt engineering helps, but this failure mode is persistent.

Audience calibration. What counts as notable depends on who is reading. An executive summary might annotate the quarterly trend. A technical deep-dive might call out the anomaly on March 14th. The LLM does not know the audience unless you tell it, and even then, its judgment about what a particular audience cares about is approximate. This is the strongest argument for the curation model: the author knows their audience. The LLM provides the raw material.

Suggest and curate, not generate and accept

The right mental model for AI-assisted annotation is a knowledgeable colleague looking over your shoulder. They point at the chart and say: "That spike in August looks important. Also, the flat stretch from March to June is interesting. Oh, and this point here is a local minimum." You nod at two of the three, rephrase the text, and move on.

You would not hand them the keyboard and walk away. And you would not blindly accept all their suggestions. The value is in having someone identify candidates so you can spend your editorial energy on selection and refinement rather than discovery.

The curation ratio. In practice, an LLM suggesting 5 annotations where the author keeps 2 or 3 is the sweet spot. If the author keeps all 5, the model is not suggesting enough variety. If the author keeps 0, the model does not understand the chart or the audience. The ratio of accepted to suggested is a useful quality signal.

This pattern extends beyond annotation. It is a general model for LLM-human collaboration in visualization: the machine drafts, the human curates. The machine's advantage is speed and exhaustiveness (it will not miss the outlier buried in month 17). The human's advantage is judgment and context (they know the outlier in month 17 was a data entry error and should not be highlighted).

Integration with moonshine

In the moonshine workflow, the /shine command runs iterative refinement on a block. An annotation suggestion step could fit naturally into this pipeline. After the chart is rendered and passes visual inspection, the system takes a screenshot, sends it along with the data to the LLM, and gets back annotation candidates. These appear in the iteration log for the author to review.

The d3-power-tools annotation skill already provides the rendering side: it knows how to draw callouts, reference lines, and highlighted regions with proper connector lines and label placement. What it lacks is the discovery side. The skill tells you how to annotate. The AI-assisted annotation layer tells you what to annotate.

Combining the two means an author could build a chart, ask the system "what should I annotate?", pick the best suggestions, and have them rendered using battle-tested annotation patterns. The entire round trip, from bare chart to annotated explanation, collapses from an hour of deliberation to a few minutes of curation.

The annotation spec in practice

A more complete spec would include styling hints and collision avoidance metadata. Here is what a production-quality annotation object might look like:

{
  "type": "callout",
  "target": { "x": "2024-08", "y": 312 },
  "text": "Peak at 312, up 58% from July",
  "priority": 1,
  "confidence": 0.92,
  "label_position": "above-right",   // hint for placement
  "reasoning": "Largest month-over-month increase in the series",
  "feature_type": "local_maximum"   // semantic category
}

The confidence field reflects the model's certainty that this feature is genuinely notable. The reasoning field explains why the model flagged it, which helps the author evaluate the suggestion without re-analyzing the data themselves. The feature_type provides a machine-readable category for downstream filtering (show me only trend changes, hide all outliers).

The label_position hint is advisory. D3's annotation renderer should run its own collision detection and may override the model's suggestion. LLMs are not reliable at spatial layout reasoning, and a label placed "above-right" might overlap with an axis label or another annotation. Let the rendering layer handle final placement.

Priority and filtering

An LLM analyzing a detailed chart will find many things to say. A 24-month time series might yield 15 candidate annotations: every local maximum, every trend change, every month where the value crossed a round number. Showing all 15 would produce an unreadable chart.

Filtering by priority is the first defense. But priority assignment by the LLM is itself imperfect. The model might assign priority 1 to "the line reaches its peak" and also to "the line reaches its trough," when in context only one of those matters to the story the author is telling.

A better approach layers three filters:

Model priority. The LLM's own ranking, based on statistical salience and visual prominence. Good for an initial sort. Not sufficient alone.

Feature deduplication. If the model suggests both "local maximum in August" and "spike in August at 312," those are the same observation in different words. A deduplication pass, comparing target positions and feature types, can collapse them into a single suggestion with the better text.

Author intent. The final filter. If the author's prompt says "this chart is about the recovery in Q4," then annotations about the Q1 dip become less relevant. Incorporating a brief statement of purpose into the LLM prompt lets it calibrate its priorities toward the story the author wants to tell.

Where this can go wrong

  • Over-annotation. The most common failure. The LLM treats every visible feature as notable. A chart with 8 callouts, 3 reference lines, and 2 shaded regions is worse than one with no annotations at all. The UI should enforce soft limits and warn the author when annotation density gets high.
  • Imprecise coordinates. Even with data-space targeting, the LLM might misidentify which month a spike occurs in when reading from a screenshot. Always validate annotation targets against the actual data before rendering.
  • Describing the obvious. "Sales increased" on a line that visibly goes up. "This is the highest point" on the tallest bar. Annotations that add no information beyond what the chart already communicates. The prompt should explicitly instruct the model to add context the reader would not get from looking at the chart alone.
  • The editorial judgment gap. The LLM does not know that the spike in August was caused by a bug in the tracking code, or that the flat period in Q2 was during a leadership transition. Domain context shapes what is worth annotating, and the model does not have it unless you provide it. Annotations without editorial judgment are factually correct but narratively hollow.
  • Audience mismatch. An annotation that explains what a moving average is might be perfect for a general audience and insulting to a data science team. The model defaults to a generic middle ground that serves nobody well. Specifying the audience in the prompt helps but does not solve this fully.

From annotation to narrative

Annotation is the entry point to a larger question: can LLMs help construct the narrative layer of a visualization? Annotations are atomic, pointing at individual features. Narrative is sequential, connecting those features into a story: "The market was flat through the first half of the year, then surged in August on strong earnings, before settling into a new equilibrium."

If the LLM can identify the flat period, the surge, and the equilibrium as separate annotatable features, it can also draft the connective tissue between them. This is the path from annotation suggestion to narrative generation: not just "here are the notable features" but "here is how they relate to each other and what they collectively mean."

That is a harder problem, and the failure modes multiply. But the annotation pipeline is the foundation. Get the atomic observations right, get the curation model working, and narrative assistance becomes a natural extension.
