Future Directions

Explanation Effectiveness

Measuring whether an interactive explanation actually teaches. Pre/post comprehension, time-to-insight, misconception detection, and the gap between engagement and understanding.

Engagement is not comprehension

Every analytics platform will tell you how long a reader spent on the page, how far they scrolled, how many times they clicked. These numbers measure engagement. They do not measure whether anyone learned anything.

A reader can spend twelve minutes on an interactive explanation, click every button, drag every slider, scroll to the bottom, and leave with the same misconceptions they arrived with. The explanation was engaging. It was not effective. Worse, the reader now feels confident about something they still do not understand, because the act of interacting felt like the act of learning.

The first twenty directions in this series build explanations that are collaborative, adaptive, accessible, multilingual, embeddable. All of that work assumes the explanation works. This direction asks the question that precedes all others: how would you know?

The core problem: We have sophisticated tools for measuring whether someone looked at an explanation. We have almost no tools for measuring whether they understood it. The gap between those two capabilities is the gap between analytics and education research.

Figure 2. Hospital B is better at both routine surgeries (98% vs 95%) and complex surgeries (84.3% vs 70%). It only looks worse overall because it takes on far more complex cases. This is Simpson's paradox.

Hospital B has a higher survival rate for routine surgeries (98% vs 95%) and a higher survival rate for complex surgeries (84.3% vs 70%). But its overall rate is lower because it handles four times as many complex cases. The aggregate statistic inverts the conclusion that every subgroup supports.

This is Simpson's paradox: a trend that appears in aggregated data reverses when the data is disaggregated into meaningful subgroups. It is not a mathematical curiosity. It appears in real medical studies, in university admissions data, in batting averages, in treatment efficacy trials. Wherever a confounding variable controls the mix of subgroups, aggregation can lie.

Four approaches to measuring effectiveness

If analytics cannot tell us whether an explanation worked, what can? Education research offers four main strategies, each with its own tradeoffs.

1. Pre/post comprehension testing

Ask a question before the explanation. Ask a related question after. The difference is the learning delta. You just experienced this above. The pre-test captures prior knowledge. The post-test captures what the explanation added. The delta isolates the explanation's contribution.

The challenge is writing good questions. A question that is too close to the explanation tests recall. A question that requires transfer to a new context (like the school district scenario above) tests understanding. The distinction matters enormously: a reader can memorize "Hospital B is better" without understanding why aggregate statistics can mislead. Transfer questions reveal whether the concept generalized.

Question typeMeasuresExample
RecallMemory"Which hospital had the higher survival rate for complex cases?"
ComprehensionUnderstanding"Why does the aggregate rate disagree with the subgroup rates?"
TransferGeneralization"A batting average paradox appears in baseball stats. How?"
ApplicationSkill"Given this new dataset, identify whether Simpson's paradox is present."

2. Time-to-insight

How long does it take for a reader to find the key pattern? In an interactive explanation, you can instrument the moment of insight. For the Simpson's paradox exploration above, insight arrives when the reader toggles the group coloring and sees the trend lines reverse. The timestamp of that interaction, relative to when the section loaded, is the time-to-insight.

Shorter is better, but with a caveat. A reader who takes two seconds may have already known the concept. A reader who takes two minutes may be genuinely learning. Time-to-insight is most useful in aggregate, across many readers, as a comparison metric between two different explanation strategies for the same concept.

3. Misconception detection

Certain interactions reveal misunderstanding. If a reader repeatedly hovers over the wrong column in the data table, they may be looking at the aggregate instead of the subgroup. If they answer the post-test by choosing District X, they are applying the same logic that Simpson's paradox exposes as flawed. These are not random errors. They are diagnostic.

A misconception detector watches for known error patterns and can intervene. Not with a correction (that feels like a test) but with a nudge: "Try toggling the group coloring on and off" or "Look at the survival rate for complex surgeries specifically." The explanation adapts to what the reader does not yet understand.

4. Longitudinal retention

Does the reader remember the insight a week later? This is the hardest measurement because it requires the reader to come back. Few do. Strategies include follow-up emails with a single question, spaced repetition prompts, or embedding retention tests into subsequent explanations in the same series. None of these are easy. All of them are more informative than scroll depth.

The proxy problem

The bottom row is the only one with strong correlation, and it is also the only one that requires designing a test, not just installing a tracking script. This asymmetry explains why engagement metrics dominate: they require zero pedagogical work. They are also nearly useless for evaluating whether an explanation teaches.

The illusion of understanding

Interactive explanations face a specific failure mode that static text does not: the illusion of understanding. When a reader drags a slider and watches a chart update, the visual feedback creates a sense of control and comprehension. "I moved the parameter. I saw what happened. I get it." But understanding requires more than observation. It requires the ability to predict what will happen with a new input, to explain the mechanism, to recognize the same pattern in an unfamiliar context.

This is not speculation. Research on "desirable difficulties" in learning shows that making things feel harder (retrieval practice, spaced repetition, interleaving) actually produces better long-term retention than making things feel easy (rereading, highlighting, fluent presentation). An interactive explanation that feels effortless may teach less than a static explanation that forces the reader to pause and think.

The uncomfortable implication: Optimizing for a smooth, delightful interactive experience may actively work against comprehension. The moments of confusion, the pauses where the reader has to think, the friction of not immediately understanding the chart: these may be where the learning actually happens.

A/B testing explanations

If you have pre/post testing, you can run controlled experiments. Show half your readers Explanation A (a scrollytelling narrative) and the other half Explanation B (an exploratory sandbox). Measure learning delta for both groups. The explanation that produces the larger delta is more effective, regardless of which one gets more clicks or higher time-on-page.

This is how education research has worked for decades. It is vanishingly rare in data visualization. Most interactive explanations are evaluated on engagement metrics (if they are evaluated at all), never on comprehension. The tooling gap is real: there is no Google Analytics for understanding.

Connection to autoresearch

The d3-power-tools iterate pipeline generates explanations and evaluates them with an automated audit. The audit measures visual quality, code correctness, design coherence. These are all properties of the explanation as an artifact. None of them measure what happens in the reader's mind.

What if the iterate loop optimized for measured comprehension instead? You would need a concept to explain, a pre-test, multiple candidate explanations generated by the proposer, and a post-test administered to real readers. The explanation that produces the highest learning delta wins, and the iterate loop generates more variations in that direction.

This is expensive. Each iteration requires human readers, not just automated audits. But it is the only approach that optimizes for the thing that actually matters. A hybrid is possible: use automated audits for visual quality (fast, cheap) and reserve human comprehension testing for the top candidates (slow, expensive, decisive).

// Hypothetical iterate loop with comprehension testing
for (const candidate of proposer.generate(concept, 5)) {
  const auditScore = await visualAudit(candidate);   // fast, automated
  if (auditScore < threshold) continue;          // gate: must look good first

  const delta = await comprehensionTest(candidate, {
    preTest: conceptQuiz.pre,
    postTest: conceptQuiz.post,
    readers: 30,                              // minimum for statistical power
  });
  candidate.effectivenessScore = delta.mean;   // the metric that matters
}

Connection to moonshine

Moonshine's premise is that starting from a real story in real data produces better explanations than starting from a visualization technique and looking for data to showcase it. Effectiveness measurement is the feedback signal that validates (or refutes) this premise.

If moonshine explanations consistently produce higher learning deltas than technique-first explanations, the approach is vindicated. If they do not, the approach needs revision. Without effectiveness measurement, the premise remains an assertion. With it, the premise becomes a testable hypothesis.

This is also the path to self-improvement. Each moonshine explanation ships with a pre-test and a post-test. Each reader's delta feeds back into a dataset of explanation effectiveness. Over time, patterns emerge: which narrative structures produce the highest deltas? Which interaction modalities? Which levels of complexity? The answers will not come from design intuition. They will come from measurement.

Risks and tensions

  • Testing fatigue. Quizzes feel like school, not exploration. A pre-test at the top of every explanation will drive readers away. The test must be woven into the experience so naturally that answering feels like participating, not being evaluated. The hospital question above attempted this, but the format still reads as a quiz.
  • Measurement changes behavior. Readers who know they are being tested optimize for the test, not for understanding. This is Goodhart's Law applied to education: once the delta becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Subtle testing (embedded questions that feel like exploration) partially addresses this, but never fully.
  • Sample size. One reader's comprehension test is noise. You need hundreds for statistical significance. Most explanations do not have hundreds of readers. A/B testing two explanation strategies doubles the required audience. For niche technical topics, this may be impossible.
  • Privacy. Tracking comprehension is more personal than tracking clicks. "This reader did not understand the concept after the explanation" is sensitive data. Aggregated and anonymized metrics are the minimum standard. Even then, consent should be explicit.
  • Optimizing for tests vs. understanding. A good test measures understanding. But any fixed test can be gamed. If the iterate loop optimizes for a specific post-test, it may produce explanations that teach to that test rather than building genuine understanding. The post-test must be varied, unpredictable, and focused on transfer.

What this means in practice

Effectiveness measurement is the hardest problem in the entire moonshine vision because it requires bridging two disciplines that rarely talk to each other. Data visualization is a design field. Learning assessment is an education field. The tools, methods, vocabulary, and evaluation criteria are almost entirely disjoint.

The practical starting point is modest: embed one pre-question and one post-question in each explanation. Measure the delta. Collect enough data to be meaningful. Resist the temptation to treat engagement metrics as proxies. Report the results honestly, including when the explanation fails to teach.

That last part is the hardest of all. It is much easier to report time-on-page (always flattering) than comprehension delta (sometimes zero). But zero is the most informative number an explanation can produce, because it is the number that tells you where to invest your effort next.
