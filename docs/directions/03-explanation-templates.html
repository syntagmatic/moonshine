Explanation Templates

Most interactive explanations reinvent the same handful of structural patterns. What if we named them, built scaffolding for each, and let authors focus on content?

Open ten interactive explanations at random. Strip away the specifics: the datasets, the color palettes, the domain jargon. Look at the skeleton. You will find the same four or five structures, repeated with minor variations.

One explanation has a slider that updates a chart. Another puts two visualizations side by side and highlights their differences. A third builds up complexity in layers, adding one element at a time. A fourth is a chart with prose callouts that point at notable features. A fifth lets the reader drag variables onto encoding channels and explore freely.

These are not random similarities. They reflect the small number of questions that explanations exist to answer. "What happens when I change this?" leads to a parameter explorer. "How do these two things differ?" leads to compare-and-contrast. "What is this made of?" leads to progressive disclosure. "What should I notice here?" leads to annotation-driven narrative. "What can I discover on my own?" leads to build-your-own exploration.

The structural patterns of interactive explanations are not infinite. They cluster around a handful of archetypes, each corresponding to a distinct type of question the reader wants answered. A template library would name these archetypes, provide scaffolding for each, and let authors spend their time on content rather than rebuilding the same interaction plumbing.

This is a direction for d3-power-tools: a library of explanation templates, one per archetype, that handle the structural boilerplate while staying out of the way of the specific content. Not components (those are visual primitives like axes and legends). Templates are structural: they define where the controls go, how state flows, and what the interaction loop looks like. The author fills in the domain-specific parts.

Archetype 1: Parameter Explorer

"What happens when I change X?" is the most common question in interactive explanation. The parameter explorer answers it with immediate, continuous feedback: drag a slider, see the chart update. The structure is simple. A control region (sliders, toggles, numeric inputs) sits adjacent to a visualization. Every control maps to a parameter. Every parameter change triggers a re-render. The loop is tight: adjust, observe, adjust again.

This archetype appears everywhere. Learning rate schedules in ML explanations. Filter cutoff frequencies in signal processing. Tax brackets in policy analysis. Physics simulations. Any time the explanation's point is "the relationship between this input and that output is nonlinear and surprising," a parameter explorer is the right structure.

The template handles: laying out controls alongside the chart, binding each control to a state variable, debouncing rapid input, and providing a reset button. The author provides: the chart rendering function, the parameter definitions (name, range, default), and optionally a description of what each parameter does.

Archetype 2: Compare and Contrast

"How do A and B differ?" demands juxtaposition. The compare-and-contrast archetype places two (or more) conditions side by side and provides tools for spotting differences. This can be literal side-by-side charts, a toggle that swaps between conditions, or an overlay that highlights where conditions diverge.

The miniature demo above shows two groups of bars. In "both" mode, you see them side by side. In "differences" mode, the bars that differ most between groups are highlighted. This simple mechanism turns a passive comparison into an active one: the reader does not have to scan for differences; the structure surfaces them.

Algorithm comparisons (quicksort vs. mergesort), before/after interventions, treatment vs. control in clinical trials, and historical changes over time all use this archetype. The template handles: synchronized scales, alignment of corresponding elements, difference highlighting, and the toggle between modes. The author provides: the two (or N) datasets or conditions, and the comparison metric.

Archetype 3: Progressive Disclosure

"Build up complexity layer by layer" is the pedagogical workhorse. You start with something simple (just the axes, or just the raw points) and add complexity one piece at a time. Each layer makes sense because the reader already understands everything beneath it.

Fourier decomposition is the canonical example: start with the fundamental, add the first harmonic, then the second, and watch the approximation converge. Neural network explanations use this structure too: start with a single neuron, add a layer, add activation functions, add backpropagation. Map-based explanations layer geographic features: terrain, then roads, then demographic data, then the analytical overlay.

The template handles: the layer stack and ordering, enter/exit animations for each layer, the buttons or scroll triggers that advance the stack, and the ability to go backward (removing layers is as important as adding them). The author provides: the layer definitions and their rendering functions.

Archetype 4: Annotation-Driven Narrative

"Here is what to notice" is the structure of data journalism. The chart exists as a canvas, and the prose points at specific features: this outlier, that trend, the gap between these two clusters. The annotation is the argument; the chart is the evidence.

This is the archetype that best maps to traditional writing. The author has a point to make, and the interactive element is that the reader can see which part of the data supports each claim. Hover over a sentence, and the relevant data points highlight. Click on a callout, and the chart zooms to the relevant region. The interaction is not exploratory; it is guided. The author controls the narrative.

The template handles: positioning callout annotations relative to data coordinates, managing focus (which annotation is active), coordinating prose and chart highlighting, and responsive repositioning when the chart resizes. The author provides: the annotations (text, target coordinates, optional styling) and the chart itself.

Archetype 5: Build Your Own

"Assemble an explanation from pieces" is the most open-ended archetype. Instead of the author prescribing the view, the reader constructs it. Assign a variable to the x-axis, another to the y-axis, choose a color encoding, toggle between chart types. This is exploratory data analysis dressed up as an explanation.

The risk is that the reader gets lost. Without guidance, a build-your-own tool is just a chart builder, and chart builders are not explanations. The archetype works best when there are guardrails: a curated set of variables, sensible defaults, and optional "suggested views" that the author has pre-selected as particularly revealing. The freedom is real, but the space is bounded.

The template handles: the encoding assignment UI (dropdowns, drag targets), re-rendering on encoding changes, scale inference from data types, and suggested-view presets. The author provides: the dataset, the variable metadata (name, type, description), and the preset configurations.

Story discovery questions map to archetypes

Moonshine's story discovery process begins with questions. What story does this data tell? What should the reader take away? What is surprising? These questions are not random. They cluster, and the clusters align with the five archetypes.

"What happens when..." is a causal or parametric question. It leads to a parameter explorer. The story is about a relationship between input and output, and the reader needs to feel that relationship through direct manipulation.

"How do these differ..." is a comparative question. It leads to compare-and-contrast. The story is about the gap between two conditions, and the reader needs juxtaposition to see it.

"What is this made of..." is a compositional question. It leads to progressive disclosure. The story is about how simple parts combine into complex wholes, and the reader needs to see the buildup.

"What should I notice..." is an editorial question. It leads to annotation-driven narrative. The story is about specific features in the data, and the reader needs the author to point at them.

"What can I find..." is an exploratory question. It leads to build-your-own. The story is that there is no single story; the data has multiple facets, and the reader should discover the one that matters to them.

If the story discovery question type determines the archetype, then an LLM that understands the question can select the right template before generating any code. This collapses a large design decision (what structure should this explanation have?) into a classification problem (what type of question is this explanation answering?).

Templates are not components

This distinction matters because the word "template" gets overloaded. A component is a visual primitive: an axis, a legend, a tooltip, a brush. Components are the bricks. A template is a floor plan: it says where the bricks go, how they connect, and what the interaction flow looks like. You can swap the bricks (use a different chart type, a different control widget) without changing the template. And you can reuse the same bricks across different templates.

A parameter explorer template does not care whether the chart is a line chart, a scatter plot, or a heatmap. It cares that there is a control region and a chart region, that controls map to state variables, and that state changes trigger re-renders. A compare-and-contrast template does not care whether the comparison is between bar charts, maps, or network diagrams. It cares that there are two (or N) renderings of different conditions, that they share scales, and that the toggle between modes is smooth.

This separation means templates compose with the existing d3-power-tools component library. Use the axis component, the legend component, the tooltip component inside any template. The template provides the structural scaffolding; the components provide the visual elements. Neither knows about the other's internals.

The reusability-specificity tension

Every template is a bet that the archetype is general enough to cover many explanations but specific enough to provide real scaffolding. Make the template too general and it collapses into a blank page with some event wiring. Make it too specific and it fits only the original use case.

The parameter explorer is the safest bet. Its structure is genuinely universal: controls on one side, chart on the other, state in between. There is almost no specificity to argue over. The annotation-driven narrative is the riskiest. Annotations are deeply tied to the content: their placement, their wording, their sequencing all depend on the specific story. A template that handles the mechanics (positioning, focus management, responsive layout) without constraining the editorial choices is hard to get right.

Build-your-own is risky for a different reason. The archetype is powerful but requires the most metadata from the author: variable types, sensible defaults, encoding constraints (do not put a categorical variable on a linear axis). If the template demands too much upfront configuration, the author might as well write the exploration tool from scratch.

Progressive disclosure falls in the middle. The layer-stack structure is common, but the transitions between layers vary widely. A Fourier decomposition adds curves. A network diagram adds nodes and edges. A map adds geographic layers. The template can handle the stack management and the advance/retreat controls, but the layer-specific transitions are the author's problem.

Amber risk: template lock-in and the uncanny valley

  • Template lock-in. Once authors start using templates, switching away is costly. The template's assumptions about state management, layout, and interaction flow permeate the author's code. If the template does not quite fit, the author faces a choice: contort the content to fit the template, or abandon the template and rewrite from scratch. Neither option is good. Templates need clean ejection paths: a way to "graduate" from the template to raw code without starting over.
  • The uncanny valley of almost-fitting templates. An explanation that is 90% parameter-explorer but needs one unusual interaction (say, a two-dimensional brush instead of a slider) will fight the template at every turn. The last 10% of customization consumes 90% of the effort, and the result feels stitched together. Worse, the reader can sense the template underneath, the way you can sense a WordPress theme behind a corporate website. The explanation feels generic even if the content is novel.
  • LLM defaulting to templates. If d3-power-tools provides templates, LLMs will reach for them reflexively. This is efficient when the template fits, but dangerous when it does not. A skilled author would recognize that a particular explanation needs a custom structure and build one. An LLM, trained to minimize effort, will jam the content into the nearest template. The result is cookie-cutter explanations that answer the letter of the question but miss the spirit. Worse, the evaluator may not catch this because the template produces structurally valid output. The explanation looks right but does not communicate well.
  • Creativity constraints. The best interactive explanations often break the mold. Bret Victor's explorable explanations, Nicky Case's interactive essays, the Pudding's visual stories: these succeed precisely because their structure is tailored to their content. A template library, by definition, encourages the common case. It may subtly discourage the inventive structures that make the best explanations memorable.

What templates would look like in practice

A template is a function, not a file. You call it with a configuration object describing your content, and it returns a structured explanation with all the interaction wiring in place. Something like:

const explorer = parameterExplorer({
  container: "#my-viz",
  data: myDataset,
  params: [
    { name: "smoothing", type: "range", min: 0, max: 1, default: 0.3 },
    { name: "showOutliers", type: "toggle", default: false }
  ],
  render(svg, data, params) {
    // Your D3 code here. Called on every param change.
  }
});

Listing 1. A parameter explorer template call. The template handles layout, control rendering, state binding, and debouncing. The author provides the data, the parameter definitions, and a render function.

The render function is the escape hatch. It is raw D3. The template does not generate the chart; it manages the structure around the chart. This keeps the template general (any chart type works) while providing real value (you never write slider-event-to-state-update boilerplate again).

For the LLM workflow, the template selection could happen automatically. The proposer identifies the story discovery question, classifies it into an archetype, and generates code that uses the corresponding template. The evaluator checks that the archetype fits the content. If the fit is poor (say, the content is really a progressive disclosure story but the proposer used a parameter explorer), the evaluator flags it, and the iterate step can restructure.

This is the real leverage of templates for moonshine. Not that they save typing for human authors (though they do). Templates give the evaluate-iterate loop a vocabulary for structural feedback. Instead of "the interaction model is confusing," the evaluator can say "this is a compare-and-contrast story but it is structured as a parameter explorer; restructure using the compare-and-contrast template." That is actionable. That is fixable in one iteration.

Five structures, infinite content

The claim is not that all explanations fit into five boxes. The claim is that most explanations have a structural skeleton that resembles one of five archetypes, and that skeleton is worth standardizing separately from the content it supports. The bones are generic. The flesh is specific.

The strongest argument for templates is pedagogical. When a new author asks "how do I structure an interactive explanation?", the answer today is "look at examples and figure it out." With a template library, the answer is "what question is your explanation answering?" If the answer is "what happens when I change this variable," you use a parameter explorer. If the answer is "how do these two algorithms compare," you use compare-and-contrast. The question determines the structure. The author's job is to fill in the content.

The strongest argument against templates is aesthetic. The explanations that stick in memory are the ones with structure so perfectly tailored to their content that you cannot imagine the content presented any other way. Templates, by definition, are not tailored. They are ready-made. And ready-made structures, like ready-made suits, fit most people adequately and nobody perfectly.

The honest answer is probably both. Use templates for the 80% of explanations where the archetype fits cleanly. Build custom structures for the 20% where it does not. The templates handle the boring part (layout, state, controls, responsiveness) so that human attention goes to the interesting part (the story, the data, the insight). And when the template does not fit, it should be easy to set aside, not a cage you have to break out of.
