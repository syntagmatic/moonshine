Future Directions

Adaptive Complexity

Visualizations that adjust their complexity based on viewer behavior, meeting readers where they are instead of where the author assumes they are.

The one-size problem

Every data visualization makes a bet about its audience. A dense, multi-panel dashboard with brushing, filtering, and six color encodings assumes the viewer already knows what they are looking at. A single bar chart with a headline assumes they do not. Both bets are wrong most of the time.

The dashboard alienates newcomers. The simple chart bores experts. And most viewers fall somewhere in between, in a place that shifts as they spend time with the data. The same person who glances at a chart in a news feed might study it carefully if the topic catches their attention. The visualization cannot know in advance which person will show up. But it can watch and adapt.

Responsive design adapts to the device. Adaptive complexity adapts to the behavior. A phone user who has been exploring a chart for five minutes is not the same as a desktop user who just arrived.

Signals of engagement

To adapt, a visualization needs evidence of engagement. Not identity, not demographics, not tracking cookies. Just behavioral signals from the current session:

Time on page. The most basic signal. Someone who has been on the page for two minutes is more invested than someone who arrived one second ago. Time alone is imperfect (a forgotten tab accumulates time without engagement) but it sets a floor.

Interactions. Hovers, clicks, brushes, zooms, scrolls. Each interaction is a small declaration of interest. Three hovers on different data points suggest curiosity. A brush selection suggests analysis. Zooming into a region suggests the viewer is hunting for something specific.

Scroll depth. A viewer who has scrolled past three sections is reading, not glancing. Scroll depth combined with pause duration (lingering on a section vs. racing through) separates readers from skimmers.

Return visits. If localStorage records a previous session, the returning viewer likely does not need the gentle introduction again. They came back for a reason.

None of these signals are perfect individually. But combined, they paint a reliable picture of engagement. The key constraint: all signals stay local. No server calls, no analytics beacons, no fingerprinting. The adaptation happens entirely in the viewer's browser.

Signal noise and latency

Not all engagement signals are created equal. Each one carries different noise characteristics and responds on a different timescale. Treating them as interchangeable leads to bad adaptation decisions.

Time on page is the noisiest signal. It accumulates whether or not the viewer is paying attention. A tab left open during lunch registers the same elapsed time as focused reading. Time is also the slowest signal: you need at least ten seconds before time tells you anything useful, and you need a minute or more before it reliably distinguishes readers from tab-hoarders. The saving grace of time is that it requires no action from the viewer. It is the signal of last resort when nothing else is available.

Scroll depth is moderate noise but faster. A viewer who scrolls to the third section within twenty seconds is skimming, not reading. A viewer who scrolls there over ninety seconds, pausing along the way, is engaged. The signal becomes useful only when you pair depth with velocity. Raw depth alone cannot distinguish the reader who absorbed three sections from the one who hit Page Down looking for a specific chart. Scroll depth also has an architectural constraint: it measures engagement with the page, not with any particular visualization. A chart in the middle of a long article might not correlate with the scroll position at all.

Hover and interaction count is lower noise and faster still. A hover is voluntary. The viewer moved their cursor to a specific point on the chart, which takes intent. Three hovers in different regions of a chart are strong evidence of curiosity. But interaction count has its own failure modes. Touchscreen users do not hover. Power users who mouse across the chart on their way to the scroll bar generate false positives. And the signal saturates: the difference between 3 hovers and 30 hovers is not 10x more engagement. It might be the same person idly dragging their mouse.

Zoom level is the cleanest signal and the rarest. A viewer who zooms into a region of a chart is hunting for something specific. This is almost always genuine engagement. But zoom interactions are uncommon, most viewers never zoom, so you cannot rely on zoom level as a primary driver. It works better as a strong override: if someone zooms, advance them immediately, regardless of what the other signals say.

The pipeline, then, is not a simple sum. It is a weighted combination where each signal plays a different role. Time sets a floor. Scroll depth provides context. Interactions confirm intent. Zoom overrides everything. The weights matter more than the signals themselves, and getting them wrong produces adaptation that feels either sluggish (too much weight on time) or twitchy (too much weight on interactions).

Four complexity tiers

Adaptive complexity works through progressive disclosure, revealing layers as engagement increases. Each tier adds to the previous one rather than replacing it.

Glance 0 to 5 seconds Title, one key number, a simple shape. The elevator pitch.
Read 5 to 30 seconds Axes, labels, trend line, basic color encoding. Context for the number.
Explore 30s to 5 minutes Brush, filter, hover tooltips, multiple views. Self-directed inquiry.
Analyze 5 minutes or more Full controls, download data, share state, alternate encodings.
Figure 2. Four tiers of adaptive complexity, each adding a layer on top of the previous one. The tier diagram above highlights whichever level the interactive chart below has reached.

Glance is the elevator pitch. A large number, a short label, and a tiny sparkline. This is what a viewer sees in a social media embed, a dashboard thumbnail, or the first fraction of a second on the page. It answers one question: "Is this going up or down?"

Read adds context. Axes appear, the sparkline becomes a proper chart with a time scale, labels identify the data, and a trend line summarizes the trajectory. This is what a newspaper chart provides. It answers: "By how much, and since when?"

Explore hands the viewer tools. Brushing selects a time range. Hovering reveals exact values. The viewer can ask their own questions instead of only reading the author's narrative. It answers: "What about this specific part?"

Analyze opens the full toolkit. A details panel shows computed statistics for the selection. A download button exports the data. This tier treats the viewer as a peer, someone who might have a question the author never anticipated.

Progressive disclosure as mechanism

The tiers are not four separate charts. They are one chart with layers that become visible. The Glance sparkline and the Read chart show the same data. The brush interaction operates on the same SVG. This matters for two reasons.

First, continuity. When axes fade in around the sparkline, the viewer understands that the context is being added to the thing they already saw, not replacing it. The big number stays visible (repositioned, but present). Each transition says "here is more" rather than "forget what you saw."

Second, implementation simplicity. One dataset, one set of scales, one rendering pipeline. The complexity tiers control opacity and pointer-events, not separate codepaths. Progressive disclosure is a CSS concern layered on top of a D3 concern.

// The core idea: tiers control visibility, not rendering
function advanceTo(tier) {
  if (tier >= 1) readLayer.classList.add('visible');
  if (tier >= 2) exploreLayer.classList.add('visible');
  if (tier >= 3) analyzeLayer.classList.add('visible');
}

Not responsive design

Responsive design and adaptive complexity solve different problems. Responsive design asks: "What can this screen physically display?" Adaptive complexity asks: "What is this person ready to understand?"

A responsive chart on a phone might hide the legend to save space. An adaptive chart hides the legend because the viewer just arrived and has not yet shown interest in what the colors mean. The phone user who has been exploring for three minutes absolutely should see the legend, even on a small screen. The desktop user who arrived one second ago probably should not see it yet, even though there is plenty of room.

In practice, both systems operate simultaneously. Responsive design constrains the layout. Adaptive complexity constrains the information density within that layout. They are orthogonal.

The moonshine connection

Moonshine explanations are built for readers who want to understand something, not just glance at it. But "want to understand" is not binary. Some readers arrive skeptical and need the headline to hook them. Others arrive already curious and want the deep mechanics immediately.

An adaptive moonshine explanation could start every reader with a single, vivid claim, then reveal the evidence and interactivity as engagement grows. The reader who bounces after three seconds still got the main idea. The reader who stays gets progressively richer tools to interrogate that idea.

The best explanations already do this implicitly through prose structure: hook, context, detail, nuance. Adaptive complexity makes the same structure explicit and interactive, letting the visualization participate in the pacing rather than dumping everything at once.

For LLM-generated explanations, adaptive complexity adds a useful constraint. Instead of generating one chart at one complexity level (and hoping it matches the audience), the generator produces a layered chart with four levels of detail. The generation task becomes more structured: what is the one-number summary? What context does the chart need? What interactions serve exploration? What analysis tools close the loop?

Risks and open questions

This direction has real problems

  • The creepy-tracking feeling. Even when all behavior tracking stays local and benign, users may feel surveilled. "The chart noticed I hovered three times" is an unsettling sentence. Transparency helps (the complexity meter is deliberately visible), but discomfort may remain. Any deployment should make the adaptation obvious and the mechanism inspectable.
  • The complexity ratchet. Once a viewer has seen the brush interaction, hiding it feels like a bug. Progressive disclosure works in one direction: you can reveal features, but retracting them breaks expectations. The "reset" button in the demo is an escape hatch, but in production, viewers who refresh the page and lose their Analyze-level tools will be frustrated. The ratchet suggests that localStorage should remember the high-water mark, which creates its own tension with the "no tracking" principle.
  • Accessibility. Screen readers cannot "browse casually." A blind user navigating with a screen reader is, by interaction count, immediately at the Explore tier. But their needs are different from a sighted user who has been hovering curiously. Time-based and interaction-based signals conflate physical interaction modality with cognitive engagement. Any real implementation must detect assistive technology and skip the progressive disclosure entirely, presenting the full chart from the start.
  • LLM-generated adaptive behavior. If an LLM decides what to hide at each tier, it might bury important information behind interaction gates that some users never reach. A critical data point hidden at the Analyze tier is invisible to 90% of viewers. The tier assignments need human review, or at minimum, a rule that no tier should contradict or materially change the story told by a lower tier.

These are not reasons to abandon adaptive complexity. They are reasons to build it carefully, with the adaptation visible, the mechanism simple, and the full version always reachable by anyone who wants it.

The complexity ratchet

The second risk in the list above deserves its own treatment, because it is not just an implementation detail. It is a fundamental tension in progressive disclosure systems, and adaptive complexity runs straight into it.

The ratchet works in one direction: up. When a chart reveals its brush interaction, or fades in axis labels, or opens a details panel, those additions feel like the interface becoming more capable. The viewer's mental model expands to include the new tools. Removing them violates that model. A button that was there a moment ago and is now gone is not "simplification." It is breakage.

This asymmetry is well-known in product design. Features are easier to add than to remove. But adaptive complexity makes the problem acute, because the adaptation implies a promise: "We showed you this because you earned it through engagement." Taking it away says: "Actually, we have decided you no longer deserve it." That framing may be unintentional, but it is how it feels.

The reverse ratchet is equally problematic. A "simplify" button that strips the chart back to its Glance state is a reasonable escape hatch in theory. In practice it feels patronizing, as if the system is saying: "Let me dumb this down for you." Viewers who voluntarily simplify a chart are rare. Viewers who are forcibly simplified against are angry.

There are a few strategies for managing the ratchet, none of them fully satisfying:

Remember the high-water mark. Use localStorage to record the highest tier a viewer has reached. On return visits, start at that tier instead of Glance. This preserves continuity across sessions, but it conflicts with the privacy-first principle of keeping no persistent state. It also assumes the returning viewer wants the same experience, which may not be true if they are now showing the chart to a colleague who has never seen it.

Offer explicit tier controls. Let the viewer manually select their tier at any time, separate from the adaptive system. This turns adaptation into a default rather than a constraint. The system suggests a tier; the viewer overrides if they want. The cost is UI complexity: you now need tier-selection controls alongside whatever controls the chart already has.

Make adaptation gentle and reversible. Instead of hard tier boundaries (brush is either enabled or not), use continuous transitions. Fade controls in gradually. Let the viewer notice them arriving rather than having them snap into existence. For the reverse direction, do not remove features. Instead, visually de-emphasize them: dim the brush handles, collapse the details panel to a thin strip. The tools are still reachable, just less prominent. This is softer but more complex to implement.

Accept the asymmetry. Some systems simply do not ratchet down. Once you see the full version, that is what you get. The adaptation controls only the initial experience, the on-ramp. After that, the chart is a static full-complexity visualization. This is the simplest approach and probably the most honest one.

Does adaptation actually help?

The strongest argument for adaptive complexity is intuitive: meet people where they are, and they will understand the data better. The strongest argument against it is equally intuitive: the adaptation machinery itself is a source of confusion, and a well-designed static chart at the right complexity level might outperform a clever adaptive one. The question is empirical, and the evidence is thin.

A/B testing adaptive complexity is harder than testing most UI changes, because the treatment is not a fixed variant. The adaptive version is a distribution of experiences that depends on viewer behavior. Two viewers in the "adaptive" arm might see entirely different charts depending on how long they stayed and how much they interacted. This makes standard A/B metrics slippery. Completion rate, time to insight, comprehension scores: all of these are confounded by the fact that the treatment is correlated with the behavior you are measuring.

Consider the simplest test: static full-complexity chart versus adaptive chart. You randomize viewers and measure comprehension (via a post-viewing quiz). The adaptive group will show higher variance, because some viewers in that group saw only the Glance level while others reached Analyze. The mean comprehension score might be similar, but the adaptive group will have a bimodal distribution: quick bouncers who learned almost nothing and engaged readers who learned a lot. The static group will cluster in the middle. Which distribution is better? That depends on what you are optimizing for. If you want to maximize the floor (everyone learns something), static wins. If you want to maximize the ceiling (engaged viewers learn everything), adaptive wins.

A more revealing test compares adaptive complexity against static-at-each-tier. Give one group the adaptive chart. Give four other groups the chart locked at Glance, Read, Explore, and Analyze respectively. Now measure comprehension and satisfaction across all five groups. If the adaptive group outperforms the best-performing static group, adaptation is genuinely adding value beyond tier selection. If the adaptive group merely matches the static group whose tier best fits the viewer, then adaptation is just a convenient way to guess the right tier, and a simpler "choose your complexity" dropdown would work as well.

There is also the question of what to measure. Comprehension is one axis, but not the only one. Engagement duration matters: does the adaptive chart keep viewers longer than the static one? Does it convert more Glance-level viewers into Read-level viewers? This conversion metric is specific to adaptive systems and has no analogue in static A/B tests. If the adaptive chart converts 40% of glancers into readers while the static Read-level chart bounces 60% of its viewers entirely, the adaptive approach is doing something valuable even if the two charts produce similar average comprehension.

Finally, there is the confound of novelty. An adaptive chart is unfamiliar. The first time a viewer watches a chart grow more complex in response to their behavior, the novelty itself drives engagement. Long-term A/B tests (weeks, not sessions) are necessary to separate the novelty effect from genuine usability improvement. No one has run those tests yet, at least not in a published, reproducible way. The idea is ahead of the evidence.

The most honest framing: adaptive complexity is a hypothesis about comprehension, not a proven technique. Building it is worthwhile because the engineering forces you to think carefully about what information matters at each engagement level. Whether the adaptation itself helps viewers more than a well-chosen static default remains an open question that can only be answered by testing with real audiences on real data.
