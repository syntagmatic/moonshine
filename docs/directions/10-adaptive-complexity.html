Future Directions

Adaptive Complexity

Visualizations that adjust their complexity based on viewer behavior, meeting readers where they are instead of where the author assumes they are.

The one-size problem

Every data visualization makes a bet about its audience. A dense, multi-panel dashboard with brushing, filtering, and six color encodings assumes the viewer already knows what they are looking at. A single bar chart with a headline assumes they do not. Both bets are wrong most of the time.

The dashboard alienates newcomers. The simple chart bores experts. And most viewers fall somewhere in between, in a place that shifts as they spend time with the data. The same person who glances at a chart in a news feed might study it carefully if the topic catches their attention. The visualization cannot know in advance which person will show up. But it can watch and adapt.

Responsive design adapts to the device. Adaptive complexity adapts to the behavior. A phone user who has been exploring a chart for five minutes is not the same as a desktop user who just arrived.

Signals of engagement

To adapt, a visualization needs evidence of engagement. Not identity, not demographics, not tracking cookies. Just behavioral signals from the current session:

Time on page. The most basic signal. Someone who has been on the page for two minutes is more invested than someone who arrived one second ago. Time alone is imperfect (a forgotten tab accumulates time without engagement) but it sets a floor.

Interactions. Hovers, clicks, brushes, zooms, scrolls. Each interaction is a small declaration of interest. Three hovers on different data points suggest curiosity. A brush selection suggests analysis. Zooming into a region suggests the viewer is hunting for something specific.

Scroll depth. A viewer who has scrolled past three sections is reading, not glancing. Scroll depth combined with pause duration (lingering on a section vs. racing through) separates readers from skimmers.

Return visits. If localStorage records a previous session, the returning viewer likely does not need the gentle introduction again. They came back for a reason.

None of these signals are perfect individually. But combined, they paint a reliable picture of engagement. The key constraint: all signals stay local. No server calls, no analytics beacons, no fingerprinting. The adaptation happens entirely in the viewer's browser.

Four complexity tiers

Adaptive complexity works through progressive disclosure, revealing layers as engagement increases. Each tier adds to the previous one rather than replacing it.

Glance 0 to 5 seconds Title, one key number, a simple shape. The elevator pitch.
Read 5 to 30 seconds Axes, labels, trend line, basic color encoding. Context for the number.
Explore 30s to 5 minutes Brush, filter, hover tooltips, multiple views. Self-directed inquiry.
Analyze 5 minutes or more Full controls, download data, share state, alternate encodings.
Figure 1. Four tiers of adaptive complexity, each adding a layer on top of the previous one. The tier diagram above highlights whichever level the interactive chart below has reached.

Glance is the elevator pitch. A large number, a short label, and a tiny sparkline. This is what a viewer sees in a social media embed, a dashboard thumbnail, or the first fraction of a second on the page. It answers one question: "Is this going up or down?"

Read adds context. Axes appear, the sparkline becomes a proper chart with a time scale, labels identify the data, and a trend line summarizes the trajectory. This is what a newspaper chart provides. It answers: "By how much, and since when?"

Explore hands the viewer tools. Brushing selects a time range. Hovering reveals exact values. The viewer can ask their own questions instead of only reading the author's narrative. It answers: "What about this specific part?"

Analyze opens the full toolkit. A details panel shows computed statistics for the selection. A download button exports the data. This tier treats the viewer as a peer, someone who might have a question the author never anticipated.

Progressive disclosure as mechanism

The tiers are not four separate charts. They are one chart with layers that become visible. The Glance sparkline and the Read chart show the same data. The brush interaction operates on the same SVG. This matters for two reasons.

First, continuity. When axes fade in around the sparkline, the viewer understands that the context is being added to the thing they already saw, not replacing it. The big number stays visible (repositioned, but present). Each transition says "here is more" rather than "forget what you saw."

Second, implementation simplicity. One dataset, one set of scales, one rendering pipeline. The complexity tiers control opacity and pointer-events, not separate codepaths. Progressive disclosure is a CSS concern layered on top of a D3 concern.

// The core idea: tiers control visibility, not rendering
function advanceTo(tier) {
  if (tier >= 1) readLayer.classList.add('visible');
  if (tier >= 2) exploreLayer.classList.add('visible');
  if (tier >= 3) analyzeLayer.classList.add('visible');
}

Not responsive design

Responsive design and adaptive complexity solve different problems. Responsive design asks: "What can this screen physically display?" Adaptive complexity asks: "What is this person ready to understand?"

A responsive chart on a phone might hide the legend to save space. An adaptive chart hides the legend because the viewer just arrived and has not yet shown interest in what the colors mean. The phone user who has been exploring for three minutes absolutely should see the legend, even on a small screen. The desktop user who arrived one second ago probably should not see it yet, even though there is plenty of room.

In practice, both systems operate simultaneously. Responsive design constrains the layout. Adaptive complexity constrains the information density within that layout. They are orthogonal.

The moonshine connection

Moonshine explanations are built for readers who want to understand something, not just glance at it. But "want to understand" is not binary. Some readers arrive skeptical and need the headline to hook them. Others arrive already curious and want the deep mechanics immediately.

An adaptive moonshine explanation could start every reader with a single, vivid claim, then reveal the evidence and interactivity as engagement grows. The reader who bounces after three seconds still got the main idea. The reader who stays gets progressively richer tools to interrogate that idea.

The best explanations already do this implicitly through prose structure: hook, context, detail, nuance. Adaptive complexity makes the same structure explicit and interactive, letting the visualization participate in the pacing rather than dumping everything at once.

For LLM-generated explanations, adaptive complexity adds a useful constraint. Instead of generating one chart at one complexity level (and hoping it matches the audience), the generator produces a layered chart with four levels of detail. The generation task becomes more structured: what is the one-number summary? What context does the chart need? What interactions serve exploration? What analysis tools close the loop?

Risks and open questions

This direction has real problems

  • The creepy-tracking feeling. Even when all behavior tracking stays local and benign, users may feel surveilled. "The chart noticed I hovered three times" is an unsettling sentence. Transparency helps (the complexity meter is deliberately visible), but discomfort may remain. Any deployment should make the adaptation obvious and the mechanism inspectable.
  • The complexity ratchet. Once a viewer has seen the brush interaction, hiding it feels like a bug. Progressive disclosure works in one direction: you can reveal features, but retracting them breaks expectations. The "reset" button in the demo is an escape hatch, but in production, viewers who refresh the page and lose their Analyze-level tools will be frustrated. The ratchet suggests that localStorage should remember the high-water mark, which creates its own tension with the "no tracking" principle.
  • Accessibility. Screen readers cannot "browse casually." A blind user navigating with a screen reader is, by interaction count, immediately at the Explore tier. But their needs are different from a sighted user who has been hovering curiously. Time-based and interaction-based signals conflate physical interaction modality with cognitive engagement. Any real implementation must detect assistive technology and skip the progressive disclosure entirely, presenting the full chart from the start.
  • LLM-generated adaptive behavior. If an LLM decides what to hide at each tier, it might bury important information behind interaction gates that some users never reach. A critical data point hidden at the Analyze tier is invisible to 90% of viewers. The tier assignments need human review, or at minimum, a rule that no tier should contradict or materially change the story told by a lower tier.

These are not reasons to abandon adaptive complexity. They are reasons to build it carefully, with the adaptation visible, the mechanism simple, and the full version always reachable by anyone who wants it.
