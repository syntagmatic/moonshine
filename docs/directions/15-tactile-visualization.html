Future Directions

Tactile Visualization

SVG-to-tactile export: embossed diagrams, 3D-printable data sculptures, and haptic feedback for touch. Visualization you can hold in your hands.

Who this is for

Sonification gives blind users the shape of data through sound. But sound is fleeting. It unfolds in time, and once a tone passes, it is gone. You cannot put your finger on the peak and hold it there while you reach for the valley with your other hand. You cannot feel two bars side by side and compare their heights in the same instant. Sound is sequential. Touch is simultaneous.

For the roughly 39 million people worldwide who are blind, and the far larger population with low vision, tactile graphics have been a tool for decades. Raised-line diagrams on swell paper. Thermoform copies of maps. Hand-crafted models of molecular structures. These are not new. What is new is the possibility of generating them automatically from the same data and code that produce screen-based charts.

Tactile graphics also serve sighted users in ways that screens cannot. A teacher explaining a distribution can hand a student a 3D-printed histogram and say "feel where the mass is." A museum visitor can run their fingers across a ridge that traces a century of temperature data. A researcher can pick up a data sculpture and rotate it, finding angles that a flat screen does not offer. Touch adds a channel that vision alone does not cover, the same way sonification adds a channel that vision misses.

The goal is not to make charts touchable as a novelty. It is to produce physical artifacts from the same D3 code that produces screen graphics, so that a single explanation can reach readers through eyes, ears, and hands.

Three output formats

Tactile graphics come in three distinct formats, each with its own production process, constraints, and strengths. A tactile export pipeline needs to handle all three, because no single format serves every context.

Swell paper (microcapsule paper)

Swell paper is coated with heat-sensitive microcapsules. You print a high-contrast black image onto it using a standard laser printer, then run the sheet through a fuser (a machine that blows hot air across the surface). Wherever the toner is dark, the microcapsules expand, raising the surface by about 0.5mm. The result is a raised-line diagram that can be read by touch.

The production constraint is simple: everything that should be raised must be black. Everything that should be flat must be white. There is no gray. There is no color. The SVG that goes to a swell-paper printer needs thick black strokes (at least 1mm wide, preferably 1.5mm), solid black fills for areas that should be raised, and no reliance on color to distinguish elements. Where a screen chart uses red and blue to differentiate two series, a swell-paper chart uses different hatching patterns: diagonal lines for one, cross-hatching for another, dots for a third.

Braille labels replace printed text. Each Braille cell is 6.2mm wide and 10.1mm tall, with specific dot spacing (2.5mm between dot centers within a cell, 3.75mm between cells). Labels need clearance: at least 3mm between the Braille dots and any adjacent raised line, or the reader's fingers cannot distinguish them. This means swell-paper charts have far fewer labels than screen charts. An axis that shows 10 tick labels on screen might show 3 on paper.

3D printing

A bar chart on screen uses height (or length) to encode value. A 3D-printed bar chart does the same thing physically: taller bars are taller columns. The reader picks up the model, runs a finger across the tops of the columns, and feels the differences directly. Position and height, the two most accurate visual encodings according to Cleveland and McGill, become the two most natural tactile encodings: finger position and column height under the fingertip.

The pipeline starts with SVG and ends with STL (the standard mesh format for 3D printers). The transformation is a heightfield extrusion: each element in the SVG becomes a 3D solid. A bar becomes a rectangular column. A line chart becomes a ridge with width. A filled area becomes a plateau. The base plate is a flat rectangle (typically 3mm thick for rigidity). Elements rise from it by an amount proportional to their data value.

Minimum feature size for consumer FDM printers is roughly 0.8mm, meaning thin lines in the SVG need to be thickened before extrusion. Maximum practical model size is about 200mm on a side, which sets the scale for the entire chart. The aspect ratio and element spacing must account for finger width: two features closer than 3mm will feel like one.

Haptic touchscreens

The third format does not produce a physical object. Instead, it uses the vibration motor in a phone or tablet to create tactile feedback as the user drags across a chart. Stronger vibration means a higher value. A change in vibration pattern (continuous to pulsed, for instance) signals a category boundary. The user feels the data through the glass.

The Web Vibration API provides the basic mechanism: navigator.vibrate(pattern) takes an array of durations (vibrate, pause, vibrate, pause). The resolution is coarse compared to dedicated haptic hardware, but it is available on most Android devices and can communicate simple data features. A bar chart where dragging across a tall bar produces a long, strong vibration and a short bar produces a brief, weak one gives a rough tactile encoding of the data.

Haptic feedback is the weakest of the three formats in terms of information density. The vibration motor has no spatial resolution (it shakes the whole device), the intensity range is narrow, and patterns must be simple enough to parse through fingertips on glass. But it is the most accessible in terms of equipment. No swell-paper fuser. No 3D printer. Just a phone that the user already owns.

SVG stroke (visual) → Raised line, 1-1.5mm wide (swell paper) Color fill (visual) → Hatching pattern (swell paper) / texture (3D print) Bar height (visual) → Column height (3D print) / vibration intensity (haptic) Text label (visual) → Braille cells (swell paper) / raised lettering (3D print) Color category (visual) → Hatching angle or dot density (swell paper) Hover tooltip (visual) → Vibration pulse + audio label (haptic)

Design constraints for touch

A sighted user can distinguish two lines that are 0.5 pixels apart on a retina display. A finger cannot. The spatial resolution of touch is roughly 1mm at the fingertip, and practical discrimination requires more margin than that. The constraints below apply to all tactile output, whether swell paper, 3D print, or haptic.

Minimum line width: 1mm. Lines thinner than this are not reliably felt. For swell paper, 1.5mm is safer because the expansion process is not perfectly uniform. For 3D printing, 0.8mm is the mechanical minimum, but 1.2mm produces a more comfortable tactile experience.

Minimum element spacing: 3mm. Two raised features closer than 3mm tend to blur into one under a fingertip. This applies to parallel lines, adjacent bars, tick marks next to axis lines, and Braille labels next to chart elements. A screen bar chart with 20 narrow bars spaced 2 pixels apart becomes a tactile bar chart with 6 or 7 bars spaced 4mm apart. The data must be binned or sampled down.

Braille cell dimensions: 6.2mm wide, 10.1mm tall. Each Braille character occupies a fixed cell. A five-character label is 31mm wide. A tactile chart axis that runs 150mm can fit roughly four labels with clearance. Screen charts routinely show 10 or more labels on an axis the same physical length. Tactile labeling demands ruthless editing.

Maximum element count: 10 to 15. A sighted user can scan a chart with 50 data points in a glance. A tactile reader explores sequentially, moving from element to element. More than 10 to 15 distinct elements overwhelms working memory and makes the chart feel like a wall of undifferentiated texture. The rule of thumb: if you cannot describe the chart's message in one sentence, it has too many elements for tactile output.

Simplified geometry. Curves must be approximated with fewer control points. Decorative elements (drop shadows, gradients, rounded corners) must be stripped. The SVG that reaches the tactile pipeline should be the simplest geometry that preserves the data relationship.

The tactile grammar

Visual encoding has a well-studied hierarchy. Position along a common scale is most accurate. Length is next. Angle, area, color saturation, and color hue follow in decreasing order of perceptual accuracy.

Tactile encoding has its own hierarchy, less studied but grounded in the same psychophysical principles:

Position is primary. Where your finger is on the surface encodes the spatial relationship between elements. Left-right, up-down, and near-far are all perceived with good accuracy. This is why bar charts and simple maps translate well to tactile format: position is their dominant encoding.

Height is secondary, available only in 3D printing. The difference between a 5mm column and a 15mm column is immediately obvious under a fingertip. Height discrimination is roughly 1mm, meaning six to ten distinguishable levels are practical over a typical 5mm to 20mm range. For swell paper, height is binary: raised or flat. There is no "more raised."

Texture is tertiary. Different hatching patterns (diagonal lines, horizontal lines, cross-hatch, dots) are distinguishable but require deliberate attention. A reader must pause, press, and feel the pattern. Three to four distinct textures are reliably differentiated. More than that and the patterns start to feel similar. Texture is the tactile equivalent of color: good for categorical distinction, poor for quantitative comparison.

Size is a weak channel. The difference between a 5mm circle and a 10mm circle is perceivable, but finer distinctions are not. Two or three size levels at most. Do not map a continuous variable to tactile size.

The SVG-to-tactile pipeline

A d3-power-tools skill for tactile export would sit between D3's SVG output and the production format. The pipeline has four stages:

Stage 1: Simplify. Strip decorative attributes (gradients, drop shadows, opacity, rounded corners). Flatten nested transforms into absolute coordinates. Reduce path complexity using Douglas-Peucker or Visvalingam simplification. Remove any element that does not carry data meaning.

Stage 2: Thicken. Set all strokes to a minimum width (1mm at the target physical size). Increase spacing between elements to meet the 3mm minimum. If bars overlap or touch, merge or space them. If tick marks crowd the axis, cull to a smaller set.

Stage 3: Re-encode. Replace color fills with hatching patterns. Replace text labels with Braille representations (or mark them for Braille conversion). Replace tooltips with fixed labels positioned with clearance. The chart must carry all its information through geometry alone, with no reliance on color, transparency, or hover states.

Stage 4: Export. For swell paper, output a high-contrast black-and-white SVG or PDF sized for the target paper (typically A4 or letter). For 3D printing, extrude the SVG into an STL mesh with a base plate and proportional heights. For haptic, generate a JSON map of regions to vibration intensities that the runtime haptic engine will use.

// Sketch of a tactile export pipeline
function exportTactile(svgElement, options) {
  const simplified = simplifyPaths(svgElement, {
    tolerance: options.minFeatureSize || 1.0,   // mm
    removeDecorative: true
  });

  const thickened = enforceMinimums(simplified, {
    strokeWidth: 1.0,      // mm
    elementSpacing: 3.0,   // mm
    maxElements: 12
  });

  const reencoded = replaceEncodings(thickened, {
    colorToHatch: true,
    textToBraille: true,
    brailleClearance: 3.0  // mm from nearest raised line
  });

  if (options.format === 'swell') {
    return exportSwellPaperSVG(reencoded, {
      paperSize: 'A4',
      fillBlack: true
    });
  } else if (options.format === 'stl') {
    return extrudeToSTL(reencoded, {
      basePlateThickness: 3,  // mm
      maxHeight: 20,           // mm
      modelWidth: 150          // mm
    });
  } else if (options.format === 'haptic') {
    return generateHapticMap(reencoded, {
      resolution: [20, 15],  // grid cells
      intensityRange: [0, 255]
    });
  }
}

Braille in SVG

Braille labels in a tactile SVG are not text. They are patterns of raised dots. Each dot is a filled circle with a diameter of 1.5mm, spaced 2.5mm center-to-center within a cell and 3.75mm center-to-center between adjacent cells. The six dots of a Braille cell (arranged in a 2-by-3 grid) map to a specific character depending on which dots are raised.

A tactile export skill would include a lookup table that converts ASCII characters to Braille dot patterns, and a renderer that draws those patterns as SVG circles at the correct positions. The labels are positioned with clearance from adjacent chart elements, and the total label length is constrained by the available space. If a label does not fit, the skill truncates or abbreviates it, because overflowing Braille is worse than no label at all. A reader encountering a partial Braille word with dots running into a chart element cannot parse either one.

// Braille cell layout (Grade 1 Braille, simplified)
// Dot positions within a cell (column, row):
// 1=(0,0) 4=(1,0)
// 2=(0,1) 5=(1,1)
// 3=(0,2) 6=(1,2)
const BRAILLE_DOT_SPACING = 2.5;  // mm center-to-center
const BRAILLE_CELL_WIDTH  = 6.2;  // mm
const BRAILLE_DOT_RADIUS  = 0.75; // mm

function renderBrailleLabel(svgGroup, text, x, y, mmPerPx) {
  const chars = text.toLowerCase().split('');
  chars.forEach((ch, i) => {
    const dots = BRAILLE_MAP[ch] || [];
    const cellX = x + i * BRAILLE_CELL_WIDTH / mmPerPx;
    dots.forEach(([col, row]) => {
      svgGroup.append('circle')
        .attr('cx', cellX + col * BRAILLE_DOT_SPACING / mmPerPx)
        .attr('cy', y + row * BRAILLE_DOT_SPACING / mmPerPx)
        .attr('r', BRAILLE_DOT_RADIUS / mmPerPx)
        .attr('fill', 'black');
    });
  });
}

Haptic feedback on the web

The Web Vibration API is minimal. navigator.vibrate(200) vibrates for 200 milliseconds. navigator.vibrate([200, 100, 200]) vibrates, pauses, vibrates. That is the entire interface. There is no intensity control in the standard API, no spatial targeting, and no frequency modulation. On most devices, you get on-off vibration with coarse timing.

Despite these limits, a chart can map data to vibration duration. As the user drags across a bar, the vibration pulse length is proportional to the bar's value. A tall bar produces a 300ms vibration; a short bar, 50ms. The user feels the data as a series of stronger or weaker buzzes. It is crude. But for a blind user exploring a simple chart on their phone, it provides a direct physical signal that the finger is "on" a data element and a rough sense of how large that element is.

More sophisticated haptic feedback requires hardware beyond what the Web API exposes. Apple's Taptic Engine supports intensity modulation and sharp vs. soft taps. Android's haptic feedback API (at the platform level, not the web level) offers richer patterns. A d3-power-tools haptic skill would need a fallback strategy: use rich haptics where available, degrade to simple vibration pulses where not, and do nothing gracefully on devices with no vibration motor at all.

Supporting moonshine

A moonshine explanation lives on a screen. You scroll through it, interact with the figures, and close the tab. Tactile export makes it possible for an explanation to leave the screen. A teacher reads a moonshine explanation about income inequality, exports the key chart as an STL file, prints it on the school's 3D printer, and hands the physical model to a blind student. The student feels the bars, finds the tallest one, traces the trend. The explanation has become an artifact.

This matters for a specific reason. Moonshine explanations are designed to build understanding through interaction. But interaction assumes a screen, a pointer, and sight. A tactile export does not replicate the interaction. It does something different: it produces a static physical object that carries the data relationship in its shape. The understanding comes not from hovering and clicking but from holding and touching. Different modality, same goal.

A d3-power-tools skill for tactile output would generate three files from a single chart: a swell-paper SVG, an STL mesh, and a haptic map. The author would not need to design for tactile separately. The skill would apply the design constraints automatically (thicken strokes, replace colors, add Braille, simplify). The dual-output challenge, producing something that looks good on screen and works on paper, would be handled by keeping two render paths: one for the screen (full color, thin strokes, many labels) and one for touch (black and white, thick strokes, few labels, hatching).

The deeper possibility is that tactile export changes what counts as an explanation. If an explanation is something you can hold, it is no longer purely digital. It is physical. It has weight. It can be passed from hand to hand. It persists without electricity. It occupies space in a classroom, not just a browser tab. For a project called "moonshine" that treats explanations as things worth building with care, the idea that an explanation could become a physical object is a natural extension of the core commitment.

Where this can go wrong

  • Specialized equipment is required. A swell-paper fuser costs several hundred dollars. A 3D printer costs more. Most people who would benefit from tactile graphics do not own this equipment. The gap between "the software can generate tactile output" and "the user can hold the tactile output" is filled by institutions (schools, libraries, rehab centers) that own the hardware. Software alone does not solve the distribution problem.
  • Simulated tactile preview is not tactile experience. The "tactile preview" mode on this page shows thick lines and hatching, but you cannot feel it. Designing for touch by looking at a screen is like designing for sound by reading a waveform. It helps, but it misleads. Real tactile testing requires printing the output and asking a tactile reader to evaluate it. No amount of simulated preview substitutes for fingers on paper.
  • Oversimplification that loses the data story. Reducing a 50-bar chart to 7 bars for tactile output requires aggregation decisions. Which bars to merge? What label to give the merged group? These choices can obscure the very pattern the chart was designed to show. A skill that automates simplification without understanding the author's intent risks producing a tactile chart that is well-formed but meaningless.
  • LLMs generating tactile-optimized SVGs that look ugly on screen. If an LLM is told to "make this chart tactile-ready," it may apply thick strokes and hatching to the on-screen version, producing a chart that looks like a 1990s fax printout. The dual-output approach is essential: one SVG for screen, one for touch. A skill that conflates the two produces output that serves neither audience well.
  • Braille label errors. Grade 2 Braille uses contractions (common words are abbreviated to save space). A naive character-by-character translation produces Grade 1 Braille, which is technically correct but verbose and unfamiliar to experienced Braille readers. Getting Braille right requires a proper translation library, not a simple lookup table.

From screen to hand

Vision is the dominant channel for data communication. Nearly every tool, tutorial, and design guideline assumes a sighted user looking at a screen. Sonification challenged that assumption by adding an audio channel. Tactile export challenges it further by adding a physical channel.

The technical pieces exist. D3 generates SVG. SVG can be optimized for swell paper through stroke thickening, color removal, and Braille label insertion. SVG geometry can be extruded into STL meshes for 3D printing. Vibration patterns can be driven by data values through the Web Vibration API. No fundamental technology is missing. What is missing is the authoring infrastructure that makes these conversions routine.

A d3-power-tools skill for tactile export would need to be opinionated about design constraints (enforce minimums, limit element counts, require hatching for categories) while remaining configurable for specific production equipment (swell-paper fuser temperature affects line quality, printer nozzle diameter affects minimum feature size). It would need to handle the dual-output problem cleanly, keeping screen and tactile renders as parallel branches from the same data binding. And it would need to produce output that is not just geometrically valid but practically useful, meaning output that has been tested with actual tactile readers and refined based on their feedback.

The hardest part is not the code. It is the testing. A sighted developer cannot evaluate a tactile graphic by looking at it. The feedback loop requires blind and low-vision testers, specialized equipment, and a willingness to iterate on a modality that the developer cannot directly perceive. This is uncomfortable. It is also exactly the kind of work that matters. If moonshine explanations are worth building with care, they are worth building for hands as well as eyes.
