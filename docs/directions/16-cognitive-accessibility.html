Future Directions

Cognitive Accessibility

Beyond screen readers: visualizations adapted for ADHD, dyslexia, low numeracy, and cognitive fatigue. Reaching the people that traditional accessibility overlooks.

The narrow definition

When visualization developers hear "accessibility," they think about screen readers and keyboard navigation. These matter. They are also the floor, not the ceiling. WCAG compliance, ARIA labels, tab indexing, alt text: these solve a specific and important problem, which is that some users cannot see the chart or cannot use a mouse. But they do not address the larger population of people who can see the chart just fine and still cannot use it.

Consider the numbers. Roughly 15 to 20 percent of the population has dyslexia. ADHD affects about 5 to 7 percent of adults worldwide, though many are undiagnosed. Low numeracy is harder to pin down, but surveys in OECD countries consistently find that 20 to 30 percent of adults struggle with tasks like interpreting percentages or reading a graph with two axes. And cognitive fatigue is not a fixed population at all. It is a state that everyone passes through: after a long shift, during illness, in old age, or simply at 4 PM on a Wednesday.

Add these groups together, accounting for overlap, and you get something like a third of your audience on any given day. These people do not need a screen reader. They need the chart to be clearer, calmer, and more direct. They need the cognitive demands of the visualization to fit within their available processing capacity.

Traditional accessibility asks: "Can the user perceive the chart at all?" Cognitive accessibility asks a harder question: "Can the user understand the chart given their current cognitive resources?" The first question has binary answers. The second is a spectrum.

Four adaptation categories

The cognitive barriers that charts create fall into roughly four categories. Each calls for different adaptations, though there is significant overlap in the solutions.

ADHDSustained attention on complex, multi-element displaysReduce clutter, highlight one thing at a time, minimize gratuitous animation, offer a summary-first mode

ADHD: one thing at a time

A complex multi-series bar chart is an attention minefield. The eye has to hold multiple series in working memory, compare heights across groups, track a legend, read axis labels, and assemble a narrative. For someone with ADHD, the chart produces a wall of visual noise before the signal can emerge.

The adaptation is not to remove information. It is to sequence it. Show one bar (or one series) at a time. Let the user step through the data at their own pace, with each step carrying a single clear message: "Healthcare spending was 420 million." Next step: "Education was 310 million." The total picture assembles through a sequence of digestible fragments rather than a simultaneous dump.

Animation is a double-edged problem. On one hand, smooth transitions between steps can help maintain context. On the other hand, gratuitous animation (bouncing bars, spinning pie slices, decorative particle effects) hijacks attention. The rule for ADHD-friendly charts is: animate to explain, never to decorate. And always let the user control the pace.

Dyslexia: the text layer

Charts are visual, but they depend heavily on text. Axis labels, tick marks, legends, titles, annotations, tooltips. For a dyslexic reader, each of these text elements is a potential stumbling block. Small text is harder. Serif fonts are harder. Italics are harder. Low contrast between text and background is harder. Tight letter spacing is harder.

The fixes are straightforward. Use a sans-serif font with generous letter spacing (0.05em to 0.12em extra). Increase the minimum label size. Never use italics for data labels. Ensure a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 between text and its background. These changes cost nothing in information content. They just make the text layer less hostile to people whose brains process letterforms differently.

OpenDyslexic is a purpose-built typeface that weights the bottom of each letter to reduce the perceived "flipping" that many dyslexic readers experience. It is not universally preferred (some dyslexic readers dislike it), but it represents the kind of specialized intervention that a preference system should offer.

Low numeracy: making quantities concrete

A bar chart showing "Healthcare: 42%" assumes the reader knows what a percentage means. This seems obvious, until you consider that in large-scale numeracy assessments, roughly a quarter of adults in developed countries cannot reliably perform percentage calculations. A logarithmic scale, which appears in countless scientific visualizations, is even more opaque. "103" requires understanding exponentiation. "One thousand" does not.

The adaptations here are about translation, not simplification. Instead of "42%," write "42 out of every 100." Instead of a bar, show an icon array: 42 filled icons and 58 empty ones out of a 10-by-10 grid. Instead of a logarithmic axis labeled "10, 100, 1000, 10000," write "10 times more" at each step. Instead of "450 square kilometers," write "about the size of a small city."

These translations preserve the information. They replace mathematical notation with concrete language that maps onto physical intuition. A reader who cannot calculate 42% of 500 can still look at 42 filled dots and 58 empty ones and get the proportion instantly, because counting and pattern recognition are lower on the cognitive stack than arithmetic.

Cognitive fatigue: less is available

Fatigue does not change what you know. It changes what you can recruit at any given moment. A data scientist at 9 AM might comfortably read a multi-panel faceted chart with dual axes and a color-coded legend. The same person at 6 PM, after eight hours of meetings, might struggle with the same chart. The knowledge is there. The processing bandwidth is not.

Progressive disclosure is the primary tool. Start with the simplest possible version of the chart: a single number, or a single bar, or a one-sentence summary. Then let the user request more complexity. "Show me the breakdown." "Add the trend line." "Show me last year for comparison." Each step adds one layer. The user stops adding layers when their current capacity is reached.

This is the opposite of how most dashboards work. Dashboards show everything at once and expect the user to filter down. For cognitive fatigue, the right default is minimal, with complexity available on demand.

Progressive disclosure is not dumbing down. It is respecting the difference between available information and available attention. Every layer of complexity is still there. The user controls when to engage with it.

The overlap with cognitive-load measurement

D3-power-tools already has a cognitive-load skill that measures the complexity of a generated chart: counting visual elements, estimating the number of comparisons a reader must make, flagging charts that exceed reasonable working-memory limits. Cognitive accessibility is the other half of that equation. Where the cognitive-load skill asks "how hard is this chart?", the cognitive accessibility adaptations ask "how can we make it easier for people who need it to be easier?"

The two should work together. The cognitive-load score gives you a number. The adaptations give you levers to reduce that number for specific audiences. A chart that scores 7 out of 10 on cognitive load might drop to 4 in focus mode and 3 in plain-numbers mode. The measurement informs the adaptation, and the adaptation validates the measurement.

// Sketch: cognitive accessibility as a rendering parameter
const chart = renderBarChart(data, {
  cognitiveProfile: "focus",   // or "dyslexia", "plain-numbers", "fatigue"
  maxCognitiveLoad: 4,        // target score, adaptations applied to meet it
  progressiveSteps: true,     // enable step-through for focus/fatigue modes
  labelStyle: "accessible",   // larger, higher contrast, more spacing
});

Preferences vs. detection

There are two ways to deliver cognitive adaptations. You can let the user choose, or you can try to detect their needs automatically.

User preferences are the safe path. A settings panel with options like "reduced complexity," "larger text," "simplified numbers," and "step-through mode" lets people self-select the adaptations they need. This respects autonomy. It avoids false assumptions. It works for people whose needs do not fit neatly into diagnostic categories.

Automatic detection is tempting and dangerous. You could track mouse jitter (fatigue), reading speed (dyslexia), or interaction abandonment (attention). But these signals are noisy, the inferences are unreliable, and the experience of having a system decide you are cognitively impaired is, for many people, insulting. A system that silently switches to "easy mode" because it detected slow reading is making a medical judgment it is not qualified to make.

The right approach is preferences with good defaults. Offer the settings. Make them easy to find (not buried three menus deep). Remember the choice across sessions. And make the default rendering as cognitively accessible as possible without triggering the adaptations, which means clean design, good contrast, and reasonable information density are not accessibility features. They are just good design that happens to reduce the gap between the standard mode and the adapted modes.

The moonshine connection

Moonshine explanations exist to teach. They are shared URLs that people encounter through search results, social links, and forwarded messages. The reader might be a graduate student with ADHD, a municipal planner with low numeracy, a retiree with cognitive fatigue trying to understand their pension fund's performance chart, or a parent with dyslexia helping their kid with a homework visualization.

None of these people will install a browser extension. None of them will configure accessibility settings before reading an explanation. If the explanation itself does not adapt, it will simply fail them, and they will close the tab, confirming their belief that "data stuff" is not for people like them.

For d3-power-tools, cognitive accessibility would mean that every generated visualization includes a mode switcher (or at minimum a "simplified view" toggle) as standard equipment. Not as an afterthought bolted on after the chart is done, but as part of the rendering architecture from the start. The skill that generates a bar chart should also generate the focus-mode variant, the dyslexia-friendly labels, and the icon-array alternative. These are not separate charts. They are the same chart rendered through different cognitive lenses.

This is where the LLM angle gets interesting. An LLM generating a D3 chart can, in principle, generate all four modes simultaneously, because generating the adaptations is a text-generation task, not a design task. The model writes code that produces a bar, and also writes code that produces the icon-array equivalent. The engineering challenge is not generating the variants. It is ensuring that the variants are correct, that the icon array actually represents the same data as the bar, and that the focus mode sequences the data in a narratively coherent order rather than just showing random bars one at a time.

Amber risks

  • Patronizing design. There is a fine line between "clearer" and "dumbed down." If the plain-numbers mode replaces a well-designed chart with a wall of text that reads like it was written for a child, it will alienate the very people it is meant to serve. The goal is to translate, not to condescend. "42 out of 100" is not simpler than "42%." It is more concrete. The difference matters.
  • The settings-menu trap. Offering too many cognitive accessibility options is itself a cognitive burden. A panel with twelve toggles for font size, spacing, contrast, animation, progressive disclosure, icon arrays, number format, and summary mode will overwhelm the very users it is designed for. Group adaptations into coherent profiles. Offer three or four presets, not a control panel.
  • LLMs applying adaptations globally. If a skill always generates the focus-mode variant, every chart becomes a step-through chart, even when the reader does not need or want that. Adaptations must be opt-in preferences, not global defaults. The base chart should be well-designed for a general audience, with modes available for those who need them.
  • Performance cost of multiple rendering paths. Generating four variants of every chart means four times the DOM nodes, four times the layout calculations, and four code paths to maintain. Lazy rendering (only build the variant when the user selects it) helps, but the code complexity remains. This is a maintenance burden that compounds across every chart type.
  • Testing with actual users. Checking a "dyslexia-friendly" box without testing with dyslexic readers is accessibility theater. The adaptations described here are based on research and established guidelines, but guidelines are averages. Real users have specific, sometimes contradictory, preferences. User testing with people from each target population is not optional. It is the only way to know whether the adaptations actually work.

What it would take

Building cognitive accessibility into d3-power-tools requires work at three levels. First, a rendering architecture that supports mode switching: the same data flowing through different visual encodings, with shared state so that switching modes does not lose the user's scroll position, selection, or navigation context. Second, a set of adaptation primitives (icon-array renderer, step-through controller, accessible label formatter, progressive-disclosure manager) that skills can compose rather than reinventing per chart type. Third, a preference system that stores the user's chosen profile and applies it across every chart on the page, with a simple, low-cognitive-load interface for changing it.

The harder part, as with multilingual support, is not the code. It is the testing and iteration with actual users. A developer without ADHD cannot reliably judge whether a focus-mode chart is genuinely helpful or just different. A numerate programmer cannot gauge whether "42 out of 100" is truly more accessible than "42%" for someone who struggles with percentages. These judgments require user research, and that research requires recruiting participants from populations that are chronically underrepresented in usability studies.

The payoff, though, is large. Every adaptation that makes a chart more accessible to someone with ADHD also makes it clearer for someone in a hurry. Every dyslexia-friendly label is also easier to read on a phone screen in bright sunlight. Every icon array is also more engaging for someone who is simply bored by bar charts. Cognitive accessibility is not a niche concern. It is a set of design principles that makes visualizations better for everyone, with the largest gains going to the people who need them most.
