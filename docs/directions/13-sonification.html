Future Directions

Sonification

Data as sound: pitch mapping, rhythm encoding, audio sparklines, and screen-reader-friendly data exploration via audio. Visualization that works when you cannot see the screen.

Who this is for

Roughly 2.2 billion people worldwide have some form of visual impairment. For blind and low-vision users, a D3 line chart is invisible. Alt text helps. Data tables help more. But neither captures the shape of the data the way a well-made chart does for sighted users. You can describe a peak in words. You cannot make someone feel its steepness through a sentence.

Sonification is the mapping of data to non-speech audio. A rising pitch for a rising value. A faster beat for higher density. A silence for missing data. It translates the spatial grammar of visualization into the temporal grammar of sound, letting listeners perceive patterns through the ear instead of the eye.

Blind users are the clearest beneficiaries, but they are not the only ones. A surgeon monitoring patient vitals during an operation cannot look at a dashboard. A driver following traffic trends cannot glance at a chart. A cook with flour-covered hands cannot scroll through data. In all these cases, the eyes are busy but the ears are free. Sound fills a channel that vision cannot reach.

There is a third audience: pattern augmentation. Some data features are easier to hear than to see. A periodic signal buried in noise can be difficult to spot in a scatterplot, but a repeating pitch pattern in a sonification jumps out. Sonification does not replace visualization. It reveals things that visualization misses, and it reaches people that visualization cannot.

The goal is not to make charts audible for their own sake. It is to preserve the perceptual directness that makes charts powerful. A sighted user sees a peak and knows "something happened here" without reading a number. Sonification aims to give a listening user that same instant, pre-verbal recognition.

The mapping principles

Every sonification starts with a mapping: which data property controls which sound property. The choices are fewer than in visual encoding, and the constraints are tighter.

Pitch is the primary channel. It maps most naturally to continuous quantitative values. Higher values produce higher frequencies; lower values produce lower ones. The mapping is intuitive because we already talk about data going "up" and "down." The Web Audio API gives you this through OscillatorNode, where setting the frequency property to a value in hertz produces a tone at that pitch. A comfortable range for data sonification is roughly 200 Hz to 1000 Hz. Below that, pitch differences become muddy. Above that, the sound becomes grating.

Volume is the second channel. It maps well to magnitude or importance. A louder tone signals a larger value or a more significant data point. GainNode in the Web Audio API controls this. Volume is coarser than pitch as a perceptual channel. People can distinguish maybe five or six discrete volume levels without training. It works for a secondary dimension, not a primary one.

Duration encodes density or emphasis. A longer tone means "spend more time here." A short staccato tap means "this is one of many." Duration is how sonification encodes the difference between a single outlier and a dense cluster.

Stereo position adds a spatial dimension. Panning a tone left or right can encode a categorical grouping or a second continuous axis. Two data series playing simultaneously are distinguishable if one sits in the left ear and the other in the right. The Web Audio API provides StereoPannerNode for this.

Timbre encodes category. A sine wave sounds different from a sawtooth wave, which sounds different from a triangle wave. Changing the oscillator type distinguishes between data series without changing pitch or volume. This is the sonic equivalent of using different shapes in a scatterplot.

Continuous value (y-axis) → Pitch (200 Hz to 1000 Hz) Secondary magnitude → Volume (GainNode amplitude) Time / sequence position → Temporal position in playback Category / series → Timbre (sine, sawtooth, triangle) Spatial group → Stereo pan (left to right) Missing or null data → Silence (gap in playback)

Perceptual limits

Position along a common scale is the most accurate visual encoding. Cleveland and McGill showed that people can judge aligned positions with roughly 1-2% error. Pitch perception is far coarser. Untrained listeners can distinguish pitch differences of about 5%, or roughly a musical semitone. Trained musicians do better, around 1-2%, but you cannot assume musical training in your audience.

This means sonification is inherently lower-resolution than visualization for precise value comparison. If two data points differ by 3%, a sighted user might notice the difference in a bar chart. A listener probably will not hear it. Sonification excels at conveying shape (rising, falling, peaked, oscillating) rather than precise quantity. It is a gestalt channel, not a precision instrument.

Temporal resolution adds another constraint. A sonification that plays 50 data points needs enough time per point for the listener to register each one. At one point every 40 milliseconds, 50 points take two seconds. That is about the minimum. Faster than that, individual tones blur into a texture. Slower and the overall shape becomes hard to hold in working memory. The two-to-five-second range for a full-series playback is the sweet spot. This is why the concept of an "audio sparkline" works: a brief sonic gesture that conveys the contour of a data series, the way a visual sparkline conveys it in a tiny graphic.

Memory is the final limit. You can glance back at a chart to compare two regions. You cannot "glance back" at a sound that has already passed. Sonification is a sequential medium. The listener must hold the beginning of the series in memory while hearing the end. Playback controls, especially the ability to scrub back and forth, are not optional. They are essential for making sonification usable rather than merely impressive.

Psychoacoustics of data sonification

Pitch discrimination is not uniform across the frequency range. The ear is most sensitive to pitch changes between roughly 500 Hz and 4000 Hz, the region where speech formants live. Below 200 Hz, the just-noticeable difference between two tones widens considerably. Two bass notes that differ by 5 Hz are perceptually identical to most listeners, while two tones at 1000 Hz and 1005 Hz produce a detectable wobble. This has a direct consequence for sonification design: compressing data into the low-frequency range wastes discriminability. The 200 Hz to 1000 Hz range used in most sonification work is not arbitrary. It sits in the region where pitch resolution is good enough to convey data contours without being so high that the sound becomes irritating over sustained listening.

The Weber fraction for pitch, the proportional change needed for a listener to notice a difference, is approximately 0.3% for trained listeners at mid-frequencies and closer to 3% for untrained listeners. That means if your pitch scale maps a data range from 300 Hz to 600 Hz (one octave), an untrained listener can distinguish roughly 30 to 35 discrete levels. A trained musician might hear 100 or more. For comparison, a well-calibrated color scale might encode 7 to 10 perceptually distinct bins. Pitch is actually a high-resolution channel, but only if the listener is paying attention and the sound is played in isolation. In practice, the effective resolution drops because other factors degrade it.

Masking is the most important of those degrading factors. When two sounds occur simultaneously, the louder one can render the quieter one inaudible, or at least harder to parse. Frequency-domain masking is worst when the two tones are close in pitch: a 400 Hz tone at moderate volume can completely obscure a quieter 420 Hz tone. This is why dual-channel sonification (pitch for one dimension, a second tone for another) is treacherous. If the two pitch tracks converge to similar frequencies, the listener loses one of them. Temporal masking works similarly. A loud tone makes it harder to hear a quiet tone that immediately follows it, even after the loud tone ends. The forward-masking window lasts roughly 20 to 50 milliseconds. In a fast sonification where tones follow each other every 40 ms, this means each tone partially masks its successor. Designing around masking means keeping simultaneous channels separated in frequency and keeping sequential tones far enough apart in time for each to register cleanly.

Auditory stream segregation determines whether the listener perceives one sound sequence or two. When two interleaved tone streams are close in pitch, the brain fuses them into a single stream. When they are far apart, the brain segregates them into distinct perceptual objects, the way two instruments playing in different registers sound like separate voices rather than a single texture. The crossover point depends on tempo and pitch separation. At slow tempos (below 4 notes per second), streams with pitch gaps as small as a major third remain segregated. At faster tempos (8 to 12 notes per second), the pitch gap needs to be an octave or more before the ear reliably splits them apart.

This matters for multi-series sonification. If you play two data series simultaneously by interleaving their tones, the listener will hear them as two streams only if the pitch ranges are sufficiently far apart. Two stock prices whose sonified ranges overlap will merge into a single warbling mess. Two series mapped to separate octaves, one between 200 Hz and 400 Hz and the other between 600 Hz and 1200 Hz, will separate cleanly. Stream segregation is the auditory equivalent of using distinct colors in a multi-line chart. Without it, the lines overlap and become unreadable.

The practical takeaway from psychoacoustics: pitch is a strong channel but a fragile one. It degrades under masking, compresses at low frequencies, and loses separability when streams overlap. Designing sonification well means respecting these constraints the same way good visualization design respects color-blindness and contrast sensitivity.

Musical vs non-musical mappings

A tempting shortcut in sonification design is to quantize pitch to a musical scale. Instead of mapping data values to arbitrary frequencies, you snap them to the notes of C major, or A minor pentatonic, or some other familiar scale. The result sounds pleasant. People describe it as "musical." And this is exactly the problem.

A musical scale imposes structure that may not exist in the data. The pentatonic scale has five notes per octave, meaning your data gets binned into five levels regardless of its actual distribution. The major scale has seven. These bins are not evenly spaced in frequency: the intervals between notes follow a pattern (whole, whole, half, whole, whole, whole, half for major) that has nothing to do with the data. A listener hearing a "melody" will apply melodic expectations. If the sonified output happens to move in stepwise motion up a scale, it sounds like a rising melody, and the listener infers intent and direction that may just be an artifact of the quantization.

Worse, musical scales carry emotional connotations. Major keys sound "happy." Minor keys sound "sad." The Dorian mode sounds "medieval." None of these associations have anything to do with unemployment statistics or river discharge rates, but the listener will feel them anyway. A sonification of declining GDP that happens to land on notes in A minor sounds ominous in a way that a linear frequency mapping would not. The musical framing adds an editorial layer that the designer may not have intended and the listener cannot easily ignore.

Non-musical mappings, where pitch varies continuously and is not quantized to any scale, avoid this problem. A continuous glide from 300 Hz to 700 Hz carries no melodic expectation. The listener hears "rising" without hearing "happy" or "resolving to the tonic." The tradeoff is that continuous mappings sound less pleasant. They can sound clinical or alien. For sustained listening (a 30-second data exploration session), this unfamiliarity is a real usability cost.

The pragmatic middle ground is to use a large scale with many degrees. A chromatic scale (12 notes per octave) provides fine enough resolution that the quantization loss is small, while still producing tones that do not sound eerily out of tune. Alternatively, you can use just intonation intervals without enforcing any particular key, so that each tone has a clean harmonic relationship to its neighbors without implying a tonal center. The point is to be deliberate about the choice. If you quantize to a scale, know that you are adding structure. If you leave pitch continuous, know that you are sacrificing familiarity. Neither is wrong. Both are design decisions with consequences.

The question to ask is: does this sonification sound like music? If yes, does the "music" communicate anything true about the data, or is it just the sound of the scale imposing its own grammar? A sonification that sounds like a jaunty tune while representing a public health crisis has not failed technically. It has failed rhetorically.

The earcon vocabulary

Beyond continuous pitch mapping, sonification benefits from a vocabulary of distinct sound events. These are called earcons, the auditory equivalent of icons.

Outlier click. When a data point exceeds a threshold, a short percussive click or chime overlays the tone. The listener hears the pitch mapping continue, but the click says "this one is unusual." A brief noise burst, easily generated by creating a very short oscillator with a fast decay envelope, works well.

Trend-change chirp. When the direction of the data reverses (from rising to falling, or vice versa), a short ascending or descending glissando signals the turn. The listener does not need to detect the reversal from pitch alone. The chirp annotates it.

Null gap. Missing data produces silence. Not a zero-pitched tone, not a special frequency, just absence. Silence is unambiguous and immediately noticeable in an otherwise continuous stream.

Boundary tick. At regular intervals (every 10 data points, or every temporal unit), a soft tick provides orientation. Without ticks, the listener loses track of position within the series. With them, there is a rhythmic scaffold to anchor the pitch contour against.

These earcons are learned quickly. After hearing two or three playbacks with the click-for-outlier convention, listeners internalize the vocabulary. The key requirement is consistency: the same sound must always mean the same thing, across every sonification in the system.

Audio sparklines

A visual sparkline is a tiny chart, perhaps 80 pixels wide and 20 tall, embedded in a table cell or a sentence. It sacrifices axes, labels, and precision for compactness. What it preserves is shape. You see at a glance whether a series is rising, falling, volatile, or flat.

An audio sparkline does the same thing for the ear. It is a two-to-three-second audio clip that compresses an entire data series into a pitch contour. No axis labels, no numeric readout, just the sonic shape of the data. You hear the rise, the peak, the dip, the recovery.

Audio sparklines could appear anywhere visual sparklines do. Imagine a data table where each row has a tiny play button. Pressing it plays a three-second sonification of that row's trend. A blind user navigating the table with a screen reader encounters the button, presses it, and hears the shape. Without the sparkline, they would need to read each individual value and construct the shape mentally. With it, they hear the gestalt in three seconds.

The technical implementation is straightforward. Pre-schedule all the tones using AudioContext.currentTime, let the Web Audio API handle playback, and the entire sparkline runs in a single scheduling call. No animation loop, no requestAnimationFrame, just a batch of oscillator start and stop times computed up front.

// Schedule an audio sparkline: all tones pre-computed
function playSparkline(values, ctx, duration = 2.5) {
  const pitchScale = d3.scaleLinear()
    .domain(d3.extent(values))
    .range([220, 880]);
  const dt = duration / values.length;
  const now = ctx.currentTime;

  values.forEach((v, i) => {
    const osc = ctx.createOscillator();
    const gain = ctx.createGain();
    osc.type = 'sine';
    osc.frequency.value = pitchScale(v);
    gain.gain.value = 0.15;
    osc.connect(gain).connect(ctx.destination);
    osc.start(now + i * dt);
    osc.stop(now + i * dt + dt * 0.9);
  });
}

Multi-channel sonification

A scatterplot encodes two dimensions simultaneously: x-position and y-position. A sonification can do the same by layering two audio channels. Pitch for one dimension, volume for the other. Or pitch for one dimension, stereo pan for the other. The listener parses the two channels in parallel, the same way a viewer parses horizontal and vertical position in parallel.

The interactive figure above demonstrates a simple version: pitch encodes the primary y-value, and volume encodes the secondary dimension (dot size). Larger dots sound louder. This dual encoding lets the listener detect correlations. If louder tones consistently occur at higher pitches, the two dimensions are positively correlated. If louder tones appear at all pitch levels equally, there is no relationship.

More ambitious multi-channel designs are possible but dangerous. Three simultaneous audio dimensions (pitch, volume, pan) approach the limit of what most listeners can parse. Adding a fourth (timbre) pushes past it. The visual equivalent would be encoding four dimensions with position, color, size, and shape simultaneously. Technically possible, perceptually overloaded. Two channels are reliable. Three are feasible for trained users. Four are research territory, not production design.

The four available audio channels, listed in descending order of perceptual resolution, are: pitch (roughly 30+ distinguishable levels for untrained listeners), volume (5 to 6 levels), stereo pan (3 to 5 positions: hard left, left, center, right, hard right), and timbre (3 to 4 categories before they start blending). Pitch dominates. It is the only channel precise enough to carry continuous quantitative data. The others serve as categorical or low-resolution supplements. This asymmetry is why most practical sonification designs use pitch as the backbone and one other channel as a supporting dimension.

The two-channel limit for untrained listeners is not a guideline. It is an empirical finding from listening experiments. When researchers ask participants to judge three simultaneous audio attributes (pitch, loudness, and timbre of a single tone), accuracy on each attribute drops by 15 to 25% compared to judging them in isolation. The interference is not because the channels share hardware in the ear. It is because the channels share attention in the brain. Pitch and loudness are processed by overlapping neural populations in the auditory cortex, and attending to one partially suppresses the other. Two channels work because the listener can alternate attention between them fast enough to track both. Three channels exceed the switching rate for most people.

For trained listeners, the story is different. Musicians who spend years parsing complex textures (a string quartet has four simultaneous pitch streams, each with independent volume and timbre) can handle three or even four sonification channels. But designing for trained listeners means designing for a narrow audience. The accessibility argument for sonification, that it should serve people excluded from visual charts, is undermined if the sonification itself requires specialized training to interpret. Design for two channels. Test with three. Do not ship four unless you have evidence that your specific audience can parse them.

Playback control and linked interaction

The most important feature of any sonification interface is not the sound. It is the ability to control the sound. Play, pause, speed adjustment, and scrubbing are the minimum. Without them, the listener is trapped in a linear stream with no way to revisit, compare, or explore.

Scrubbing is the critical interaction. In the figure above, clicking or dragging on the chart plays the tone at that position. This is the sonic equivalent of hovering over a data point to see its tooltip. The listener moves the cursor (or a finger, on touch devices) and hears the pitch change in real time. Patterns become explorable. You can linger on the peak, sweep back and forth across the valley, compare two distant points by scrubbing between them.

Linked interaction connects the visual and auditory channels. When the sonification plays, the cursor on the chart moves in sync. When the user scrubs the chart, the sound follows. This linkage serves both sighted and blind users. A sighted user sees the cursor and hears the tone, reinforcing both channels. A blind user using a screen reader navigates the chart by keyboard (arrow keys to step through points) and hears the pitch change at each step.

Speed control is less about convenience and more about resolution. At 0.5x speed, the listener hears each data point for twice as long, making subtle pitch changes more noticeable. At 2x speed, the overall shape compresses into a quicker gesture, useful for comparing the contours of two series back to back. The speed control trades temporal resolution for gestalt perception.

The Web Audio API in practice

The Web Audio API provides everything needed for data sonification. AudioContext is the root object. OscillatorNode generates tones. GainNode controls volume. StereoPannerNode handles left-right positioning. These nodes connect in a graph: oscillator to gain to panner to destination (the speakers).

The critical concept is scheduling. Instead of playing tones imperatively ("play this now, wait, play that now"), you schedule them against AudioContext.currentTime. This is a high-precision clock that the audio hardware drives. Scheduling a tone to start at currentTime + 0.5 guarantees it will start exactly 500 milliseconds from now, regardless of what JavaScript is doing on the main thread. This precision is what makes smooth sonification possible. A setTimeout-based approach would produce audible jitter.

// Basic Web Audio graph for a data tone
const ctx = new AudioContext();
const osc = ctx.createOscillator();
const gain = ctx.createGain();

osc.type = 'sine';             // timbre
osc.frequency.value = 440;      // pitch in Hz
gain.gain.value = 0.2;          // volume (0 to 1)

osc.connect(gain).connect(ctx.destination);
osc.start(ctx.currentTime);
osc.stop(ctx.currentTime + 0.1); // 100ms tone

One pitfall: each OscillatorNode can only be started once. After it stops, it is dead. For a sonification with 50 data points, you create 50 oscillators, schedule them all, and let the garbage collector clean up after playback. This feels wasteful if you are used to reusing objects, but it is how the API is designed and it performs well even for hundreds of tones.

For the speech mode shown in the figure, the approach is different. The Web Speech API's SpeechSynthesisUtterance reads values aloud. This is the most accessible option for screen-reader users, though it is also the slowest. Speaking "twenty-three, forty-one, sixty-seven" takes far longer than playing three tones. Speech sonification trades speed for explicitness.

Supporting moonshine

Moonshine explanations are built for the screen: interactive charts, scroll-driven narratives, visual figures. Sonification extends them to work without the screen. Not as a replacement for the visual content, but as a parallel channel that carries the same information through sound.

Consider a moonshine explanation that walks through a dataset using a scrollytelling narrative. At each scroll stage, a chart transitions to reveal a new pattern. A sonification layer could play a brief audio sparkline at each stage, giving a blind user the same "here is the shape" moment that the sighted user gets from the visual transition. The explanation becomes bimodal: visual and auditory, synchronized through the same scroll position.

A d3-power-tools skill for sonification would provide the mapping utilities (data-to-pitch, data-to-volume), the audio graph construction (oscillator chains with gain envelopes), the playback control primitives (play, pause, scrub, speed), and the earcon vocabulary (outlier click, trend-change chirp, null gap). An author building an explanation would write something like "add sonification to this line chart" and the skill would handle the audio plumbing, the same way existing skills handle axis construction or transition choreography.

The deeper connection to moonshine's purpose is about who gets to learn from interactive explanations. If every moonshine explanation assumes a sighted user, the audience is limited by default. Sonification skills would not make every explanation fully accessible on their own. But they would make accessibility a capability that the authoring system supports, rather than an afterthought that each author must build from scratch.

Where this can go wrong

  • Annoying sounds in shared spaces. A sonification that auto-plays in an open-plan office is not accessible. It is hostile. Every sonification must start muted or behind a deliberate user action (a Play button, a keyboard shortcut). The default state is silence. Always.
  • Crude mappings that sound like noise. Mapping 500 data points to rapid-fire tones produces an unpleasant screech, not insight. Sonification requires the same design care as visualization: choosing the right resolution, filtering out noise, aggregating where appropriate. A bad sonification is worse than a data table, because a data table is at least quiet.
  • The temptation to sonify everything. Not every dataset benefits from sonification. A categorical bar chart with five bars is better served by a data table that a screen reader can navigate. Sonification is strongest for temporal series, continuous distributions, and pattern detection. Applying it universally dilutes the cases where it genuinely helps.
  • Browser autoplay restrictions. Modern browsers require a user gesture before audio can play. An AudioContext created without a click or keypress starts in a "suspended" state and produces no sound. Any sonification implementation must handle this: resuming the context on first interaction, or creating it lazily inside a click handler. Code that works in local development and fails silently in production because of autoplay policy is a common trap.
  • LLMs generating Web Audio code without understanding scheduling. The Web Audio API's scheduling model is unintuitive. An LLM that generates sonification code may use setTimeout instead of AudioContext.currentTime, producing jittery playback. Or it may reuse an oscillator that has already stopped, producing silence. Or it may forget to connect the gain node to the destination, producing nothing at all. The audio graph pattern is specific enough that a dedicated skill with correct boilerplate is safer than open-ended generation.

From novelty to infrastructure

Most sonification projects are demos. They appear at conferences, get shared on social media, and then sit unused. The gap between "this is a cool sound" and "this is a tool I rely on" is enormous. Crossing it requires the same investment that visualization made over decades: consistent conventions, user testing, integration with real workflows, and boring reliability.

For d3-power-tools, the question is whether sonification skills can become part of the standard toolkit rather than a novelty add-on. That depends on whether authors actually use them. Which depends on whether the authoring experience is easy enough to justify the effort. Which depends on whether the skills handle the boilerplate (audio graph setup, scheduling, playback controls, autoplay workaround) so the author can focus on the mapping decisions that require design judgment.

The interactive figure on this page uses roughly 200 lines of Web Audio code. Most of that is plumbing: creating oscillators, connecting gain nodes, scheduling start and stop times, handling the play/stop state machine, managing the cursor animation. The design-relevant code is about 20 lines: the pitch scale, the volume scale, the duration calculation. A well-designed skill would encapsulate the 180 lines of plumbing and expose the 20 lines of mapping. That ratio, 90% infrastructure to 10% design, is exactly where skills are most valuable.

Visualization was once a novelty too. There was a time when "putting data on a screen" was the demo, not the tool. Sonification is earlier on that curve. The underlying technology (Web Audio API) is mature and well-supported. The perceptual research exists. The missing piece is the authoring infrastructure that makes sonification routine rather than heroic. That is the piece d3-power-tools could provide.
