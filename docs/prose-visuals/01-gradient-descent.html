How Gradient Descent Finds Minima

Finding the bottom of a bowl you cannot see.

Imagine you are blindfolded, standing somewhere on hilly terrain, and your only goal is to reach the lowest point. You cannot see the landscape. But you can feel the slope under your feet: which direction goes downhill, and how steep it is. So you take a step downhill. Then another. Then another. That is gradient descent.

In machine learning, the "terrain" is a loss surface: a function that measures how wrong your model is. The horizontal axes are the model's parameters. The vertical axis (or color, in a map view) is the loss. Training a model means finding parameters that make the loss as small as possible. Gradient descent is the algorithm that does the walking.

The Loss Landscape

A loss surface with two parameters looks like a topographic map. Dark regions are valleys (low loss, good). Bright regions are ridges and plateaus (high loss, bad). The contour lines connect points of equal loss, like elevation lines on a hiking map.

Click anywhere on the map below to drop a walker. It will follow the gradient downhill from wherever you place it. Drop several walkers and watch: starting position determines everything. Some walkers find the global minimum. Others get trapped in local minima, unable to sense the deeper valley on the other side of the ridge.

The gradient is a vector that points in the direction of steepest ascent. Gradient descent goes the opposite way, stepping in the direction of steepest descent. At any point, the gradient tells you which way is downhill and how steep the slope is.

Learning Rate: Step Size Matters

The gradient tells you which direction to step, but not how far. That distance is the learning rate. Too small, and you inch toward the minimum over thousands of steps, wasting computation. Too large, and you overshoot the valley, bouncing back and forth across it or flying off entirely.

There is a sweet spot. The optimal learning rate depends on the shape of the loss surface, which changes as you move through it. This is why modern optimizers adapt the learning rate during training.

Beyond Vanilla: Momentum and Adaptive Methods

Plain gradient descent (often called vanilla SGD) has a problem with elongated valleys. The gradient points along the steep walls of the valley rather than toward the bottom, so the optimizer zigzags back and forth, making slow progress along the valley floor.

Three ideas fix this. Momentum adds a velocity term: the optimizer accumulates speed in consistent directions and dampens oscillations. Adam goes further, tracking both the first moment (mean) and second moment (variance) of gradients per parameter, adapting the effective learning rate for each dimension independently.

Adam is not always better. On some problems, well-tuned SGD with momentum generalizes better than Adam. The choice of optimizer is an empirical question, not a settled one.

Stochastic: Learning from Samples

Everything above assumes you compute the gradient using your entire dataset. In practice, datasets are enormous, and computing the full gradient is expensive. Instead, you estimate the gradient from a small random subset: a mini-batch.

The estimate is noisy. A single sample gives a gradient that points in roughly the right direction but wanders. A larger batch gives a cleaner estimate but costs more. The slider below controls batch size. Watch how the gradient arrows become more aligned as the batch grows.

This noise is not purely a nuisance. Stochastic gradients help the optimizer escape shallow local minima: the noise can bounce it out of a bad valley and into a better one. It also acts as a form of implicit regularization, biasing the model toward flatter minima that tend to generalize better.

Gradient descent is a conversation between the model and the data, one step at a time. The gradient says "you are wrong in this direction." The learning rate says "how much to adjust." The batch says "according to these examples." And the optimizer says "given everything I have seen so far, here is where to step next."
