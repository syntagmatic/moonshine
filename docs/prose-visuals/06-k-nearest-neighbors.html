K-Nearest Neighbors

The simplest classifier, and the first lesson in the bias-variance tradeoff.

You are the average of your K closest friends. This folk wisdom is also a classification algorithm. Given a new data point, look at its K nearest neighbors in the training data. Whatever class the majority of those neighbors belong to, that is the prediction. No model to fit, no parameters to learn. Just memory and proximity.

KNN is the most literal form of "learning by example." It stores the training data and does all its work at prediction time, measuring distances and counting votes. The simplicity is the point. It makes everything visible: how neighborhood size shapes decisions, why distance matters, and where the method breaks down.

The Algorithm: Find Neighbors, Take a Vote

Click anywhere in the scatter plot below to place a query point. A circle will expand outward from your click until it encompasses K nearest neighbors. Those neighbors vote, and the majority wins.

Figure 1. Click to classify. The circle grows to capture K neighbors, then the majority vote decides. Try different locations, especially near class boundaries.

Near the center of a cluster, every neighbor agrees. The prediction is confident. But near the boundary between two classes, the vote is tight. A single neighbor can flip the outcome. This is where K matters most.

Choosing K: The Bias-Variance Dial

K is not a minor detail. It is the single control that determines how complex the model is. At K=1, every training point has its own territory. The decision boundary is jagged, perfectly memorizing the training data, including its noise. At high K, the boundary smooths out, capturing the real structure but potentially blurring genuine class distinctions. At K = N (all points), the entire space is classified as the majority class. No structure at all.

The bias-variance tradeoff in one sentence: low K means low bias but high variance (sensitive to noise). High K means high bias but low variance (stable but blunt). The goal is not to minimize either one, but to find the K where their sum is smallest.

The Curse of Dimensionality

KNN relies on a simple assumption: nearby points are similar. In two dimensions, this works. A small circle around a query point captures a meaningful neighborhood. But in higher dimensions, "nearby" stops meaning what you think it means.

Consider: to capture K neighbors out of N uniformly distributed points, the neighborhood must cover a fraction of the total space proportional to K/N. In 2D, covering 5% of the space means a circle with a modest radius. In 10 dimensions, covering the same 5% of the space requires a neighborhood whose side reaches across 74% of each axis. In 20 dimensions, it reaches 86%. The "local" neighborhood is nearly global.

The slider below shows this effect. As the number of dimensions grows, the neighborhood needed to capture a fixed fraction of the space approaches the full extent of the data.

To cover 5% of the volume in 2D, the neighborhood spans 22% of each axis.
Figure 3. The curse of dimensionality. The left panel shows the neighborhood in data space. The right panel shows how the required side length grows with dimensionality. In high dimensions, every point is far from every other point, and "nearest" neighbors are barely closer than the farthest point.

This is why KNN struggles with high-dimensional data. When every point is roughly equidistant from every other point, the concept of a nearest neighbor becomes meaningless. The algorithm still runs; it just stops learning anything useful. Feature selection and dimensionality reduction are not optional preprocessing steps for KNN. They are survival strategies.

Distance Matters: The Shape of a Neighborhood

We have been assuming Euclidean distance, but that is a choice, not a law. Different distance metrics produce differently shaped neighborhoods, and the shape determines which points are considered "near."

Toggle between three metrics below. The neighborhood shape changes, the set of K nearest neighbors changes, and the decision boundary shifts.

Figure 4. The shape of "nearby" depends on the distance metric. Euclidean distance produces circular neighborhoods. Manhattan distance produces diamonds. Chebyshev distance produces squares. Click to place a query point and see how the K nearest neighbors change under each metric.

Euclidean distance treats all directions equally, producing circular neighborhoods. Manhattan distance (the sum of absolute differences along each axis) favors axis-aligned movement, producing diamond-shaped neighborhoods. Chebyshev distance (the maximum absolute difference along any axis) produces square neighborhoods. For many real datasets, the choice of metric matters less than the choice of K. But for data where certain axes have different scales or meanings, the metric can be decisive.

KNN teaches the most important lesson in machine learning: the right amount of complexity depends on how much data you have. Too little smoothing and you memorize noise. Too much and you erase signal. Every model, from decision trees to neural networks, faces this same tradeoff. KNN just makes it visible.
