Bayes' Theorem

Updating beliefs with evidence, one observation at a time.

You take a medical test. It comes back positive. How worried should you be?

Your first instinct is to trust the test. If the test is 95% accurate, then there is a 95% chance you have the disease. Right?

Wrong. And the gap between intuition and reality here is not small. It is enormous. Understanding why requires a shift in how you think about probability, and that shift is exactly what Bayes' theorem provides.

The Base Rate Trap

Suppose a disease affects 1% of the population. A test for it has a 95% sensitivity (it correctly identifies 95% of sick people) and a 95% specificity (it correctly identifies 95% of healthy people). You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

The answer is about 16%. Not 95%. Not even close.

The figure below makes the arithmetic visible. The unit square represents the entire population. Its area is divided into four rectangles representing the four possible outcomes: true positives, false negatives, false positives, and true negatives. Look at where the positive tests come from.

Figure 1. The area model of Bayes' theorem. Each rectangle's area is proportional to the joint probability of that outcome. The highlighted regions show everyone who tested positive. Most of them do not have the disease.

The base rate is the hidden variable. When a condition is rare, even a very accurate test produces more false positives than true positives, because the "no disease" group is so much larger that even its small false-positive rate generates more cases in absolute terms.

Drag the base rate slider up and watch the posterior climb. At 10% prevalence the same test yields a posterior above 67%. The test did not change. The population changed. That is the lesson: the meaning of evidence depends on what you believed before you saw it.

Bayes' Theorem

What you just saw has a formula behind it. Bayes' theorem says:

P(disease | positive) = P(positive | disease) × P(disease) / P(positive)

In words: the probability of the hypothesis given the evidence equals the likelihood of the evidence under the hypothesis, times the prior probability of the hypothesis, divided by the total probability of the evidence.

The formula is simple. The insight is not. The denominator, P(positive), accounts for all the ways you could test positive, including false positives from the much larger healthy group. That denominator is what drags the posterior down when the base rate is low.

But Bayes' theorem is not just for one-shot medical tests. It is a general rule for updating beliefs with evidence. And the real power shows when you apply it repeatedly.

Sequential Updates

Imagine you do not know the bias of a coin. Is it fair? Loaded? You have no idea, so you start with a uniform prior: every bias from 0 to 1 is equally plausible. Then you start flipping.

After 20 the distribution is tight. After 50 you are quite sure. The belief does not jump to certainty; it migrates there gradually, proportional to the evidence.

How Much Does the Prior Matter?

A common objection to Bayesian reasoning: "The prior is subjective. Different people start with different beliefs. How can the result be trustworthy?"

The answer is convergence. Below, three observers watch the same coin flips but start with different priors. One is uniform (no opinion). One is skeptical (believes the coin is probably fair). One is credulous (believes it is probably biased toward heads). Watch what happens as evidence arrives.

Figure 3. Three priors, same evidence. With few observations the estimates diverge. With many, they converge. The data overwhelms the prior.

This is the Bayesian promise: reasonable people who start with different beliefs will converge to the same conclusion given enough evidence. The prior matters early on, when data is scarce. It matters less and less as evidence accumulates. Subjectivity in the prior is not a flaw; it is a feature that gets washed out.

Bayesian vs. Frequentist

The Bayesian approach is not the only way to reason about uncertainty. The frequentist tradition, dominant in much of classical statistics, takes a different path. Same data, different philosophy, different output.

Below, you generate coin flips. On the left, the frequentist view: a point estimate (the fraction of heads) with a 95% confidence interval. On the right, the Bayesian view: a full posterior distribution over the coin's bias. Both get narrower with more data. Both converge to the truth. But the Bayesian posterior tells you more: not just "the estimate is around 0.6" but "the probability that the bias is between 0.55 and 0.65 is 73%."

Figure 4. Same coin, same data, two worldviews. The frequentist gives a point estimate and confidence interval. The Bayesian gives a full probability distribution. Both converge with enough data, but the posterior carries richer information at every step.

Neither approach is wrong. They answer different questions. The frequentist asks: "If I repeated this experiment many times, what range would contain the true value 95% of the time?" The Bayesian asks: "Given what I have seen, what do I believe about the true value right now?" For a single decision under uncertainty, the Bayesian answer is often more directly useful.

Being Less Wrong

Bayesian reasoning is not about reaching certainty. It is about becoming less wrong, one observation at a time. You start with whatever you believe. Evidence arrives. You update. Your beliefs shift. Not all at once, not by fiat, but proportionally, mechanically, honestly.

The base rate trap shows that context matters more than you think. Sequential updating shows that patience pays: each observation makes the picture a little sharper. Prior sensitivity shows that starting beliefs wash out under sufficient evidence. And the comparison with frequentist methods shows that carrying a full distribution, rather than a point estimate, preserves information you might need.

The formula is three terms multiplied and divided. The worldview behind it is deeper: probability is not a property of the world. It is a property of your state of knowledge. And knowledge is not binary. It updates.
