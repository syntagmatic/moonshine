Markov Chains

Stochastic processes with no memory. The next state depends only on the current state, never on how you got there.

Tomorrow's weather depends on today's, not yesterday's. If it's raining now, the chance of sun tomorrow is the same whether yesterday was sunny or cloudy. This is the memoryless property, and it defines a Markov chain: a system that wanders between states, choosing its next step based only on where it stands right now.

The idea sounds restrictive. History is thrown away at every step. Yet this simple rule produces rich behavior: random walks that settle into stable patterns, chains that trap you forever, and distributions that emerge from chaos. The mathematics is a 3x3 matrix. The intuition comes from watching the process run.

States, Transitions, and the Walk

A weather model with three states: Sunny, Cloudy, and Rainy. Each arrow carries a probability. From Sunny, there's a 0.6 chance of staying sunny, a 0.3 chance of clouds, and a 0.1 chance of rain. These probabilities must sum to 1, because the system always goes somewhere on each step.

The particle below walks the chain. Watch it choose its next state at random, weighted by the arrow probabilities. The trail at the bottom records where it has been.

Run it long enough and patterns emerge. Sunny appears more often than Rainy, even though every step is random. That is not an accident; it is the chain's steady-state distribution at work, which we will compute shortly.

The Transition Matrix

The same information, different form. Each row of the matrix is a state you might be in now. Each column is a state you could move to next. The cell at row i, column j is the probability of transitioning from state i to state j. Every row sums to exactly 1.

Click any cell to watch that transition happen in the state diagram above. Drag up or down on a cell to change its probability. The row renormalizes automatically, the diagram redraws, and the particle's behavior changes.

Figure 2. The transition matrix. Rows are "from" states, columns are "to" states. Click a cell to trigger that transition above. Drag vertically on a cell to edit probabilities.

The matrix is the complete description of a Markov chain. Every question about the chain's behavior can be answered by doing arithmetic on this matrix.

Where You End Up

Run the chain for ten steps and count how often it visited each state. The fractions are noisy, sensitive to the random start. Run it for a hundred steps and the fractions stabilize. By a thousand steps, they have converged to fixed values that barely change with more steps.

Those fixed values are the steady-state distribution. It does not matter where the chain starts. Given enough time, the fraction of visits to each state settles to a value determined entirely by the transition probabilities. The dashed lines show the theoretical steady state, computed from the matrix's dominant eigenvector.

Figure 3. Empirical state frequencies (bars) converge to the theoretical steady state (dashed lines) as the number of steps increases. Scrub the slider to watch convergence happen.

Convergence is not guaranteed for every chain. It requires that every state can eventually be reached from every other state (irreducibility), and that the chain does not cycle with a fixed period (aperiodicity). The weather model satisfies both, because every state has a self-loop.

When You Cannot Escape

Not all chains converge to a steady state. Some have absorbing states: states you cannot leave once you enter them.

The gambler's ruin problem is the classic example. A gambler starts with some amount of money and bets $1 on each round. With probability 0.4 they win (gaining $1), and with probability 0.6 they lose (dropping $1). The game ends when they hit $0 (ruin) or reach $10 (the goal). Both endpoints are absorbing: once there, you stay forever.

Set a starting wealth and watch 100 simultaneous walks. Most paths drift toward ruin. A few make it to the goal. The house edge is only 0.1 per round, but it compounds relentlessly.

Figure 4. Gambler's ruin: 100 simultaneous random walks between absorbing states at $0 (ruin) and $10 (goal). The unfavorable odds (p=0.4 win) make ruin the likely outcome from most starting positions.

The gambler's ruin is not about gambling. It is the template for any process with absorbing barriers: population genetics (fixation), queueing theory (server failure), and random walks on finite graphs. The question is always the same: given where you start, what is the probability of reaching each absorber?

The Simplest Model of Sequential Uncertainty

A Markov chain strips a stochastic process down to its minimum: a set of states, a matrix of probabilities, and the rule that only the present matters. Throw away history, keep the local transition structure, and you still get convergence, absorption, mixing, and periodicity. It is the simplest model of sequential uncertainty, and it appears everywhere: language models predicting the next token, PageRank computing web importance, MCMC sampling from difficult distributions, and DNA sequences evolving one mutation at a time.

The four figures above cover the essential pieces. The state machine gives the topology. The matrix gives the algebra. Convergence shows that randomness has a destination. Absorption shows that some destinations are traps. Everything else in the theory is arithmetic on these foundations.
