Word Embeddings

Representing words as vectors in a space where geometry encodes meaning.

You can't do math on words. Unless you give them coordinates.

A computer sees the word "cat" as an ID, maybe token 4827 in a vocabulary of 50,000. That ID tells the machine nothing about what a cat is, what it does, or what other words share its world. One-hot encoding turns that ID into a vector with a single 1 among 49,999 zeros. Every word is equally distant from every other word. "Cat" is no closer to "dog" than it is to "democracy."

Word embeddings fix this by assigning each word a short, dense vector of real numbers, usually 100 to 300 dimensions. These coordinates are not assigned by hand. They are learned from text, positioned so that words appearing in similar contexts end up near each other. The result is a space where geometry carries meaning.

These clusters are not built in. Nobody told the model that "cat" and "dog" are both animals. The model learned it from millions of sentences where those words appear in similar roles: "The ___ sat on the mat," "She fed the ___," "The ___ ran across the yard." Words that substitute for each other end up at similar coordinates.

The geometry is the meaning. Distance measures semantic similarity. Direction encodes semantic relationships. Clusters reveal categories the model discovered on its own.

The Analogy Test

The most striking property of word embeddings is that relationships between words show up as consistent directions in the vector space. The classic example: take the vector for "king," subtract "man," add "woman," and the result lands near "queen." The direction from "man" to "king" encodes something like "royalty." That same direction, applied from "woman," produces the female equivalent.

This works because the embedding space has learned to encode relational categories as directions. Gender is roughly a direction. Country-to-capital is roughly a direction. Tense is roughly a direction. "Roughly" matters here: these analogies work well on average but not perfectly in every case. The space encodes statistical tendencies from the training corpus, not logical rules.

Similarity as Distance

If geometry is meaning, then we can measure how related two words are by how close their vectors sit. The standard measure is cosine similarity: the cosine of the angle between two vectors, ranging from 1 (identical direction) to -1 (opposite). Synonyms score near 1. Unrelated words hover around 0.

Figure 3. Cosine similarity from the selected word. Closer to the center means more similar. Rings mark similarity thresholds at 0.9, 0.7, 0.5, and 0.3.

Cosine similarity measures the angle between vectors, not the distance. Two vectors pointing in the same direction have high cosine similarity regardless of their magnitudes. This is why we normalize when comparing: direction carries meaning, length often does not.

How Embeddings Are Trained

Where do these coordinates come from? The core idea is simple: a word's meaning is the company it keeps. If you can predict which words appear near a given word, you can learn what that word means.

One approach (skip-gram, from Word2Vec) works like this: take a sentence, pick a center word, and try to predict the surrounding context words. Another approach (CBOW) goes the other direction: given the context, predict the center word. Either way, the model adjusts the word vectors so that predictions improve. Words that appear in similar contexts get pulled toward similar coordinates.

In practice, training runs over billions of words. Each sentence nudges the vectors a tiny amount. After enough nudges, the space settles into a configuration where similar words cluster, analogies hold, and the geometry reflects the statistical structure of language. The model never sees a dictionary or a thesaurus. It reconstructs semantic relationships purely from co-occurrence patterns.

The Map and Its Distortions

Word embeddings are a map of meaning. Like any map, they distort. A Mercator projection exaggerates the size of Greenland; an embedding space trained on internet text exaggerates the relationships that internet text emphasizes. Biases in the training data become biases in the geometry. If the corpus associates certain professions with certain genders, those associations show up as directions in the vector space.

The analogy test cuts both ways. "Doctor minus man plus woman equals nurse" is not a fact about language. It is a fact about the training data, and about the world that produced it.

Still, the core insight holds. By giving words coordinates, we make them computable. We can measure similarity, detect analogies, cluster topics, and feed meaning into downstream models. Modern language models like transformers build on this foundation: they learn contextual embeddings that change depending on the sentence, so "bank" near "river" gets different coordinates than "bank" near "money." But the principle is the same. Geometry encodes meaning. Distance is relationship. Direction is transformation.

The space is not language. But it is a surprisingly faithful map of how language uses meaning.
