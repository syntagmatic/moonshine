Clustering

K-means, hierarchical clustering, and the question of whether structure is discovered or imposed.

The data doesn't come with labels. No column says "Group A" or "Group B." There are only measurements, coordinates in some space, and the hope that points near each other share something meaningful. Clustering algorithms look for that structure, partitioning data into groups so that points within each group are more similar to each other than to points outside it.

The simplest and most widely used approach is K-means. The idea fits in one sentence: find K centers, assign each point to the nearest one, adjust, repeat.

K-Means, Step by Step

K-means alternates between two operations. First, assign each point to whichever centroid is closest. Second, update each centroid to the mean position of its assigned points. These two steps repeat until the centroids stop moving. The algorithm always converges, though where it converges depends on where it starts.

How Many Clusters?

K-means requires you to choose K before running. How? One common approach: run the algorithm for several values of K and plot the within-cluster variance. As K increases, variance always decreases (at K=N every point is its own cluster, variance is zero). But there is usually a bend in the curve where adding more clusters stops helping much. That bend is the "elbow."

Figure 2. Within-cluster variance vs. K. Click a value in the elbow plot or in the small multiples below to see that clustering on the main scatter plot. The elbow at K=3 suggests three groups, though K=4 isn't unreasonable.

The elbow method is a heuristic, not a proof. Silhouette scores, the gap statistic, and BIC-based methods offer more principled alternatives, but they all require judgment. There is no algorithm for choosing the right number of clusters.

Hierarchical Clustering

Instead of choosing K up front, hierarchical clustering builds a tree of merges. Start with every point as its own cluster. Find the two closest clusters and merge them. Repeat until everything belongs to a single cluster. The result is a dendrogram: a binary tree whose height at each merge encodes the distance between the merged clusters.

The useful part: you can "cut" the dendrogram at any height to get a different number of clusters. A low cut produces many small clusters; a high cut produces fewer large ones. Drag the cut line up and down to see how the clustering changes.

Single linkage chains: it defines cluster distance as the minimum point-to-point distance, so elongated chains of points merge greedily. Complete linkage uses the maximum distance, producing rounder clusters. Average linkage splits the difference. The choice is an editorial decision about what "close" means.

When Clustering Fails

K-means assumes clusters are roughly spherical and similar in size. When the real structure violates those assumptions, the algorithm produces confident but wrong assignments. Three common failure modes show this clearly.

Figure 4. Three datasets where K-means gives misleading results. Left column: K-means. Right column: a density-based alternative (DBSCAN). Concentric rings, unequal sizes, and elongated shapes all break K-means's assumption of convex, equally sized clusters.

Concentric rings have no convex boundary between them, so K-means cuts them with straight Voronoi lines. Clusters of very different sizes get distorted because K-means minimizes total variance, which favors splitting the large cluster over preserving the small one. Elongated clusters get sliced perpendicular to their long axis because K-means measures distance from a single center point.

DBSCAN, shown in the right column, uses a different definition of "cluster": a dense region separated from other dense regions by sparse space. It doesn't need K. It can find non-convex shapes. But it has its own parameters (neighborhood radius and minimum points), and it struggles with varying densities.

What Clustering Finds

Every clustering algorithm imposes a definition of structure. K-means finds K convex blobs. Hierarchical clustering finds a binary tree of nested merges. DBSCAN finds dense regions. The clusters you get depend as much on the algorithm you chose as on the data itself.

This isn't a flaw. It's the nature of unsupervised learning. There is no ground truth label to check against. Clustering finds what you tell it to find: K clusters of a certain shape, or a tree of merges under a certain linkage rule. The real question, always, is whether the structure the algorithm reveals is meaningful for your purpose, or whether you're just carving noise into convenient pieces.
