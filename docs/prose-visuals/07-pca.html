Principal Component Analysis

Finding the directions of maximum variance in data, and projecting down to fewer dimensions.

If you had to flatten 100 dimensions to 2, which 2 directions would you choose?

Not the original axes. Those are arbitrary. If your data lives in a 100-dimensional space but really only varies along a handful of directions, most of those axes are carrying noise. The signal lives in the directions where the data spreads out the most. Principal component analysis finds those directions.

The idea is geometric. Think of a cloud of points in space. That cloud has a shape. It might be stretched long in one direction and flat in another, like a cigar or a pancake. PCA finds the axes of that shape. The longest axis is the first principal component. The next longest, perpendicular to the first, is the second. And so on. Each axis captures a share of the total variance in the data, and by keeping only the axes that carry the most, you can collapse dimensions without losing much.

Variance Has Direction

Variance is usually taught as a single number: how spread out the data is. But when your data has more than one dimension, variance depends on which direction you measure it in. Project the data onto one axis and measure the spread. Rotate the axis, and the spread changes.

Try it. The scatter plot below shows 50 correlated data points in an elliptical cloud. Drag the projection axis to rotate it through the data. Watch the projected points collapse onto the line, and the histogram of projected values update alongside. The variance readout tells you how much spread the current axis captures.

When the axis aligns with the major axis of the ellipse, variance is maximized. Perpendicular to it, variance is minimized. The direction of maximum variance is the first principal component.

When the axis aligns with the long direction of the ellipse, the histogram spreads wide and the variance percentage peaks. When the axis is perpendicular, the histogram compresses and variance bottoms out. The direction of maximum variance is the first principal component. You just found it by hand.

Variance is information. If you project onto a direction where all points land on top of each other, you have learned nothing about the differences between them. A direction with high variance separates the data. A direction with low variance collapses it.

Finding the Components

PCA automates what you just did by dragging. The algorithm proceeds in a few clean steps: center the data, then find the directions where it stretches most. These directions come from the eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix. That sounds heavier than it is. The covariance matrix measures how much each pair of variables moves together. Its eigenvectors point in the directions of maximum and minimum variance. Its eigenvalues tell you how much variance each direction captures.

Watch the algorithm run on the same data. It centers the cloud, finds the first principal component (the direction of maximum spread), then finds the second (perpendicular to the first). The coordinate system rotates to align with the new axes.

Figure 2. The PCA algorithm animated. First the mean is computed, then the data is centered. PC1 grows as the direction of maximum variance, PC2 perpendicular to it. The bar chart on the right shows the eigenvalues, the amount of variance each component captures.

What the algorithm is doing, geometrically, is rotating the coordinate system to align with the data's natural axes of variation. The original x and y axes were arbitrary. The principal components are the axes the data wants. After this rotation, each new axis is uncorrelated with the others. The first few carry most of the information, and the rest are noise you can drop.

Projection and Reconstruction

Once you have the principal components, projection is straightforward. To go from the original high-dimensional space to a lower one, you keep only the first few PCs and drop the rest. Each dropped component loses some variance, some information about the original data. The question is: how much do you lose?

The figure below shows a three-dimensional dataset shaped like a flattened ellipsoid. Toggle the principal components on and off to see the effect. With all three components, you see the full 3D data. Remove PC3 (the thinnest direction) and the data collapses to a plane. The lost variance is small because the data was already nearly flat in that direction. Remove PC2 and it collapses further to a line. Now the reconstruction error is substantial.

Figure 3. A 3D point cloud rendered in isometric projection. Toggle principal components off to project the data into lower dimensions. The reconstruction error (sum of squared distances from original to projected positions) grows as you remove components that carry variance.

Reconstruction error is the sum of squared distances between the original points and their projections. It is exactly equal to the total variance of the dropped components. This is the fundamental tradeoff of PCA: fewer dimensions means simpler data but higher reconstruction error. The art is in choosing how many to keep.

The Scree Plot

When your data has many dimensions, the scree plot answers the question: how many principal components do you actually need? It shows the variance explained by each component in order. The first component captures the most, the second the next most, and so on. The cumulative line shows the running total.

The characteristic shape is a steep drop followed by a leveling off. The "elbow" where the curve bends is the point of diminishing returns. Components before the elbow carry structure. Components after it carry noise. Choose the number of components at the elbow and you keep the signal while discarding the noise.

In practice, the elbow is not always sharp. Noisy data has a gradual slope. Data with clear low-dimensional structure has a sharp drop. Some practitioners use a threshold instead: keep enough components to explain 90% or 95% of the variance. Others use cross-validation, holding out data to see whether additional components improve predictions. The scree plot is a starting point for that judgment, not a mechanical rule.

PCA does not find clusters, categories, or nonlinear patterns. It finds directions. The first principal component of a ring of data points passes straight through the center, missing the ring entirely. PCA works when the important structure in your data aligns with the directions of maximum variance. When it does not, you need a different tool.

What PCA Gives You

PCA is a rotation followed by a truncation. You rotate the coordinate system to align with the data's own axes of variation, then you throw away the axes that carry the least. What remains is a lower-dimensional representation that preserves as much of the original variance as possible.

This is useful whenever you need to compress, visualize, or denoise high-dimensional data. Gene expression datasets with 20,000 dimensions collapse to a handful of components that separate cell types. Image datasets with millions of pixels have principal components that look like eigenfaces. Financial returns across hundreds of stocks compress to a few market factors.

But PCA does not find structure. It finds directions. The structure is what is left after you project. If the important patterns in your data happen to align with the directions of greatest spread, PCA will find them. If they live in directions with small variance, PCA will throw them away. That is not a flaw. It is a definition. PCA keeps what varies most, and it is your job to decide whether what varies most is what matters.
