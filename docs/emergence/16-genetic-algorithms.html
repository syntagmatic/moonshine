Genetic Algorithms

Natural selection, applied to code, finds solutions no programmer would design

You have a problem with a huge number of possible solutions and no formula to jump straight to the best one. A scheduling problem, say, or a circuit layout. You could try them all, but that would take longer than the age of the universe. You could use a clever heuristic, but you don't have one.

There is another approach. Create a population of random guesses. Score each one. Let the better guesses reproduce while the worse ones die off. Introduce small random changes. Repeat. Over many generations, the population drifts toward good solutions. Nobody programs the answer. The algorithm discovers it the same way biology does: through variation and selection.

This is a genetic algorithm. It needs only three things: a way to encode a candidate solution, a fitness function that scores how good each candidate is, and a set of genetic operators (selection, crossover, mutation) to produce the next generation. The rest is evolution.

1. A Population of Guesses

The classic demonstration: evolve a string of characters to match a target phrase. Each individual in the population is a random string of the same length. Fitness is the number of characters that match the target. Each generation, the best individuals are selected, paired up, and their "genes" (characters) are crossed over and mutated.

Type a target phrase below and press Start. Watch how quickly selection pressure drives the population toward the answer, even though every starting string is pure noise.

The speed is surprising. A 28-character phrase drawn from 27 possible characters (letters plus space) has 2728 possibilities. Brute-force search would take billions of years. But with selection pressure, the algorithm converges in a few hundred generations. Each generation preserves what works and explores nearby. Evolution is not random search. It is directed exploration guided by fitness.

2. Evolving Shapes

Strings are one thing. Can evolution optimize something visual? Here the candidates are collections of translucent triangles drawn on a canvas. The target is a simple geometric shape. Fitness is measured by how closely the candidate image matches the target, pixel by pixel. Mutation nudges a vertex, changes a color, or adjusts transparency. Crossover swaps triangles between parents.

The approximation starts rough, just colored blobs. But over hundreds of generations, the triangles creep into position, layering and overlapping to reproduce the target's edges and shading. The result is never pixel-perfect, but it is recognizably close, and the path it takes to get there is nothing a human artist would choose.

The mutation rate slider reveals a fundamental tension. Low mutation means steady refinement but slow exploration. High mutation explores broadly but keeps disrupting good solutions. Somewhere in the middle is the sweet spot, and that sweet spot shifts as the population converges. Early on, big mutations help. Later, fine-tuning needs small ones. Real evolutionary systems have evolved their own mutation rates.

3. The Fitness Landscape

Imagine plotting every possible solution on a flat plane, with height representing fitness. The result is a landscape of hills and valleys. Evolution is a population of climbers on this landscape, pulled uphill by selection pressure and nudged sideways by mutation. A single hill-climber gets stuck on the nearest peak. A population can spread across multiple peaks, hedging its bets.

Below is a 2D fitness landscape. The colored dots are individuals in the population. Click anywhere to place a new fitness peak and watch how the population responds. With low mutation, individuals cluster tightly on the nearest peak. With high mutation, they spread out and discover distant peaks. This is the exploration versus exploitation tradeoff at the heart of every optimization method.

Notice what happens when you place a tall peak far from the current population. With low mutation, nobody finds it. The population stays stuck on its local hill. Crank up the mutation rate and a few scouts wander far enough to discover the new peak. Once they do, selection amplifies their discovery and the population flows toward it. This is why diversity matters. A uniform population is efficient but fragile. A diverse one is slower but adaptive.

Genetic algorithms have designed NASA antenna shapes that outperform hand-engineered ones, optimized neural network architectures, and evolved strategies for the Prisoner's Dilemma. The solutions are often alien-looking because evolution optimizes for function, not human aesthetics. The algorithm does not care what the solution looks like. It only cares that it works.
