Wave Interference

When waves overlap, they create new patterns that neither wave contains alone.

Drop two pebbles into still water. Each one sends out circular ripples. Where the ripples meet, something interesting happens. In some places the crests line up and the water rises higher than either wave alone. In other places a crest meets a trough and the water barely moves at all. The resulting pattern of bright and dark bands is not in either wave. It emerges from their overlap.

This is interference. It follows from one rule: when two waves occupy the same point, their amplitudes add. That is the principle of superposition. It is simple arithmetic. But the patterns it produces are anything but simple.

1. Two Sources

Below, two point sources emit circular waves into a 2D medium. Red means the amplitude is positive (crest). Blue means negative (trough). White means zero. Where two crests arrive at the same time, you see intense red. Where a crest and a trough collide, they cancel to white. The stable bright and dark bands are the interference pattern.

Drag either source to reposition it. Change the frequency or move the sources closer together and watch the pattern reorganize.

Notice the geometry. Between the sources, the bright bands fan out in smooth hyperbolas. These are the loci of constant path difference. Along a bright band, the waves from each source arrive in phase. Along a dark band, they arrive half a wavelength apart and cancel. More sources, higher frequency, or wider separation all produce finer, more complex patterns.

2. Standing Waves

Something special happens when two waves of the same frequency travel in opposite directions. They do not just interfere in passing. They lock into place. Certain points, called nodes, never move at all. Between the nodes, the medium oscillates with maximum amplitude. This frozen pattern is a standing wave.

The three panels below show this. The top two are the individual traveling waves (one moving right, one moving left). The bottom panel is their sum. Watch how the sum oscillates up and down but certain points stay fixed at zero. Those are the nodes.

Standing waves are everywhere. A guitar string vibrates this way. So does the air inside a flute. The fixed endpoints force nodes at both ends, which constrains which frequencies are possible. Only those wavelengths that fit an integer number of half-waves between the endpoints survive. These are the natural frequencies, or harmonics, of the system.

3. Chladni Patterns

In two dimensions, standing waves form on vibrating plates. Sprinkle sand on a metal plate and bow its edge. The sand bounces off the vibrating regions and collects at the nodes, the lines where the plate does not move. These lines trace out geometric figures first studied by Ernst Chladni in the 18th century.

The simulation below models a vibrating square membrane. Select a mode to see its nodal pattern. Higher modes have more complex structure, with the nodal lines dividing the plate into smaller regions. The sand (bright pixels) accumulates where the amplitude is near zero.

Figure 3. Chladni patterns on a vibrating square plate. Each mode (m,n) corresponds to a distinct frequency. Sand (bright lines) collects at the nodal lines where the plate stays still. Higher modes produce finer, more intricate geometry.

Chladni patterns were first demonstrated to Napoleon in 1809. Today, the same physics governs the modes of vibrating guitar strings, the resonances of concert halls, and the structure of electron orbitals in atoms.
