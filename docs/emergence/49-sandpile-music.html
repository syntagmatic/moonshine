Sandpile Music

Listen to self-organized criticality. Each grain triggers a sound, and avalanches become cascading rhythms.

The sandpile model from explainer 08 produces avalanches with a power-law size distribution. Most drops trigger nothing or a small toppling. Occasionally one grain sets off a massive cascade. The interesting question: what does this sound like?

When you map avalanche sizes to pitch and duration, the sandpile's statistics become audible. Small avalanches are quick, high-pitched clicks. Large avalanches are deep rumbles that linger. The overall rhythm has no repeating pattern, but it has a recognizable texture. The power spectrum follows 1/f, which sits exactly between the randomness of white noise and the predictability of a periodic beat.

Notice the rhythm. In the early phase, when the grid is filling and most drops do nothing, the sound is a steady stream of high plinks at roughly equal intervals. Once the pile reaches criticality and avalanches begin, the texture shifts. Silences between the deep tones are punctuated by clusters of rapid clicks. The pattern never repeats, but it never sounds fully random either. That is the audible signature of 1/f noise.

The Rhythm of Criticality

Power spectrum. Log-log plot. A slope of −1 indicates 1/f noise.

The white noise comparison has a flat spectrum: all frequencies carry equal power. The periodic signal has a single dominant peak. The sandpile's spectrum slopes downward at roughly −1, meaning low frequencies carry more power than high frequencies, but not overwhelmingly so. This 1/f slope is the spectral fingerprint of self-organized criticality.

Try setting the timbre to sawtooth and widening the pitch range. The result can sound like an alien percussion ensemble. With sine waves and a narrow range, it resembles raindrops on a roof. The deep rumbles of large avalanches give the sandpile a natural sense of dynamics that synthetic music usually has to work hard to achieve.

1/f noise, also called pink noise, is ubiquitous: in music (the tempo fluctuations of human performers), in the flow of the Nile river, in the flicker of a candle flame, and in the electrical activity of the brain. The sandpile model produces it naturally. Some researchers believe 1/f noise is a signature of self-organized criticality in the underlying system.
