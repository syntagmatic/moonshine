Morphogenesis: How Embryos Build Themselves

Chemical gradients tell cells where they are, and simple rules tell them what to become

A developing embryo does not follow a blueprint in the way a builder follows architectural plans. There is no foreman pointing at cells and telling them what to do. Instead, cells read local chemical concentrations, interpret them using threshold rules, and differentiate accordingly. Global body plans emerge from local chemical conversations between cells.

The chemicals doing this signaling are called morphogens. They are produced at specific locations, diffuse outward through tissue, and get degraded along the way. The result is a concentration gradient: high near the source, tapering off with distance. Each cell reads the local concentration and responds. That is enough to build a body.

1. The French Flag Model

Lewis Wolpert proposed the French Flag model in 1969 to explain how cells could use a single morphogen gradient to adopt three different fates. Imagine a row of cells exposed to a morphogen source on the left. The concentration is high on the left and drops off toward the right. Set two thresholds. Cells above the upper threshold become blue. Cells between the two thresholds become white. Cells below the lower threshold become red. Three regions, one gradient, two thresholds. A French flag.

The simplicity is the point. You do not need a cell-by-cell address system. You need one signal and two decision boundaries. Cells figure out where they are by measuring what they are bathing in.

2. Diffusion Creates Gradients

Where does the gradient come from? A morphogen source pumps molecules into the tissue from one side. Those molecules diffuse outward, random-walking through the extracellular space. At the same time, they are being degraded: enzymes chew them up, cells absorb them, they decay. The balance between diffusion (spreading out) and degradation (disappearing) sets the shape of the steady-state gradient.

The characteristic length scale, how far the morphogen reaches, is proportional to the square root of the diffusion rate divided by the degradation rate. Fast diffusion and slow degradation produce a long, gentle gradient. Slow diffusion and fast degradation produce a short, steep one. 3. Two Morphogens, Complex Patterns

A single gradient can establish an axis, but real embryos need to specify positions in two dimensions. The solution is straightforward: add a second morphogen with a different source location. Now every cell reads two concentrations simultaneously. Think of it as coordinates. Morphogen A tells the cell its position along one axis. Morphogen B tells it the position along the other. The combination of the two concentrations places each cell in a two-dimensional fate space.

Divide that fate space into four quadrants. High A and high B gives one cell type. High A and low B gives another. The intersections of the two gradients create boundaries, and those boundaries can form stripes, patches, or nested regions depending on where the sources are placed and how far each morphogen reaches. The Bicoid protein in fruit fly embryos forms exactly this kind of gradient. Bicoid mRNA is deposited at the anterior (front) end of the egg by the mother. After fertilization, the protein diffuses toward the posterior, forming an exponential concentration gradient. Cells with high Bicoid become head structures. Cells with low Bicoid become abdominal segments. Mutations that disrupt Bicoid produce flies with two tails and no head. The position information encoded in the gradient is what tells cells at the front end to become head cells.
