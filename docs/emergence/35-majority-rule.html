Majority Rule

When each cell adopts the opinion of its majority, domains form, boundaries straighten, and one opinion wins.

Imagine a town where every person holds one of two opinions. Each day, everyone looks at their immediate neighbors and switches to whichever opinion the majority holds. That is the entire rule. No persuasion, no media, no leaders. Just local copying.

What happens is striking. Within a few steps, random noise crystallizes into large domains of agreement. The boundaries between those domains are sharp and curved. Over time the boundaries straighten, smaller domains shrink, and eventually one opinion takes over the entire grid. The process looks exactly like soap bubbles merging.

1. The Rule

A grid of cells, each black or white. Every step, each cell counts the colors in its Moore neighborhood: the 8 surrounding cells plus itself, making 9 votes total. The cell adopts whichever color has the majority. With 9 voters, ties are impossible, so every cell gets a clear answer.

Start with a random 50/50 assignment and press Play. Within the first two or three steps the grid transforms from static noise into something that looks like a map: large contiguous regions of black and white separated by sharp, curved borders. That initial snap is fast. What follows is slower and more interesting. The borders drift, always moving to reduce their curvature. Small pockets get eaten by their surroundings. Watch long enough and you will see it settle on a single color.

Why does the grid not stay random? Think about what the rule does to a cell sitting inside a large patch of black: all 9 votes agree, so nothing changes. Now think about a cell right on the border between a black region and a white region. Its vote depends on the exact shape of the boundary near it. If the boundary curves toward the cell, the cell is more likely to be on the concave side, where its own color is outnumbered. It flips. The net effect is that curved boundaries retreat, and small regions (which have high curvature everywhere) collapse quickly.

Straight boundaries, on the other hand, are stable. A perfectly straight vertical line between black and white will not move at all, because every border cell sees roughly equal votes. This is why the late-stage dynamics are so slow: the surviving boundaries are nearly straight, and only their slight remaining curvature drives further change.

2. Domain Coarsening

Once the initial domains form, we can ask a quantitative question: how does the typical domain size grow over time? Physicists measure this using the density of boundary cells. If boundaries are dense, domains are small. If boundaries are sparse, domains are large. The characteristic domain size L is roughly proportional to the inverse square root of the boundary density.

The theory predicts that L grows as the square root of time: L ~ sqrt(t). This is the Allen-Cahn scaling law, well known from the physics of phase ordering. The boundaries between domains are not just visual artifacts. They are dynamical objects with their own physics. Each boundary segment moves with a velocity proportional to its local curvature. A convex bump sticking into enemy territory retreats; a concave dent gets filled in. The result is that boundaries always get straighter over time, which is another way of saying domains get larger. Physicists call this curvature-driven motion, and it appears in contexts far from opinion dynamics: the growth of metallic grains in a cooling alloy, the ordering of magnetic domains in a demagnetized material, the slow evolution of foam.

3. Bias and Nucleation

With exactly 50% black and 50% white, the winner is random. The initial assignment has no preferred color, but the outcome is always decisive. Whichever color happens to form the larger early domains gets a structural advantage that compounds over time. This is a true symmetry-breaking event: nothing in the rule favors either color, yet one always wins.

Now add a slight bias. Start with 52% black instead of 50%. Black will always win, but it still takes many steps for the majority to fully take over. The bias means that at every boundary, the black side has a slightly higher density of reinforcement. Black regions expand on average while white regions contract.

Domain coarsening under majority rule follows the same mathematics as phase separation in alloys, the evolution of magnetic domains, and the formation of grain boundaries in metals. The Allen-Cahn equation describes all of them. The characteristic length scale grows as sqrt(t), boundaries move at a speed proportional to their curvature, and the late-stage morphology is statistically self-similar: if you rescale the pattern by sqrt(t), snapshots taken at different times look the same.

The majority rule is one of the simplest models of opinion formation. It contains no psychology, no strategy, no social network structure. Just a grid and a local vote. And yet it reproduces the core phenomenon: patchy agreement crystallizes from noise, boundaries between factions smooth themselves out through a mechanical process, and one side eventually wins. The winner is not determined by the strength of the argument, but by the geometry of the initial fluctuations.

This is what makes the model useful as a thinking tool. It strips opinion dynamics down to pure geometry. The physics of the boundaries tells you everything. Curved boundaries retreat. Small domains die young. Straight boundaries persist. And a tiny initial advantage, invisible in the noise of a random start, snowballs into total dominance through the relentless mathematics of curvature.
