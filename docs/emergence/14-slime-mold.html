Slime Mold

A single-celled organism that solves mazes, designs efficient networks, and approximates the Tokyo rail system

Physarum polycephalum is not smart. It has no brain. It has no neurons. It is a single-celled organism, a plasmodial slime mold that oozes across the forest floor looking for food. Yet it solves optimization problems that would impress an engineer.

The trick is simple. The organism extends tubes in all directions, exploring its environment. When a tube connects two food sources, flow through that tube increases. Increased flow makes the tube thicker. Tubes that carry no useful flow shrink and disappear. After a few hours, what remains is a near-optimal transport network. No planning. No memory. Just local feedback between flow and structure.

1. Growth and Exploration

The simulation below models Physarum using thousands of tiny agents. Each agent deposits a chemical trail, senses the trail concentration ahead of it, and turns toward higher concentrations. This is the same logic as the real organism's cytoplasmic streaming. The agents do not communicate directly. They read and write a shared chemical field.

The result is branching, vein-like patterns that look strikingly biological. Adjust the parameters to see how they change the network's character. High sensor angle makes agents spread out. Low decay keeps trails visible longer, producing thicker veins. Play with the sliders and watch the morphology shift.

2. Solving a Maze

A famous experiment by Toshiyuki Nakagaki in 2000 placed Physarum in a small maze with food at the entrance and exit. The slime mold initially spread throughout the entire maze, filling every corridor. Then something remarkable happened. The organism withdrew from dead ends and redundant paths, leaving behind a single tube connecting the two food sources along the shortest route.

The simulation below lets you recreate this. Draw walls to build a maze, then click to place two food sources. The slime agents will initially fill all available space. Then watch the network simplify. Tubes connecting food along efficient routes get reinforced. Everything else fades. The shortest path emerges without any pathfinding algorithm.

The mechanism is flow-based optimization. Chemical concentration accumulates near food sources. Agents near food deposit more trail. This creates a gradient that pulls nearby agents toward the food. Agents that happen to travel between the two food sources reinforce the connecting path. Agents trapped in dead ends receive no gradient signal and their trails decay. The maze solves itself.

3. Network Design

Optimization gets more interesting with more than two targets. Place several food sources on the canvas below and the slime mold will grow to connect them all. The network it builds is not the minimum spanning tree. It is something more interesting: a compromise between total length and fault tolerance.

A minimum spanning tree connects all nodes with the least total wire, but cutting any single edge disconnects the network. The slime mold often adds a few extra connections, creating cycles that keep the network connected even if one tube fails. Toggle the MST overlay to compare.

The slime is not computing a spanning tree. It does not know graph theory. But the physics of its growth naturally produce a trade-off. Short paths carry more flow. More flow thickens tubes. But flow also distributes across parallel paths for redundancy. The result is a network that a transport engineer might design on purpose, arrived at through nothing but local tube dynamics.

In 2010, researchers placed oat flakes at locations matching Tokyo's major rail stations. The slime mold produced a network strikingly similar to the actual Tokyo rail system, achieving comparable efficiency and fault tolerance. A brainless organism, given the same constraints, converged on the same solution as decades of human engineering.
