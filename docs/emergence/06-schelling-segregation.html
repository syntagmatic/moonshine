Schelling's Segregation

Even slight preferences for similar neighbors can produce starkly segregated neighborhoods

In 1971, economist Thomas Schelling proposed a thought experiment. Imagine a checkerboard city where two groups live side by side. Each person has a mild preference: they want at least some fraction of their neighbors to be like them. Not a majority. Not all. Just some.

If that preference is not met, the person moves to an empty spot. That is the entire model. No hostility. No coordination. No zoning board. Just individuals making small, reasonable choices about where to live.

The result is striking. Complete segregation emerges even when no one wants it.

Try a tolerance of 50%. That means "I just want half my neighbors to be like me." Sounds reasonable. But watch how quickly the grid separates into solid blocks.

Watch Segregation Emerge

Words are not enough for this one. You have to watch it happen. The simulation below auto-runs and plots the segregation index in real time. The segregation index is the average fraction of same-type neighbors across all agents. In a random grid it starts around 0.50. In a fully segregated grid it approaches 1.0.

Set tolerance to 33%. That is just wanting one in three neighbors to match. Press Play and watch the line climb.

The Tolerance Spectrum

The relationship between individual tolerance and collective segregation is nonlinear. At 0% tolerance everyone is happy anywhere, so nothing moves. At 20% you get mild clustering. At 33% the grid is visibly segregated. At 50% or above, the separation is nearly total.

Each thumbnail below shows the final state after 80 steps at a given tolerance level. The number underneath is the final segregation index.

Figure 3. Final states at six tolerance thresholds. The jump from 20% to 33% is where mild preference becomes visible segregation.

The critical insight is the gap between intention and outcome. At 33%, every agent would be perfectly happy in an integrated neighborhood. They do not prefer segregation. They just want one-third of their neighbors to look like them. But when every agent applies this preference simultaneously, the cascade of moves produces a pattern no one chose.

Three Groups

Schelling's original model used two types. What happens with three? The same dynamic holds, but the geometry changes. With two groups, you get large solid blocks. With three, you get a more fragmented patchwork. Each group clusters with itself but must share more boundary with the other two.

The same mild tolerance that barely segregates two groups creates noticeable fragmentation with three. More types means more potential for someone to be the odd one out.

Schelling's model is not really about race, though that was the original framing. It applies wherever agents sort on any visible trait: language, income, political leaning, even seating at a cafeteria. The math does not care what the trait is.

The lesson is the same one that runs through every emergence story. Local rules create global structure. A preference as mild as "one in three" becomes a segregated city. No one planned it. No one wanted it. It just fell out of the interactions.
