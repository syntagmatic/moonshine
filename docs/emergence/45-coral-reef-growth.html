Coral Reef Growth

Corals compete by growing upward toward light and outward for space, producing the complex 3D architecture of a reef

A reef is not designed. Each coral polyp follows the same local rules: grow toward light, extend into open water, avoid bumping into neighbors. Different species encode these rules differently. Branching corals push upward fast and grab the best light, but their thin skeletons snap in storms. Massive corals grow slowly into dense mounds that endure heavy surf for centuries. Encrusting species hug the substrate and spread sideways, colonizing whatever crevice they can find.

The reef's architecture is the accumulated result of this competition: who grew where, who shaded whom, who survived the last storm. No architect, just polyps doing polyp things.

Branching corals are the weeds of the reef. They grow fast and steal the light, but their success creates vulnerability. A single storm can wipe out decades of branching growth in minutes, reopening space for slower species that had been shaded out.

2. Storm Disturbance

Reefs are not static. Cyclones, waves, and bleaching events periodically reset the competition. Hit the storm button and watch what happens. Damage is proportional to height and fragility: tall branching corals suffer most. Massive corals mostly survive. Gaps open on the substrate, and new colonization begins. This cycle of destruction and regrowth is what maintains diversity. Without storms, branching corals would dominate everything.

This is the intermediate disturbance hypothesis in action. Too few storms and one species dominates. Too many and nothing has time to grow. Maximum diversity occurs at moderate disturbance frequency, keeping the reef in a perpetual state of succession.

3. The Light Field

Light is the currency of the reef. Toggle the light field to see how the canopy of corals intercepts photons from above. Tall branching colonies cast deep shadows. Below them, only shade-tolerant species survive. This vertical gradient of light availability drives the zonation you see on real reefs: fast-growing branching corals dominate shallow, well-lit zones, while massive and encrusting forms persist in deeper or shaded areas.

Figure 3. The light field reveals shadow zones cast by the canopy. Yellow is full light, dark blue is deep shade. Reef zonation follows from this light gradient and wave exposure.

On a real reef, wave exposure adds another axis. Exposed reef crests favor massive, wave-resistant corals. Sheltered back-reef areas let fragile branching species thrive. The combination of light gradients (vertical) and wave exposure (horizontal) produces the mosaic of zones that divers see: spur-and-groove, reef flat, reef crest, fore-reef slope, each with its own community. All from two environmental gradients and three growth strategies.

Coral reefs are sometimes called the rainforests of the sea, and the analogy runs deep. In both systems, competition for light drives vertical structure. Canopy species shade understory species. Disturbance opens gaps. Succession fills them. The resulting architecture looks designed but is simply the accumulated record of organisms following local rules in a shared environment.
