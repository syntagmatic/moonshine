Flocking: How Birds Fly Together

Three simple rules produce the mesmerizing coordination of a murmuration

Watch a flock of starlings at dusk. Thousands of birds twist and pulse through the sky as a single shape. It looks choreographed, but there is no choreographer. No bird leads. No bird knows what the flock is doing. Each bird reacts only to its nearby neighbors, and the global pattern falls out for free.

In 1986, Craig Reynolds built a computer simulation showing that three simple steering rules are enough to produce this behavior. He called the simulated birds "boids." The rules are separation, alignment, and cohesion. That is all you need.

The Three Rules

Each boid looks at its neighbors and computes three steering forces:

Separation. Steer away from neighbors that are too close. This prevents collisions and keeps the group from collapsing into a single point.

Alignment. Steer toward the average heading of nearby neighbors. This makes the group move in roughly the same direction.

Cohesion. Steer toward the average position of nearby neighbors. This keeps the group together instead of drifting apart.

The simulation below runs all three at once. Try dragging each slider to zero and watch what happens. With no separation, birds pile on top of each other. With no alignment, they swirl without direction. With no cohesion, they scatter.

Vision and Neighborhoods

Real birds do not sense every other bird in the sky. They respond to a small number of nearby neighbors, typically within a limited field of view. A bird behind you does not influence your steering.

In the simulation, each boid has a vision radius and a roughly 270-degree field of view. Only neighbors inside this cone count. Click on any boid below to highlight it and see its vision cone. Neighbors it can see are drawn in blue. Those outside its perception are gray.

With a very small radius, each boid sees almost no one. The group fragments into tiny clusters that drift independently. Increase the radius and the flock tightens, because each bird can coordinate with more of the group. But even a modest radius is enough. You do not need global awareness to produce global order.

From Local to Global

The three rules produce large-scale flow structures: rivers of birds curving through space, splitting at obstacles, merging at the edges. These patterns are hard to see when you only watch the birds. Trails make them visible.

Breaking the Flock

What happens when you introduce danger? Click anywhere in the simulation below to place a predator. Birds within range flee from it, adding a fourth force that overrides the usual flocking behavior near the threat.

Watch the flock split around the predator and reform on the other side. No bird decides to split the flock. No bird signals "regroup." The splitting and reforming are entirely emergent.

No bird decides to split the flock. Each bird simply steers away from danger, and the split emerges from hundreds of individual escape responses.

This is the central lesson of emergence. You do not need a leader or a plan. You need a population of agents following local rules. The large-scale order is a consequence, not a cause. Flocking, traffic patterns, market dynamics, neural synchrony. The recipe is the same: simple rules, local interactions, global structure.
