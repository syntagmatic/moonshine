Spatial Predator-Prey

When predators and prey occupy space, their oscillations become traveling waves

The classic Lotka-Volterra model treats predators and prey as two numbers: total predator count and total prey count. The populations oscillate forever. But real ecosystems are not well-mixed beakers. Rabbits graze in one meadow, foxes hunt in another, and both move around. When you give predators and prey a landscape to live on, the uniform oscillations shatter into something far more interesting: traveling waves of pursuit and escape.

Prey bloom in one region. Predators converge on the bounty. They eat through the local prey population and start to starve. Meanwhile, prey in distant patches are multiplying undisturbed. The predators move on, and the cycle sweeps across the landscape as a wave. What the non-spatial model describes as a single global oscillation becomes a spatial pattern: waves, spirals, and turbulence, depending on the parameters.

1. Adding Space

The model below places predators and prey on a 2D grid. Each cell holds a prey density and a predator density. Three things happen at each cell on every time step:

Prey grow logistically: prey increase at rate a, capped by carrying capacity
Predators eat: predation follows a saturating response (type II) at attack rate b
Predators die: predators starve at rate m in the absence of food
Both diffuse: prey and predators spread to neighboring cells (predators faster)

Green intensity encodes prey density. Red intensity encodes predator density. Where both are present, you see yellow. Watch the simulation run from a random initial state. After a brief transient, spatial waves emerge on their own. Nobody programs the wave pattern. It falls out of local growth, local predation, and local diffusion.

2. Spiral Waves

With the right balance of growth, predation, death, and diffusion, the pursuit waves curl into rotating spirals. The mechanism is similar to spiral waves in the Belousov-Zhabotinsky reaction: a wavefront that gets broken (by a boundary, or by random fluctuation) has a free end that pivots and curls back on itself. The result is a self-sustaining spiral that emits waves from its tip, spinning indefinitely.

The spiral regime is not the only possibility. Increase the prey growth rate and the system can tip into chaotic turbulence, where spirals form and break apart continuously. Decrease diffusion and the oscillations become more local, approaching the well-mixed limit where everything oscillates in unison. Use the sliders and presets below to explore the different regimes.

The spiral state is the most visually striking, but it also has deep ecological significance. In a spiral regime, predator and prey populations never go extinct globally, because the wave pattern ensures that at any given moment, some region of the landscape is in the "prey boom" phase while another region is in the "predator boom" phase. The spatial desynchronization stabilizes coexistence.

3. Refugia and Barriers

Real landscapes have obstacles: rivers, ridgelines, roads, fences. These barriers block or slow the movement of both predators and prey. What happens to spatial wave patterns when the landscape is not a featureless plane?

Draw walls on the canvas below by clicking and dragging. Walls are impassable: neither prey nor predators can cross them, and nothing diffuses through. Watch how barriers change the wave dynamics. A wall can shelter prey on one side while predators on the other side starve and go locally extinct. The sheltered pocket becomes a refugium: a safe zone where prey persist at high density. From the refugium, prey spill out and recolonize depleted areas, providing a steady source of food that can sustain the broader ecosystem.

The maze preset is especially revealing. In a fragmented landscape, the large-scale traveling waves break down entirely. Each compartment oscillates at its own frequency, determined by its size and the width of the corridors connecting it to its neighbors. Small compartments with narrow corridors tend to lock into stable coexistence, while large open areas can still support spiral waves internally.

Spatial predator-prey models help explain the persistence of prey species that the non-spatial Lotka-Volterra equations predict should go extinct. Space creates refugia, asynchronous oscillations, and recolonization dynamics that stabilize coexistence. The same principle operates in conservation biology: habitat corridors, nature reserves, and landscape connectivity all matter because they shape the spatial waves that keep predator-prey systems from collapsing.
