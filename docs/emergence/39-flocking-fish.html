Schooling Fish: Underwater Flocking

Fish schools move as one liquid body, splitting around predators and reforming seamlessly

A school of herring can number in the hundreds of millions, yet the group moves as if it were a single organism. No fish is in charge. Each individual follows three local rules: stay close to neighbors, match their heading, and avoid collisions. These are the same separation/alignment/cohesion rules that Craig Reynolds described for bird flocks in 1986. But fish live in water, and that changes everything.

Water is roughly 800 times denser than air. A fish pushing forward creates a pressure bow wave. A fish turning sends vortices spinning outward. Every movement broadcasts a signal that nearby fish can detect without looking. This is the key difference between fish schools and bird flocks: fish have a sense organ that birds lack, and it rewires the entire collective behavior.

Notice how the balanced state (all sliders near 1.0) produces the most lifelike schooling. The fish maintain a roughly constant spacing, flow together, and occasionally wheel around in collective turns. Real fish schools show exactly this kind of motion, and biologists measure it with the same statistics you see in the readout: polarity (how aligned the headings are, from 0 = random to 1 = perfect) and NND (nearest-neighbor distance, how tightly packed the school is).

2. Predator Attack: Flash Expansion

Click anywhere on the simulation below to spawn a predator (the red shape). Watch the school split apart in a "flash expansion," then reform once the threat passes. Real schools perform this maneuver in under 100 milliseconds per fish, fast enough that predators often come away empty-jawed. The splitting creates a "vacuole," a hole in the school that the predator sails through without catching anyone.

The flash expansion is not simple fleeing. Each fish does not just swim away from the predator. Instead it turns away from the approaching threat at a sharp angle, and the geometry of many fish doing this simultaneously produces the splitting pattern. The two halves of the school stream past the predator on either side, then rejoin behind it. You can spawn multiple predators to see how the school handles compound threats.

The speed of the flash expansion is remarkable. Each fish reacts to the neighbor that reacted to the neighbor that saw the predator. The panic propagates outward faster than any single fish swims. This is not just a metaphor. Measurements of real herring schools show the "startle wave" traveling at 5 to 15 body lengths per second, while an individual fish's top speed is about 10 body lengths per second. The collective response outruns any individual.

3. Information Wave

Click near the school below. The fish closest to your click will turn sharply, and that turn propagates through the school as a visible wave. The wave moves faster than any individual fish because each fish begins its turn before the neighbor that triggered it has finished turning. It is predictive, not reactive: each fish anticipates the next fish's movement using the pressure signals it detects, and begins turning early.

The lateral line organ. Fish do not need to see their neighbors to school. The lateral line, a row of pressure-sensitive cells running along each side of the body, detects tiny pressure changes in the surrounding water. This gives fish a 360-degree sense of their neighbors' positions and velocities, working even in murky water and total darkness. It is why blind cave fish can still school, and why fish schools are packed more tightly than bird flocks: the lateral line provides faster, denser neighbor information than vision alone.

Water's incompressibility means pressure signals travel nearly instantaneously compared to a fish's reaction time. When one fish accelerates, the pressure wave reaches its neighbors in microseconds. The bottleneck is neural processing, not signal travel. This is fundamentally different from bird flocks, where each bird must visually track its neighbors and information propagates at the speed of a wingbeat reaction.

The result: fish schools are some of the fastest collective decision-makers in biology. A school of anchovies can reverse direction in about 15 milliseconds per fish in the chain, far quicker than any individual fish's full sensory-to-motor reaction time. The school, in a real sense, thinks faster than any of its members. The collective processes information at a rate that no individual nervous system can match on its own.
