Self-Avoiding Walks

A random walk that cannot cross its own path behaves fundamentally differently from one that can

Take a random walk on a grid. At every step, pick a direction uniformly at random and move one cell. Now add a single constraint: you may never revisit a cell you have already occupied. That is it. One rule. But it changes everything about how the walk behaves.

A self-avoiding walk (SAW) models a polymer chain in solution. Each step is a monomer, and the walk is the backbone of the molecule. Physical polymers cannot overlap themselves, so a SAW captures the key geometric constraint. The result is that SAWs expand faster than ordinary random walks. How much faster? That question leads to one of the deepest unsolved problems in mathematical physics.

2. Scaling Laws

For an ordinary random walk of N steps, the expected end-to-end distance grows as N0.5. That is the square root law. For a self-avoiding walk in two dimensions, the exponent jumps to exactly 3/4 = 0.75. This is one of the few exact results in the theory, coming from conformal field theory and SLE. The SAW exponent is usually written as the Greek letter nu. In 3D the exponent drops to roughly 0.588, the Flory value named after the polymer chemist who first estimated it in 1949.

The chart below generates many SAWs at each of several chain lengths, measures the mean end-to-end distance, and plots the results on a log-log scale. On a log-log plot, a power law appears as a straight line, and the slope is the exponent. The simulation uses a kinetic growth method (at each step, choose uniformly from unvisited neighbors), which is biased and gives an effective exponent around 0.59 to 0.65 rather than the true 0.75. Unbiased sampling requires fancier algorithms like the pivot method. Still, the exponent is clearly above the random walk value of 0.5.

3. Getting Trapped

A self-avoiding walk can paint itself into a corner. If all four neighbors of the current position have already been visited, the walk is trapped and cannot continue. On a 2D square lattice, most SAWs do eventually trap, but they tend to survive surprisingly long before it happens. Short walks almost never trap. As the walk grows, the probability of trapping rises, but slowly.

Self-avoiding walks model real polymers in solution. In two dimensions the exact exponent is nu = 3/4, proven via conformal field theory. In three dimensions the exponent is roughly 0.588, which predicts how polymer radius scales with chain length, confirmed by neutron scattering experiments. Despite 60+ years of work, no exact formula for the number of SAWs of length N on a square lattice is known. The connective constant (roughly 2.638 for the square lattice) was only proven rigorously for the hexagonal lattice in 2012.
