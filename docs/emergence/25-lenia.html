Lenia: Continuous Game of Life

Smooth the grid, smooth the rules, and Life's blocky gliders become organic creatures

Conway's Game of Life operates on a binary grid. Cells are alive or dead. Time advances in discrete ticks. The neighborhood is a fixed 3x3 square. These constraints produce remarkable complexity, but the creatures that emerge always look pixelated, locked to the lattice.

What happens if you relax every one of those constraints? Replace binary states with real numbers between 0 and 1. Replace the square neighborhood with a smooth kernel. Replace the hard birth/death rule with a continuous growth function. Replace discrete ticks with a small time step. The result is Lenia, and it produces organisms that look startlingly alive.

1. From Discrete to Continuous

The side-by-side below shows the transformation. On the left, a classic Game of Life glider walks across a grid of square cells. On the right, the same idea taken continuous: an orbium, Lenia's simplest traveling creature, glides across a smooth field. Same underlying logic (local rules, spatial neighborhoods, iterated updates), but the visual difference is dramatic. The orbium looks less like a cellular automaton and more like an organism under a microscope.

The orbium's shape is not painted on. It emerges from the same kind of local update rule that drives Life, just generalized to continuous values. Each point in the field holds a real number between 0 and 1 representing how "alive" that location is. At every time step, the field updates according to two components: a kernel that defines the neighborhood, and a growth function that says how much each location should grow or shrink.

2. The Kernel and Growth Function

The Lenia update rule has two parts. First, for each point in the field, compute a weighted sum of the neighborhood. The weights come from the kernel, a ring-shaped function that peaks at a certain radius and falls off smoothly. Think of it as each cell looking outward through a donut-shaped lens.

Second, pass that neighborhood sum through a growth function: a bell curve centered at some value μ with width σ. When the neighborhood sum lands near the peak, the cell grows. When it lands far away, the cell shrinks. The interplay between kernel shape and growth peak determines whether a creature is stable, traveling, pulsating, or chaotic.

Try pushing μ higher. The creature needs a denser neighborhood to sustain itself, so it may contract or collapse. Lower σ makes the growth function sharper, demanding a more precise neighborhood sum. The creature becomes fragile. Widen σ and the creature tolerates more variation, but may lose its coherent shape and dissolve. Each creature in Lenia's world occupies a narrow parameter island where all the numbers conspire to produce self-sustaining motion.

Lenia was discovered by Bert Chan in 2018. Some Lenia creatures exhibit behaviors remarkably similar to biological organisms: chemotaxis (moving toward food), mitosis (self-replication), and even predation. All from one equation. The system has since been extended to multiple channels and higher dimensions, producing ever more lifelike artificial organisms. The original paper title says it all: "Lenia: Biology of Artificial Life."
