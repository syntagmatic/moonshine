Random Walks

A particle buffeted by random forces traces a path with deep mathematical structure.

Imagine a particle that has no will. At each tick of the clock it steps in a random direction. Up, down, left, or right, each equally likely. The path looks aimless. It doubles back, loops, wanders off, then drifts back. There is no pattern in any single trajectory. But run the experiment many times and precise statistical laws emerge.

The distance from the origin grows as the square root of time. This single fact connects pollen grains jittering in water, molecules diffusing through a gas, stock prices fluctuating on an exchange, and animals foraging across a landscape. The random walk is one of the most universal models in science.

2. The Square Root Law

One walker is noisy. But launch hundreds from the same origin and measure how far they spread. The cloud of walkers expands over time, and the radius of that cloud grows as the square root of the number of steps. Not linearly. The square root. A walker that has taken 10,000 steps is, on average, only 100 step-lengths from where it started.

The circle below has radius proportional to sqrt(steps). It tracks the root-mean-square distance of the population. The chart on the right plots that RMS distance against time. The curve is a clean square root, no matter how many walkers you use.

3. Return to the Origin

Will a random walker ever come back to where it started? The answer depends on the number of dimensions. In one dimension (a number line) and two dimensions (a plane), the walker returns to the origin infinitely often. With probability 1, it always comes back. But in three dimensions, there is roughly a 34% chance the walker escapes forever, never returning. The mathematician Shizuo Kakutani put it memorably: "A drunk man will find his way home, but a drunk bird may get lost forever."

Toggle between dimensions below. The simulation launches many independent walks and tracks what fraction return to the origin within a given number of steps. In 1D and 2D, the return fraction climbs toward 100%. In 3D, it plateaus well below.

4. Biased Walks and Drift

What happens if the coin is not fair? Suppose the walker has a slightly higher probability of stepping right than left. Even a tiny bias completely changes the long-term picture. Instead of diffusing symmetrically, the walker drifts. Over time, the drift dominates the randomness and the walker moves steadily in one direction.

The slider below controls the probability of stepping right (vs. left). At 50% the walk is unbiased. Move it to 51% and the effect is invisible at first. But let it run and the drift becomes unmistakable. This is relevant to stock prices: the efficient market hypothesis says that in an ideal market, price changes are an unbiased random walk. Any detectable bias would be exploited by traders until it disappeared.

Einstein's 1905 paper on Brownian motion used random walks to prove the existence of atoms. By predicting the exact sqrt(t) spreading law, he gave experimentalists a quantity they could measure, settling a debate that had lasted decades.
