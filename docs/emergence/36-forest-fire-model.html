Forest Fire Model

Trees grow, lightning strikes, fires burn clusters. The resulting dynamics produce a power-law distribution of fire sizes.

Imagine a landscape where trees grow slowly and steadily on empty ground. Every so often, lightning strikes a random spot. If it hits a tree, fire spreads instantly to every connected tree, burning the entire cluster to ash. Then the cycle restarts: regrowth, another strike, another burn.

This is the Drossel-Schwaab forest fire model, introduced in 1992. It has two competing rates. Trees grow with probability p per empty cell per time step. Lightning strikes a random tree with probability f, where f is much smaller than p. The ratio between these two rates determines everything.

The remarkable finding: the system tunes itself to a state where fire sizes follow a power law. Many small fires, fewer medium fires, rare catastrophic ones. No one adjusts a dial to produce this distribution. It emerges from the competition between growth and destruction.

The Ratio Matters

The single number that controls the model's behavior is the ratio f/p, lightning frequency relative to growth rate. When f/p is high, lightning strikes before trees have time to form large clusters. Fires stay small. The landscape is a patchwork of tiny groves separated by gaps.

When f/p is very low, trees have plenty of time to grow and merge into continent-sized forests before a spark arrives. When lightning finally strikes, a single fire can burn thousands of trees. The resulting dynamics are bursty: long quiet periods of growth punctuated by catastrophic burns.

In between, at an intermediate ratio, you get the power-law regime. Clusters of all sizes coexist. Small fires are common, medium fires less so, and occasional large fires reshape the landscape. This is the self-organized critical state.

Fire Size Distribution

The real test of criticality is the fire size distribution. Count how many trees each fire burns and plot the frequency of each size on logarithmic axes. If the system is critical, the points fall on a straight line: a power law. The slope of that line is the critical exponent.

The forest fire model was proposed by Drossel and Schwaab in 1992 as a minimal model of self-organized criticality. Unlike the Bak-Tang-Wiesenfeld sandpile, which reaches criticality through a single slow drive, the forest fire model balances two explicit rates: growth and ignition. The resulting power-law distribution of fire sizes appears without any external tuning.

Similar dynamics may explain why both actual wildfire sizes and earthquake magnitudes follow power laws. The system tunes itself to the boundary between sparse and connected. When forests are sparse, fires are small and growth dominates. When forests are dense, huge fires thin them out. The feedback loop holds the system near the critical point where clusters of all sizes coexist.
