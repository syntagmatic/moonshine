Galaxy Formation

A cloud of particles collapses under gravity, and conservation of angular momentum flattens it into a rotating disk with spiral arms

Start with a diffuse cloud of matter, give it a bit of rotation, and wait. Gravity pulls everything inward. But angular momentum prevents total collapse along the plane of rotation. The cloud flattens into a disk. Instabilities within that disk create spiral density waves, and a galaxy takes shape. No blueprint, no plan. Just gravity and spin.

All of this follows from a single force law. Newton's gravitational attraction, inversely proportional to the square of distance, applied to millions of particles. The richness of galactic structure is not coded in; it falls out of the math. This is emergence at cosmological scale, and we can watch it happen with a few hundred particles in the browser.

1. Gravitational Collapse

The simulation below starts with ~500 particles arranged in a random cloud with slight net rotation. Each particle exerts a gravitational pull on every other (softened at close range to prevent numerical blowup). Watch the cloud collapse inward. Particles along the spin axis fall quickly toward the center, while those in the plane of rotation maintain their orbits. The cloud flattens into a rotating disk.

Try adjusting the initial spin. Low spin produces a near-spherical collapse (an elliptical galaxy). High spin keeps the disk puffed out and slowly rotating. The gravity slider controls how strong the attraction is; higher gravity makes collapse faster and more dramatic.

The key is angular momentum conservation. Gravity can pull particles inward along the spin axis (because there is no net angular momentum in that direction), but it cannot remove the sideways momentum in the rotation plane. Collapse happens freely in one direction but is resisted in the other. A flat disk is the inevitable compromise.

2. Density Waves and Spiral Arms

Once the disk forms, look closely. You will often see regions where particles bunch together, forming arc-shaped or spiral-shaped overdensities. These are density waves. A density wave is like a traffic jam: the jam is stationary (or slowly moving), but individual cars pass through it. Similarly, spiral arms are regions where stars temporarily slow down and pile up. Individual stars enter an arm, linger, then exit the other side.

Below, particles start in a pre-formed disk so you can see the density patterns sooner. Color encodes local density: dark blue is empty, bright yellow is crowded. Toggle the tracer to follow one specific particle. Watch it orbit through high-density arms and low-density gaps. The arms are patterns, not objects.

3. Galaxy Merger

When two rotating disks approach each other, their mutual gravity produces tidal forces that stretch stars into long tails and bridges. These features look dramatic, but they require no new physics. They emerge from the same inverse-square law that formed the disks in the first place.

Click "Collide" to send the two pre-formed disk galaxies toward each other. Watch for tidal tails (long streams of stars pulled outward), bridges (streams connecting the two galaxies), and eventual merger into a single, larger, more chaotic system. The resulting structure often looks nothing like either parent galaxy.

The Toomre brothers' 1972 paper "Galactic Bridges and Tails" was a turning point. Using only a few hundred test particles on early computers, they reproduced the tidal tails and bridges seen in real interacting galaxies like the Antennae (NGC 4038/4039) and the Mice (NGC 4676). Structures that had puzzled astronomers for decades turned out to be the straightforward consequence of Newtonian gravity.

Galaxy formation is emergence at cosmic scale. Gravity is the only rule. Disk formation, spiral arms, density waves, and merger debris all follow from that single interaction applied to many particles. The universe does not need a galaxy-shaped mold. It just needs mass, angular momentum, and time.

What makes this especially striking is the range of structures that emerge. Elliptical galaxies, spiral galaxies, barred spirals, ring galaxies, tidal tails, stellar streams: all of these are solutions to the same equation under different initial conditions. The diversity of galactic morphology is not evidence of diverse mechanisms. It is evidence of one mechanism applied to diverse starting points. Change the spin, the mass distribution, the encounter geometry, and gravity does the rest.

Our own Milky Way is a barred spiral, currently in the process of absorbing several smaller satellite galaxies (the Sagittarius Dwarf being the most prominent). In about 4.5 billion years it will collide with the Andromeda galaxy, and the two will merge into a single elliptical galaxy sometimes called "Milkomeda." The physics behind that future merger is exactly what you see in the simulation above, scaled up by a factor of a hundred billion.
