Diffusion-Limited Aggregation

Release a random walker and let it stick where it lands. The result is a fractal tree that resembles lightning, coral, and frost.

Start with a single particle. Release another particle far away and let it wander randomly. When the walker bumps into the first particle, it sticks. Release another. And another. The structure that grows is not round. It is not smooth. It is a fractal tree with branches that keep branching.

This is diffusion-limited aggregation, or DLA. The growth is "diffusion-limited" because the random walk (diffusion) of incoming particles is the bottleneck. Particles arrive slowly. They stick fast. That asymmetry is what makes fractals.

1. The Algorithm

The simulation below places a seed at the center of the grid. Walkers are released from a circle around the cluster. Each walker steps randomly on a lattice: up, down, left, or right. When a walker lands next to an occupied cell, it sticks and becomes part of the cluster. Then a new walker is released.

Particles are colored by arrival order. Early particles are dark blue. Late particles are pale yellow. Watch the branches form. Notice how the outer tips grow fastest.

The shape looks organic. It looks like something that grew. That is because it did grow, one particle at a time, governed by nothing but randomness and contact. No blueprint. No symmetry constraint. Just a rule: stick where you land.

2. The Screening Effect

Why branches? Why not a round blob? The answer is screening. A random walker approaching the cluster is far more likely to hit an outer tip than to penetrate deep into a crevice. Tips intercept walkers before they can reach the interior. This means tips grow faster. Small bumps become big branches. Big branches develop their own bumps. The process is self-reinforcing.

The heatmap below visualizes this. It shows the probability that a random walker reaches each empty cell adjacent to the cluster. Bright cells are easy to reach. Dark cells are screened. Notice that the tips glow hot while the gaps between branches are nearly invisible.

Figure 2. Click "Show Heatmap" to overlay arrival probability on the cluster boundary. Bright = high probability (exposed tips). Dark = low probability (screened crevices). This gradient is why DLA produces branches, not blobs.

Screening is a geometric fact. A random walker coming from far away sees the cluster as a silhouette. The tips poke out. The crevices hide behind them. The deeper a gap, the less likely a walker finds its way in. This is the same reason lightning follows branching paths: charge diffuses through air and preferentially strikes protruding conductors.

3. Stickiness and Density

So far every walker sticks on first contact. What happens if you reduce the sticking probability? A walker that touches the cluster but does not stick continues its random walk. It bounces around near the surface. Eventually it sticks somewhere, but that somewhere is more likely to be deep inside a crevice because the walker had time to explore.

Low stickiness defeats the screening effect. The cluster becomes denser, rounder, less branchy. High stickiness amplifies screening. The cluster becomes sparse and wildly branched. The sticking probability controls the fractal dimension: how much the structure fills space.

4. Multiple Seeds

Place two or more seeds on the canvas below by clicking. Each seed starts its own cluster. As they grow, they compete for incoming walkers. A branch from one cluster screens territory from the others. The boundaries between clusters trace out curves resembling Voronoi cells.

This is how competing DLA structures partition space. Each cluster captures the walkers nearest to its exposed surface. The pattern is reminiscent of mineral dendrites growing on a rock face or frost crystals competing for moisture on a windowpane.

DLA was discovered by Tom Witten and Leonard Sander in 1981. The same growth rule explains the branching of electrodeposition, mineral dendrites, bacterial colonies, and dielectric breakdown (lightning). Any system where particles diffuse and irreversibly attach produces these fractal trees.
