Excitable Media

The same wave dynamics that create BZ spirals can cause lethal cardiac arrhythmias

Heart muscle is an excitable medium. Each cell sits quietly at rest until a stimulus pushes it past a threshold, and then it fires: a rapid spike in voltage called the action potential. After firing, the cell enters a refractory period where it cannot fire again, no matter how hard you push. Only after recovering does it become excitable once more. Resting, excited, refractory, resting. Three states, cycling in strict order.

This simple loop has enormous consequences. Normally, a wave of excitation sweeps through the heart in a coordinated pulse, and the muscle contracts as a unit. But if a wave breaks, the broken end curls into a rotating spiral. That spiral fires the tissue faster than the natural pacemaker, overriding it. One spiral is ventricular tachycardia. Many spirals breaking apart is ventricular fibrillation, and without intervention, it kills in minutes.

2. Normal Conduction

Spread the excitable cells across a 2D sheet and you have a simple model of heart tissue. A small pacemaker region in the top-left fires at a steady rate, just like the sinoatrial node. Each firing launches a wave that sweeps across the tissue in a smooth, planar front. Every cell fires once per heartbeat, in order, producing a coordinated contraction.

The pacing rate slider controls how often the pacemaker fires. Slow it down, and the waves space out. Speed it up, and the waves crowd together, but only up to a point. If you pace faster than the refractory period allows, the tissue cannot keep up and some pulses fail to propagate. The maximum sustainable rate depends on how long cells take to recover.

3. Spiral Formation and Fibrillation

Now for the dangerous part. If a wavefront breaks, the broken end has nowhere to go but sideways. It curls around, re-entering tissue that has already recovered, and forms a self-sustaining rotating spiral. The spiral fires faster than the natural pacemaker, taking over the rhythm. This is re-entrant tachycardia: the heart beats too fast because the excitation wave keeps chasing its own tail.

Click "Create spiral" to break a wavefront and watch the spiral form. The firing rate jumps immediately. Then try reducing the refractory period with the slider. As cells recover faster, the spiral's wavefront becomes thinner and more fragile. Eventually it breaks into multiple fragments, each fragment spawning its own spiral. The tissue devolves into chaos, with dozens of small spirals firing asynchronously. No region contracts in unison. This is ventricular fibrillation.

A defibrillator does not restart the heart. It simultaneously depolarizes all cells, wiping out the chaotic spiral waves. With every cell in the same state at the same time, there are no gradients to sustain re-entry. The heart's natural pacemaker can then resume coordinated conduction. Understanding excitable media has directly improved defibrillator design: modern devices use biphasic shocks calibrated to the tissue's refractory dynamics, lowering the energy needed and reducing damage to the heart muscle.

The mathematics here is the same as in the Belousov-Zhabotinsky chemical reaction, in slime mold aggregation, and in forest fire spread. Excitable media are everywhere in nature. The heart just happens to be the case where the spirals can kill you.
