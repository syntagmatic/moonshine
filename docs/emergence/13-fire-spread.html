Fire Spread: Percolation and Critical Thresholds

Whether a fire burns through a forest depends on a single number — the density of trees

Plant a forest at random. Some cells get a tree, some stay empty. Now light one tree on fire. The fire spreads to neighboring trees each time step. Empty cells cannot burn. Burned trees cannot burn again.

If the forest is sparse, the fire dies quickly. A few trees burn and the rest survive. If the forest is dense, one spark can burn everything. The transition between these two regimes is not gradual. It is sharp. There is a critical density below which fires fizzle and above which they consume the grid. That critical point is the percolation threshold.

The Critical Threshold

To see the threshold precisely, we need to run many experiments. For each density, generate a random forest and ignite the entire left edge. Measure what fraction of trees burn. Do this many times, average the results, and plot fraction burned versus density.

The result is an S-curve. It stays near zero for low density, then shoots up around 59.27%. That is the site percolation threshold for a square lattice. It is a number from mathematics, not a parameter anyone chose. Drag the vertical line on the chart to set the density in the forest above.

Percolation as Connectivity

Fire is one way to think about it. Another is pure connectivity. Forget the fire. Just look at the trees. Two trees are connected if they are neighbors (up, down, left, right). A connected cluster is a group of trees where you can walk from any tree to any other through adjacent trees.

Below the threshold, all clusters are small and isolated. Above the threshold, one giant cluster appears that spans the entire grid. At exactly the critical point, clusters of all sizes exist and their size distribution follows a power law.

Cluster size distribution

Count how many clusters of each size exist. On a log-log plot, at the critical point, the distribution is a straight line. That is a power law. Away from the critical point, the distribution bends. This power-law behavior at criticality is a hallmark of phase transitions.

Fig. 3. Cluster size distribution on log-log axes. At the critical density pc ≈ 0.5927, the distribution follows a power law (a straight line). Below or above criticality, it curves away.

The percolation threshold is universal. It depends only on the lattice geometry, not on what is percolating. The same math describes forest fires, epidemics in sparse networks, the flow of coffee through grounds, and the conductivity of random resistor networks.

For a square lattice with site percolation, pc ≈ 0.5927. For a triangular lattice, it is exactly 1/2. For bond percolation on a square lattice, also exactly 1/2. The geometry sets the number. Everything else is detail.
