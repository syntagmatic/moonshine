Opinion Dynamics

Simple rules of social influence can produce either agreement or entrenched camps, depending on one parameter

You are at a dinner party. Twenty people, each with an opinion on some topic, scored on a scale from 0 to 1. Zero is one extreme, one is the other. People talk in pairs. When two people talk, they nudge each other toward a compromise. But there is a catch: people only listen to those they already mostly agree with. If the gap is too wide, they ignore each other entirely.

This is the Deffuant model of opinion dynamics. It has one free parameter: the confidence threshold. How close must two opinions be before their holders will engage? That single number determines whether the dinner party ends in harmony or deadlock.

1. The Deffuant Model

Below, each dot on the number line is an agent with an opinion between 0 and 1. Color maps directly to opinion: deep blue at 0, deep red at 1. At each step, two agents are chosen at random. If their opinions differ by less than the confidence threshold, they each move halfway toward the other. If the gap is too wide, nothing happens.

Set the threshold high (say 0.4 or above) and press Play. All opinions collapse toward the center. Set it low (say 0.15) and watch clusters form. The agents separate into camps that cannot hear each other.

Try threshold 0.25. That is the critical region. Some runs converge to a single cluster, others freeze into two or three. The outcome is sensitive to the random initial configuration. This is the phase transition at work.

2. The Phase Transition

Run the Deffuant model many times at different threshold values and count how many opinion clusters survive. A cluster is a group of agents whose opinions have converged to nearly the same value.

At high thresholds (above 0.3), there is always one surviving cluster: consensus. At low thresholds (below 0.15), many clusters survive. The transition between these regimes is sharp, centered around threshold 0.25. This is not a gradual fade. It is a phase transition, the same kind of sudden shift that turns water to ice.

3. Network Effects

The original Deffuant model assumes anyone can talk to anyone. Real societies have structure. You talk to your neighbors, your coworkers, your friends. To capture this, place agents on a network and restrict interactions to connected pairs.

Below, agents sit on a small-world network. Start with a ring lattice where each node connects to its nearest neighbors. The rewiring slider controls how many connections are randomly redirected to distant nodes. At zero rewiring, the network is a regular lattice. At full rewiring, it approaches a random graph.

Small-world networks (low rewiring) create echo chambers. Tightly connected clusters reinforce their own opinions and resist outside influence. Add more random long-range connections and those chambers break open, letting distant opinions mix. The same bounded-confidence rule, the same threshold, but the network topology changes the outcome.

Bounded confidence models help explain why societies can simultaneously become more connected (social media) and more polarized. More connections do not guarantee convergence if people filter who they listen to.

The lesson here is sharp. Consensus and polarization are not opposites that require different explanations. They are two outcomes of the same mechanism. The only difference is a threshold: how far are you willing to listen? Widen the window and opinions merge. Narrow it and they crystallize. The transition is not gradual. It snaps.
