Predator-Prey: The Lotka-Volterra Dance

Two species locked in an endless oscillation that neither controls

Imagine a meadow with rabbits and foxes. Rabbits eat grass and multiply. Foxes eat rabbits and multiply. When rabbits are plentiful, foxes thrive. But thriving foxes eat more rabbits. Fewer rabbits means starving foxes. Fewer foxes lets rabbits rebound. The cycle starts again.

Nobody orchestrates this. No fox decides to eat less so the rabbits can recover. No rabbit plans to breed faster when foxes are scarce. The oscillation is an emergent property of two populations following simple rules about growth and consumption. Each species responds only to its own birth rate, death rate, and how much food is available right now. The global pattern falls out for free.

Two Equations, Infinite Loops

In the 1920s, Alfred Lotka and Vito Volterra independently wrote down a pair of differential equations that capture this dynamic. The model is intentionally simple. Prey grow exponentially unless eaten. Predators starve unless they eat.

dx/dt = αx − βxy   prey growth minus predation
dy/dt = δxy − γy   converted food minus starvation

Here x is prey count and y is predator count. Prey grow at rate α and get eaten at rate β per encounter. Predators convert each meal into offspring at rate δ and die at rate γ. Four parameters. Two equations. That is the whole model.

What makes it interesting is what comes out. The populations oscillate forever. They never settle to equilibrium. They never blow up. The cycle is baked into the structure of the equations, not tuned by hand.

The orbits never cross. This is not a coincidence. The Lotka-Volterra system has a conserved quantity, an analog of energy. Along each orbit the value

V(x,y) = δx − γ ln(x) + βy − α ln(y)

stays constant. Different starting conditions give different values of V, and each value traces out a different ring. The rings nest concentrically around the equilibrium point, where V reaches its minimum. This is why the system never converges or diverges. It just orbits, forever, on whichever contour it started on.

What Happens When You Harvest

Now for the counterintuitive part. Suppose you start harvesting prey. You remove a constant fraction of the prey population each year. Common sense says: fewer prey, so predators should benefit from less competition for each remaining rabbit. The opposite happens.

Harvesting removes prey directly. Fewer prey means less food for predators. Predator populations drop. With fewer predators hunting, prey experience less predation pressure. The net effect: prey populations actually increase on average while predators decline. Removing prey helps prey. This is the Volterra principle.

The modified system adds a harvest term to the prey equation:

dx/dt = αx − βxy − hx   original dynamics minus harvest
dy/dt = δxy − γy   predator equation unchanged

This is equivalent to reducing the effective prey growth rate from α to (α − h). The equilibrium shifts. Predator equilibrium drops from α/β to (α − h)/β. Prey equilibrium stays at γ/δ, unchanged. But the time-averaged prey population, which equals the equilibrium value for these closed orbits, now orbits a point where predators are fewer.

Push the slider far enough and the effective growth rate drops to zero. At that point the prey equation has no net growth. Predators crash. The ecosystem collapses. There is a critical harvest threshold beyond which the system cannot sustain itself.

In the 1920s, Adriatic fisheries noticed that when fishing declined during World War I, predatory fish increased while their prey decreased. Volterra's equations explained why: removing prey (by fishing) had been inadvertently helping the prey population by suppressing predators even more.

This result feels backwards until you trace the logic step by step. Harvesting hits both species, but it hits predators harder because they depend on prey for food. The indirect benefit to prey (relief from predation) outweighs the direct cost (being harvested). It is a reminder that in coupled systems, the obvious intervention often produces the opposite of the intended effect.

The same pattern appears in ecology, economics, and public health. Suppress one part of an oscillating system and you may amplify the thing you were trying to suppress. The Lotka-Volterra model is the simplest version of this lesson. Two equations. Four parameters. And a result that still surprises people a century later.
