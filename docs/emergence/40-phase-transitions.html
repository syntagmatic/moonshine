Phase Transitions: The Ising Model

Magnets, boiling water, and opinion formation all share the same mathematical phase transition

Imagine a grid of tiny magnets, each pointing either up or down. Each magnet wants to align with its neighbors (that lowers energy), but thermal jiggling tries to randomize them. At low temperature, alignment wins and the whole grid magnetizes. At high temperature, randomness wins and the spins are scrambled. Somewhere in between is a critical temperature where something strange happens: the system cannot decide, and fluctuations appear at every scale simultaneously.

This is the 2D Ising model, proposed by Wilhelm Lenz in 1920 and solved exactly by Lars Onsager in 1944. It is perhaps the simplest model that exhibits a genuine phase transition, and its mathematics turns up in contexts far beyond magnetism: the liquid-gas transition in fluids, the mixing of binary alloys, the formation of consensus in social networks.

1. The Spin Grid

Below is a 200 by 200 grid of spins. Blue is spin-up (+1), orange is spin-down (-1). The simulation uses the Metropolis algorithm: each step, pick a random spin, compute how much the total energy would change if that spin flipped, and accept the flip with a probability that depends on the temperature. At low T, only energy-lowering flips are accepted, so the system orders. At high T, almost any flip is accepted, so the system scrambles. Drag the slider to watch the transition happen in real time.

The Metropolis algorithm works by sampling the Boltzmann distribution. At each attempted flip, if the energy change dE is negative (the flip lowers energy), we always accept. If dE is positive (the flip costs energy), we accept with probability exp(-dE/T). This simple recipe produces thermodynamically correct behavior: at equilibrium, configurations are sampled with probability proportional to exp(-E/T), exactly as statistical mechanics requires.

Try starting from "All up" (a perfectly ordered state) and raising the temperature. Below Tc, you will see small islands of orange appear, but the background stays overwhelmingly blue. The ordered state is robust. As you approach Tc, the islands grow and merge until you cannot tell which color dominates. Above Tc, the grid becomes a random salt-and-pepper mixture within seconds. This is the phase transition happening in front of you.

A useful experiment: set T well below Tc (say T = 1.5), click "Reset random," and watch. The initially random grid will spontaneously magnetize, choosing either mostly-blue or mostly-orange. Which one it picks is random, a broken symmetry. The energy function treats up and down identically, but the system must choose one. This spontaneous symmetry breaking is the defining feature of the ordered phase.

2. Magnetization Curve

How magnetized is the grid at a given temperature? Click "Sweep temperatures" to run the simulation at a range of temperatures and plot the absolute magnetization |M| versus T. The resulting curve shows the hallmark of a second-order phase transition: magnetization drops continuously to zero at Tc, but with infinite slope at the transition point. Below Tc, the magnetization rises as a power law: |M| ~ (Tc - T)1/8. Above Tc, it is essentially zero (just random noise around zero).

Each point is the average |M| after equilibrating at that temperature. The vertical dashed line marks Tc. You can also add individual points by adjusting T in the grid above, waiting for equilibrium, and clicking "Add current T".

The critical exponent 1/8 for the 2D Ising model was derived exactly by Onsager. It is universal: it does not depend on whether the lattice is square, triangular, or honeycomb. It depends only on two things: the dimensionality (2D) and the symmetry (spins can be +1 or -1, a Z2 symmetry). Any other system with the same dimension and symmetry will have the same exponent. This is the first hint of a deep principle.

The dashed gray curve on the chart shows Onsager's exact solution for comparison. Your simulation points should follow it closely below Tc and sit near zero above Tc. Deviations near the critical point are expected on a finite grid: the exact solution describes an infinite lattice, and finite-size effects round off the sharp transition somewhat. On our 200 by 200 grid, the transition is already quite sharp, but on a 10,000 by 10,000 grid it would be sharper still.

3. Critical Fluctuations

Set the temperature slider to exactly Tc (about 2.27) and look at the spin grid. You will see domains of all sizes, from tiny clusters of a few spins to system-spanning blobs. This is scale invariance: there is no characteristic size for the domains. At any other temperature there is a characteristic domain size, but at Tc the domain size distribution follows a power law, meaning all sizes are present.

Below, we visualize the domain boundaries. The white pixels are cells where at least one neighbor has the opposite spin. At low temperature, boundaries are rare and enclose large uniform regions. At high temperature, boundaries are everywhere but enclose only tiny random patches. At Tc, boundaries form a fractal pattern that looks the same at every scale.

Universality. The remarkable thing about the Ising transition is that its critical exponents do not depend on the microscopic details of the model. Square lattice or triangular, nearest-neighbor or next-nearest-neighbor coupling: the exponents are the same. This extends beyond magnetism. The liquid-gas critical point of water, the demixing transition of binary alloys, and the "flocking transition" in certain models of collective motion all belong to the same universality class. The microscopic details wash out at the critical point. Only symmetry and dimensionality determine the universal behavior.

Onsager showed in 1944 that Tc = 2J/ln(1+√2) for the square lattice with coupling constant J (we use J = 1 here). The exact solution was a landmark of mathematical physics, one of the first proofs that statistical mechanics could produce sharp phase transitions in the thermodynamic limit. It took another century to fully understand why: the renormalization group, developed by Kenneth Wilson in the 1970s, showed that at a critical point the system looks the same at every scale. Zooming in or out changes nothing. This is why the critical exponents are universal, they describe the geometry of a fixed point in an abstract space of all possible physical systems.

Today, the Ising model remains the starting point for understanding critical phenomena across physics, biology, and even social science. Its simplicity is its power: two states, four neighbors, one temperature, and an entire universe of collective behavior.
