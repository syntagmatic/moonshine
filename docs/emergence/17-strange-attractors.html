Strange Attractors

The Lorenz system is perfectly deterministic, yet two nearby starting points diverge exponentially. This is the butterfly effect.

Take three simple equations. Feed the output of each step back in as input. There is no randomness anywhere. Given the same starting values, the system produces the same trajectory every time. And yet the behavior looks random. Predictions become impossible. This is deterministic chaos.

The paradox is geometric. The trajectory never repeats, never settles down, never escapes to infinity. Instead it traces out a shape of infinite complexity confined to a finite region of space. That shape is the strange attractor. It is the signature of chaos: beautiful, bounded, and utterly unpredictable in detail.

1. The Lorenz Attractor

In 1963, Edward Lorenz was running a simplified weather model on a Royal McBee computer. The model had just three variables and three coupled differential equations. The result was this butterfly-shaped object, now the most famous attractor in mathematics. The trajectory orbits one lobe, crosses to the other, loops back. The number of orbits on each side before switching is never the same twice.

The three parameters control the shape. Sigma governs the rate of rotation. Rho controls the height of the attractor and whether chaos occurs at all (below about 24.7, the system settles to a fixed point). Beta sets the aspect ratio of the lobes. The classic values that produce the iconic butterfly are sigma = 10, rho = 28, beta = 8/3.

2. Sensitive Dependence

Here is the heart of chaos. Launch two trajectories from starting points that differ by just 0.001 in one coordinate. For a while, the two paths are indistinguishable. They track each other through the same loops, the same crossings. Then, without warning, they split. One goes left while the other goes right. Within a few more orbits they have nothing in common.

The divergence chart below the attractor quantifies this. The vertical axis is the distance between the two points on a logarithmic scale. A straight line on a log plot means exponential growth. That rate of exponential separation is the Lyapunov exponent, and it is the mathematical definition of chaos.

Try increasing the initial separation. You will notice the trajectories diverge sooner, but the rate of divergence (the slope of the line) stays roughly the same. That slope is a property of the attractor, not the initial conditions.

Edward Lorenz discovered this in 1963 while simulating weather. He re-entered initial conditions rounded to three decimal places instead of six. The simulation diverged completely. This ended the hope of long-range deterministic weather forecasting.

Chaos is not randomness. A chaotic system follows exact equations, but tiny differences in initial conditions grow exponentially. The strange attractor is the shape this unpredictability traces: a geometric object of infinite complexity in finite space.
