Market Bubbles

When traders follow each other instead of fundamentals, prices detach from reality

Every few years a market goes parabolic. Tulips, railroads, dot-coms, housing, crypto. The pattern repeats because the mechanism is structural, not cultural. It lives in the feedback loop between price and behavior.

A bubble does not require irrational people. It requires a system where the actions of participants feed back into the signals those participants act on. Rising prices attract buyers. Buying raises prices. The loop amplifies small perturbations into spectacular departures from reality.

The simulations below let you build that loop from the ground up.

1. Fundamentalists vs. Chartists

Two kinds of traders operate in our model market. Fundamentalists compare the current price to a true underlying value. When price is below value, they buy. When above, they sell. They are the stabilizing force. Left alone, they keep price tethered to reality.

Chartists do something different. They look at the recent price trend. If price has been rising, they buy more. If falling, they sell. They do not care about underlying value. They care about momentum. This is the destabilizing force. Chartists do not cause bubbles on purpose. They just follow the signal that is loudest: the price itself.

The simulation starts with 20% chartists. Price wobbles but stays close to the dashed fundamental-value line. Now drag the chartists slider toward 60% or higher. The blue line will detach. Sometimes it overshoots wildly, then snaps back when the fundamentalists overpower the momentum. That snap-back is a crash.

Try the sensitivity slider too. It controls how aggressively chartists chase the trend. Even a moderate chartist fraction becomes explosive at high sensitivity.

2. Anatomy of a Bubble

Bubbles are not shapeless. They follow a recognizable arc that economists have catalogued. The sequence goes roughly like this:

Stealth. Smart money enters early. The price begins to drift above fundamental value, but the move is small enough that most participants ignore it.

Awareness. The trend becomes visible. Chartists pile in. Media coverage begins. The price rise accelerates.

Mania. Everyone is buying. The narrative justifies any price. Skeptics are dismissed. The deviation from fundamental value becomes extreme.

Blow-off. Some event or simply exhaustion tips the balance. The price peaks and begins to fall. But chartists are still buying the dip, so the drop is choppy at first.

Capitulation. Trend-followers capitulate. Selling feeds selling. The price crashes back toward fundamental value, often overshooting to the downside.

3. Herding and Contagion

The models above treat the chartist fraction as a fixed dial. In real markets, traders switch strategies. A fundamentalist watches her neighbors get rich by following the trend and thinks: maybe they see something I do not. She starts trend-following too. This is herding.

Herding is rational locally but destructive globally. If your neighbor is profiting, copying her strategy is a reasonable bet. The problem is that when everyone copies, the strategy stops working. The price is now supported entirely by the feedback loop. The moment new buyers stop arriving, the loop reverses.

Below, each node is a trader connected to neighbors in a small-world network. Blue nodes are fundamentalists. Orange nodes are chartists. At each step, agents can flip their strategy based on what their neighbors are doing and how profitable that strategy has been recently.

Watch for clusters. When a pocket of chartists forms, it reinforces itself. The cluster's buying pushes price up, which validates the chartist strategy, which converts more neighbors. The bubble is a spatial phenomenon, not just a statistical one. Crank herding pressure high and watch orange clusters engulf the network. Then watch them collapse when the price reverts.
