Tumor Growth

Cancer cells compete for oxygen, and the shape of the tumor emerges from this competition.

A tumor begins as a small cluster of cells that have lost the normal brakes on division. They divide. They crowd together. And immediately they face a problem: nutrients, especially oxygen, can only reach the cells at the surface. The interior is starved.

This sets up a competition. Cells near the edge have access to oxygen and divide. Cells in the core are suffocated and die. The tumor does not grow as a solid mass. It grows as an expanding shell, driven outward by a nutrient gradient that penalizes depth and rewards exposure.

1. Growth at the Edge

The simulation below models a cluster of tumor cells on a grid. Oxygen diffuses inward from the surrounding tissue (the boundary). Cells at the surface sit in an oxygen-rich zone and can divide into adjacent empty space. Cells buried deep in the interior receive almost no oxygen and eventually die. The result is a growing ring: a viable rim of proliferating cells surrounding a necrotic core.

The background heatmap shows the oxygen concentration field. Bright regions are oxygen-rich. Dark regions are depleted. Watch how the tumor hollows itself out as it grows. The living shell stays thin because oxygen cannot penetrate far into dense tissue.

The shape stays roughly circular at first. Oxygen diffuses symmetrically, so all parts of the surface grow at similar rates. But this symmetry is fragile. Any small irregularity on the surface changes the local nutrient supply, and that change feeds back into the growth pattern.

2. The Instability

A perfectly circular tumor front is unstable. Imagine a small bump on the surface. That bump protrudes into the surrounding tissue, where oxygen concentration is higher. It receives more nutrient than the flat parts of the surface. So it divides faster. The bump grows. It becomes a finger. The finger itself develops bumps. Those bumps become branches.

This is the Mullins-Sekerka instability, the same mechanism behind the branching of snowflakes and the fingering of viscous fluids. A smooth interface amplifies perturbations because protruding regions have better access to the diffusing field.

Below, two tumors grow side by side. The left one starts from a nearly smooth circle. The right one starts from a circle with random noise on its surface. Watch how the noisy tumor develops fingers faster. The smooth tumor eventually fingers too, but it takes longer because the initial perturbations are smaller and grow from numerical noise alone.

The instability is not a defect. It is a mathematical consequence of diffusion-limited growth. Any interface that advances by consuming a diffusing field will develop fingers. The tumor does not "choose" to branch. Branching is what happens when growth is controlled by a gradient.

3. Angiogenesis

A tumor growing by diffusion alone hits a ceiling. Once it exceeds about one millimeter in diameter, the interior is so far from external oxygen that no amount of surface growth can sustain the mass. The tumor needs a new supply line. It needs blood vessels.

Real tumors solve this by secreting vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), a chemical signal that diffuses outward from the hypoxic core. Nearby blood vessels detect VEGF and sprout new capillaries that grow toward the tumor. When these vessels reach the tumor, they deliver oxygen directly to the interior, relieving the nutrient bottleneck and allowing a new burst of growth.

Toggle angiogenesis on in the simulation below. Red lines represent vessel sprouts growing toward the tumor along the VEGF gradient. Where vessels penetrate the tumor, the local nutrient supply jumps and growth accelerates. The result is faster, more irregular expansion. The tumor no longer grows as a uniform shell. It bulges outward wherever vessels feed it.
