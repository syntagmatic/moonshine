Lightning Paths

An electric field finds the path of least resistance through a probabilistic branching process

Lightning does not follow a predetermined path. The bolt you see is the end result of an invisible search: a stepped leader channel extends downward from the cloud, probing the air in front of it. At each step, the channel tip breaks down the air in a direction chosen probabilistically, biased by the local electric field. Tips in high-field regions grow faster. Where two tips compete, one wins and the other dies. The result is a branching tree, a fractal carved by the field it creates.

The dielectric breakdown model (DBM) captures this process on a grid. A seed conductor grows from the top boundary (the cloud) into a lattice of resistive cells. The electric potential is solved at each step, and the channel extends into the neighbor with the highest field, with randomness controlled by an exponent. The same math governs river networks, mineral dendrites, and diffusion-limited aggregation.

1. Dielectric Breakdown

The simulation below grows a discharge from the top of the grid (the thundercloud) toward the bottom (the ground). At each step, the algorithm identifies all empty cells neighboring the current channel, computes the electric field at each one, raises it to the power eta, and picks a growth site proportional to that weight. The background color encodes the electric potential: bright near the channel, dark far away.

The channel does not "know" where the ground is. It simply follows the local field gradient, which happens to point generally downward because the ground is held at zero potential. But the path is tortuous, branching, and different every time. The global pattern emerges from purely local decisions.

2. The Branching Exponent

The exponent eta controls how strongly the electric field biases growth. Move the slider and watch the character of the discharge change. At eta = 0, every neighbor is equally likely, producing a bushy, space-filling tree. At eta = 1, the discharge is DLA-like with moderate branching. At eta = 4 or higher, only the highest-field tip grows, producing a nearly linear channel. Real lightning sits somewhere around eta = 1 to 2.

3. Universal Branching

Lightning, river networks, and diffusion-limited aggregation all produce similar branching trees. This is not a coincidence. All three are governed by a Laplacian field: the electric potential for lightning, the elevation field for rivers, the diffusion field for DLA. Growth occurs at the boundary where the field gradient is steepest. Lightning and rivers are dual problems. Lightning grows from the cloud downward, a single source branching toward a distributed ground. Rivers grow from distributed sources (rain) converging toward a single outlet (the ocean). Flip the direction, and the branching pattern inverts, but the underlying Laplacian growth model is identical.

The next time you see a lightning bolt frozen in a photograph, notice how it looks like a river delta turned upside down, or a tree root system, or a crack in dried mud. These patterns are not similar by analogy. They are similar because they are solutions to the same equation: grow where the gradient is steepest, with a little randomness mixed in.
