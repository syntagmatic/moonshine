Epidemic Spreading: Contagion on Networks

How a disease that spreads person-to-person can explode or fizzle depending on network structure

A disease does not spread through a population uniformly. It travels along contacts. You catch the flu from a coworker, not from a stranger across town. This means the pattern of who-knows-who matters enormously. The same pathogen can burn through one population and fizzle in another, depending on how people are connected.

Epidemiologists model this with networks. Each person is a node. Each contact is an edge. The disease hops from node to node with some probability. The question is: does it take off, or does it die out?

The SIR Model

The simplest epidemic model divides people into three states. Susceptible means you can catch the disease. Infected means you have it and can spread it. Recovered means you are immune. Each time step, an infected node transmits to each susceptible neighbor with probability β, and recovers with probability γ.

R₀ and the Tipping Point

The basic reproduction number R₀ is the average number of new infections caused by a single infected person in a fully susceptible population. When R₀ > 1, each case generates more than one new case and the epidemic grows exponentially. When R₀ < 1, the chain of transmission breaks and the outbreak fizzles.

The transition at R₀ = 1 is sharp. Near the threshold, outcomes become bimodal: the disease either dies almost immediately or infects a large fraction of the network. There is very little middle ground. This is a phase transition, the same kind of discontinuity that appears in percolation and magnetism.

Network Topology Matters

The same disease behaves differently on different networks. A random graph, where edges are assigned uniformly, spreads infection at a steady rate. A small-world network, where most connections are local but a few are long-range shortcuts, lets the disease jump between distant clusters. A scale-free network, where a few hubs have many connections, is especially vulnerable: those hubs act as superspreaders.

Vaccination Strategy

If you can vaccinate only a fraction of the population, who should you target? Random vaccination immunizes people uniformly. Targeted vaccination goes after the highest-degree nodes first. On a scale-free network, the difference is stark. Vaccinating a few hubs can break the transmission backbone. Random vaccination, even at higher coverage, often fails to stop the outbreak.
