Spin Glass

When not everyone can be satisfied simultaneously, the system freezes into a disordered but structured state.

Imagine a room of people at a dinner party. Some pairs are friends who want to sit on the same side of the table, and some are rivals who want to be on opposite sides. If every constraint could be satisfied, there would be an obvious seating arrangement. But when the friendships and rivalries form loops of odd length, some pair will always be unhappy. That is frustration, and it is the central idea behind spin glasses.

A spin glass is a material where magnetic atoms interact with random couplings. Some bonds are ferromagnetic (wanting neighbors to align) and some are antiferromagnetic (wanting neighbors to oppose). The randomness means that on certain triangles, no assignment of spins can satisfy all three bonds at once. The system cannot settle into a single neat ground state. Instead, it has a vast number of nearly equivalent configurations, separated by energy barriers. Cooling does not bring order. It brings frozen disorder.

Below is a triangular lattice. Each circle is a spin: red for up (+1), blue for down (−1). Notice that no matter how you flip the spins, you cannot get the frustrated triangle count to zero. Some triangles have an odd number of antiferromagnetic bonds. For those, every configuration leaves at least one bond unsatisfied. This is the geometric origin of frustration. It is not about having many antiferromagnetic bonds. It is about having them form inescapable loops.

2. Simulated Annealing

In thermal physics, a spin flips when a random fluctuation gives it enough energy to cross a barrier. The Metropolis algorithm captures this: pick a random spin, compute the energy change from flipping it, and accept the flip if it lowers energy. If it raises energy, accept it anyway with probability exp(−ΔE / T), where T is temperature. High temperature means almost any flip is accepted. Low temperature means only downhill moves survive.

Start the annealing process below. At high temperature the spins churn randomly. As the temperature drops, the system freezes. Run it several times. Each run freezes into a different configuration with a different final energy. There is no single ground state that the system reliably finds. This is the signature of a spin glass: the energy landscape has many valleys, and which one you land in depends on the random path you took while cooling.

3. The Energy Landscape

To see the landscape directly, we need a small enough system to enumerate every possible state. With 7 spins, there are 128 configurations. Below, each bar represents one configuration, sorted from lowest to highest energy. The ruggedness is obvious: many bars cluster near the bottom, forming a forest of local minima separated by energy peaks. There is no clean funnel leading to a single answer.

Hover over a bar to see that configuration. The number beneath it is the Hamming distance from the current lowest-energy state, counting how many spins differ. States with similar energy can be far apart in configuration space, meaning the system would have to cross tall barriers to move between them. That is what makes spin glasses computationally hard.

Spin glasses earned Giorgio Parisi the 2021 Nobel Prize in Physics. The mathematical tools he developed to solve them (replica symmetry breaking) now appear in neural network theory, error-correcting codes, and combinatorial optimization. The traveling salesman problem can be mapped to a spin glass.
