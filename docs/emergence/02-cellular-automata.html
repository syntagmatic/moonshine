Cellular Automata: Computation from Nothing

A grid of cells following one rule can produce anything from static patterns to universal computers.

Take a row of cells. Each cell is on or off. To decide what happens next, look at each cell and its two neighbors. That is three bits, eight possible inputs. A rule assigns an output to each input. There are 256 such rules. Some produce nothing interesting. A few produce everything.

Elementary Automata

Stephen Wolfram catalogued all 256 one-dimensional rules in the 1980s and found they fall into strikingly different classes. Rule 90 gives a perfect Sierpinski triangle. Rule 30 produces apparent randomness. Rule 110 sits at the boundary, generating structures complex enough to be proven Turing complete.

Try switching between Rule 30 and Rule 90 with the same starting cell. One gives a fractal; the other gives apparent noise. Same mechanism, same seed, different rule number. The difference between order and chaos can be a single bit in the rule table.

Conway's Game of Life

In two dimensions the same idea gets richer. John Conway's Game of Life uses a single rule set on a square grid: a dead cell with exactly three live neighbors is born; a live cell with two or three neighbors survives; everything else dies. That is it. Four words of logic.

From those four words come gliders that walk across the grid, oscillators that pulse in place, and glider guns that emit a steady stream of new objects. People have built working computers inside Life, complete with logic gates and memory.

The R-pentomino is worth watching. Five cells. It takes 1103 generations to stabilize, producing six gliders and a collection of still lifes and oscillators along the way. Nobody could have predicted that from the rule alone. The behavior is not hidden in the initial state or the rule. It is a property of their interaction across time.

The Edge of Chaos

Wolfram grouped elementary automata into four classes. Class I rules produce uniform grids. Class II produces repeating patterns. Class III produces apparent randomness. Class IV sits between II and III, at what Chris Langton called the "edge of chaos," producing localized structures that interact in complex ways.

This boundary matters. Class IV rules are the ones capable of supporting computation. Too much order and information cannot flow. Too much chaos and information cannot persist. Computation requires both.

Rule 110 is proven to be Turing complete. A one-dimensional row of cells, updating by looking at three neighbors, can in principle compute anything any computer can compute. Complexity does not require complex ingredients.

This is the core lesson of cellular automata. You do not need complicated rules to get complicated behavior. You do not need a central controller to get coordination. You do not need to design complexity. You just need a rule, a grid, and time.
