Vortex Streets

Place an object in a flow and watch alternating vortices shed from each side in a perfectly regular pattern.

Hold a stick in a stream and look at the water behind it. You will see swirls peeling off alternately from one side, then the other, drifting downstream in a staggered zigzag. This is a von Kármán vortex street. It was first described mathematically by Theodore von Kármán in 1911, but the pattern itself is older than any human observer. Satellite photos show vortex streets stretching for hundreds of kilometers behind islands in the ocean.

The pattern is not designed. It emerges. A uniform flow hits a blunt obstacle, the boundary layers on each side become unstable, and the instability self-organizes into a periodic alternating shedding. One vortex growing on one side feeds the detachment of a vortex on the other. The result is a metronomic rhythm that depends only on the flow speed, the obstacle size, and the viscosity of the fluid.

2. The Reynolds Number

Whether the flow is smooth or chaotic depends on a single dimensionless quantity: the Reynolds number. It is defined as Re = ρvL/μ, where ρ is the fluid density, v is the flow speed, L is the obstacle diameter, and μ is the dynamic viscosity. You can think of it as the ratio of inertial forces (the fluid's tendency to keep moving) to viscous forces (the fluid's internal friction that damps out disturbances).

At low Re the viscous forces win. The flow is laminar, meaning smooth and predictable. As Re increases, inertia begins to dominate. The boundary layer separates from the obstacle and curls into vortices. In a narrow band of Re (roughly 50 to 300), the shedding is remarkably periodic: a clean vortex street. Push Re higher still and the wake becomes turbulent, the regularity breaks down into chaos.

3. Obstacle Shape

A cylinder is the classic case, but any blunt body placed in a flow will shed vortices. The shape of the obstacle changes the details: the width of the wake, the frequency of shedding, and how cleanly the vortices detach. Engineers characterize the shedding frequency with the Strouhal number, St = fL/v, where f is the shedding frequency, L is the obstacle width, and v is the flow speed. For a cylinder, St sits close to 0.2 across a wide range of Reynolds numbers. Other shapes give different values.

The Strouhal number is remarkably stable. For a cylinder it stays near 0.2 whether the fluid is air, water, or honey, whether the obstacle is a millimeter or a meter wide. This universality is why engineers can predict vortex-induced vibrations from a single dimensionless chart.

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapsed in 1940 partly because wind-driven vortex shedding matched the bridge's natural frequency. The same physics makes power lines hum on windy days and flags flutter. Tall chimneys often have helical strakes (spiral fins) wrapped around them specifically to break up the regular shedding pattern and prevent destructive oscillation.
