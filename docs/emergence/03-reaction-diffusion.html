Reaction-Diffusion: How Leopards Get Their Spots

Two chemicals that activate and inhibit each other can paint the patterns of the natural world

Two Chemicals, One Pattern

Imagine two chemicals spreading across a surface. Chemical U activates growth. Chemical V inhibits it. Both diffuse outward like ink in water, but V spreads faster than U.

When U concentrates somewhere, it tries to produce more of itself and also produces V. But V suppresses U. The result is a tug-of-war. U forms peaks where it can outpace V locally. V catches up and pushes U down. The peaks settle into stable patterns: spots, stripes, labyrinths.

This is the Gray-Scott model. Two reactions (feed U, kill V) plus diffusion. That is enough to generate the patterns on leopard fur, zebrafish skin, and seashell surfaces.

The Parameter Landscape

The feed rate f controls how fast fresh U flows in. The kill rate k controls how fast V decays. Together, these two numbers determine everything about the pattern. A tiny change can flip the system from spots to stripes, or from quiet stripes to a pulsing, dividing mass.

Painting with Chemistry

Initial conditions matter. A single seed point in a uniform field can grow into a spreading coral. A stripe of V can break apart into chains of spots. Random noise produces the most organic textures.

Alan Turing proposed this mechanism in 1952, decades before anyone could observe the chemicals in living tissue. The math predicted the biology. He showed that two substances diffusing at different rates can spontaneously break symmetry, creating pattern from uniformity. Biologists have since confirmed morphogen gradients in zebrafish, mice, and many other organisms.
