Power-Law Networks

The rich get richer, and the resulting network has hubs that connect everything.

Most real networks are not built all at once. They grow. The World Wide Web adds pages one at a time, and each new page links to pages that are already popular. A new scientist co-authors with established collaborators. A new airport opens routes to the busiest hubs. In each case, the newcomer prefers to connect to nodes that already have many connections.

This process, called preferential attachment, produces networks with a distinctive signature: a power-law degree distribution. Most nodes have very few connections. A handful of nodes become massive hubs. And the gap between the two is not gradual. It spans orders of magnitude.

Growing a Network

Start with a tiny seed. At each step, a new node arrives and connects to existing nodes. The probability of connecting to a given node is proportional to how many connections that node already has. A node with 10 links is twice as likely to attract the next connection as a node with 5. The result: early arrivals accumulate connections at an accelerating rate. They become hubs.

The Degree Distribution

The hallmark of a scale-free network is its degree distribution. If you count how many nodes have degree k and plot that count against k on logarithmic axes, you get a straight line. This is the power law: P(k) ~ k−γ, where γ is typically between 2 and 3 for real networks.

Compare that to an Erdős-Rényi random graph, where edges are placed uniformly at random. Its degree distribution is a Poisson, which looks like a tight bell curve on log-log axes. Random graphs have no hubs. Every node has roughly the same number of connections.

Robust Yet Fragile

A scale-free network has a paradoxical property. Remove nodes at random and the network barely notices. Most random removals hit low-degree nodes that are peripheral, and the giant connected component stays intact. This is why the Internet keeps working even though routers fail all the time.

But remove the hubs deliberately and the network falls apart fast. Knock out just a few of the highest-degree nodes and the giant component shatters into disconnected fragments. The same structure that provides robustness to accidents creates catastrophic vulnerability to targeted attacks.

The Internet, airline route maps, protein interaction networks, and citation networks all show power-law degree distributions. This is why the Internet survives router failures daily but would collapse if a few key exchange points were knocked out simultaneously.
