Trail Systems

People walk where others have walked before, and the footpath network designs itself

Imagine a park with several buildings but no paved walkways. People cut across the grass, choosing their own routes. At first the paths are scattered, but over days and weeks a clear network of trails appears in the worn turf. Nobody designed it. The trails designed themselves.

Each pedestrian faces a simple trade-off: walk the shortest geometric path to the destination, or follow an already-worn trail that is faster underfoot even if a bit longer. The worn trail wins more often, which wears it further, which makes it win even more often. This positive feedback loop is the engine behind desire paths.

The model here is based on Dirk Helbing's "active walker" framework. Each pedestrian lays down a trace of wear on the ground. Subsequent walkers sense the ground ahead, preferring worn terrain (which is firmer and faster) over untouched grass. The balance between shortcut seeking and trail following is captured in a single parameter: the path preference.

1. Watch Trails Self-Organize

Below, colored circles are destinations and the white diamond is the spawn point. Pedestrians appear, pick a random destination, and walk. They leave faint marks on the ground. Over time, popular routes accumulate into visible trails. Adjust the path preference slider to control how much walkers favor worn ground over the direct route.

At low path preference, walkers mostly beeline toward their target. The result is a scattered fan of faint marks. At high preference, walkers strongly favor existing wear and the system converges on a small number of heavily used trunk routes with branches splitting off near destinations. The sweet spot in the middle produces efficient, natural-looking trail networks.

The feedback is self-reinforcing: a path used by 10 people attracts the 11th more strongly than a path used by 2. Small early fluctuations get amplified into the dominant network. If you reset and run again, the exact layout will differ, but the overall structure will be similar. The topology is robust even though the geometry is stochastic.

2. Trail Efficiency vs. Direct Paths

Are desire paths actually efficient, or just popular? The chart below tracks the ratio of actual walked distance to straight-line distance over time. Early walkers take long, wandering routes because there are few trails to follow and they compromise between direction and worn ground. As the trail network matures, the ratio drops toward 1.0. The emergent network approaches the geometric optimum, while offering faster walking speed on packed earth.

This is a remarkable result. Nobody optimized the network. Each walker optimized only their own immediate next step. But the aggregate outcome is a nearly optimal transport graph. The efficiency emerges from the feedback between individual choice and collective infrastructure.

Figure 2. Ratio of trail distance to straight-line distance, averaged over sliding windows of 10 pedestrians. As the network matures, the ratio approaches 1.0. Spikes correspond to walkers headed to distant destinations where detours along trunk trails cost more.

3. Adding a New Destination

What happens when the landscape changes? Click the button below to drop a new destination onto the field. The existing trail network was optimized for the old layout. Watch how new branches form to connect the newcomer, while the old network mostly stays intact. The system reorganizes incrementally, not from scratch.

This incremental adaptation is a hallmark of emergent infrastructure. Planned systems often require expensive redesigns when conditions change. Emergent systems absorb changes continuously, extending the existing structure rather than replacing it. The old trails become scaffolding for the new ones.

University campus planners sometimes wait a full year before paving walkways on a new quad. They let students wear desire paths into the grass, then pave those. The emergent design often outperforms anything an architect would have drawn, because it encodes actual usage patterns rather than assumed ones. Michigan State, Virginia Tech, and the University of Oregon have all used this approach.

The trail system is a feedback loop between individual choice and collective infrastructure. No one plans the network; everyone builds it by walking. Each person's decision is local and selfish (get to my destination quickly), but the aggregate result is a globally efficient transport network. This is emergence in its most literal, ground-level form.
