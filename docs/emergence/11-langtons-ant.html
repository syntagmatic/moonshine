Langton's Ant

A two-rule automaton that wanders chaotically for 10,000 steps, then suddenly builds a highway to infinity

In 1986, Chris Langton invented one of the simplest possible cellular automata. An ant sits on an infinite grid of white cells. It has only two rules. And from those two rules, something unexplained happens.

The ant wanders. It flips cells. It makes a mess. For about 10,000 steps, the trail looks random. Then, without any trigger, the ant locks into a repeating pattern and builds a diagonal highway that extends forever.

Nobody knows why.

The Rule

The rule fits in one sentence. On a white cell: turn right 90 degrees, flip the cell to black, step forward. On a black cell: turn left 90 degrees, flip to white, step forward. That is it. No randomness, no memory, no lookahead.

Press play and watch the ant walk. The colored triangle is the ant. Its direction shows which way it will step next. The grid starts all white.

For the first ~500 steps, the pattern is roughly symmetric. Then the ant escapes its initial region and starts wandering erratically. The trail fills an irregular blob with no obvious structure. If you did not know what was coming, you would guess this continues forever.

The Highway Appears

It does not continue forever. Around step 10,000, something changes. The ant stops wandering and starts building. It lays down a repeating diagonal stripe, two cells wide, extending to the upper-right. Every 104 steps, the pattern repeats exactly, shifted two cells diagonally. This is the highway.

The highway is not a fluke of the initial conditions. Start the ant on a blank grid, a single black cell, or a random scatter of cells. In every known case, after some transient, the ant eventually produces a highway.

Multiple Colors

Langton generalized the ant to more than two colors. Instead of just "right on white, left on black," you specify a rule string. Each character is L or R, one per color. The ant cycles through colors in order, turning as the character says, then flipping the cell to the next color.

For example, RL is the classic ant. LLRR means: on color 0, turn left (flip to 1); on color 1, turn left (flip to 2); on color 2, turn right (flip to 3); on color 3, turn right (flip to 0). It is an open problem in mathematics whether Langton's Ant always produces a highway eventually, regardless of initial conditions. After decades of study, no one has found a counterexample, but no one has a proof either.

The problem is sometimes called the "Langton's Ant highway conjecture." It connects to deep questions about undecidability, since generalized ants can simulate Turing machines. Simple rules, hard questions.
