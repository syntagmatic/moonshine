Crystal Growth

Hexagonal symmetry and branching complexity emerge from water molecules attaching one at a time.

A snowflake is a single crystal of ice. It starts as a tiny hexagonal prism, no bigger than a dust grain, floating inside a cloud. Water vapor molecules in the surrounding air drift toward it and latch on. One by one. That is all that happens.

Yet the result is not a featureless lump. It is a structure with six-fold symmetry, intricate branching, and a shape so specific that it records the atmospheric conditions in which it grew. How does local attachment produce global order?

The answer comes in two parts. The symmetry comes from the crystal lattice: the hexagonal geometry of ice forces every new molecule into a position that respects the six-fold pattern. The complexity comes from a competition between two physical processes: diffusion, which delivers vapor to the surface unevenly, and surface energy, which tries to keep things smooth. The interplay between these two forces decides whether a snowflake grows simple plates or wild fern-like branches.

1. The Hexagonal Lattice

Water molecules form hydrogen bonds at angles that favor a hexagonal arrangement. When a molecule joins the crystal, it slots into a position dictated by the lattice. This is where the six-fold symmetry comes from. It is not imposed from outside. It is built into the bonding geometry of water itself.

The simulation below grows a crystal on a hexagonal grid. A seed sits at the center. Vapor is represented as a diffusion field: each cell holds a concentration of available molecules. When a boundary cell of the crystal accumulates enough vapor, it freezes and joins the crystal. The vapor near the crystal surface gets depleted, and must be replenished by diffusion from farther away.

Try the temperature slider. At cold temperatures the attachment threshold is low, so even small local concentrations trigger freezing. Tips that poke outward into fresh vapor grow quickly. The result is branching dendrites. At warmer temperatures the threshold is higher. Growth is slower and more uniform, producing flat plates and faceted hexagons.

Notice how the six arms develop in lockstep. Each arm sits in an identical position relative to the crystal center. Each sees the same vapor field, which inherits the hexagonal symmetry of the grid. So each arm grows at the same rate and branches at the same points. The symmetry is not coordinated across arms. It is a consequence of identical local conditions acting on identical local geometry.

This is the deep lesson of crystal symmetry: the shape of the whole is encoded in the shape of the parts. Water molecules know nothing about the snowflake they are building. They only know the angles at which they can bond. The global symmetry is a shadow of the local bonding rules, amplified by repetition across millions of attachment events.

2. Diffusion vs. Faceting

Two forces compete during crystal growth. Diffusion delivers vapor molecules to the crystal surface, but it delivers them unevenly. Tips and corners that protrude into the vapor field intercept molecules before they can reach the flat faces behind them. This is the Mullins-Sekerka instability: any small bump on the surface grows faster than its surroundings, amplifying itself into a branch.

Working against this is surface energy, which penalizes sharp features. A flat crystal face is energetically favorable. Molecules that land on a bump may rearrange to smooth it out. The crystal lattice also matters: certain orientations (the six prism faces) are naturally more stable than others, so growth tends to preserve flat facets along those directions.

When surface energy dominates, the crystal stays faceted and compact. When diffusion dominates, branches proliferate. The ratio between the two is controlled by supersaturation: the amount of excess vapor available beyond what is needed for equilibrium.

High supersaturation means abundant vapor. Diffusion dominates and branches run wild. Low supersaturation means scarce vapor. The crystal barely grows beyond a hexagonal prism. Real snowflakes form at supersaturations that sit right in the middle zone, where both forces matter and the resulting shapes are complex.

The physicist Ukichiro Nakaya charted this in the 1930s. By growing crystals under controlled conditions, he mapped the relationship between temperature, humidity, and crystal shape. The "Nakaya diagram" shows that plates form near -2 C and -10 C, columns near -5 C, and the most elaborate dendrites near -15 C at high humidity. 3. A Snowflake Gallery

A falling snowflake tumbles through layers of atmosphere with different temperatures and humidities. Its shape at any moment reflects its entire history. A crystal that starts as a plate might sprout branches when it falls into a colder, more humid layer. Those branches might then develop sidebranches of their own if conditions shift again.

The six crystals below grow under different predefined trajectories. Each trajectory changes temperature and humidity over time, mimicking the changing conditions a snowflake encounters as it falls. Some spend their early life in warm, dry air and then pass through a cold, humid layer. Others experience the reverse. The result in each case is a distinct shape.

No two snowflakes are alike because each one tumbles through a unique column of atmosphere. But the six arms of any single snowflake are nearly identical because they all experience the same conditions at the same moment.

The snowflake is a record written in ice. Its branching pattern encodes the sequence of temperatures and humidities it encountered on its way down. Read the branches from center to tip and you are reading a history of the sky. The center formed first, in conditions that may have been quite different from what the tips experienced later. Every bump, every branch point, every flat section corresponds to a moment in the crystal's descent.

And yet each snowflake grows from nothing but the local physics of one molecule bonding to the next. No plan. No blueprint. Just a hexagonal lattice, a diffusion field, and the long fall through the atmosphere.
