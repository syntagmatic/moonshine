Termite Mounds

Termites coordinate by writing instructions into the environment itself

A termite mound in the African savanna can stand three meters tall, house millions of inhabitants, and regulate its own temperature within a degree. No termite designed it. No termite has seen the blueprint. Each termite follows a handful of local rules involving mud, pheromone, and whatever is directly in front of its face.

The mechanism is stigmergy. A termite picks up a pellet of mud, mixes it with pheromone from its own body, and drops it somewhere. That pheromone-laced pellet becomes a signal. Other termites smell it and are drawn to it. They drop their own pellets nearby. The pile grows. The pile attracts more termites. The signal amplifies itself through the structure it creates.

The structure is the communication medium. The plan is the building. These are not two separate things.

1. Mud and Pheromone

Start simple. Termites (small dots) wander a 2D field. Each one picks up mud pellets from the ground, carries them around, and eventually drops them. Without pheromone, drops are random. Pellets scatter evenly and nothing interesting happens.

Now add the key ingredient. Each dropped pellet emits pheromone that diffuses outward and slowly evaporates. A carrying termite is more likely to drop its pellet where pheromone concentration is already high. This single rule creates positive feedback: pellets attract pellets. Scattered mud coalesces into distinct piles.

Play with the evaporation slider. Turn it up and piles become transient. Pheromone fades before enough pellets accumulate to sustain the signal. Turn it down and piles become permanent but slow to form. The sweet spot produces piles that consolidate quickly and persist.

Turn pheromone strength all the way down. Without chemical attraction, termites drop pellets at random and the field stays uniform. The pheromone is what breaks the symmetry. A random fluctuation (a few pellets landing close together by chance) gets amplified into a macroscopic structure. This is the same symmetry-breaking mechanism that drives Turing patterns and Rayleigh-Benard convection.

2. From Piles to Pillars

Piles are just the beginning. In a real mound, termites build vertically. Piles become pillars. When two pillars grow close enough, termites on top of each pillar are within pheromone range of the other pillar. They start building sideways, toward the signal. The two columns lean into each other and eventually meet, forming an arch.

The simulation below shows a 2D cross-section. Termites deposit material that stacks upward under gravity. Pheromone diffuses from each deposit. When the tops of two adjacent columns enter each other's pheromone fields, termites begin bridging the gap. The arch threshold controls how strong the cross-pillar signal needs to be before termites start building laterally.

Set the arch threshold low and arches form easily. Pillars connect at short heights, producing many small chambers. Set it high and pillars must grow tall before the cross-signal is strong enough to trigger lateral building. You get fewer, larger chambers with higher ceilings.

Notice that the chamber size is not encoded anywhere. No termite carries a number for "ideal room width." The chamber dimensions fall out of the interaction between pillar spacing (set by pheromone diffusion range in Section 1) and arch threshold (set by how sensitive termites are to lateral signals). Two parameters, neither of which describes a room, produce rooms.

3. Ventilation Emerges

The most striking feature of a real termite mound is its ventilation system. The interior stays around 30 degrees Celsius while the exterior swings from 2 to 40 across the day. The mound breathes. Hot air from the colony's metabolism rises through central channels, pulling cool air in through porous peripheral walls. The result is passive convection, like a chimney with no fire.

The simulation below models this as a temperature field on a cross-section of the mound. A heat source at the base (the colony) warms the interior. Air rises through channels and exits at the top. Termites respond to local temperature: where it is too hot, they open vents by removing material. Where it is cool, they seal gaps by adding material. No termite measures the mound's overall temperature. Each one reacts to what it feels right where it stands.

Crank up the heat source and watch the mound respond. More vents open. Convection intensifies. The core temperature rises initially but then stabilizes as the ventilation system catches up. Turn it down and vents close, retaining warmth. The system is a thermostat built from dumb agents and mud.

The sensitivity slider controls how aggressively termites respond to local temperature. High sensitivity means vents open and close rapidly, producing a twitchy but responsive system. Low sensitivity means the mound is sluggish, tolerating larger temperature swings before adjusting. Real termite colonies operate somewhere in between, and different species have tuned this parameter differently through evolution.

Stigmergy was coined by Pierre-Paul Grassé in 1959, from the Greek stigma (sign) and ergon (work). The same principle drives Wikipedia editing, open-source software development, and trail formation in cities. An edit to a wiki page is a pheromone deposit. It changes the artifact, and the changed artifact influences what the next editor does. The artifact is both the product and the coordination mechanism.

The termite mound is a building that built itself. No architect, no blueprint, no foreman. Just mud, pheromone, and local rules. The structure records its own construction history in a chemical language that only makes sense to the agents still building it. When the structure is the message, coordination is free.
