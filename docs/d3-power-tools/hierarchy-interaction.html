Hierarchy Interaction

Expand, collapse, and navigate large trees without losing context.

A tree with four levels and a branching factor of four already has over 80 nodes. Show them all at once and the diagram becomes an unreadable tangle of crossing links and overlapping labels. But hiding everything behind a single root is equally useless. The viewer needs to control what is visible, opening branches they care about and closing the ones they do not.

The collapsible tree below represents a small file system. It starts with only the first two levels expanded. Click any node that has children to toggle it open or closed. Watch how entering nodes emerge from their parent and exiting nodes collapse back into it. That spatial continuity is what makes the interaction legible: without it, nodes would just pop in and out with no connection to the structure.

Large trees need expand/collapse and zoom to stay legible. You cannot show everything at once. Interaction lets the viewer decide what matters, reducing a 60-node tree to the 15 nodes they actually need to see.

Figure 1. A collapsible file system tree. Click a node to expand or collapse its children. The breadcrumb trail above tracks the path to the most recently clicked node.

How the Toggle Works

D3's tree layout only visits node.children. To collapse a node, you move its children to a private _children property and set children to null. When the layout runs again, it skips the hidden subtree entirely. The trick to smooth animation is stashing each node's position after every update. When a child enters, it starts at its parent's stashed position and transitions to its computed position. When it exits, it does the reverse. This makes children visually grow out of and shrink back into their parent.

The breadcrumb trail above the tree shows the path from root to the last node you clicked. Each crumb is itself clickable. Click an ancestor in the breadcrumb bar to collapse everything below it and refocus the tree, giving you a quick way to back out of a deep branch without clicking through each level individually.

The _children Pattern in Detail

The convention of storing hidden children on _children is not a D3 API feature. It is a user-space pattern that exploits how d3.hierarchy traverses the tree: the layout calls node.children and ignores everything else. By moving the array to any other property, you make those nodes invisible to the layout without losing them.

The toggle function is deceptively simple. On click, check whether d.children exists. If it does, stash it on d._children and set d.children to null. If it does not, check d._children and restore it. Then re-run the layout and transition. The subtlety is what happens to the enter and exit sets. Entering nodes (just restored from _children) have no position in the current layout. If you let them start at (0, 0), they fly in from the top-left corner. Instead, initialize their position at the parent's stashed coordinates (source.x0, source.y0). They emerge from the parent and fan out to their computed positions. Exiting nodes do the reverse: they converge on the parent's new position and then get removed from the DOM.

This stashing is what d.x0 = d.x; d.y0 = d.y; does at the end of each update. Every node remembers where it was, so the next transition can use that as the starting point for entering children and the ending point for exiting ones.

Pre-collapse vs Show All

A common design question: should the tree start fully expanded or collapsed to a certain depth? The answer depends on the ratio of interesting nodes to total nodes. If your tree has 300 nodes and the viewer needs to find one of them, showing all 300 makes the starting state overwhelming. Collapsing to depth 2 means the viewer sees maybe 15 nodes and can selectively explore.

But if your tree has 20 nodes total and the structure itself is the point (say, an organizational chart), pre-collapsing hides the very thing the viewer came to see. Show everything and skip the interaction entirely.

The heuristic: pre-collapse when the expanded tree would show more than about 30 visible nodes. The precise number depends on the layout, but 30 is roughly where a node-link tree stops fitting on a typical screen without scrolling. For treemaps and sunbursts, the threshold is higher because those layouts use space more efficiently.

Pre-collapse to the shallowest depth that shows the tree's top-level structure without overwhelming the viewer. For most trees, that means depth 1 or 2. Let interaction reveal the rest.

Zoomable Treemap

Expand/collapse works well for node-link trees, but treemaps need a different interaction model. In a treemap, every node is a rectangle nested inside its parent. There are no visible links to collapse. Instead, the natural interaction is zoom: click a cell to make it fill the entire view, revealing its children at a readable size. Click a breadcrumb to zoom back out.

The technique behind this is the scale-domain swap. You compute the treemap layout once. Every cell gets fixed coordinates (x0, y0, x1, y1) in the layout's coordinate space. To zoom into a cell, you narrow the x and y scale domains to that cell's bounds. All descendants rescale automatically because their positions map through the same scales. No need to recompute the layout. The transition is just the scales changing their domain, and every rect follows.

The treemap below shows the same file system data as Figure 1. Click any folder cell to zoom into it. Its children expand to fill the space. The breadcrumb trail tracks your position; click any ancestor to zoom back out.

Figure 2. A zoomable treemap of the same file system. Click a folder to zoom in; its children fill the available space. Breadcrumbs above track the zoom path. Click any breadcrumb to zoom back out.

Without clipping, cells overshoot their parent's bounds during the transition. The linear interpolation of old and new positions does not respect containment at intermediate frames. In a production implementation, apply a clipPath to each group and transition its dimensions alongside the cell rect. The figure above uses opacity fading to sidestep this: non-descendant cells fade out while the target's children fade in, avoiding the visual artifact.

Zoomable Sunburst

A sunburst represents the hierarchy as concentric rings. The root is the center; each level adds an outer ring. Clicking an arc makes it the new root: its angular span expands to fill the full circle, and its children become the visible rings. The parent arcs shrink to zero and disappear.

The implementation differs from treemaps in a key way. You cannot just swap scale domains because arcs are parameterized by angles and radii, not rectangles. Instead, you compute target angles and radii for every node relative to the clicked node, then interpolate from current to target using attrTween. Each frame calls the arc generator with the interpolated parameters. This is why sunburst zoom uses d3.interpolate on the numeric properties and regenerates the path string, rather than interpolating the path string directly (which would produce garbled arcs at intermediate frames).

One trade-off specific to sunburst zoom: the part-to-whole comparison that justifies using a sunburst in the first place breaks down after zooming. When a child arc fills the entire circle, the viewer can no longer see how it relates to its siblings or its grandparent. If the viewer's task is "compare the size of these two branches," a zoomable treemap preserves rectangular proportions better. Sunburst zoom works best for pure drill-down navigation where the viewer wants to explore one path at a time.

Focus + Context

Zooming solves the detail problem but creates a context problem. Once you zoom into a subtree, the rest of the hierarchy vanishes. The viewer cannot see where they are within the larger structure. Two patterns address this.

Breadcrumbs are the simplest form of context. They show the path from root to the current focus as a clickable trail. Both figures above use them. Breadcrumbs solve the "where am I?" problem but not the "what else exists?" problem. The viewer knows their position in the tree but not what sibling branches they have not explored.

Minimap solves the broader context problem. A small rendering of the full tree sits in the corner of the figure, with the current viewport or focused subtree highlighted. The viewer can see the entire structure at a glance and understand which region they are examining. The navigation page uses this pattern for pan+zoom scatter plots, but it applies equally to hierarchies. For a collapsible tree, the minimap would show all nodes (expanded and collapsed) as tiny dots, with the currently visible region outlined.

The challenge with minimaps for trees is that they require a second layout computation at a different scale, and the minimap must update whenever the main view changes. For very large trees (500+ nodes), the minimap itself becomes a dense blob where individual nodes are indistinguishable. At that point, it functions more as a scroll position indicator than a structural overview, which is still useful but less informative.

Fisheye distortion is a third option: magnify the focused region while compressing but not hiding the surroundings. It preserves global context at the cost of distorting proportions. This works better for network layouts than for strict hierarchies, because trees have a strong spatial structure that fisheye distortion breaks (parent-child links bend in confusing ways).

Animated Expand/Collapse with Position Stashing

The animation model for expand/collapse relies entirely on position stashing. After every layout update, each node records its current position as d.x0 and d.y0. On the next update, entering nodes initialize at their parent's stashed position and transition to their computed layout position. Exiting nodes transition from their current position to their parent's new position, then get removed.

This creates the visual effect of children growing out of their parent on expand and shrinking back into it on collapse. Without stashing, entering nodes would start at (0, 0) or at their last known position from a previous expand, causing them to fly in from unpredictable locations.

Duration matters more than people think. The default 400ms used in Figure 1 feels natural for toggling a single node. But navigating from depth 0 to depth 3 requires three clicks and three 400ms transitions, totaling 1.2 seconds of animation the viewer must wait through. For deep trees, consider reducing the duration to 200-250ms per transition, or implementing multi-level expand (click with a modifier key to expand an entire subtree at once rather than one level at a time).

Transition duration should scale inversely with interaction frequency. If the viewer will click many times in rapid succession, keep individual transitions short. A single dramatic reveal can be slow; a navigation sequence cannot.

When This Skill Hurts

Lost context. Zooming into a subtree hides the rest of the hierarchy. The viewer forgets what else exists. The skill encourages zoom as a primary interaction pattern but does not always pair it with context mechanisms. After two zoom levels into a treemap, the viewer has no idea what other top-level branches exist, how large they are, or where the current focus sits within the whole. Breadcrumbs help with "where am I?" but not "what am I missing?" Always pair zoom with at least breadcrumbs; for complex hierarchies, consider a minimap or a persistent overview panel.

Animation as delay. Expand/collapse transitions that take 500ms each add up. Navigating four levels deep in a collapsible tree requires four clicks and four transitions: two full seconds of waiting. An LLM using this skill tends to apply its default durations (400-750ms) without considering the cumulative interaction cost. For exploration-heavy tasks where the viewer will toggle dozens of nodes, those durations should drop to 150-250ms. For a single dramatic reveal (e.g., expanding the root on page load), a longer duration is fine. The cost is proportional to the number of transitions the viewer must sit through, not the duration of any single one.

Click ambiguity. Does clicking a node expand it, zoom into it, or select it? This skill describes all three patterns: toggle for collapsible trees, zoom for treemaps and sunbursts, and selection for linked views. An LLM sometimes combines them on the same click target, creating interactions where clicking a tree node both expands it and zooms the viewport and updates a detail panel. The viewer cannot predict what will happen, and there is no way to do just one of the three. Keep click semantics singular. If you need multiple actions, use modifiers (shift-click to select, plain click to expand) or separate interaction targets (click the circle to toggle, click the label to select).

State management complexity. A zoomable, collapsible hierarchy with breadcrumbs, a minimap, and linked detail views requires tracking: which nodes are expanded, which are collapsed, the current zoom focus, the breadcrumb path, the minimap viewport, and any selection state. Each interaction must update multiple pieces of state consistently. The skill describes these patterns individually but understates how they compose. In practice, this state management becomes the majority of the code, and bugs manifest as desynced breadcrumbs, stale minimaps, or zoom targets that no longer exist because the node was collapsed. Start with the simplest interaction that serves the viewer's task. Add layers only when the simpler version demonstrably fails.
