Color

Color is an encoding channel, not decoration. The wrong palette can misrepresent your data or become invisible to 8% of male viewers.

Most people reach for color first when building a chart. It feels expressive, it looks polished, and rainbow gradients are satisfying. But color is treacherous. The human visual system does not perceive color uniformly: we are far more sensitive to lightness differences than to hue shifts, and roughly 8% of males have some form of color vision deficiency that collapses red-green distinctions entirely. A palette that "looks good" to the designer can quietly lie about the data or lock out a significant fraction of the audience.

The core principle is simple: color should encode data, not decorate it. A sequential palette should produce a monotonic lightness ramp so that "darker means more" is unambiguous. A diverging palette needs a perceptual midpoint. A categorical palette needs colors that remain distinct under color vision deficiency. The figures below make these tradeoffs visible.

Quick test for any sequential palette: convert it to grayscale. If you can still read the ordering, the palette works. If you cannot, the palette is relying on hue differences your audience may not share.

What Goes Wrong with Rainbow

Rainbow (and its cousin "jet") maps hue linearly across the data range. The problem is that hue is not perceived linearly. The yellow band is a narrow spike of high perceived lightness; the transition from blue to cyan creates an artificial boundary that draws the eye to a data threshold that does not exist. Researchers have documented how rainbow palettes cause viewers to see four or five discrete bands in smoothly varying data. Viridis, designed by Stéfan van der Walt and Nathaniel Smith, fixes this by varying lightness monotonically while keeping the palette colorblind-safe.

Perceptual Uniformity: Lab and HCL

RGB is a hardware space. It describes what voltages the monitor needs, not what the viewer sees. Interpolating between two colors in RGB often produces muddy, desaturated midpoints because the straight line through the RGB cube passes through a perceptual dead zone. The classic example: interpolating from blue to yellow in RGB passes through gray, while the same interpolation in Lab or HCL stays chromatic the entire way.

Lab (also called CIELAB) was designed so that a unit step in any direction corresponds to roughly the same perceived difference. The L channel is lightness, a and b are chromatic axes. HCL is the cylindrical form of Lab, recasting a and b into Hue, Chroma, and Lightness. D3 provides d3.interpolateLab and d3.interpolateHcl for this reason. When you build a two-stop gradient for a sequential scale, using Lab or HCL ensures that evenly spaced data values produce evenly spaced perceived differences.

There is a practical caveat: HCL interpolation through a neutral midpoint (white, gray, or black) hits an undefined hue. The chroma drops to zero, and the hue becomes meaningless, so the interpolator can jump unpredictably. This matters for diverging scales that pass through a light gray center. D3's built-in diverging interpolators handle this case correctly, but if you build your own two-stop diverging interpolation in HCL, you will hit this bug. For the neutral zone, fall back to Lab.

OKLab and its cylindrical form OKLCH (Bjorn Ottosson, 2020) improve on Lab by fixing a longstanding hue-shift problem in the blue-purple range. OKLCH is now native in CSS (oklch(70% 0.15 240)), which makes it the best choice for generating categorical palettes programmatically. You can space hues evenly in OKLCH and the results actually look even, something that HCL only approximates.

Rule of thumb: use D3's built-in HCL interpolation for standard two-stop gradients and diverging scales. Switch to OKLCH (via CSS or the Culori library) when you need to generate palettes from scratch with evenly spaced hues.

Alpha, Overdraw, and Compositing

When thousands of semi-transparent points overlap, the resulting saturation encodes local density. This is not an accident to correct; it is a signal to design for. The relationship between per-element alpha, overlap count, and resulting opacity follows a simple formula: result = 1 - (1 - alpha)^n, where alpha is the opacity of each element and n is the number of overlapping elements. An alpha of 0.05 with 50 overlapping points produces a combined opacity of about 0.92. That same alpha with only 3 overlaps gives 0.14. The curve is nonlinear, which means sparse regions stay faint while dense clusters punch through to full saturation.

Getting the alpha right requires knowing your data. A useful heuristic is alpha = clamp(100 / data.length, 0.01, 0.8), but this only works when density is roughly uniform. If your scatter plot has one dense cluster and a long sparse tail, the dense region will saturate to a solid blob while the sparse points become invisible. For that situation, use Canvas compositing with globalCompositeOperation = "lighter" on a black background: additive blending keeps accumulating brightness without clamping, so you can resolve structure inside the dense core and still see isolated points.

The figure below demonstrates how different alpha values interact with overlapping points. Each panel shows the same spatial distribution of 600 semi-transparent circles, but with different per-element alpha. Low alpha reveals density gradients beautifully but makes sparse regions hard to see. High alpha saturates quickly, collapsing all density information into a solid mass. The sweet spot depends on the maximum overlap in your specific dataset.

On Canvas, globalAlpha stacks multiplicatively with the alpha in rgba(). Setting globalAlpha = 0.5 and filling with rgba(70, 130, 180, 0.5) gives an effective alpha of 0.25, not 0.5. This is a common source of confusion when porting SVG code (where opacity and fill-opacity also stack) to Canvas. Pick one mechanism and set the other to 1.

Dark Mode

Inverting a palette designed for a white background does not produce a good dark-mode palette. The problem runs deeper than lightness. On a white background, darker values carry more visual weight, so "darker means more" is intuitive for sequential scales. On a dark background, that convention breaks: dark values merge with the background, and bright values carry the weight. A sequential scale on a dark background needs to run from dim to bright, not from bright to dark.

For categorical palettes, a simple HCL lightness inversion (c.l = 100 - c.l) works as a starting point but usually needs manual adjustment. Colors that were dark and saturated become pale and washed out; colors that were light become illegibly dark. Paul Tol's Vibrant palette was designed specifically for dark backgrounds and avoids these problems entirely.

The safest approach is to maintain two separate palette definitions rather than computing one from the other. Match each light-mode color to a dark-mode counterpart by hand, testing both against their respective backgrounds. This costs more upfront but avoids the subtle failures that automated inversions produce. For sequential scales, d3.interpolateMagma and d3.interpolateInferno read well on dark backgrounds without modification because their lightness trajectories already run from dark to bright.

Test dark-mode palettes on actual dark backgrounds, not by imagining them. A color that looks "bright enough" in a design tool swatch can disappear against a dark gray or navy background at small sizes. The prefers-color-scheme media query lets you serve different palettes per OS setting.

Wide Gamut

Display P3 covers roughly 50% more colors than sRGB, mostly extending the range of saturated reds, greens, and oranges. For data visualization, this matters in one specific situation: categorical palettes that need maximum chroma separation. The wider gamut gives you more room to place colors that are perceptually distinct, especially in the red-green region where sRGB runs out of space.

For sequential and diverging scales, wide gamut is less useful. These scales rely on lightness variation, not chroma extremes, and the extra gamut adds little. The effort of supporting both sRGB fallbacks and P3 primaries is rarely justified unless you are building a categorical palette for a known P3 display (most Apple devices since 2016, many modern Android phones, and increasingly laptop panels).

To use P3 in Canvas, request the color space at context creation: canvas.getContext("2d", { colorSpace: "display-p3" }). For SVG, use oklch() in style attributes and let the browser render P3-level chroma natively. You can detect support with window.matchMedia("(color-gamut: p3)").matches and fall back to sRGB hex colors for older displays. Always define your sRGB fallback first and enhance for P3, not the other way around.

WCAG Contrast for Text on Color

Placing text labels on colored chart elements (bar labels, choropleth annotations, legend text) is a contrast problem. WCAG 2.2 defines minimum contrast ratios: 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text (18pt or 14pt bold). In practice, most chart labels fall into the "normal text" category, so 4.5:1 is the target. The formula computes relative luminance from the sRGB values of the text and background colors, then takes the ratio of the lighter to the darker (with a 0.05 offset to avoid division by zero).

WCAG 2's contrast formula is symmetric: it gives the same ratio whether you test light-on-dark or dark-on-light. This is a known limitation. In reality, light text on a dark background is harder to read than dark text on a light background at the same measured ratio, especially at small sizes. The APCA (Advanced Perceptual Contrast Algorithm), a candidate for WCAG 3.0, addresses this with an asymmetric formula that produces different scores depending on polarity. For chart annotations on dark backgrounds, APCA is more accurate, though WCAG 2.2 remains the legal standard as of early 2026.

A practical function for choosing black or white text on an arbitrary background color: compute the relative luminance of the background and pick white text if luminance is below 0.4, black text otherwise. For colored text on colored backgrounds (which you should generally avoid in charts), compute the full contrast ratio and reject any pairing below 4.5:1.

Do not rely on color alone to convey information and then place text on that same color as the only fallback. Redundant encoding (position, shape, texture) means the text label is supplementary, not critical. When the label is the only way to decode the mark, contrast requirements are non-negotiable.

When This Skill Hurts

Failure modes to watch for

Rainbow and jet palettes. LLMs still generate rainbow and jet color scales despite years of published warnings. The problem is that rainbow "looks good" in a superficial sense, and training data is full of examples using it. The perceptual non-uniformity creates false contour lines in smoothly varying data, the yellow band draws attention to an arbitrary data threshold, and the palette is hostile to colorblind viewers. If a generated visualization uses d3.interpolateRainbow or a manual red-yellow-green-blue gradient for sequential data, it is wrong. Replace it with Viridis, Magma, or Cividis.

Diverging palettes without a meaningful midpoint. A diverging palette (like RdBu or PRGn) encodes "above center" and "below center" as different hues. This only makes sense when the midpoint represents something real: zero in a change-from-baseline chart, the national average in a deviation map, a policy threshold in a compliance dashboard. LLMs apply diverging palettes to data that has no natural midpoint, like population counts or revenue, because the two-hue scheme looks more "interesting" than a single-hue sequential ramp. The result misleads: the viewer assumes the color break means something, but it is just the median or the scale midpoint, carrying no inherent significance.

Too many categorical colors. Human color discrimination tops out at about 6 to 8 categories for unrelated hues. Beyond that, viewers cannot reliably match legend swatches to marks, especially on scatter plots where marks are small and surrounded by other colors (simultaneous contrast shifts the perception). LLMs will happily assign 15 or 20 categorical colors because a palette like Tableau10 technically has 10 entries, and adding more is just a matter of extending the array. The correct response for high-cardinality categories is to group into "top N + other," or to switch to a different encoding like small multiples.

Dark mode inversion that breaks palettes. A naive c.l = 100 - c.l lightness inversion can produce colors that are out of gamut, desaturated, or simply ugly. Bright yellows become murky dark colors that vanish on a dark background. Carefully tuned ColorBrewer sequential schemes lose their perceptual uniformity because the lightness mapping was optimized for the original direction. LLMs apply the mechanical inversion and report success because the code runs without errors. The visual result is a palette that no longer communicates the data faithfully. Use pre-built dark-mode palettes (Tol Vibrant, Magma, Inferno) or hand-match each color.
