Exploratory Design

The data already knows what it wants to be. Your job is to listen before you draw.

Most visualization tutorials start with a chart type. Bar chart tutorial, scatter plot tutorial, line chart tutorial. The skill assumes you already know what you want to build. But in practice, the first question is almost never "how do I make a bar chart?" It is "what should I make?"

Exploratory design inverts the usual order. Instead of picking a chart and fitting data into it, you look at the data first. You notice its columns, their types, how they relate. You try mapping different columns to different visual channels and watch what happens. Some assignments reveal structure. Others produce noise. The data's shape suggests its own representation.

Three Questions Before You Draw

The exploratory-design skill reduces the entire chart-selection problem to three questions asked in sequence. Each one narrows the space of reasonable choices. By the time you finish the third, the form has usually declared itself.

What is the data's natural form?

Before anything visual, look at the raw material. How many columns? What are their types? Is the data tabular rows of independent observations, or does it have hierarchical nesting, network edges, geographic coordinates, temporal ordering? A table of country-level GDP measurements over decades is fundamentally different from a social network graph, even if both happen to arrive as CSV files. The structure constrains the form. You would not represent a tree as a scatter plot, and you would not represent a bivariate correlation as a treemap.

Profiling the data means counting dimensions, checking distributions, looking for clusters and gaps, noting which columns are categorical and which are continuous. This is not glamorous work. It is the most important five minutes of any visualization project.

What comparison matters?

A single dataset can support dozens of valid visualizations, each answering a different question. Revenue by region is a different chart than revenue over time, even though both use the same revenue column. The question is not "what chart should I make?" but "what does the viewer need to compare?" Part-to-whole relationships call for stacked forms. Trends over time call for position along a shared axis. Correlations between variables call for scatter layouts. Rankings call for sorted bars.

Defining the comparison is an act of editorial judgment. You decide what matters. The data does not make that decision for you. But once you have made it, the data's types and the comparison's nature together eliminate most chart forms. You are not choosing from a universe of possibilities. You are recognizing which small handful of forms can actually support the comparison you need.

What is the simplest encoding that answers the question?

Once you know the comparison, look for the encoding that communicates it with the least visual machinery. If sorted bars answer the question, do not build a radial chart. If a single line shows the trend, do not add a second y-axis. The goal is the minimum encoding that makes the relevant pattern legible. Anything beyond that adds cognitive cost without adding information.

This is where most visualization goes wrong, and where LLMs go wrong most reliably. The instinct is to add more: more color, more axes, more interactivity. But each addition demands attention from the viewer. The simplest encoding is not the most boring one. It is the one where every visual element earns its place.

Matching Complexity to Data

A dataset with one numeric column and one categorical column calls for a bar chart. A dataset with three numeric columns and a time dimension might need coordinated views. The visualization's complexity should mirror the data's dimensionality, not exceed it.

When the data has one or two dimensions, a single chart type handles it. Position along one or two axes is the most powerful visual channel, and for low-dimensional data it is enough. When the data has three to five dimensions, you start needing secondary channels: color to encode a third variable, size for a fourth, perhaps faceting to split by a fifth. Beyond five dimensions, single-chart encodings break down. You move to coordinated views, small multiples, or dimensionality reduction. The jump from "one chart" to "multiple linked views" is not a smooth gradient. It is a phase transition in design complexity, and you should cross it deliberately, not accidentally.

The failure mode is building a five-dimensional encoding for two-dimensional data. A scatter plot colored by category, sized by a third variable, faceted by a fourth, and animated over a fifth looks impressive in a portfolio. But if the viewer's actual question is "which group has higher revenue," a bar chart answers it faster and with less ambiguity. Complexity that exceeds the data's own complexity is noise.

The Thinking Space

Before committing to code, work in a space where changing your mind costs nothing. Sketch on paper. Scribble on a whiteboard. Use a napkin. The medium does not matter. What matters is that the fidelity is low enough that you feel free to throw things away.

A hand-drawn rectangle with five rough bars takes ten seconds. It communicates the structure of the chart clearly enough to evaluate whether bars are the right form. A hand-drawn scatter plot with twelve dots and a circled cluster takes fifteen seconds. It is enough to test whether a scatter layout reveals the pattern you care about. These sketches are not deliverables. They are thinking tools. They let you iterate on the form before you have invested the thirty minutes it takes to write the D3 code.

The workbench above is a computational version of this thinking space. It lets you try different column-to-channel assignments instantly, without writing code. The cost of trying a wrong combination is one click. That low cost is the point. When trying the wrong thing is cheap, you try more things, and you discover forms you would not have considered if your first instinct had to survive a full implementation.

Thinking spaces share one property: the cost of a bad idea is nearly zero. Paper, whiteboards, dropdown selectors, thumbnail grids. The tool does not matter. The permission to be wrong is what enables discovery.

Encoding Channels: When to Use What

Visual encoding channels are not interchangeable. Decades of perceptual research, starting with Cleveland and McGill's 1984 ranking, have established a hierarchy of accuracy. Position along a common axis is the most precisely decoded channel. Length and angle come next. Area and color saturation are less accurate. Shape and texture are essentially categorical: viewers can distinguish a circle from a triangle, but cannot read quantitative differences from shape alone.

Position is the workhorse. It supports both categorical and quantitative data, and viewers decode it with high precision. Use position for the primary comparison. In a scatter plot, both axes carry the most important variables. In a bar chart, the bar height (position of the top edge) encodes the value.

Color is powerful but expensive. For categorical data, color distinguishes groups without consuming axis space. For sequential quantitative data, color can encode a third variable in a scatter plot. But color has hard limits: most viewers can distinguish about seven to ten categorical hues reliably, and quantitative color scales lose precision in the middle ranges. Color also fails first under accessibility constraints. If your chart relies on color for its primary message, roughly 8% of male viewers will struggle with it.

Size works for quantitative data when precision is not critical. Bubble charts map a third variable to circle area. The encoding is intuitive (bigger means more), but area comparison is imprecise. Viewers systematically underestimate differences in circle area. Use size when "roughly twice as big" is enough precision. Do not use size when the viewer needs to read exact values.

Shape is strictly categorical. Circles, squares, triangles, crosses. Shape distinguishes groups in a scatter plot where color is already occupied. But shape has a lower ceiling than color: more than four or five distinct shapes become hard to distinguish at small sizes, and shape does not work at all in dense layouts where marks overlap.

When This Skill Hurts

Failure modes to watch for

Skipping exploration entirely. LLMs associate data descriptions with chart types and jump straight to implementation. "Sales data over time" triggers a line chart. "Comparing categories" triggers a bar chart. The association is often correct, but the exploration step never happens. The three questions go unasked. The data might have a hierarchical structure that a flat bar chart ignores, or a bimodal distribution that a line chart hides. When the first instinct happens to be wrong, there is no process to catch it, because the process was skipped.

Analysis paralysis from open-endedness. The skill says "explore before you commit." For a model or a person with high conscientiousness, that invitation can become a trap. Every column combination gets tried. Every chart type gets considered. The exploration phase stretches on because nothing feels final. The fix is time-boxing: give yourself ten minutes to sketch five options, then pick one and move forward. Exploration that never converges into a decision is not design. It is procrastination with a methodology.

Exploratory charts becoming the final deliverable. The thinking-space sketch was meant to test an idea. It works well enough, so it ships. But exploratory artifacts have rough edges: unlabeled axes, default color palettes, no annotation, no title, missing legends. They communicate the pattern to the person who made them, because that person already knows the context. They do not communicate the pattern to anyone else. The gap between "I can see the trend" and "the viewer can see the trend" is annotation, polish, and editorial framing. Exploration and presentation are different phases, and the skill's emphasis on exploration can obscure the need for the second phase.

Confusing "explore the data" with "build a complex dashboard." The skill talks about coordinated views, interaction, and multi-dimensional encodings. An LLM reading this can interpret "exploration" as architectural complexity: linked scatter plots, cross-filtering histograms, brushable parallel coordinates. But exploration is a mindset, not an artifact. The most effective exploratory visualization is often a single, simple chart with one clear encoding. The complexity in the skill exists to handle genuinely high-dimensional data. Reaching for it when the data has two columns is not exploration. It is overengineering.
