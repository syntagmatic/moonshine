Ensemble Auditing

Using multiple auditors to produce scores you can trust

The auditor scores a 7.2. Is that good? What if it scored 6.8 yesterday? Same block, same prompt, same model. The only difference is the random seed inside the language model. That 0.4-point swing is not a rounding error. It is the auditor disagreeing with itself.

Run the same evaluation ten times and you get a distribution, not a number. Our measurements put the standard deviation at roughly 0.25 points on a 10-point scale, which means a single evaluation can easily land anywhere in a range of about 1.0 around the true quality. Most of the time, that noise washes out. But when a block sits near a decision boundary, the noise becomes the decision. 

block "radial-tree-nav"
  evaluation 1:  composite 7.2   (keep, above -0.3 threshold)
  evaluation 2:  composite 6.8   (discard, below -0.3 threshold)

  same block, different luck

The regression threshold in autoresearch is -0.3. If an experiment's composite drops by more than 0.3 from the previous best, it gets discarded. With measurement noise of plus or minus 0.5, a block that genuinely improved by 0.1 has a real chance of getting thrown away because the second score drew from the low end of its distribution while the first drew from the high end.

The core problem is not that scores are wrong. It is that a single sample from a noisy distribution gets treated as a precise measurement. A composite of 7.2 does not mean the block is exactly 7.2 quality. It means the block is probably somewhere between 6.7 and 7.7, and the auditor happened to land on 7.2 this time.

The fix: ask more than once

The simplest version of ensemble auditing is to run the same evaluation multiple times and take the median. Three evaluations instead of one. The median of {6.8, 7.2, 7.0} is 7.0. That is a better estimate than any single number in the set. The variance of the median shrinks by roughly a factor of the ensemble size, so three evaluations reduce the effective noise from 0.5 to about 0.3. Not perfect, but enough to stop most spurious decision flips.

This is the same-model, multiple-runs strategy. It costs 3x, it requires no new infrastructure, and it catches the most common failure mode: the auditor having a bad roll.

But same-model ensembles only fix random variance. They do not fix systematic bias. If Claude consistently underscores radial layouts because its training data had few of them, three Claude evaluations will converge on the same wrong answer. You need different perspectives for that.

Four strategies

Strategy What varies Catches Cost
Same model, N runs Random seed Random variance Nx base
Different models Model weights Model-specific bias Nx base (varies by model)
Different prompts Audit focus Dimension blindness Nx base
Calibrated ensemble All of the above + weights Everything above Nx base + calibration data

Different models means sending the same block to Claude, Gemini, and GPT. Each model has its own aesthetic priors and failure modes. When all three agree, you have high confidence. When they disagree by more than a point, the block is in genuinely ambiguous territory. That disagreement is information, not noise.

Different prompts means keeping the same model but changing the audit's focus. One prompt asks the auditor to concentrate on color and typography. Another focuses on layout and whitespace. A third evaluates interaction responsiveness. Instead of one generalist reviewer, you get three specialists. The specialists often catch problems the generalist glosses over, because a prompt that says "focus on color" actually forces the model to spend tokens on color rather than spreading its attention across everything.

Calibrated ensemble takes it further. Track each auditor's historical accuracy against some ground truth (human ratings, or a held-out test set). If Gemini consistently overscores stress_test by 0.5 and Claude is well-calibrated on visual_critic, weight their contributions accordingly. This requires historical data, which means it only works once you have enough labeled examples to build the calibration table.

Disagreement as signal

When three auditors score a block 7.5, 7.3, and 7.1, the block is clearly a 7.3. But when they score 8.2, 6.1, and 7.4, something interesting is happening. The spread is 2.1 points. That is not noise. That is genuine ambiguity about the block's quality.

High disagreement usually means one of three things. The block does something unusual that one model appreciates and another penalizes. Or the block has a real flaw that only one auditor notices. Or the block's quality depends heavily on which dimension you prioritize, and the auditors are weighting dimensions differently.

In any of these cases, auto-deciding is risky. A useful heuristic: when the ensemble spread exceeds 1.0 point, flag the block for human review instead of making the keep/discard call automatically. The human can look at the auditor notes, examine the screenshot, and make a judgment that accounts for the ambiguity.

Disagreement is the most valuable output of an ensemble. A single auditor gives you a number. Three auditors that agree give you a confident number. Three auditors that disagree give you a question worth investigating.

The cost-accuracy tradeoff

Ensemble auditing multiplies cost linearly. Three evaluations at $0.03 each cost $0.09 per experiment. Over 100 experiments, that is $9 instead of $3. Over 1,000, it is $90 instead of $30. The question is whether the accuracy improvement justifies the spend.

Consider what a wrong decision costs. A false discard throws away a genuine improvement. The proposer has to rediscover that improvement in a later iteration, which costs its own API calls. A false keep accepts a regression, which the next iteration has to fix. Both waste cycles downstream. If wrong decisions cost an average of 2 extra iterations, and each iteration costs $0.15 (proposer + auditor), then preventing one wrong decision saves $0.30. An ensemble that prevents even one wrong decision per 10 experiments pays for itself.

The math depends on your noise level, your threshold, and how often blocks sit near the decision boundary. In our experiments, roughly 15% of blocks are within 0.3 points of the threshold on any given evaluation. Those are the experiments where ensemble auditing changes the outcome. For the other 85%, the single auditor gets it right, and the ensemble just confirms.

Risks of ensemble auditing. Cost scales linearly with ensemble size, and the benefit has diminishing returns past 3 to 5 evaluations. Model disagreement can be paralyzing: if you flag every high-spread block for human review, you may end up reviewing 20% of experiments manually, which defeats the purpose of automation. Calibration requires historical data that may not exist for new block types or new audit dimensions. And ensembles can develop a committee conservatism where novel designs get penalized because they confuse the majority of auditors. A radial force-directed layout that genuinely works might get a spread of 2.0 not because it is ambiguous but because it is unfamiliar.

When to use it

Ensemble auditing is not always worth the overhead. Use it when: the regression threshold is tight (below 0.5), you are iterating on blocks near the quality boundary, or you are running a long optimization where cumulative wrong decisions compound. Skip it when: blocks are clearly high- or low-quality, the cost per evaluation is high relative to your budget, or you are in an early exploration phase where precision matters less than coverage.

The simplest starting point is three runs of the same model with median aggregation. It costs 3x, requires zero new infrastructure, and handles the most common failure mode. Upgrade to multi-model or multi-prompt ensembles only when you have evidence of systematic bias in the single-model approach.
