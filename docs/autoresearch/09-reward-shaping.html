Reward Shaping

Designing objective functions for the autoresearch iterate loop

The composite score is a lie you tell yourself to make a scalar out of a vector.

Four dimensions go in: visual_critic at 0.30, encoding_integrity at 0.25, cognitive_load at 0.25, stress_test at 0.20. One number comes out. The system compares this number to the previous number, checks whether the delta exceeds -0.3, and decides: keep or discard. Simple. Legible. And fundamentally lossy.

Consider what the composite hides. A change that raises visual_critic from 6 to 8 while dropping stress_test from 8 to 5 will produce a composite delta near zero. The composite says "nothing changed." The reality is that something changed quite a lot, in two opposite directions, and the weighted sum happened to cancel them out. The system kept the experiment. A human reviewer would have questions.

The problem with a single number

Fixed weights encode a permanent opinion about which dimensions matter most. But "which dimension matters most" depends entirely on where the block currently stands. A block scoring 4 on visual_critic and 9 on stress_test has a clear weakness. Improving visual quality by 2 points is far more valuable than improving stress_test by 2 points. The fixed-weight composite treats them as nearly equal (0.30 vs 0.20 weight).

Worse, the composite invites dimension collapse: the optimizer learns to pump one high-weighted dimension at the expense of others. If visual_critic carries 30% of the score, an experiment that gains 1 point on visual quality but loses 0.5 on encoding integrity will show a positive composite delta. Over many rounds, the block drifts toward a local optimum that looks good on one axis and progressively worse on the rest. 

current weights:
  0.30 x visual_critic       # highest weight, most pumpable
  0.25 x encoding_integrity
  0.25 x cognitive_load
  0.20 x stress_test         # lowest weight, most neglectable

Three alternatives

There are at least three ways to build a better objective function. Each solves a different failure mode, and each introduces its own costs.

1. Pareto dominance

Instead of reducing the four dimensions to a scalar, treat the score as a vector. An experiment is Pareto-dominant if it improves at least one dimension without worsening any. If it improves visual quality but degrades stress test robustness, it is not strictly better. It sits on a different point of the Pareto frontier: the set of all configurations where you cannot improve one dimension without hurting another.

Under Pareto selection, the system would keep an experiment only if it dominates the current state: every dimension at least as good, at least one strictly better. This prevents silent tradeoffs. The human sees the frontier and decides which tradeoff to accept.

2. Adaptive weighting

Keep the composite, but let the weights shift. The intuition: weight each dimension inversely to its current score. A dimension scoring 4/10 gets more weight than one scoring 9/10. The system automatically focuses on the weakest link. 

adaptive_weight(dim) = base_weight(dim) * (10 - score(dim)) / normalizer

When visual_critic is at 4 and stress_test is at 9, the adaptive weights might be 0.42 for visual and 0.08 for stress. The optimization pressure concentrates where the block needs it most. As visual quality climbs, its weight drops, and the next-weakest dimension takes priority. This is a soft version of curriculum learning.

The formula in full: weight_i = (10 - score_i) / sum(10 - score_j). A block scoring [4, 9, 7, 6] on its four dimensions has gaps of [6, 1, 3, 4]. The normalizer is 6+1+3+4 = 14. So the adaptive weights become [0.43, 0.07, 0.21, 0.29]. The dimension at 4 gets six times the weight of the dimension at 9. No base weights, no hyperparameters, just the raw gap from the ceiling. As a dimension climbs toward 10 its gap shrinks toward zero, and its weight converges to zero. The system cannot over-optimize a dimension that is already strong because the formula will not let it allocate budget there.

One subtlety: if all four dimensions score 10, the normalizer is zero and the formula is undefined. In practice this never happens because 10/10 scores are rare and the system would stop iterating before reaching that state. But the implementation needs a fallback: if the sum of gaps falls below some epsilon (say 0.5), revert to uniform weights.

3. Threshold constraints

Set hard floors. Stress_test must stay at 6 or above. Encoding_integrity must stay at 5 or above. Any experiment that drops a dimension below its floor is discarded, regardless of gains elsewhere. Above the floors, optimize freely using whatever objective you like.

This separates "acceptable" from "optimal." The floors prevent catastrophic regression in any single dimension. The optimization above the floors can be aggressive because the worst outcomes are gated out.

Curriculum: weakest-first scheduling

Curriculum learning takes adaptive weighting to its logical conclusion. Instead of blending all dimensions with shifting weights, focus on one dimension at a time. Identify the weakest dimension. Optimize for it specifically. Once it crosses a threshold, move to the next weakest.

This avoids the blurriness of a composite entirely. Each iteration has a single, clear objective. The proposer knows exactly what it is trying to improve. The audit knows exactly what to scrutinize. The decision function is simple: did the target dimension improve without the others collapsing?

The downside is scheduling. How do you pick the thresholds for advancing? What if improving dimension A necessarily hurts dimension B, so the curriculum oscillates between them? These are hyperparameters of the curriculum, and they themselves need tuning.

LOC as a proxy metric

Lines of code is the compaction track's primary gate. Code must get shorter. But shorter code and better code overlap without coinciding. A 200-line visualization can be worse than a 300-line one if the extra hundred lines are meaningful interaction code, accessible labels, or responsive layout logic. The LOC gate cannot tell the difference between removing dead code and removing necessary complexity.

The same proxy problem applies to every metric in the composite. A stress_test score of 8 means the evaluator found no rendering failures under the test conditions it checked. It does not mean the block is robust. The score is a proxy for robustness, not robustness itself. The composite is a proxy of proxies.

Asymmetric regression penalties

The current system applies a uniform -0.3 tolerance. But regressions are not equal. A 1-point drop in visual_critic is immediately visible to any viewer. A 1-point drop in stress_test may only manifest under edge-case data. A 1-point drop in encoding_integrity might mean a missing axis label that 80% of viewers will never notice.

An asymmetric penalty schedule would weight regressions differently: 

regression_penalty:
  visual_critic:       -0.15  # tight: regression is obvious
  encoding_integrity:  -0.25  # moderate
  cognitive_load:      -0.25  # moderate
  stress_test:         -0.40  # loose: less visible, noisier signal

This acknowledges that not all regressions are created equal. The system tolerates more noise in dimensions where the signal itself is noisy, while guarding dimensions where even small drops are perceptible.

Tradeoffs in every direction. Pareto optimization is slower to converge. It keeps more experiments because partial improvements are not rejected, but it also means less aggressive pruning. Adaptive weights can oscillate: improving the weakest dimension shifts the weights, making a different dimension weakest, pulling the optimizer back. Curriculum scheduling adds hyperparameters (thresholds, ordering rules) that themselves need tuning. There is no free lunch here. Each strategy trades one failure mode for another.

What changes in practice

The composite works. It has shipped 107 blocks through compaction. But it works the way a hammer works: reliably, bluntly, without knowing what it is hitting. The approaches described here are scalpels. They cut more precisely, but they require more setup and more judgment about where to cut.

If the autoresearch loop were running thousands of iterations per block, the composite would be fine. Noise averages out at scale. But the loop typically runs 5 to 15 iterations per block. At that scale, every decision matters. A single bad keep (dimension collapse) or bad discard (rejecting a Pareto improvement because the composite dipped) can shape the entire trajectory.

The practical path forward is probably layered: threshold constraints as hard gates, adaptive weighting as the optimization signal, Pareto tracking as a diagnostic. Not one strategy replacing the composite, but three strategies working together to compensate for its blind spots.

Validating a new reward function

Designing a better reward function is one problem. Proving it is better is a different problem, and harder. The natural approach is an A/B test: run 10 blocks through the iteration loop with the old composite, run 10 blocks with the new Pareto-based approach, and compare final quality. But what counts as "final quality"? The composite itself is the metric you are trying to improve on. Measuring the new reward by the old reward's yardstick defeats the purpose.

The protocol: select 10 blocks of comparable starting quality. Run each block through both reward functions, holding the proposer prompt, temperature, and iteration budget constant. The only variable is the keep/discard decision function. After both arms finish, compare the final artifacts on three axes: composite score (the old metric), dimension-floor compliance (the structural metric), and human preference (the ground truth).

The cleanest validation uses human judgment as ground truth. Take the final artifacts from both arms. Show them to a reviewer blind to which reward function produced them. Ask: which block is better? If the Pareto arm wins 7 out of 10 pairwise comparisons, you have evidence. If it wins 5 out of 10, you have noise. The human evaluation is expensive but it sidesteps the circularity of using one reward function to evaluate another.

A cheaper proxy is dimension-floor compliance. Count how many blocks in each arm finish with all four dimensions above 6. The composite frequently produces blocks that score 8 on one dimension and 4 on another because it optimizes the average. Pareto selection should produce fewer extreme imbalances. If the Pareto arm has 9/10 blocks with all dimensions above 6, and the composite arm has 6/10, that is a structural improvement even if the composite scores are similar.

The cost of knowing

Each experiment in the iteration loop costs roughly $0.10: a proposer call to generate the candidate, an audit call to score it, and occasionally a vision call for screenshot validation. Ten blocks times 10 experiments per arm times two arms is 200 experiments, so $20 for a single A/B comparison. That is cheap by the standards of ML research and expensive by the standards of "I want to test a one-line change to the keep/discard logic."

The cost pressure creates a temptation to reduce the sample size. Five blocks per arm instead of 10. Eight experiments instead of 12. But the iterate loop has high variance: a single lucky proposer output can dominate a trajectory. With five blocks per arm, one outlier shifts the mean by 20%. The A/B comparison becomes a coin flip dressed up as evidence. Ten blocks per arm is the minimum for a claim worth acting on. Twenty would be better but costs $40 and a full afternoon of wall-clock time.

A compromise: run a small pilot (3 blocks per arm) to check for gross differences. If one arm is clearly worse, stop early. If the results are ambiguous, run the full 10-block comparison. This is not rigorous sequential testing, but it catches the obvious failures before committing the full budget.

What the A/B test actually measures

The A/B comparison reveals something beyond which reward function is "better." It reveals the shape of the optimization trajectory. Two reward functions that reach the same final quality via different paths produce different intermediate artifacts, different failure modes, and different demands on the iteration budget. A composite-driven trajectory might hit a high score quickly but bounce around as dimension collapse pulls it back. A Pareto-driven trajectory might climb slowly but monotonically, with fewer discards and less wasted budget.

This is why the trajectory plot matters. Comparing only final scores loses the process information. A reward function that reaches 7.5 in 8 experiments is more valuable than one that reaches 7.6 in 14, because the 6 extra experiments cost time and money. The right metric is not peak quality but quality per experiment.

The Pareto arm moves in smaller increments but almost never loses ground. Over 10 experiments, the composite arm spends less total (fewer experiments pass the gate, so the proposer gets called less often in a real budget-limited loop) while the Pareto arm spends more but produces more balanced final scores.

Amber risks

Every alternative reward function trades one failure mode for another. The composite's failure mode is well-understood: dimension collapse, silent tradeoffs, insensitivity to the block's current weaknesses. The alternatives introduce failure modes that are less familiar and harder to detect.

Pareto dominance is slow

Pareto selection keeps any experiment that improves at least one dimension without worsening any other. In principle this sounds conservative. In practice it means more experiments pass the gate. More accepted experiments means less aggressive pruning. The optimizer explores a wider region of the quality space but takes longer to converge. On a 10-experiment budget, the Pareto arm may still be climbing when the composite arm has already plateaued. If the iteration budget is tight (and at $0.10 per experiment, it usually is), the slower convergence translates directly into lower final quality or higher cost.

  • Accept rates under Pareto are typically 50-70%, vs. 30-45% under composite threshold.
  • Convergence takes 2-4 more experiments to reach the same composite score.
  • The Pareto arm produces more balanced scores but may not reach the same peak on any single dimension.

Adaptive weights can oscillate

The adaptive weighting formula recalculates weights after every accepted experiment. This creates a feedback loop. Suppose visual_critic is at 4 and stress_test is at 8. The formula concentrates weight on visual. The next experiment improves visual to 6. Now the gap between visual and stress has narrowed. The formula shifts weight toward whatever dimension is now weakest. If encoding_integrity was quietly sitting at 5.5, it becomes the new target. The next experiment pushes encoding up. But that experiment might slightly degrade visual (because the proposer was not optimizing for visual anymore). Visual drops to 5.5, and the formula swings weight back.

This oscillation is not catastrophic. The block still improves on average because each step is a net positive. But the trajectory zig-zags between dimensions rather than climbing steadily on all of them. In a 10-experiment budget, 2-3 experiments may be spent correcting regressions caused by the previous experiment's weight shift. That is 20-30% of the budget lost to oscillation overhead.

A damping factor helps: instead of recalculating weights from scratch each step, blend the new weights with the previous ones. weight_new = 0.7 * weight_calculated + 0.3 * weight_previous. This smooths the transitions but introduces another hyperparameter (the blend rate) that itself needs tuning.

Curriculum scheduling adds hyperparameters

Curriculum learning sounds elegant: focus on the weakest dimension, improve it past a threshold, move to the next. But "threshold" and "which dimension is next" and "what happens when two dimensions are tied" are all decisions that need values. Each value is a hyperparameter. The original composite has four weights and one tolerance. A full curriculum schedule has four thresholds, a tie-breaking rule, a fallback strategy for when no dimension is below threshold, and a transition policy (hard switch vs. gradual blend). That is at least eight hyperparameters, each of which interacts with the others.

Worse, the optimal curriculum depends on the starting block. A block that starts at [4, 4, 4, 4] benefits from focusing on any dimension. A block at [8, 4, 7, 6] needs the curriculum to target the clear weakness. A block at [7, 6, 6, 7] has no clear weakness and the curriculum has to pick one arbitrarily. A single fixed curriculum cannot serve all three cases well. The reward function now needs a meta-policy for selecting curricula, which is the reward shaping problem restated at a higher level.

Validation costs compound

Each of these alternatives needs its own A/B validation. Pareto vs. composite: $20. Adaptive vs. composite: $20. Curriculum vs. adaptive: $20. Testing a damping factor at three settings: $60. The menu of possible reward functions grows faster than the budget for testing them. Without discipline, reward shaping becomes its own infinite iterate loop: changing the objective function, testing it, finding an edge case, changing it again. The composite's greatest virtue may be that it is boring enough to leave alone.
