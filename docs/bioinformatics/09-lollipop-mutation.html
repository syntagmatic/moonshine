Lollipop Mutation Plot

Mapping mutations onto a protein's linear sequence to see where damage clusters and why certain positions matter more than others.

A protein is a long chain of amino acids, folded into a three-dimensional shape. But when we want to ask where a mutation falls, we often unfold the protein back into a line. The lollipop mutation plot does exactly this: the x-axis is amino acid position, colored blocks along the bottom mark functional domains, and vertical stems topped with circles show where mutations occur and how frequently.

TP53 is the most frequently mutated gene in human cancer. Its protein product, p53, is 393 amino acids long and acts as a transcription factor that activates genes like CDKN1A in response to DNA damage. When p53 carries the R175H mutation in its DNA-binding domain, it loses the ability to bind DNA and activate its targets. But where exactly does R175H sit, and why is it so consequential?

TP53 Hotspot Mutations

The plot below shows the full TP53 protein. Notice how the tallest lollipops cluster in the DNA-binding domain (blue) rather than being spread evenly. This is not random: the DNA-binding domain is the business end of p53, and mutations there directly disable its tumor-suppressing function. R175H, highlighted in red, is the single most common TP53 mutation across all human cancers.

Figure 1. TP53 lollipop mutation plot. Lollipop height encodes mutation frequency across patients. The R175H hotspot is highlighted in red. Hover any lollipop for details. Click a domain block to zoom in (Figure 2).

Six residues — R175, G245, R248, R249, R273, R282 — account for roughly 30% of all TP53 missense mutations. Five of the six directly contact DNA or stabilize the structure that contacts DNA. Evolution has optimized this protein so tightly that even a single amino acid change at a critical position is enough to abolish function.

CDKN1A: The Target That Doesn't Need Mutating

If TP53 is the general, CDKN1A is the soldier it sends to halt the cell cycle. CDKN1A encodes p21, a 198 amino acid protein with two functional domains: a CDK-binding domain that blocks cell division and a PCNA-binding domain that stalls DNA replication. You might expect cancer to mutate p21 just as aggressively as p53. It doesn't.

Figure 3. CDKN1A lollipop plot at the same frequency scale as Figure 1. The protein is shorter, simpler, and far less mutated. Cancer silences p21 by breaking TP53 upstream rather than mutating CDKN1A directly.

This is the key lesson of the TP53-CDKN1A axis: cancer follows the path of least resistance. Why mutate CDKN1A when you can knock out TP53 and silence CDKN1A along with dozens of other tumor-suppressing targets at the same time? One mutation, many consequences. The lollipop plots make this asymmetry visible at a glance.

Mutation Type Coloring

Not all mutations are created equal. A missense mutation swaps one amino acid for another — the protein is made but may be broken. A nonsense mutation introduces a premature stop codon, truncating the protein. A frameshift shifts the reading frame, scrambling everything downstream. Toggle between coloring schemes below to see that the TP53 hotspots are overwhelmingly missense: the protein is still produced, but it folds wrong or can't bind DNA.

Missense-dominant hotspots are a hallmark of oncogenic "gain of function." The R175H mutant p53 doesn't just lose its tumor-suppressing ability — it actively promotes cancer by binding and disabling other proteins. This is why missense mutations at these positions are selected for in tumors: a truncated protein would be less harmful than a corrupted one.

Proteins as One-Dimensional Maps

The lollipop plot's power lies in its simplicity. By unfolding a three-dimensional protein into a line, it makes mutation patterns legible at a glance. The clustering of hotspots in the DNA-binding domain, the asymmetry between TP53 and CDKN1A, the dominance of missense mutations at key residues — each of these observations maps directly onto a biological mechanism.
