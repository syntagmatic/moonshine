ChIP-seq Peak Visualization

Chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by sequencing: measuring where a protein binds the genome, and what happens when it can't.

The previous explainers asked which genes change expression, where they sit in the genome, and whether those changes predict survival. But gene expression is an effect. ChIP-seq asks about the cause: which transcription factors are physically bound to a gene's regulatory region, and are they still bound when mutated?

The technique works by crosslinking proteins to DNA, shearing the DNA into fragments, pulling down the protein of interest with an antibody, and sequencing the attached fragments. The result is a signal track: at each genomic position, a number reflecting how enriched that position is for the protein. Tall peaks mean strong binding.

Our question: TP53 is a transcription factor that binds the promoter of CDKN1A to activate it. The R175H mutation is the most common TP53 mutation in cancer. Can mutant TP53 still bind?

The Binding Evidence

Below is a simulated ~10 kb window around the CDKN1A transcription start site. The blue track shows wildtype TP53 ChIP-seq signal; the red track shows the R175H mutant. In the wildtype, a sharp peak rises directly over the promoter, exactly where TP53 is expected to bind. In the mutant, the signal is flat noise.

Figure 1. ChIP-seq signal at the CDKN1A locus. Blue: wildtype TP53 shows a strong binding peak at the promoter. Red: the R175H mutant shows no enrichment. Hover for position and signal value. The gene model below shows the exon/intron structure of CDKN1A.

This is the direct molecular evidence that R175H disrupts DNA binding. The mutation sits in TP53's DNA-binding domain and destabilizes its zinc-coordination structure. Without binding, TP53 cannot activate CDKN1A, and the cell loses a critical brake on proliferation.

The Epigenetic Cascade

Loss of TP53 binding doesn't just remove one signal. It triggers a cascade of chromatin changes. Activating histone marks disappear, and repressive marks accumulate. Below, four tracks are shown at the same locus. Toggle each track to see how the epigenetic landscape shifts from active to repressed.

Figure 2. Multiple ChIP-seq tracks at CDKN1A. TP53 binding is lost in the mutant. H3K4me3 (active promoter) is reduced. H3K27ac (active enhancer) disappears. H3K27me3 (repressive mark) accumulates. Toggle tracks on and off.

The epigenetic cascade explains why a single mutation has such outsized effects. Losing TP53 binding doesn't merely fail to activate CDKN1A; it allows repressive chromatin to spread across the locus, silencing the gene entirely and potentially affecting neighboring genes as well.

Peak Calling

Raw ChIP-seq signal is noisy. Peak calling is the computational step that distinguishes real binding events from background noise. A peak caller scans the signal, fits a statistical model, and assigns each candidate peak a significance score. Only peaks that exceed a threshold are called as real binding events.

Drag the threshold slider below. As you raise it, weaker peaks disappear. The TP53-WT peak at the CDKN1A promoter is strong and survives even stringent thresholds. The mutant's noise peaks vanish quickly. This illustrates why peak calling is essential: without it, you'd mistake noise for binding.

Figure 3. Peak calling at varying thresholds. Blue markers: WT peaks. Red markers: mutant peaks. Raise the threshold to see weak/spurious peaks disappear. The CDKN1A promoter peak in WT (marked with an arrow) survives even the highest threshold. The mutant never shows a significant peak there.

In practice, peak callers like MACS2 use a local background model and a Poisson test. The threshold is usually set at a false discovery rate (FDR) of 0.01 or 0.05. The result is a list of genomic coordinates where the protein binds, which can be intersected with gene annotations to find direct targets.

From Peaks to Biology

The three figures above tell a complete story. Figure 1 shows that mutant TP53 cannot bind the CDKN1A promoter. Figure 2 shows that this binding loss triggers an epigenetic cascade from activation to repression. Figure 3 shows that peak calling distinguishes real binding from noise with statistical rigor.

ChIP-seq is one of the workhorses of functional genomics. Combined with the other visualizations in this series, it connects the dots from sequence (genome browser) to regulation (ChIP-seq) to expression (heatmaps, volcano plots) to clinical outcome (survival curves).
