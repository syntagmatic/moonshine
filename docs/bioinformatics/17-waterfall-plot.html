Waterfall Plot

Which patients show the biggest CDKN1A drop when TP53 is mutated? Sort everyone by a value and let the pattern cascade.

Previous explainers in this series looked at TP53 as a gene with widespread effects and CDKN1A as one of its downstream targets. But how do individual patients line up? The waterfall plot answers this by ranking every sample in a cohort along the x-axis, creating a cascading bar chart that sorts from highest to lowest.

The waterfall's power is simplicity: sort by one measure, color by another, and the eye instantly catches whether those two variables are linked. If TP53-mutant samples cluster at one end of the CDKN1A expression waterfall, the relationship is visible without a single statistical test.

The Cascade

Below, 60 patients are sorted by their CDKN1A expression level (log2 scale). Each bar is one patient. Red bars are TP53-mutant; blue bars are TP53-wildtype. A horizontal dashed line marks the cohort median. Notice where the red bars concentrate.

Figure 1. CDKN1A expression waterfall for 60 patients, sorted descending. The dashed line marks the cohort median. TP53-mutant samples (red) cluster toward the low-expression end, consistent with TP53's role as a transcriptional activator of CDKN1A. Hover over any bar for patient details.

CDKN1A (also called p21) is a cell cycle inhibitor directly activated by TP53. When TP53 is mutated, it loses its ability to turn on CDKN1A transcription, so mutant tumors tend to have lower CDKN1A expression. The waterfall makes this population-level trend visible at the individual patient level.

A Different Dimension: Tumor Mutation Burden

The same 60 patients can be reordered by a completely different measure: tumor mutation burden (TMB), the total number of somatic mutations in each sample. TP53-mutant tumors often have higher TMB because loss of TP53 impairs DNA repair and genomic stability. Toggle between the two measures to see how the same patients reshuffle.

Figure 2. Toggle between CDKN1A expression (sorted descending) and TMB (sorted descending). TP53-mutant samples cluster at the low end of CDKN1A but at the high end of TMB. The same patients, two stories.

This is a common pattern in cancer genomics: a single driver mutation correlates with multiple downstream phenotypes. TP53 loss both reduces CDKN1A expression (a transcriptional effect) and increases mutation burden (a genomic stability effect). Waterfall plots help you see each dimension one at a time.

The Fisher's exact test evaluates whether the proportion of TP53-mutant patients is significantly different above versus below the threshold. When the threshold sits near the median, the enrichment is strongest because you capture the clearest separation between the two groups. As you drag the line toward extremes, the group sizes become unbalanced and statistical power drops.

Reading a Waterfall

A few principles for interpreting waterfall plots in practice:

Sort order is the story. The x-axis has no inherent meaning; it's just rank. What you sort by defines the question you're asking. The same cohort can produce many different waterfalls.

Color reveals association. If the colored bars cluster at one end of the waterfall, the categorical variable (here, TP53 status) is associated with the quantitative variable (CDKN1A expression). Random mixing means no association.

Outliers stand out. A few wildtype patients with very low CDKN1A might have other mutations affecting the same pathway. The waterfall makes these cases easy to spot for follow-up investigation.

Thresholds are choices. Where you draw the line between "high" and "low" affects your conclusions. The threshold explorer above demonstrates that enrichment depends on the cutoff. Report your threshold and justify it.
