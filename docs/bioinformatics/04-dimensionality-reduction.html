Dimensionality Reduction

When every cell is its own experiment: how UMAP, t-SNE, and PCA compress thousands of dimensions into a picture you can see.

Single-cell RNA sequencing measures gene expression in individual cells, not bulk tissue. A typical experiment profiles 5,000 to 50,000 cells, each described by the expression levels of 20,000+ genes. Each cell is a point in a 20,000-dimensional space.

No human can visualize 20,000 dimensions. Dimensionality reduction algorithms compress those dimensions down to two, producing a scatter plot where similar cells land near each other. Different cell types form distinct clusters.

Three Algorithms, One Dataset

Below is a simulated single-cell dataset: 600 cells from five cell types. Toggle between PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP to see how each algorithm arranges the same cells. The colors represent true cell type.

Figure 1. The same 600 cells visualized by three different algorithms. UMAP preserves both local and global structure. t-SNE emphasizes local neighborhoods. PCA preserves variance but can overlap distinct types.

No dimensionality reduction is "correct." Each makes trade-offs. PCA is linear and fast but can mix overlapping clusters. t-SNE separates clusters well but distorts distances between them. UMAP balances both and is currently the most popular for single-cell data.

What the Clusters Mean

In real experiments, the clusters don't come with labels. Biologists identify cell types by looking at which genes are highly expressed in each cluster. Click a cluster below to see its marker genes.

The Danger of Over-Reading

It's tempting to interpret every gap and distance in a dimensionality reduction plot. But many visual features are artifacts of the algorithm, not the biology:

  • The distance between clusters in t-SNE is meaningless. Two clusters far apart might be more similar than two clusters nearby.
  • The size of a cluster is partly an artifact of local density. t-SNE expands dense regions and compresses sparse ones.
  • Different random seeds produce different layouts. If a pattern only appears with one seed, it's not real.
seed: 42

Figure 3. Click "New random seed" to regenerate the embedding with different initialization. The clusters rearrange, but the same cells stay grouped. Absolute positions are arbitrary; relative groupings are meaningful.

The golden rule of dimensionality reduction: trust the clusters, not the coordinates. If cells consistently group together across seeds, that's a real biological signal. The spatial arrangement between clusters is negotiable.
