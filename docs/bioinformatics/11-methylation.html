DNA Methylation

Is CDKN1A silenced by promoter methylation in TP53-mutant tumors? Visualizing CpG-level epigenetic marks to connect mutation with transcriptional silencing.

In the previous explainers we saw that TP53 directly activates CDKN1A (p21), a critical cell-cycle inhibitor. When TP53 is mutated, CDKN1A loses its transcriptional activator. But there is a second, more permanent way a cell can silence a gene: DNA methylation.

A methyl group added to the 5-carbon of cytosine in a CpG dinucleotide does not change the DNA sequence, but it changes what the cell can read. When CpG sites in a promoter are heavily methylated, transcription factors cannot bind, and the gene goes silent. This is an epigenetic mechanism — heritable across cell divisions, but reversible in principle.

The question here is whether TP53-mutant tumors show elevated methylation at the CDKN1A promoter, suggesting a two-hit silencing model: loss of activator plus epigenetic lockdown.

CpG Methylation at the CDKN1A Promoter

Illumina methylation arrays (450K, EPIC) measure the beta value at each CpG probe — the ratio of methylated signal to total signal, ranging from 0 (fully unmethylated) to 1 (fully methylated). Below, each circle is one CpG site in the CDKN1A promoter region, positioned along the genomic coordinate with the beta value on the y-axis.

Methylation Heatmap

The lollipop plot shows group means, but tumors are not uniform. A heatmap lets us see individual samples. Each row is a tumor sample, each column a CpG site. Color encodes the beta value: blue for unmethylated, red for methylated. Samples are clustered by similarity, revealing whether TP53-mutant tumors form a coherent block of high methylation.

Figure 2. Methylation heatmap of 30 samples (15 TP53-WT, 15 TP53-mut) across 20 CpG sites. Hierarchical clustering groups samples with similar methylation profiles. The TP53-mutant samples cluster together, driven by elevated methylation at promoter-proximal CpGs. Hover for sample and CpG details.

The heatmap reveals heterogeneity within the TP53-mutant group: not every mutant tumor shows high methylation. Some mutant tumors remain unmethylated at CDKN1A, suggesting that methylation is one of several silencing mechanisms — not a universal consequence of TP53 loss.

Beta Value Distribution

To quantify the difference at the most differentially methylated CpG, we can look at the full distribution of beta values across all samples. Beta values are famously bimodal: in any given sample, a CpG tends to be either mostly unmethylated (beta near 0) or mostly methylated (beta near 1), with fewer intermediate values.

Figure 3. Distribution of beta values at the most differentially methylated CpG (cg07 near the TSS). Each dot is one sample. The violin shape shows the density estimate. TP53-wildtype samples cluster near 0 (unmethylated), while TP53-mutant samples are spread bimodally, with a subset reaching high methylation.

The bimodal pattern in TP53-mutant tumors is telling: some mutant tumors acquired methylation at this CpG and some did not. This suggests methylation is a stochastic event that is selected for in the absence of TP53 — a second hit that permanently locks down CDKN1A expression.

Reading Methylation Plots

Three practical points for interpreting methylation data:

Beta vs. M-value. Beta values (0–1) are intuitive but statistically awkward near the boundaries. The M-value (log2 of beta / (1 − beta)) has better statistical properties and is preferred for differential analysis, but beta is standard for visualization.

Probe context matters. A CpG in a promoter CpG island has different implications than one in a gene body or intergenic region. Gene-body methylation is often positively correlated with expression — the opposite of the promoter effect.

Confounders. Cell-type composition, batch effects, and tumor purity all affect measured methylation levels. A tumor sample is a mixture of tumor and normal cells, so observed beta values are a weighted average. Deconvolution methods attempt to correct for this.
