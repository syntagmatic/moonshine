Oncoprint

How often is TP53 altered across patients, and what happens to its target CDKN1A when it is?

The visualizations we have seen so far — heatmaps, volcano plots, survival curves — each answer a question about a single layer of data. But cancer genomics produces many layers per patient: mutations, copy-number changes, fusions, expression. The oncoprint, pioneered by cBioPortal, compresses all of these into a single dense grid.

Columns are patients. Rows are genes. Each cell encodes whether that gene is altered in that patient and what kind of alteration was found. The result is a visual that immediately shows which genes are most frequently hit, which patients carry the heaviest mutational burden, and — most importantly — which pairs of genes tend to be altered in a mutually exclusive pattern, hinting that they act in the same pathway.

The Grid

Below is an oncoprint for 60 simulated cancer patients across eight genes involved in the p53 and growth-factor signaling pathways. Each thin column is a patient. The colored inner rectangles indicate alteration type: missense, truncating, amplification, deep deletion, and fusion. A gray background means no alteration. Patients are sorted by TP53 status first, then by total alteration count.

Figure 1. Basic oncoprint grid. Rows are genes, columns are patients (sorted by TP53 status). The bar on the right shows alteration frequency for each gene. Hover any cell for details.

TP53 is the most frequently mutated gene in human cancer — altered in roughly 40-50% of patients in many tumor types. It encodes the "guardian of the genome," a transcription factor that activates DNA repair, cell-cycle arrest (via CDKN1A/p21), and apoptosis.

Mutual Exclusivity

If two genes serve the same function in a pathway, mutating both is redundant — one hit is enough. This produces a mutual exclusivity pattern in the oncoprint: when one gene is altered, the other is usually wild-type.

The classic example is TP53 and MDM2. MDM2 is the ubiquitin ligase that degrades p53 protein. Amplifying MDM2 destroys p53 function just as effectively as mutating TP53 directly. Patients rarely carry both alterations because a single hit is sufficient to disable the pathway.

Figure 2. Mutual exclusivity between TP53 and MDM2. Toggle sorting by different genes to reveal co-occurrence and exclusivity patterns. The highlighted rows show TP53 and MDM2 are rarely co-altered.

Mutual exclusivity is a strong signal for pathway membership. When two genes are mutually exclusive in an oncoprint, they likely participate in the same biological pathway — inactivating either one is sufficient to disrupt it. Co-occurrence, by contrast, suggests that both hits together are needed for a selective advantage.

TP53 Status and CDKN1A Expression

TP53 is a transcription factor. One of its most important targets is CDKN1A, which encodes the cell-cycle inhibitor p21. When TP53 is functional, it drives CDKN1A expression to halt the cell cycle. Mutations like R175H in the DNA-binding domain prevent TP53 from activating CDKN1A, so expression drops.

The linked view below places CDKN1A expression bars directly under each patient column of the oncoprint. The connection should be immediately visible: patients with TP53 alterations (left side) tend to have lower CDKN1A expression.

Figure 3. Linked oncoprint and expression view. Top: TP53 and CDKN1A alteration status. Bottom: CDKN1A mRNA expression for each patient column, colored by TP53 mutation status (mutant vs. wild-type). The dashed lines show group means.

This is the functional consequence of TP53 loss: without a working p53 protein, CDKN1A is not transcribed, p21 is not made, and the cell-cycle checkpoint fails. The cell divides when it should not. This is why TP53 mutations are so dangerous — they uncouple DNA damage from the brake pedal.

Reading Oncoprints

A few principles for interpreting oncoprints in the wild:

Column ordering matters. cBioPortal sorts columns to cluster alterations together, making patterns like mutual exclusivity visible. Different sort orders tell different stories.

Alteration frequency is the first thing to check. The sidebar bars show what fraction of patients carry each gene alteration. Genes above ~15-20% are interesting; below that, the sample size is usually too small for pattern detection.

Pair it with expression data. As we saw with TP53 and CDKN1A, the oncoprint tells you what is altered, but linking to expression tells you what happens as a result. The best analyses combine the two.
