Forest Plot

Is the TP53→CDKN1A link consistent across studies? A meta-analysis of expression changes when the guardian gene is broken.

We have seen in earlier explainers that TP53 mutations tend to reduce CDKN1A expression. But any single study can be misleading—small samples, tissue-specific biology, and technical variation all introduce noise. A meta-analysis pools results from multiple independent studies to ask: does the effect hold up?

The forest plot is the standard way to visualize meta-analytic results. Each row is a study. Each study reports an effect size—here, the log2 fold change of CDKN1A expression in TP53-mutant versus wild-type patients—along with a confidence interval. A negative value means CDKN1A is reduced in the mutant group. A diamond at the bottom shows the pooled estimate from all studies combined.

The meta-analysis

Figure 1. Forest plot of eight studies reporting CDKN1A log2 fold change in TP53-mutant versus wild-type tumors. Squares are proportional to study weight (inverse variance). The dashed line marks the null (no effect). Hover any study for details.

Most studies show CDKN1A expression is reduced when TP53 is mutated, but the magnitude varies. The pooled diamond sits firmly in negative territory, confirming the overall direction of the effect.

Sensitivity analysis

How robust is the pooled estimate? In a leave-one-out sensitivity analysis, we remove each study in turn and recalculate. If removing one study changes the conclusion, it might be driving the result on its own. Click any study below to exclude it.

Figure 2. Leave-one-out sensitivity analysis. Click any study row to toggle its inclusion. The pooled estimate and I² heterogeneity statistic update in real time. Excluding the outlier study changes I² substantially.

The I² statistic measures what fraction of the variation across studies is due to real heterogeneity rather than sampling error. Values above 50% suggest meaningful differences between studies. Watch how excluding certain studies shifts I².

Checking for publication bias

Journals are more likely to publish studies with significant results. This creates publication bias: if small, non-significant studies go unpublished, the meta-analysis overestimates the true effect. The funnel plot is a diagnostic tool. It plots each study's effect size against its precision (inverse of the standard error). In an unbiased literature, the points should scatter symmetrically around the pooled estimate, forming an inverted funnel shape.

Figure 3. Funnel plot for detecting publication bias. X-axis is effect size; y-axis is standard error (inverted so that more precise studies sit at the top). The dashed line is the pooled estimate. Asymmetry suggests missing studies on one side.

Egger's regression test formalizes the visual check. It regresses the standardized effect (effect / SE) against precision (1 / SE). A significant intercept implies asymmetry. In well-powered collections of studies, a p-value below 0.10 is conventionally considered evidence of funnel asymmetry.

Reading forest plots

The square size matters. Larger squares mean more precise studies (larger sample, tighter CI). They pull the pooled estimate toward them. Pay attention to which studies dominate.

The diamond is the bottom line. If the diamond does not cross the null line, the pooled effect is statistically significant. Its width shows the confidence interval of the pooled estimate—narrower is more precise.

Heterogeneity tells a story. High I² does not invalidate the analysis, but it means the true effect likely varies across settings. When you see high heterogeneity, the interesting question is why—is it tissue type, sample size, sequencing technology?
