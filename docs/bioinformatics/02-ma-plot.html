MA Plot

Which genes changed between conditions, and how an old statistical trick separates real signal from technical noise.

After running a gene expression experiment with two conditions (say, treated versus control), the first question is: which genes are different? You could simply compare the average expression in each group, but that approach has a problem. Genes with low overall expression are noisy, and small absolute differences in noisy genes look artificially large.

The MA plot solves this by transforming the data into two new axes:

  • M (minus) = log2(treated / control), the log fold change
  • A (average) = the mean expression across both conditions

This rotation separates how much a gene changed (vertical) from how much total signal it has (horizontal). Genes scattered at the top and bottom of the plot are the interesting ones.

The Scatter

Each dot below is one gene. The horizontal axis shows average expression (higher means more reliable). The vertical axis shows fold change (farther from zero means more change). Most genes cluster around M = 0, meaning they didn't change.

Figure 1. MA plot of 500 simulated genes. Drag the fold-change threshold to highlight genes above the cutoff. Hover any point for gene details.

The funnel shape is characteristic: at low expression (left), fold changes are noisy and spread wide. At high expression (right), estimates tighten. This is why bioinformaticians distrust large fold changes in low-expression genes.

Why Not Just Plot Treated vs. Control?

Here is the same data plotted as a simple scatter: control expression on one axis, treated on the other. Every gene on the diagonal didn't change. Genes off the diagonal did.

Figure 2. Direct comparison plot. The diagonal line is y = x (no change). Points above are upregulated, below are downregulated. Compare this to the MA view above.

This works, but it compresses the interesting differences. Most genes are near the diagonal, so the outliers are hard to see. The MA transformation stretches the differences vertically and compresses the boring middle, making differential genes stand out.

Brushing for Candidates

In practice, bioinformaticians brush or filter the MA plot to extract candidate gene lists. Brush a region below to select genes and see them in a table.

From MA to Volcano

The MA plot shows which genes changed and how much, but it doesn't directly show statistical confidence. Is that gene at M = 2 truly different, or just a noisy outlier? The volcano plot adds a second dimension: statistical significance.
