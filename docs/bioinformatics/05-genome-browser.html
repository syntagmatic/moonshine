Genome Browser

Where genes live on a chromosome: stacked tracks layer annotations, expression, and conservation along a linear coordinate axis.

A genome browser is the Google Maps of genetics. Instead of streets and buildings, it shows a stretch of DNA. Instead of a single layer, it stacks multiple tracks of information at the same coordinates: gene models, regulatory regions, expression levels, conservation scores, and more.

The horizontal axis is always genomic position (measured in base pairs). Each track adds a different layer of biological annotation. The power comes from alignment: when a peak in expression lines up with a gene, a conserved region, and an open chromatin signal, the evidence converges.

The genome browser's key insight is that biology is fundamentally about position. Genes, regulatory elements, variants, and epigenetic marks all exist at specific coordinates. Aligning them on a common axis reveals relationships that no single dataset can show alone.

Reading the Tracks

Each track type serves a different purpose:

  • Gene models (blue arrows): The location and structure of genes. Thick blocks are exons (protein-coding), thin lines are introns. Arrows show transcription direction.
  • Expression (area chart): How much RNA is produced at each position. Peaks over genes mean the gene is active.
  • CpG islands (green): Clusters of CG dinucleotides, often near gene promoters. Methylation of CpG islands silences genes.
  • Conservation (purple): How similar this DNA is across species. High conservation means the sequence is functionally important and hasn't changed much over millions of years.

Figure 2. Anatomy of a gene model. Exons (thick) are the parts that become protein. Introns (thin lines) are spliced out. The arrow indicates transcription direction. UTRs (medium) are transcribed but not translated.
