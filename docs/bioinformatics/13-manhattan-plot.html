Manhattan Plot

The skyline of the genome: scanning millions of variants to find the handful that shape disease risk in a population.

In previous explainers we looked at individual genes — TP53 on chromosome 17, CDKN1A on chromosome 6 — through the lens of single experiments. But a deeper question lurks: are variants near these genes linked to cancer risk across the entire population?

A genome-wide association study (GWAS) tests hundreds of thousands to millions of genetic variants, one by one, asking whether each is more common in people with a disease than in healthy controls. The result is a p-value for every variant — and the Manhattan plot is how we see them all at once.

Named after the Manhattan skyline, the plot arranges variants by their genomic position (x-axis) and statistical significance (y-axis, as -log10(p)). Most variants cluster near the ground — no association. A few spike upward like skyscrapers, marking loci that warrant attention.

The Skyline

Below are ~5,000 simulated SNPs spread across 22 autosomes. Chromosomes alternate between gray and blue. Two loci spike above the genome-wide significance threshold: a strong peak at the TP53 region on chromosome 17, and a more modest signal near CDKN1A on chromosome 6. Hover any point for details. Click the TP53 peak to zoom in.

Figure 1. Genome-wide Manhattan plot. The red dashed line marks genome-wide significance (p = 5 × 10−8, −log10 = 7.3). The blue dashed line marks suggestive significance (p = 10−5, −log10 = 5). Alternating colors distinguish chromosomes.

The significance threshold of 5 × 10−8 is not arbitrary — it is the Bonferroni correction for approximately one million independent tests across the genome. At p = 0.05, you would expect 50,000 false positives from a million tests. At 5 × 10−8, fewer than one false positive is expected genome-wide.

Locus Zoom

A significant peak on the Manhattan plot tells you where to look, but not which variant is causal. Many nearby SNPs will be significant simply because they are in linkage disequilibrium (LD) — inherited together. A LocusZoom-style plot colors SNPs by their LD with the lead variant, helping distinguish the causal signal from passengers.

Below, the TP53 locus on chromosome 17 is shown at high resolution. SNPs are colored by LD (r²) with the lead SNP. The background trace shows recombination rate — where recombination is high, LD breaks down quickly, creating a natural boundary for the association signal. Gene models are drawn below.

LD is the reason GWAS hits are rarely the causal variant themselves. A SNP on a genotyping array may be 50 kb away from the true functional variant but perfectly correlated with it. Fine-mapping and functional experiments are needed to pin down causality.

QQ Plot: Was the Study Well-Behaved?

Before trusting any Manhattan peak, we check whether the study has systematic problems. A quantile-quantile (QQ) plot compares the distribution of observed p-values against the expected uniform distribution under the null hypothesis. Most points should lie along the diagonal. If the entire curve is inflated (shifted left), it suggests population stratification or technical artifacts. Only the tail — the top hits — should deviate upward.

Figure 3. QQ plot of observed versus expected −log10(p). The gray diagonal is the null expectation. Points near the top-right deviate upward, confirming genuine association signals. The genomic inflation factor (λ) near 1.0 indicates no systematic inflation.

The genomic inflation factor λ (lambda) is the median observed chi-squared statistic divided by the expected median (0.455). A λ close to 1.0 means the study is well-calibrated. Values above 1.1 raise concerns about confounding.

From Signal to Biology

A Manhattan plot is the beginning, not the end. The peaks tell you which genomic neighborhoods harbor risk variants, but converting that into biological insight requires many more steps: fine-mapping to identify the causal variant, functional assays to determine how it affects gene regulation, and pathway analysis to place the gene in a biological context.

For TP53, the story is already well known — it is the most frequently mutated gene in human cancer. But GWAS approaches have also revealed that common variants near TP53 modulate cancer risk in the general population, a subtler effect than the rare, high-penetrance mutations found in tumor sequencing.
