Gene Portrait

Everything at once — what does the complete multi-dimensional portrait of TP53/CDKN1A look like?

Every previous explainer in this series examined one dimension of the TP53/CDKN1A axis: expression changes, mutation landscapes, pathway wiring, epigenetic marks, survival outcomes. Each answered a narrow question. But a gene is not a single measurement. It is a composite of all these dimensions, viewed together.

This final explainer assembles six key views into a single integrated dashboard. A master toggle switches between TP53-wildtype and TP53-R175H mutant states, and every panel updates simultaneously. The result is a gene portrait — a compact visual summary of how one mutation reshapes an entire biological system.

The Dashboard

Six panels. One toggle. Click any panel to expand it for detail, or toggle TP53 status to watch all dimensions shift in concert. This is the overview+detail pattern: start with the gestalt, drill into the specifics.

Toggle to R175H mutant and watch the cascade: CDKN1A expression drops, methylation rises, the pathway goes dark, and the survival curves separate.

Convergent evidence. No single measurement is definitive. But when expression, methylation, mutation, pathway activity, and survival all point the same direction, the biological story becomes compelling. This is the power of the multi-dimensional portrait.

From Heatmaps to Portraits

This series began with a heatmap and dendrogram (explainer 01), the simplest way to see structure in gene expression. Each subsequent explainer added a dimension: MA plots for fold changes, volcano plots for significance, dimensionality reduction for sample relationships, genome browsers for genomic context, circos plots for structural variants, survival curves for clinical outcomes, dot plots for cell-type specificity, lollipop plots for mutation positions, oncoprints for alteration landscapes, methylation for epigenetics, ChIP-seq for binding, Manhattan plots for association, pathway diagrams for biological wiring, PPI networks for protein interactions, multi-omics heatmaps for integration, waterfall plots for patient ranking, and forest plots for effect estimation.

Each visualization answered a different question about the same biological system. Together, from heatmaps (01) through this dashboard (19), they form the visual vocabulary of modern bioinformatics — the grammar by which we read genomes, understand disease, and communicate biological discovery.
