Circos Plot

A circular layout that reveals translocations, fusions, and long-range interactions across the whole genome at once.

A genome browser shows a linear stretch of one chromosome. But some of the most important events in cancer biology involve two distant parts of the genome rearranging: a piece of chromosome 9 fusing with chromosome 22 (the Philadelphia chromosome in leukemia), or regulatory elements on one chromosome controlling genes on another.

The circos plot, created by Martin Krzywinski, arranges all chromosomes as arcs around a circle, then draws links between them. It's the genome's wiring diagram.

The Ideogram

The outer ring below shows 8 simplified chromosomes, sized proportionally. Hover any chromosome to see its connections. The inner links show structural variants: translocations (pieces swapped between chromosomes), duplications, and deletions.

The circos layout works because the circle gives every chromosome equal visual access to every other. In a linear layout, showing chr1-chr22 links would require long diagonal lines crossing the entire figure. The circle makes all connections equally legible.

Layers of Information

Like the genome browser's stacked tracks, circos plots can carry multiple data rings between the ideogram and the links. Common inner tracks include copy number, gene density, or expression levels. Each ring adds a layer of context.

Figure 2. Multi-ring circos plot. Outer ring: chromosomes. Second ring: gene expression (heatmap). Third ring: gene density (histogram). Inner links: structural variants. Toggle rings to see how each layer contributes.

When to Use (and When Not To)

Circos plots are powerful for showing genome-wide patterns and inter-chromosomal connections. They are poor for precise quantitative comparison — it's hard to compare arc lengths and band widths accurately.

Use a circos plot when:

  • You need to show connections between chromosomes
  • You want a compact overview of the whole genome
  • The story is about pattern, not precise values

Use a genome browser or bar chart when you need precise coordinates or quantitative comparison.
