Survival Curves

The Kaplan-Meier estimator: how bioinformaticians ask whether a gene, mutation, or treatment predicts how long patients survive.

All the previous visualizations in this series answer molecular questions: which genes changed, where they sit in the genome, how cells cluster. But the question that matters most to patients and clinicians is: does any of this predict outcomes?

The Kaplan-Meier survival curve answers this. It tracks a group of patients over time, plotting the fraction still alive (or disease-free) at each time point. When you split patients by a molecular marker — say, high versus low expression of a gene — the gap between the two curves tells you whether that marker predicts survival.

The Step Function

Each step down marks an event (typically a death or disease progression). The curve doesn't decrease smoothly because events happen at discrete times. Tick marks on the curve show censored observations: patients who were lost to follow-up or were still alive when the study ended. We know they survived at least that long.

Figure 1. A single Kaplan-Meier curve for 80 simulated patients. Each step is an event. Tick marks (+) are censored observations. The shaded band is the 95% confidence interval, which widens over time as fewer patients remain at risk.

Censoring is the key insight of survival analysis. Without it, patients who were still alive at study end would either be excluded (biasing results) or counted as events (underestimating survival). The Kaplan-Meier estimator handles censoring correctly by adjusting the denominator at each time point.

Splitting by a Gene

The real power comes from splitting patients into groups. Below, patients are divided by the expression level of a simulated gene. Drag the threshold slider to change where the split happens and watch the two curves separate or converge.

Figure 2. Kaplan-Meier curves split by gene expression. Red: high expression. Blue: low expression. Drag the threshold to find the split that maximizes the difference. The log-rank p-value tests whether the difference is statistically significant.

The Log-Rank Test

The visual gap between curves is suggestive, but not proof. The log-rank test is the standard statistical test for comparing survival curves. It asks: under the null hypothesis that both groups have identical survival, how surprising is the observed difference?

A small p-value (typically < 0.05) means the curves are significantly different. But beware of multiple testing: if you try 20,000 genes and find 1,000 with p < 0.05, most are false positives. The Bonferroni or FDR correction adjusts for this.

Figure 3. Three different genes, each splitting the same patients differently. Some genes show a clear survival difference; others don't. Click each gene to compare. This is why bioinformaticians run survival analysis across thousands of genes and correct for multiple testing.

The best cancer biomarkers combine genomic visualization (volcano plots, heatmaps) with survival analysis. A gene that is differentially expressed and predicts survival is a much stronger candidate than one that satisfies either criterion alone.
