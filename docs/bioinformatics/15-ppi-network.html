Protein-Protein Interaction Networks

What other proteins connect TP53 and CDKN1A? PPI networks reveal the interaction landscape around genes of interest, exposing functional modules and hub proteins that mediate cellular signaling.

Previous explainers in this series identified TP53 as a key gene and CDKN1A (p21) as one of its transcriptional targets. But genes don't act alone. Their protein products physically bind other proteins, forming intricate networks that coordinate cell behavior.

A protein-protein interaction (PPI) network maps these interactions. Nodes are proteins; edges represent physical or functional interactions validated by experiment, predicted computationally, or curated from databases like STRING. By exploring the neighborhood around TP53 and CDKN1A, we can see which proteins mediate DNA damage response, cell-cycle arrest, and apoptosis.

The TP53 Neighborhood

The network below places TP53 and CDKN1A at the center of their interaction neighborhood. Each node is a protein, colored by functional category. Drag any node to rearrange the layout. Hover for protein details.

Figure 1. Force-directed PPI network centered on TP53 and CDKN1A. Node color encodes functional category: cell cycle, apoptosis, DNA repair, ubiquitin/regulation. TP53 and CDKN1A are shown as larger nodes. Edges reflect curated interaction data.

TP53 sits at the crossroads of multiple functional modules. It directly activates CDKN1A (causing cell-cycle arrest), interacts with MDM2 (which targets it for degradation), and connects to both the DNA-repair and apoptosis machineries. This centrality explains why TP53 mutations have such broad consequences in cancer.

Degree and Hubness

Not all proteins are equally connected. In biological networks, a few hub proteins have many partners while most have few — a property called scale-free topology. Toggle between uniform and degree-scaled node sizes to see which proteins are the biggest hubs.

Figure 2. Node size scaled by degree (number of connections). TP53 is the dominant hub. The bar chart ranks all proteins by degree. Toggle to uniform size to see the network without the degree encoding.

Hub proteins like TP53 are disproportionately important. Removing a hub disrupts many pathways at once, which is why TP53 loss-of-function is the most common mutation in human cancers. Scale-free networks are robust to random failures but fragile to targeted attacks on hubs.

Shortest Path Explorer

Click any two proteins to find the shortest path between them. The path edges highlight in bold, and the distance is displayed below. Try TP53 and CDKN1A (direct neighbors, distance 1), then CDK4 and BAX (routed through TP53).

Click two nodes to find the shortest path between them.

Figure 3. Click any two proteins to highlight the shortest path. Path length (number of edges) reveals how closely connected two proteins are in the network. Most protein pairs in this neighborhood are within 2-3 steps of each other, reflecting the small-world property of biological networks.

Short path lengths matter biologically. If two proteins are separated by only one or two intermediaries, a signal (or a mutation's effect) can propagate between them quickly. The "small world" property of PPI networks means that perturbations in one module can rapidly affect distant cellular functions.

From Networks to Systems

PPI networks are a foundation of systems biology. They connect the molecular details we've explored in earlier explainers — differential expression, genomic location, survival outcomes — into a unified picture of how proteins collaborate. The next step is integrating multiple data types simultaneously.
