Multi-Omics Heatmap

Do all the layers of evidence agree? Stacking expression, methylation, mutation, and copy number data on a shared patient axis to see whether different data types converge on the same biological story.

Every earlier explainer in this series examines one kind of molecular data at a time: gene expression in the heatmap, mutations in the volcano plot, survival outcomes in the Kaplan-Meier curve. But real biology is layered. A gene can be silenced by a mutation, by methylation of its promoter, or by deletion of its chromosomal region. A multi-omics heatmap stacks these layers vertically and asks a simple question: do they tell the same story?

The columns are patients. The rows, grouped into panels, are different measurements. When you sort the columns by one variable — here, TP53 mutation status — the patterns that emerge across panels reveal whether expression, methylation, and copy number agree or disagree about which patients are disrupted.

The Stacked View

The full stack has four panels sharing a patient axis. A clinical annotation bar at the top marks TP53 status. Below that, expression, methylation, mutation, and copy number panels each add a different line of evidence. Hover on any patient column to see it highlighted across all panels simultaneously.

Figure 1. Forty patients sorted by TP53 mutation status. The annotation bar at top marks mutant (red) and wild-type (blue) patients. Four panels below show expression, methylation, mutation, and copy number. Hover a column to highlight that patient across all layers.

Notice how TP53-mutant patients (left side) tend to show higher CDKN1A expression, lower promoter methylation, and more copy number alterations. This convergence across independent data types is what makes multi-omics integration powerful: each layer alone is noisy, but together the signal reinforces.

Panel Toggle

Each panel tells a partial story. Remove one and the picture becomes ambiguous; add it back and the pattern snaps into focus. Use the checkboxes below to show or hide panels and watch the remaining ones expand to fill the space.

Figure 2. Toggle panels to see how each layer contributes. With all four visible, the convergent pattern is clear. With only one, you see noise and uncertainty.

Cross-Omics Correlation

The heatmap shows patterns visually, but to quantify relationships between layers you need a scatter plot. Click any row label in the heatmap below to select it. Once you have selected two rows from different panels, a scatter plot appears showing those two variables across all patients, colored by TP53 status.

Try clicking CDKN1A in the expression panel and then CDKN1A_CpG1 in the methylation panel. Expression and promoter methylation should be anti-correlated: when the promoter is methylated, the gene is silenced.

Click two row labels from different panels above to see their cross-omics scatter plot.

Figure 3. Click two rows from different panels to generate a scatter plot. Points are colored by TP53 status (red = mutant, blue = wild-type). Anti-correlated variables (e.g., expression vs methylation) appear as a downward trend.

Cross-omics correlations are the analytical backbone of integrative genomics. A gene whose expression anti-correlates with its promoter methylation is likely epigenetically regulated. A gene whose expression tracks with copy number is likely dosage-sensitive. These relationships survive across independent measurement platforms, making them far more trustworthy than single-omics signals.

Reading the Layers

Multi-omics heatmaps are standard in cancer genomics papers — TCGA publications routinely show stacks of five or more panels for hundreds of patients. The key to reading them is the shared column axis: your eye moves vertically through the panels for a single patient, checking whether the signals align. When they do, you have convergent evidence. When they don't, you have a biological puzzle worth investigating.

In the synthetic data above, we built the correlations in: TP53-mutant samples have higher CDKN1A expression (the p21 pathway activates), lower promoter methylation (the promoter opens up), and copy number alterations in the TP53 region. Real data is noisier, but the logic is the same. Integration is about asking the data to vote, and trusting the consensus.
