Pathway Diagrams

How does the signal flow from TP53 to CDKN1A in the p53 pathway? Pathway diagrams map the chain of molecular events from stimulus to cellular response.

Genes do not act alone. When DNA is damaged, a cascade of signals propagates through the cell: sensors detect the damage, kinases relay the alarm, transcription factors activate target genes, and the cell either repairs itself, stops dividing, or self-destructs. A pathway diagram maps this cascade as a network of nodes (genes and proteins) connected by directed edges (activation, inhibition, phosphorylation).

The p53 pathway is one of the most studied in cancer biology. The protein p53, encoded by the gene TP53, is mutated in roughly half of all human cancers. When functioning normally, p53 acts as a "guardian of the genome" — sensing DNA damage and activating genes like CDKN1A (which halts the cell cycle) and BAX (which triggers programmed cell death). When TP53 is mutated, these protective responses fail.

The Static Pathway

Below is the p53 signaling pathway laid out as bioinformaticians typically draw it: upstream signals at the top, downstream effectors at the bottom. Arrow-headed edges mean activation; flat-headed edges (T-bars) mean inhibition. Node colors reflect expression levels from an RNA-seq experiment: red for upregulated, blue for downregulated, green for the active hub.

Toggle between wildtype and TP53-mutant states to see how losing a single gene reshapes the entire downstream expression landscape.

Figure 1. The p53 signaling pathway. In wildtype cells, DNA damage activates ATM/ATR, which activates TP53. TP53 then upregulates CDKN1A (cell cycle arrest), BAX (apoptosis), and GADD45 (DNA repair), while inhibiting BCL2 (anti-apoptotic). MDM2 provides negative feedback. In TP53-mutant cells, the downstream targets are not activated.

MDM2 is a key negative regulator: it ubiquitinates p53 and targets it for degradation. Many cancers amplify MDM2 to silence p53 without mutating it. This feedback loop is a major drug target — MDM2 inhibitors (like nutlin-3) aim to restore p53 function.

Signal Flow Animation

A static diagram shows the wiring, but the pathway is a dynamic process. Below, watch a signal propagate from DNA damage through the pathway. Pulses travel along edges to show the order and timing of activation. In mutant mode, the signal reaches TP53 but cannot pass through — the downstream nodes stay dark.

Expression Overlay

In real experiments, TP53 activity is not simply on or off — it exists on a continuum. The slider below controls TP53 activity from 0% (fully mutant, no p53 function) to 100% (fully wildtype, maximal p53 activity). Watch how downstream node colors smoothly transition as predicted expression levels change. This is the core idea behind pathway analysis: given a perturbation at one node, predict the consequences across the network.

Figure 3. Expression overlay controlled by TP53 activity level. At 100%, downstream targets are fully activated (CDKN1A, BAX, GADD45 upregulated; BCL2 inhibited). At 0%, TP53 is inactive and downstream genes revert to baseline or opposite expression. Intermediate values show graded responses.

Pathway analysis tools like GSEA, SPIA, and KEGG overlay measured expression data onto curated pathway maps to find which pathways are perturbed in a disease. The slider above simulates this concept: a single upstream perturbation has predictable downstream consequences, and the pathway structure tells you what to expect.

From Pathway to Network

This pathway is simplified — the real p53 network involves hundreds of genes and cross-talk with other pathways (Wnt, MAPK, PI3K/AKT). When a pathway diagram grows too complex for manual layout, bioinformaticians turn to protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks, where layout algorithms replace hand-drawn organization.
