Heatmap & Dendrogram

What gene expression data looks like, and how clustering reveals hidden structure in a matrix of measurements.

Imagine measuring how active every gene is in a cell. Not one gene, but thousands. Not one sample, but dozens: tumor versus healthy tissue, treated versus untreated, different patients. The result is a matrix: rows of genes, columns of samples, and a number in every cell representing expression level.

This matrix is too large to read as a table. Bioinformaticians turn it into a heatmap, replacing numbers with colors. High expression gets a warm color, low expression gets a cool one. Patterns that were invisible in a spreadsheet become visible instantly.

The Raw Matrix

Here is a simulated gene expression dataset: 30 genes measured across 10 samples. Five samples come from a "treated" condition, five from "control." Some genes respond to the treatment. Others don't. Can you spot which ones?

Figure 1. Gene expression matrix in original order. Rows are genes, columns are samples. Color encodes expression level. Try different color scales to see how palette choice affects readability.

The matrix is noisy. Some genes are clearly brighter in certain columns, but the overall impression is chaotic. The genes and samples are in an arbitrary order, so related rows and columns are scattered apart.

A heatmap is only as good as its ordering. The same data can look like noise or like clear structure, depending on how rows and columns are arranged.

Clustering: Letting the Data Organize Itself

Hierarchical clustering solves the ordering problem. It measures how similar each pair of genes (or samples) is, then groups the most similar ones together. The result is a tree called a dendrogram, where nearby branches share similar expression patterns.

Reading the Dendrogram

The tree on the left of the heatmap is a dendrogram. Each leaf is a gene. When two genes are joined by a short branch, they have similar expression profiles. Long branches mean the genes are quite different.

The height where two clusters merge tells you how dissimilar they are. Low merges mean tight, coherent groups. High merges mean the groups are being forced together despite real differences.

Figure 3. Drag the cut height to slice the dendrogram at different levels. This controls how many clusters you get: cut low for many small clusters, cut high for fewer large ones. The colored bands show which cluster each gene belongs to.

There is no single "correct" number of clusters. The dendrogram gives you a hierarchy, and you choose where to cut based on what resolution is useful for your question.

Why This Matters

Heatmaps with dendrograms are often the first visualization a bioinformatician makes after running a differential expression analysis. They answer a basic question: which genes behave similarly, and do the samples group by condition?

If the samples cluster by treatment rather than by patient, the treatment has a strong transcriptomic signal. If genes cluster into clear blocks, those blocks often correspond to biological pathways: groups of genes that work together toward a common function.

Figure 4. Annotated heatmap with sample condition bar (top) and gene pathway assignments (left). When clusters align with known biology, the experiment is telling a coherent story.

The next step is usually asking which specific genes changed and by how much. That question leads to the MA plot.
