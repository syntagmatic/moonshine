Dot Plot

Two numbers per dot: which cell types express a gene, and how strongly? The dot plot encodes both in a single matrix.

Previous explainers showed how dimensionality reduction groups cells into clusters and how volcano plots identify differentially expressed genes. But once you have clusters and candidate genes, a natural question follows: which cell types actually express each gene?

A heatmap could answer this, but it only encodes one quantity (average expression) per cell. In single-cell data, averages can mislead. A gene might have a high mean in a cluster because every cell expresses it a little, or because a few cells express it intensely while most stay silent. These are biologically different situations.

The dot plot solves this by encoding two quantities in every dot. The dot's size shows what fraction of cells in the cluster express the gene at all (percent expressed). The dot's color shows the average expression among those expressing cells. Together, these two channels let you distinguish a broadly expressed housekeeping gene from a sporadically but intensely expressed marker.

Reading a Dot Plot

The matrix below shows ten genes across eight cell types drawn from a simulated tumor microenvironment. Each gene was chosen because it is a known marker for a particular cell type. CD3D marks T cells, CD14 marks monocytes, MS4A1 marks B cells, and so on.

Among these markers, look for TP53 and CDKN1A. TP53 is broadly expressed across many cell types because it serves as a general tumor suppressor. CDKN1A, its downstream target encoding p21, should track TP53 but with variation: some cell types activate the p53 pathway more vigorously than others.

A large, dark dot means the gene is both widely and strongly expressed in that cluster. A small, dark dot means it is expressed intensely in a few cells. A large, pale dot means many cells express it, but only weakly. Reading both channels together is the key to interpreting dot plots correctly.

Mutation Disrupts a Pathway

TP53 is the most commonly mutated gene in human cancers. The R175H mutation, a missense change in the DNA-binding domain, produces a protein that folds incorrectly and cannot activate its transcriptional targets. Critically, the mRNA is still transcribed and may even accumulate (because the mutant protein no longer activates MDM2, which normally degrades p53). So in single-cell RNA-seq, TP53 mRNA looks normal or even elevated, while its functional output collapses.

The dot plot below compares cells from a wildtype tumor with cells from a TP53-mutant tumor. Look at CDKN1A: in the mutant panel, both the fraction of expressing cells and the expression level drop noticeably across most cell types. This is the pathway disruption made visible. TP53 mRNA itself stays present, masking the functional catastrophe happening downstream.

This is a general principle in cancer biology: mRNA presence does not guarantee protein function. The dot plot shows gene expression, not protein activity. TP53 mRNA in a mutant cell is a decoy. Only by looking at downstream targets like CDKN1A can you see that the pathway is broken.

What the Dot Hides

Every summary statistic discards information, and the dot plot is no exception. Its two numbers per dot (fraction and mean) compress the full distribution of single-cell expression values into a tidy circle. But expression distributions are not always well-behaved. They can be bimodal, heavily skewed, or have long tails.

Figure 3. Click a dot to see the distribution that generated it. The violin shows the density estimate; individual cells are jittered horizontally. Two dots with similar size and color can hide very different shapes, from tight unimodal clusters to broad bimodal distributions.

This is why experienced bioinformaticians treat dot plots as a starting point, not a final answer. They serve as a compact summary for scanning many genes across many clusters, but any interesting pattern should be followed up with the raw distributions or with statistical tests that account for distributional shape.
