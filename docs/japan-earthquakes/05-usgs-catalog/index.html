The USGS Earthquake Catalog

A global record of where and when the earth shakes, and how to read it

If you have felt earthquakes in Japan, you probably already check the JMA (Japan Meteorological Agency) for local information. But when researchers and data analysts around the world need a single, standardized earthquake database, most of them reach for the same source: the USGS Earthquake Hazards Program catalog.

The United States Geological Survey is the science agency of the U.S. government. Their earthquake monitoring program runs a global seismic network and maintains a catalog of earthquakes going back over a century. The catalog is free, well-documented, and accessible through a clean API. It is the closest thing the world has to a single, consistent earthquake database.

This page walks through what is actually in the catalog: what the fields mean, where the data comes from, what is trustworthy and what you should treat with some caution.

Anatomy of a record

Every earthquake in the catalog gets a row with about 22 fields. Here is one real record from the 2024 Noto Peninsula earthquake, the M7.5 event on New Year's Day, with the most important fields explained.

FieldValueWhat it means
time 2024-01-01T07:10:09.476Z UTC timestamp. Add 9 hours for JST: 16:10 on New Year's Day.
latitude 37.4874 Degrees north. Noto Peninsula, Ishikawa Prefecture.
longitude 137.271 Degrees east.
depth 10 km Shallow event. Watch for 10.0 and 33.0 defaults (see quirks below).
mag 7.5 Magnitude. Large enough to cause widespread damage and tsunami.
magType mww Moment magnitude from W-phase. The preferred type for large quakes.
place 2024 Noto Peninsula, Japan Earthquake Text description. Notable events get named; most say "X km from Y."

What the catalog covers

The USGS catalog is global, but its completeness depends on when and where you look. For magnitude 4.5 and above, global coverage is reasonably comprehensive from about 1970 onward, after the World-Wide Standardized Seismograph Network (WWSSN) was deployed during the Cold War. For earlier decades, only significant or very large earthquakes were reliably recorded.

The digital era brought another jump. Starting in the early 1990s, automated processing made it possible to locate and characterize earthquakes faster and more consistently. The catalog became much denser.

M4.5+ earthquakes near Japan recorded by USGS, by year. The 2011 spike is the Tohoku earthquake and its thousands of aftershocks. Notice how the baseline count roughly doubled from the early 2000s to the 2010s as monitoring improved.

Where the earthquakes are

Japan sits at the junction of four tectonic plates, so earthquakes are everywhere. But the USGS catalog records them unevenly. Offshore subduction zones (the Japan Trench, the Nankai Trough, the Ryukyu Arc) dominate the data because these produce the larger-magnitude events that USGS can detect from global stations. Smaller onshore events are often below the USGS threshold but show up in JMA's denser local network.

Density of USGS-cataloged M4.5+ events around Japan (2000–2025). Each cell is a 2° grid square. The Pacific coast, particularly off Tohoku and Hokkaido, shows the heaviest concentration. The 2011 Tohoku aftershock sequence dominates the northeast coast.

Magnitude types

One thing that surprises people when they first look at earthquake data: there is no single "magnitude." The catalog lists magType for every event, and the type matters.

The most common types you will see:

mb (body-wave magnitude) — measured from short-period seismic waves. This is the workhorse for moderate earthquakes (roughly M4–6). It is fast to compute and works well at teleseismic distances, so USGS uses it for the majority of events worldwide. In this Japan dataset, about 78% of records use mb.

mww (moment magnitude, W-phase) — computed from very long-period waves. This is the preferred scale for large earthquakes (M6+) because it does not saturate the way mb does. The 2011 Tohoku earthquake was M9.1 on this scale.

mwc, mwr, mwb — other flavors of moment magnitude (centroid, regional, body-wave). Used when mww is not available or as alternatives computed by different methods.

ml (local magnitude) — the original Richter scale, essentially. Rarely used by USGS for the Japan region because it works best for local networks.

Magnitude type distribution in the Japan M4.5+ dataset. Body-wave magnitude (mb) dominates because it is practical for routine moderate-earthquake processing. Moment magnitude variants (mww, mwc, mwr, mwb) are used for larger and more significant events.

Why this matters: If you are comparing magnitudes across events, check that they are the same type. An mb 5.5 and an mww 5.5 measure different physical quantities and are not directly comparable. For events above M6, always prefer moment magnitude (mw-family) because body-wave magnitude saturates around 6.5 and understates the true size of large earthquakes.

Quirks and gotchas

Depth defaults

When the seismic data cannot constrain depth well, the USGS assigns a default: typically 10 km for shallow events or 33 km in some older records. If you see a suspicious number of earthquakes at exactly 10.0 km or 33.0 km, that is not geology. It means the depth was not well determined. The depthError field can help you filter these.

Location names are approximate

The place field says things like "69 km E of Yamada, Japan" or "Izu Islands, Japan region." These are computed from the coordinates and a gazetteer of named places. They describe what is nearby, not what was affected. A deep earthquake 300 km beneath the sea might list a coastal town as its reference point.

Review status

Events start as automatic and get upgraded to reviewed after a seismologist checks them. Automatic locations can be off by tens of kilometers. For research, filter to reviewed events when precision matters. Recent events (last few hours) are almost always still automatic.

Completeness varies by region

The USGS catalog is globally consistent but not globally complete. In the western U.S., the catalog is fairly complete down to about M2.5. In Japan, it reliably captures M4.5+ events but misses many smaller ones that JMA records. If you need comprehensive data for Japan specifically, consider merging USGS data with JMA catalogs — but be prepared to deal with duplicate events and different magnitude scales.

How to access the data

The USGS provides several ways to get at the catalog.

Web search: The Earthquake Catalog Search lets you filter by date range, magnitude, location (rectangle or circle), and download results as CSV or GeoJSON.

API: The same search is available as a REST API. A typical query looks like:

https://earthquake.usgs.gov/fdsnws/event/1/query?format=csv&starttime=2024-01-01&endtime=2024-12-31&minmagnitude=4.5&minlatitude=20&maxlatitude=50&minlongitude=120&maxlongitude=155

That returns all M4.5+ events in a box covering Japan for the year 2024 as a CSV file. Change format=geojson for GeoJSON. The API has a 20,000-event limit per query, so for large requests you may need to split by time range.

Real-time feeds: For monitoring, USGS publishes GeoJSON feeds updated every minute at earthquake.usgs.gov/earthquakes/feed/. These cover the last hour, day, week, or month at various magnitude thresholds.

For Japan specifically: The USGS catalog is a good starting point because it is well-documented, globally standardized, and easy to access programmatically. But JMA's own catalog has more events (especially smaller ones), more precise locations for onshore events, and the shindo intensity scale that maps more directly to what people feel. For a complete picture, you eventually want both.

Summary

The USGS Earthquake Catalog is a solid, free, well-structured dataset. For M4.5+ events globally, it is the standard reference. For Japan specifically, it captures all the significant events but is not as granular as JMA's local catalog. The key fields — time, location, depth, magnitude, magnitude type, and review status — are enough to support serious analysis, as long as you pay attention to the quirks: default depths, magnitude type mixing, and completeness boundaries.
