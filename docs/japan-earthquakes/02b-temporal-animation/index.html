When the Ground Won't Stop Shaking

Fifteen years of earthquakes around Japan, animated in time

Japan sits at the collision of four tectonic plates. If you've lived there for any length of time, you know the drill: the phone buzzes, the room sways, and you wait to see if it's going to get worse. Most of the time it doesn't. But sometimes it does. A major event isn't a bigger version of background seismicity. It's a completely different phenomenon.

How earthquake sequences unfold

Most of the time, Japan's seismicity looks like a steady background hum. A few dots per week, scattered across the map, mostly M4.5 to M5.5. This is normal stress release along plate boundaries and faults. Alarming if you're new to it, but it's the baseline. The earth is always adjusting.

Then there are the sequences. When a major earthquake strikes, it changes the stress field in the surrounding crust. Faults that were close to failure get pushed over the edge, and the result is a burst of aftershocks that can last weeks or months. In the animation, you can see this dramatically: the Tohoku M9.1 on March 11, 2011 produces a dense swarm of dots that lights up the entire northeast coast of Honshu. Hundreds of aftershocks above M4.5 in the first 24 hours alone.

The aftershock cascade. After the 2011 Tohoku earthquake, the rate of M4.5+ aftershocks was roughly 200 times the normal background rate. The stress redistribution triggered earthquakes on entirely separate fault systems across northern Japan, including a M6.6 near Akita on the opposite coast.

The tyranny of the magnitude scale

The magnitude scale is logarithmic: each whole number represents about 32 times more energy. A single M9.1 earthquake releases more energy than every other earthquake on this map combined, by a factor of roughly 1,000. Look at the sparkline. The cumulative energy line is essentially flat for most of the timeline, then jumps vertically on March 11, 2011.

Here's another way to think about it: to match the energy of one M9.1 earthquake, you would need approximately 22,000 earthquakes of M6.0, or about 700 million earthquakes of M4.5. The dots on this map that represent daily background seismicity aren't even a rounding error compared to Tohoku.

Notable sequences to watch for. Play through the full timeline and you'll spot several distinct bursts: the 2016 Kumamoto earthquakes (a double mainshock in April), the 2024 Noto Peninsula earthquake on New Year's Day, and recurring offshore swarms east of Honshu. Each has a different character. Kumamoto was shallow and destructive. Noto struck a region with many traditional wooden buildings. The offshore events mostly dissipate into the Pacific.

What this means for daily life

If you're living in Japan, the background seismicity is a constant companion. The shindo scale matters more than magnitude for how it actually feels. A deep M6.0 hundreds of kilometers offshore might produce only a gentle sway in Tokyo, while a shallow M5.5 directly beneath the city is a sharp jolt that knocks things off shelves. The animation can't show you that nuance, but it does show spatial patterns: some regions are consistently more active than others, and the offshore trench east of Honshu is the single biggest source of earthquakes.

Japan's building codes and early warning systems are among the best anywhere. But the physics is non-negotiable: another great earthquake is a matter of when, not if. Knowing what the difference between background noise and a major sequence looks like, that's part of living in earthquake country.
