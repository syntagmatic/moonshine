Scale & Frequency

Small multiples by region: Gutenberg-Richter plots for eight parts of Japan

The Gutenberg-Richter relationship is one of seismology's most reliable empirical laws: for any region, the number of earthquakes drops by roughly a factor of ten for each unit increase in magnitude. Plot the cumulative count on a log scale against magnitude and you get a nearly straight line. The slope of that line, the b-value, tells you something about the physics of faulting in that area.

Rather than cramming all of Japan's earthquake data onto one chart, small multiples let us lay out the same plot eight times, once per region, with shared axes. Your eye can jump between panels and compare: which regions are most active, which produce the largest events, which have unusual frequency-magnitude curves.

Eight regions, one visual language

Hover any panel to highlight that region on the map. Each plot shows cumulative earthquake count (log scale) versus magnitude for M4.5+ quakes, 2000-2025. The dashed line in each panel shows a reference b=1.0 slope for comparison. Computed b-values are labeled per panel. USGS catalog.

The panels are arranged roughly north-to-south: Hokkaido at the top left, Okinawa at the bottom right. The y-axis is shared across all panels on a logarithmic scale, and the x-axis covers magnitudes 4.5 through 8.0, so you are always comparing like with like.

What stands out

Tohoku dominates. Over 4,500 M4.5+ events since 2000, more than any other region by a wide margin. No surprise: the 2011 Tohoku earthquake and its aftershock sequence are a major contributor, but even setting those aside, the Japan Trench subduction zone off Tohoku's Pacific coast generates earthquakes at an extraordinary rate.

Okinawa comes in a close second in raw count, which may surprise people who think of it as "the beach prefecture." The Ryukyu Trench running south from Kyushu generates a steady stream of moderate earthquakes, and the arc extends over a huge geographic area, catching events from Taiwan's vicinity all the way north.

Reading the line's length: When a region's line extends to magnitude 7 or beyond, that region has experienced at least one very large earthquake in the observation period. Lines that drop off at magnitude 6 or below mean the largest observed events were moderate. That doesn't mean large earthquakes can't happen there, just that none occurred in this 25-year window.

Kansai and Chubu have the fewest events and the shortest lines. These regions sit in the interior of Honshu, away from the primary subduction zones. Their earthquakes tend to occur on crustal faults rather than at plate boundaries, and the rates are lower. Kansai's line ends around magnitude 5.8; Chubu's reaches 6.2. Anyone in Osaka or Nagoya might find this reassuring at first glance, but crustal earthquakes can be devastating at shallower depths, as Kobe's 1995 M6.9 demonstrated.

Variations in slope

If every region followed the Gutenberg-Richter law identically, all eight lines would be parallel, differing only in vertical position (total count). But they are not quite parallel. The b-value, the slope of each line, varies.

Tohoku and Hokkaido have b-values near 1.0, which is the global average for subduction zones. This means each unit increase in magnitude corresponds to almost exactly a tenfold decrease in frequency. Kanto shows a steeper slope (b around 1.3), suggesting a relative deficit of larger earthquakes compared to smaller ones, possibly reflecting the complex triple junction beneath the Kanto plain where three plates interact.

Chugoku/Shikoku has the lowest b-value in this dataset (around 0.8), meaning large earthquakes are somewhat less rare relative to small ones. This could reflect the influence of the Nankai Trough, which produces infrequent but very large megathrust events. A low b-value is often interpreted as a sign of higher stress accumulation, though the statistics on a small sample demand caution.

What b-values mean in practice: A b-value of 1.0 means that for every 1,000 M4.5 earthquakes, you would expect about 100 at M5.5 and 10 at M6.5. A lower b-value (like 0.8) shifts that ratio: you get proportionally more large events. A higher b-value (like 1.3) means large events are even rarer relative to small ones.

Small multiples as a thinking tool

Edward Tufte popularized the term "small multiples" for this layout: the same chart structure repeated across panels, each showing a different slice of the data. Once you learn to read one panel, you can read all eight. Your eye starts comparing slopes, line lengths, and vertical positions without needing a legend or color key to decode overlapping lines.

For earthquake data, this approach works particularly well. When all regions share the same axes, differences in seismic activity become differences in visual position rather than differences in labeling. Tohoku's line sits far above Kansai's and stretches further to the right. Those are facts about the geology, and the shared visual language makes them obvious at a glance.

Data: USGS earthquake catalog, M4.5+, 2000-2025. Region classification by latitude/longitude bounding boxes. B-values computed by least-squares fit to log10(cumulative count) vs. magnitude.
