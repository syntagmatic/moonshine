Three Datasets Compared

USGS, JMA, and NOAA each record earthquakes differently. Knowing which to reach for matters more than you might think.

If you search for earthquake data, you'll quickly find at least three major sources: the USGS (United States Geological Survey), JMA (Japan Meteorological Agency), and NOAA (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration). Each one exists because a different institution asked a different question about earthquakes.

USGS runs a global seismic monitoring network. Their job is to detect and locate earthquakes worldwide, in near-real-time. JMA does something more focused: they monitor every tremor in and around Japan, down to the faintest rumble, because their mission includes issuing tsunami warnings and earthquake early alerts to the Japanese public. NOAA takes a completely different approach. They curate a historical catalog of significant earthquakes, the ones that caused deaths, damage, or tsunamis, going back centuries.

Same planet. Same earthquakes. Three very different pictures.

The magnitude gap matters. If you ask "how many earthquakes hit Japan last year?" the answer ranges from about 20 (NOAA) to 700 (USGS, M4.5+) to 180,000 (JMA). None of these answers is wrong. They're answers to different versions of the question.

Notice the spike in 2011 across all three lines. The Tohoku earthquake and its thousands of aftershocks show up everywhere. But look at the baseline: JMA consistently records roughly 100,000-200,000 events per year, most of them too small to feel. USGS captures about 600-800 events at M4.5 and above in the Japan region. NOAA might record a handful or none at all in a quiet year.

The 2016 spike in USGS and JMA reflects the Kumamoto earthquake sequence, which generated hundreds of aftershocks above M4.5. NOAA records it as two or three significant events: the mainshock and its immediate destruction.

What Magnitudes Does Each Dataset Capture?

Each dataset slices the magnitude spectrum differently. JMA reaches down to M0 and below, catching the constant low-level seismic hum. USGS sets a practical floor around M2.5 domestically and M4.5 for distant regions like Japan. NOAA doesn't filter by magnitude at all. A M5 that kills someone gets in, but a M6 in the open ocean might not.

Magnitude distribution by dataset (Japan-related events, 2000-2024)

Each histogram shows the fraction of events at each magnitude bin. JMA's distribution peaks at low magnitudes reflecting comprehensive local monitoring. USGS cuts off sharply below M4.5 for this region. NOAA's sparse data spans the full range but with no consistent shape.

The shape of each distribution tells you what the dataset was designed to capture. JMA's curve drops off steeply from left to right, the classic Gutenberg-Richter distribution that seismologists expect. It means Japan's seismic network is sensitive enough to detect the full population of quakes. USGS shows a truncated version, starting at M4.5. NOAA's histogram is almost flat in relative terms, because the selection criterion is human impact, not seismic magnitude.

Which Dataset for Which Question?

There's no best dataset. There's only the right one for the question you're asking.

"How many earthquakes did Japan have this year?"
→
JMA
"How does Japan's seismicity compare to Chile or Turkey?"
→
USGS
"Which earthquakes caused the most deaths in the last century?"
→
NOAA
"Was that shaking I felt last night a real earthquake?"
→
JMA
"What's the global rate of M7+ earthquakes per decade?"
→
USGS
"What magnitude earthquake should I prepare for in a 100-year window?"
→
All three

Matching your question to the right dataset. JMA for local detail, USGS for global comparison, NOAA for human impact, and all three when you need the full picture.

The Tradeoffs

Completeness vs. curation. JMA records everything, which makes it the most complete catalog for Japan but overwhelming if you're trying to study major events. NOAA does the opposite: every entry is hand-curated with casualty and damage data, but you'll miss the 99.9% of earthquakes that didn't make the news.

Local depth vs. global breadth. JMA knows more about Japanese seismicity than anyone, but it won't help you compare Japan to Indonesia. USGS provides global consistency, the same magnitude scale, the same detection threshold, the same data format everywhere, at the cost of missing the small events that JMA catches.

Standardization vs. context. USGS gives you a clean, standardized dataset optimized for seismological analysis. NOAA gives you context: how many people died, how much it cost, whether a tsunami followed. JMA gives you Shindo intensity, the measure that matters most to residents, because it describes what you actually felt, not just what the rock did at the fault.

They're better together. If you're studying earthquake risk in Japan, the most honest approach uses all three: JMA for the full catalog and local intensity, USGS for internationally comparable magnitudes, and NOAA for the historical human cost. No single database answers every question.

When you're exploring earthquake data, the first question to ask is: which dataset am I looking at, and what did it leave out? The answer shapes everything that follows.
