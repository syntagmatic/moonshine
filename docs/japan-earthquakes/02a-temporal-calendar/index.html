Temporal Patterns

Fifteen years of M4.5+ earthquakes in Japan, day by day

Japan sits where four tectonic plates collide, making it one of the most seismically active countries on Earth. A large earthquake triggers aftershocks that can last weeks or months. But are there seasonal patterns? Does the Earth shake more in winter than in summer? A calendar heatmap lets you check at a glance.

How to read this chart

Each small square is a single day. Rows are years (2010 at top, 2025 at bottom) and columns run left to right through the weeks, with month labels across the top. Darker cells mean more M4.5+ earthquakes that day.

Most days see zero to two events. When a large earthquake triggers aftershocks, a streak of consecutive dark cells appears. The most dramatic example is impossible to miss: the Tohoku earthquake and tsunami of March 11, 2011.

Aftershock sequences light up the calendar

Aftershocks follow a predictable statistical pattern called Omori's law: frequency drops off roughly as the inverse of time since the mainshock. On the heatmap, that looks like a streak of dark cells gradually fading back to the background. Three sequences stand out:

2011 Tohoku (M9.1, March 11) The darkest streak on the entire chart. The first day alone recorded over 400 M4.5+ events. The aftershock sequence stayed elevated for months, and activity remained above normal into the following year.
2016 Kumamoto (M7.0, April 16) A pair of damaging earthquakes on April 14th and 16th produced a visible but shorter aftershock streak in the 2016 row.
2024 Noto Peninsula (M7.5, January 1) A devastating New Year's Day earthquake in Ishikawa Prefecture. The sequence shows up as a burst of activity at the very start of the 2024 row.

No seasonal pattern

Unlike typhoons (which peak in late summer) or heavy snow (which clusters in winter), earthquakes show no seasonal cycle. Scan any column of the heatmap, say all the July cells, and you will see about the same background rate as any other month. Tectonic stress accumulates over centuries. The timing of rupture has nothing to do with the season. A bright cell in summer or winter is random, not a pattern.

Worth confirming visually, though. Our brains are wired to find patterns, and after a memorable earthquake people sometimes speculate about "earthquake season." The calendar view makes it easy to see that it is not real.

Why temporal views matter

A map shows where. A timeline shows when. A calendar heatmap folds both dimensions of time, day-of-year and year, into a single view. You can see clustering, check for periodicity (there is none), and watch aftershock sequences decay. Fifteen years of seismic history in one chart, with the moments when the ground would not stop shaking immediately visible.
