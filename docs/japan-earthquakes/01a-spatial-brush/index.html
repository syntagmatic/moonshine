Where the Earth Cracks Open

25 years of earthquakes around Japan, and what their clustering says about the plates underneath

If you have lived in Japan for any length of time, you know the feeling: a low rumble, dishes rattling, a few seconds deciding whether to duck under the table or keep eating. Japan averages about 1,500 earthquakes of magnitude 4+ per year. They do not happen everywhere equally. They cluster.

Spatial clustering is what it sounds like: events that bunch together in space rather than scattering randomly. When earthquakes cluster, they tell you something about the geology below. In Japan's case, they trace the boundaries of four colliding tectonic plates.

What the clusters tell you

Try this: Brush the timeline to select just March 2011. You will see the Tohoku swarm, thousands of dots packed into a tight band off the northeast coast. That band follows the Japan Trench, where the Pacific Plate dives beneath the Okhotsk Plate.

Japan sits where four tectonic plates meet: the Pacific, Philippine Sea, Eurasian, and Okhotsk (North American). Each boundary produces a different style of seismicity:

The Japan Trench (east of Honshu): the Pacific Plate subducts westward beneath the Okhotsk Plate. Most of Japan's largest earthquakes come from here, including the M9.1 Tohoku event. Quakes along this boundary are shallow to moderate depth and very frequent.

The Nankai Trough (south of Shikoku and the Kii Peninsula): the Philippine Sea Plate subducts beneath the Eurasian Plate. Historically this zone produces a great earthquake roughly every 100-150 years, and people are watching it nervously.

The Ryukyu Trench (Okinawa and the southwest islands): another segment of the Philippine Sea Plate boundary, reaching toward Taiwan. You can see a chain of dots stretching southwest.

Deep-focus clusters: brush the map over the Sea of Japan side. Those deep-blue dots at 300-600 km depth? They trace the Wadati-Benioff zone, the subducted slab of the Pacific Plate still generating earthquakes as it sinks into the mantle hundreds of kilometers below.

Try this: Brush a rectangle over the deep-blue dots under central Honshu. Then look at the timeline. Deep quakes are relatively steady over time -- they don't cluster in bursts the way shallow aftershock sequences do. Different depth, different physics.

Why clustering matters

Random noise does not cluster. When earthquakes bunch together in space and time, they reveal structure: a fault, a subduction interface, a volcanic arc. Spatial clustering is one of the first things seismologists look for in a catalog. It separates signal from noise. It identifies which faults are active and where stress is building.

The bidirectional brush here is a simple technique from exploratory data analysis, but it earns its keep. Linking geography and time lets you notice patterns that neither view shows alone. The 2011 Tohoku swarm is obvious in the timeline as a spike, but only the map shows you where those quakes concentrate. The steady rhythm of deep-focus quakes is invisible on the map but clear in the timeline.

Next time the ground shakes, you will know a little more about what is happening beneath you.
