Where the Earth Cracks Open

25 years of earthquakes around Japan, and what spatial clustering reveals about plate tectonics beneath your feet

If you have lived in Japan for any length of time, you already know the feeling: a low rumble, the rattle of dishes, a few seconds of uncertainty while you decide whether to duck under the table or keep eating. Japan averages roughly 1,500 earthquakes of magnitude 4 or greater per year. But they do not happen everywhere equally. They cluster.

Spatial clustering is exactly what it sounds like: events that bunch together in space rather than scattering randomly. When earthquakes cluster, they are telling you something about the geology below. In Japan's case, they trace the boundaries of four colliding tectonic plates.

What the clusters tell you

Try this: Brush the timeline to select just March 2011. You will see the Tohoku earthquake swarm -- thousands of dots packed into a tight band off the northeast coast. That band follows the Japan Trench, where the Pacific Plate dives beneath the Okhotsk Plate.

Japan sits at the intersection of four tectonic plates: the Pacific Plate, the Philippine Sea Plate, the Eurasian Plate, and the Okhotsk (North American) Plate. Each boundary produces its own style of seismicity:

The Japan Trench (east of Honshu) -- the Pacific Plate subducts westward beneath the Okhotsk Plate. This is the source of most of Japan's largest earthquakes, including the M9.1 Tohoku event. The quakes here tend to be shallow to moderate depth and very frequent.

The Nankai Trough (south of Shikoku and Kii Peninsula) -- the Philippine Sea Plate subducts beneath the Eurasian Plate. Historically, this zone produces a great earthquake roughly every 100-150 years.

The Ryukyu Trench (Okinawa and the southwest islands) -- another part of the Philippine Sea Plate boundary, extending toward Taiwan. You can see a chain of dots stretching southwest.

Deep-focus clusters -- brush the map over the Sea of Japan side. Those deep-blue dots at 300-600 km depth? They trace the Wadati-Benioff zone: the subducted slab of the Pacific Plate, still generating earthquakes as it sinks into the mantle hundreds of kilometers below.

Try this: Brush a rectangle over the deep-blue dots under central Honshu. Then look at the timeline. Deep quakes are relatively steady over time -- they don't cluster in bursts the way shallow aftershock sequences do. Different depth, different physics.

Why clustering matters

Random noise doesn't cluster. When earthquakes bunch together in space and time, they are revealing structure: a fault, a subduction interface, a volcanic arc. Spatial clustering is one of the first things seismologists look for when analyzing a catalog. It separates signal from noise and helps identify which faults are active, where stress is building, and which areas face the greatest hazard.

The bidirectional brush you just used is a simple but powerful technique from exploratory data analysis. By linking two views of the same data -- geography and time -- you can notice patterns that neither view reveals alone. The 2011 Tohoku swarm is obvious in the timeline (a spike), but only the map shows you where those quakes concentrate. Conversely, the steady rhythm of deep-focus quakes is invisible on the map but clear in the timeline.

Next time the ground shakes, you will know a little more about what's happening beneath you.
