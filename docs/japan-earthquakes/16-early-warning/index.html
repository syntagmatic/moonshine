Earthquake Early Warning

How Japan's system buys you seconds that save lives

If you've lived in Japan for any length of time, you know the sound. That urgent, warbling tone from your phone, tididididi, tididididi, sometimes arriving seconds before the ground starts to shake. It runs on a surprisingly simple piece of physics.

Two Waves, One Earthquake

When a fault ruptures, it sends out two kinds of seismic waves. P-waves (primary, or compression waves) move fast, about 6 km/s through the crust. They're the first to arrive, but they're relatively gentle: a jolt, a rattling of dishes. S-waves (secondary, or shear waves) are slower, around 3.5 km/s, but they carry the real destructive energy. They're the ones that throw you off your feet and bring down buildings.

The gap between these two waves is the whole game. Every second that the fast-but-weak P-wave is ahead of the slow-but-violent S-wave is a second of warning you can use.

The key insight: The farther you are from the epicenter, the bigger the gap between P-wave and S-wave arrival. Tokyo, 373 km from the 2011 Tohoku epicenter, got roughly a minute of warning. Sendai, just 130 km away, got far less.

How JMA's System Works

Japan's Earthquake Early Warning system, operated by the Japan Meteorological Agency, is among the fastest in the world. The process goes roughly like this:

1. Detect. A dense network of over 1,000 seismometers blankets Japan. When the P-wave from an earthquake reaches the nearest station, it's detected within a fraction of a second.

2. Triangulate. Once three or more stations have picked up the P-wave, the system can triangulate the epicenter and estimate the depth. This happens within about 3–5 seconds of the first detection.

3. Estimate. Using the initial waveform data, algorithms estimate the earthquake's magnitude. This is the trickiest part. Early estimates can be significantly off, especially for very large events. In 2011, the initial estimate was M7.2; the actual magnitude turned out to be M9.0.

4. Predict shaking. Based on magnitude, depth, and distance, the system predicts the seismic intensity at locations across the country. Areas expected to experience shaking of intensity 4 or above on the JMA scale receive warnings.

5. Broadcast. Alerts go out simultaneously through TV, radio, cell phones (that distinctive sound), factory control systems, train operations centers, and elevator controllers. The whole chain from detection to broadcast can take as little as 3 seconds.

The 2011 test: On March 11, 2011, the system detected the Tohoku earthquake and issued warnings before the devastating S-waves reached most of Honshu. Tokyo residents got roughly 60 seconds of warning. Bullet trains along the Tohoku Shinkansen line received automatic stop commands and began braking before the shaking hit. Not a single one derailed. Factories initiated emergency shutdowns. That said, areas closest to the epicenter, including much of the Miyagi coast, had almost no warning time at all.

Limitations Worth Knowing

The system works well, but it has real limits. The most fundamental: if you're close to the epicenter, the P-wave and S-wave arrive almost simultaneously. There's no gap, so there's no warning. For a shallow earthquake directly beneath a city, the phone alert might arrive after the shaking has already started.

Magnitude estimation is another weak point. The system uses the first few seconds of P-wave data to guess how big the earthquake is, but very large earthquakes don't reveal their full size immediately. The initial M7.2 estimate for the 2011 Tohoku event meant early warnings significantly underestimated the shaking intensity. JMA has since updated its algorithms to better handle cascading ruptures.

False alarms happen too, though JMA has worked hard to reduce them. A false alarm that sends factory workers scrambling or stops trains costs money and erodes trust in the system. The balance between speed and accuracy is a constant engineering tension.

Why It Matters

Japan's earthquake early warning system comes from hard-won experience, big public investment, and a culture that takes disaster preparedness seriously. Other countries are building similar systems (Mexico's SASMEX, the US ShakeAlert along the West Coast, systems in China and Taiwan), but Japan's is still the fastest and most comprehensive.

The next time that sound hits your phone, you'll know what's happening in the background: P-waves racing ahead through the crust, seismometers lighting up one by one, algorithms crunching triangulation and magnitude in milliseconds, and a broadcast system pushing the result to every phone in the affected zone. All to buy you those few seconds to get under the table.
