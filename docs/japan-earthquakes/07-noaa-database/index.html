NOAA's Significant Earthquake Database

Four thousand years of seismic history, filtered by human consequence

If you've ever looked up an earthquake on the USGS website, you probably noticed it tells you the magnitude, location, and depth. What it doesn't tell you is how many people died, how many buildings collapsed, or how much the damage cost. That information lives in a different database entirely: NOAA's Significant Earthquake Database, maintained by the National Centers for Environmental Information.

The distinction matters. USGS catalogs every earthquake it can detect, tens of thousands per year worldwide, most too faint for anyone to feel. NOAA's database is smaller and more deliberate. It contains roughly 6,000 earthquakes going back to 2150 BCE, each one selected because it left a mark on human life: deaths, property damage, a tsunami, or sheer seismic force.

For Japan, this database is unusually deep. Japanese historical chronicles (temple records, court diaries, local histories) documented major earthquakes centuries before seismographs existed. The result is one of the longest continuous seismic records of any country on Earth.

A few things stand out. The cluster in the upper-right, large magnitudes in the modern era, partly reflects better detection and record-keeping. But the pre-modern events up in that range are genuinely massive. The 1707 Hoei earthquake (M8.6) triggered the last eruption of Mount Fuji. The 869 Jogan earthquake (M8.6) generated a tsunami that swept kilometers inland on the Sanriku coast.

The dot sizes tell a different story from the magnitudes. The 1923 Great Kanto earthquake was M7.9, not the largest on this chart, but its death toll of roughly 105,000 dwarfs everything else. Urban density and the fires that followed the shaking mattered more than raw seismic energy. And the 1896 Meiji-Sanriku earthquake was only M7.2, but its tsunami killed around 22,000 people. Magnitude alone is a poor predictor of human cost.

The impact fields (deaths, injuries, damage, houses) are what make this database different from a pure seismic catalog. They turn earthquakes from physical measurements into records of what actually happened to people. But they also introduce uncertainty. Death tolls for pre-modern earthquakes are estimates drawn from historical texts that may exaggerate or omit. Damage figures for events before the 20th century are often missing entirely or reconstructed from fragments.

What Counts as "Significant"?

NOAA's criteria for inclusion: An earthquake is "significant" if it meets any one of these conditions: magnitude 7.5 or greater, caused deaths, caused moderate damage (~$1 million or more), or generated a tsunami. This is editorial judgment, not a mechanical threshold. The database is curated by researchers who weigh historical sources and make calls about what belongs.

This curation is a feature, not a bug. A comprehensive catalog of every seismic event would be overwhelming and mostly irrelevant if you're studying earthquake impacts. The NOAA database filters for consequence. But it also means the database reflects what we know about rather than what happened. Earthquakes that struck remote or illiterate populations may be missing entirely. The further back you go, the more the database depends on which civilizations kept records. Japan kept exceptionally good ones.

The Jogan Precedent

The 869 Jogan earthquake is one of the most important entries in the database, though nobody fully appreciated that until 2011. Historical texts describe a massive earthquake off the Sanriku coast, followed by a tsunami that flooded the Sendai plain and drowned about a thousand people (the recorded figure; the actual toll was likely higher).

For most of modern seismology, Jogan was a footnote. Then on March 11, 2011, the Tohoku earthquake struck on the same subduction zone, in a similar location, with a comparable magnitude and an even larger tsunami. Geologists who had studied Jogan's tsunami deposits (sand layers found kilometers inland) had actually warned that such an event was overdue. The pre-2011 seismic hazard models for the Fukushima coast did not fully account for this historical precedent.

This is exactly the kind of connection the NOAA database makes possible. By keeping centuries-old events alongside modern instrumental records, it lets researchers see patterns that a decade or two of seismograph data would miss entirely. Japan's thousand-year seismic chronicle, preserved in a structured database, turned an obscure medieval disaster into a warning that, in hindsight, should have been heeded more seriously.

Limitations Worth Knowing

The database is useful, but it has real gaps. Magnitude estimates for pre-instrumental earthquakes are back-calculated from damage descriptions and tsunami deposits, and they carry large uncertainties. An "M8.4" in the 7th century is a best guess, not a measurement. Death tolls in historical records may lump together earthquake deaths with famine, disease, and fire in the aftermath. Damage figures in dollar terms for pre-modern events are sometimes absent, sometimes reconstructed with rough conversion methods.

The geographic coverage is also uneven. Japan, China, the Mediterranean, and parts of the Middle East have long written traditions that feed the database. Much of the rest of the world has sparse records before the 19th century. The database tells you where literate civilizations experienced earthquakes, which is not the same as where earthquakes happened.

Still, for the questions it's designed to answer (which earthquakes caused the most damage, where do tsunamigenic events recur, how have building practices changed outcomes over time), it's one of the best seismic datasets out there. And for Japan specifically, where the records run deep and the earthquakes never stop, it offers a perspective that no instrument-only catalog can match.
