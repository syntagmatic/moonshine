The JMA Seismic Database

What Japan's earthquake agency records that the rest of the world doesn't

If you've lived in Japan for any length of time, you already know what a "shindo 4" feels like. The NHK chime goes off, your phone buzzes, and the overlay appears on TV: 震度4. You brace. Maybe it rattles the dishes, maybe it doesn't.

But when you look up the same earthquake on the USGS website, it might not even appear. Or if it does, the description is a clinical "M3.8 at 40 km depth," which tells you nothing about why your coffee sloshed out of the mug.

That gap between "what the seismologists measured at the fault" and "what you felt in your apartment" is exactly what the JMA database is built to capture.

What Is JMA?

The Japan Meteorological Agency (気象庁, Kishōchō) is Japan's national agency for weather, volcanoes, tsunamis, and earthquakes. It's been monitoring seismic activity since 1884, making it one of the longest-running earthquake observation programs on Earth.

The USGS operates a global seismic monitoring network optimized for moderate-to-large earthquakes worldwide. JMA does something different: it focuses all its resources on one country that happens to sit on four tectonic plates. The result is a ridiculously dense monitoring network that picks up tiny tremors the global network would never notice.

The Shindo Scale: What You Feel, Not What the Fault Did

This is the big conceptual difference. Magnitude (the Richter-like number) describes the energy released at the earthquake's source. It's the same number no matter where you are. But shindo (震度, seismic intensity) describes what happened at a specific location on the surface. The same earthquake produces different shindo values in different cities, depending on distance, depth, and local geology.

The shindo scale runs from 0 to 7, but with a twist: levels 5 and 6 each split into "lower" and "upper," giving 10 levels total. This split was added after the 1995 Kobe earthquake, because the jump from 5 to 6 was too coarse to communicate real differences in damage.

The Shindo Intensity Scale (震度階級)
Japan's 10-level seismic intensity scale measures ground shaking at a specific point, not energy at the source. JMA instruments calculate shindo automatically from three-axis accelerometer data.
Why shindo matters more than magnitude for daily life: A magnitude 6.0 earthquake at 100 km depth might register shindo 2 (a gentle sway). A magnitude 4.5 at 10 km depth directly below a city can register shindo 5 Upper (furniture toppling, difficulty walking). The number on your TV screen is shindo, not magnitude, because shindo is the one that tells you whether to duck.

How Much More Does JMA See?

JMA operates roughly 4,000 seismic observation stations across Japan, about one for every 95 square kilometers. The USGS Global Seismographic Network, by comparison, has around 150 stations worldwide, supplemented by regional partners. In Japan specifically, the USGS catalog relies on teleseismic data and typically only records events at magnitude 4.0 or above.

The difference in detection is staggering. In a typical year, JMA records about 160,000 earthquakes in and around Japan. The USGS catalogs roughly 700 events of M4.5+ in the same region over the same period.

This isn't a knock on the USGS. It's a matter of mission. The USGS monitors the entire planet. JMA monitors one country with terrifying thoroughness. Both catalogs are good at what they're designed to do.

The Station Network

JMA's seismic network combines its own stations with instruments operated by local governments, NIED (the National Research Institute for Earth Science and Disaster Resilience), and universities. The mesh is so dense that virtually no inhabited area of Japan is more than 20 km from a seismometer.

This density is what enables Japan's Earthquake Early Warning system (緊急地震速報). When the first P-waves hit nearby stations, the system estimates magnitude and location within seconds and broadcasts warnings to areas that haven't yet felt the slower, destructive S-waves. You've probably experienced this: the phone alert arriving 5 to 15 seconds before the shaking.

What's in the Data

A JMA earthquake record typically includes:

Event-level data: origin time, hypocenter (latitude, longitude, depth), magnitude (JMA magnitude scale, similar but not identical to Mw), and region name in Japanese.

Station-level intensity: for each station that detected the event, the measured shindo value. This is what makes the JMA dataset special. A single M5 earthquake might have 500 individual intensity observations across different stations.

Tsunami assessments: whether a tsunami warning was issued and observed wave heights.

Accessing JMA Data

The primary interface is JMA's earthquake database at www.data.jma.go.jp. The website is primarily in Japanese, though some pages have English translations. The data itself is available as CSV and XML downloads covering historical catalogs going back decades.

For English-language access, NIED's www.hinet.bosai.go.jp provides a complementary interface to much of the same underlying seismic data, with English documentation and waveform downloads.

Practical tip: If you want to look up a specific earthquake you just felt, the fastest route is still the JMA website or the Yahoo! Japan earthquake page (earthquake.tenki.jp). The shindo map will show up within 2-3 minutes of the event, color-coded by intensity. It's the same data source as your phone alert.

Limitations to Know

Japan-only: JMA's high-resolution catalog covers Japan and its immediate offshore zones. For earthquakes elsewhere in the Pacific or globally, you'll still need the USGS or IRIS catalogs.

Shindo is not MMI: The JMA intensity scale (shindo) and the Modified Mercalli Intensity scale used by the USGS are conceptually similar. Both measure local shaking effects. But they use different numbers, different calculation methods, and different level definitions. A shindo 5 Lower is not the same as an MMI V. Don't try to convert between them with a simple lookup table.

Language barrier: Detailed catalogs, station metadata, and technical bulletins are in Japanese. Machine translation handles tabular data fine, but the descriptive fields and methodology documents require real reading ability or a good translator.

Magnitude differences: JMA uses its own magnitude scale (Mj) which can differ from the USGS moment magnitude (Mw) by up to ±0.5 for the same event. When comparing catalogs, keep track of which magnitude type you're looking at.

The Bottom Line

If you're investigating earthquakes in Japan, the JMA database gives you far more granular data than any global catalog: more events, station-level intensity readings, and the shindo scale that maps directly to lived experience. It's the difference between reading "M4.2 at 30 km depth" and seeing a map that tells you your neighborhood shook at intensity 4 while three wards over they barely felt it.

For someone living in Japan and learning to work with data tools, JMA is a great dataset to start with. It's large, well-structured, and directly relevant to daily life. Once you get past the language barrier, the documentation is solid too.
