Magnitude Scales Explained

Richter, moment magnitude, and shindo: three ways to measure one earthquake

If you live in Japan, you already know the drill. Your phone screams, the ground moves, and within seconds NHK is showing a map with colored boxes: 震度3 in Saitama, 震度5弱 in Chiba. That number — the shindo — tells you something fundamentally different from the "magnitude 6.2" that international news will report an hour later.

The confusion is understandable. There are at least three widely-used scales for measuring earthquakes, and they each answer a different question. Let's untangle them.

Three scales, three questions

Richter magnitude (ML) was the original. Charles Richter designed it in 1935 for Southern California, using the maximum amplitude recorded on a specific type of seismometer at a specific distance. It worked beautifully for moderate, local quakes. But it "saturates" — anything above about M7 gets compressed, because the instrument's response tops out. A magnitude 8 and a magnitude 9 look confusingly similar on Richter's scale. It's still referenced in everyday conversation, but seismologists stopped relying on it decades ago.

Moment magnitude (Mw) replaced Richter as the standard for scientific use. Instead of measuring what a seismometer needle does, it's based on the physical reality of the fault itself: how large an area ruptured, how far the rock slipped, and how rigid the rock is. Multiply those three quantities and you get the seismic moment. Mw works at all sizes, from micro-tremors to M9+ megathrust events. For small-to-moderate quakes, the numbers come out nearly identical to Richter, which is why people still casually say "Richter scale" when they really mean moment magnitude.

Shindo (震度) is Japan's seismic intensity scale, maintained by the JMA (Japan Meteorological Agency). It measures something entirely different: not the energy released at the source, but the shaking you actually experience at a specific location. Shindo varies across the affected area. A single earthquake produces one magnitude but dozens of different shindo readings. The scale runs from 0 (not felt) to 7 (catastrophic), with subdivisions at 5 and 6: 5弱 (5-lower), 5強 (5-upper), 6弱 (6-lower), 6強 (6-upper), giving 10 levels total.

Why does Japan use its own scale? Because magnitude alone is nearly useless for daily decision-making. An M7.5 quake at 500km depth beneath the Pacific barely rattles your coffee cup. An M6.5 at 10km depth directly beneath your city is terrifying. Shindo answers the question that actually matters to the person gripping the table: how bad is it right here?

The Western equivalent of shindo is the Modified Mercalli Intensity (MMI) scale, which runs from I to XII in Roman numerals. It's conceptually similar — both measure local effects — but MMI is rarely reported in real-time the way shindo is. Japan's dense seismometer network (over 4,000 stations) calculates shindo automatically within seconds, making it practical for the emergency alert system.

The energy staircase

Magnitude is logarithmic. Each whole number increase represents roughly 31.6 times more energy released. That means an M7 releases about 1,000 times more energy than an M5 (31.6 × 31.6). An M9 releases about a million times more than an M5. The 2011 Tohoku earthquake released more energy than all the other earthquakes in Japan's recorded history combined.

Each circle's area is proportional to the energy released. The M9 circle is clipped — at true scale it would fill the page.

Shindo across distance

This is the key insight about intensity vs. magnitude. A single earthquake radiates energy outward from the fault, but shaking decreases with distance. Near the epicenter, you might experience shindo 7 — buildings collapsing. A few hundred kilometers away, the same quake might register shindo 3 — a noticeable sway, nothing more.

What it means in practice

When your phone buzzes with an earthquake early warning, the number it shows you is the estimated shindo at your location, not the magnitude. That's a deliberate design choice. The JMA shindo scale maps directly to expected damage and recommended actions:

ShindoWhat you feel / what happens
0Imperceptible to people; detected only by instruments.
1Felt by some people indoors, especially on upper floors.
2Felt by most people indoors. Hanging lights sway slightly.
3Felt by most people. Dishes rattle; felt outdoors by some.
4Startling. Hanging objects swing widely. Unstable items fall.
5-Frightening. Unsecured furniture may move. Some wall tiles fall.
5+Difficult to walk. Heavy furniture may topple. Unreinforced walls crack.
6-Hard to stand. Doors jam. Wall tiles and windows break widely.
6+Impossible to stand without crawling. Heavy furniture thrown. Some buildings collapse.
7Thrown by shaking. Reinforced concrete buildings may collapse. Landslides.
JMA seismic intensity scale descriptions, simplified.

So the next time an international friend asks "was that a big earthquake?", you can say: "It was magnitude 6.1, but the shindo here was only 3 — I barely noticed it." That distinction, between source energy and local experience, is the whole point of having multiple scales.
