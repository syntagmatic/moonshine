Scale & Frequency

The Gutenberg-Richter law and Japan's earthquake catalog

If you have lived in Japan for any length of time, you know the feeling: a small tremor rolls through, the building sways, and life continues. These modest shakes happen all the time. But every few years, a genuinely large earthquake strikes, and the consequences are in a completely different category.

There is a surprisingly clean mathematical relationship between these two extremes. In 1944, Beno Gutenberg and Charles Richter described it: for every unit increase in magnitude, earthquakes become roughly ten times less frequent. This is the Gutenberg-Richter law, and it holds across almost every seismically active region on the planet.

The plot above is the classic Gutenberg-Richter diagram. The x-axis is magnitude, and the y-axis shows how many earthquakes occurred at or above that magnitude, on a logarithmic scale. The near-straight line is the signature of the GR law.

The b-value. The slope of the GR line is called the b-value. A b-value of 1.0 means exactly a factor-of-ten drop per magnitude unit. Japan's b-value is typically close to 1.0, the global average. Regions under unusual stress or with active volcanic systems sometimes deviate, which gives seismologists a useful diagnostic.

Where the earthquakes happen

Now drag the threshold on the plot above and watch the map below. Start with the threshold high, around magnitude 7. Only a handful of dots appear: the truly major events, scattered across the subduction zones.

As you pull the threshold downward, hundreds and then thousands of smaller events fill in. The pattern that emerges is not random. The dots trace out the plate boundaries: the Pacific Plate diving beneath northeast Japan, the Philippine Sea Plate pushing under the southwest, the Nankai Trough, the Ryukyu arc down toward Okinawa. The geometry of the tectonic system becomes visible, drawn entirely by the earthquakes themselves.

Why this matters

The GR law is more than a tidy graph. It is a forecasting tool. Because the relationship is so regular, seismologists can estimate how often earthquakes of any given size should occur. Japan has recorded around 16,800 earthquakes of M4.5+ in the USGS catalog over the last 25 years, but only about 33 reached M7.0 or above. The GR law predicts this ratio almost exactly.

Of course, the law says nothing about when the next large earthquake will happen, only how often they happen on average. But that statistical regularity is what makes earthquake engineering, insurance models, and preparedness planning possible. The straight line on the log-frequency plot is, in a real sense, a map of the risk you live with.
