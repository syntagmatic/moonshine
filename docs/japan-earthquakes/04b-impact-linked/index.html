Magnitude gets the headlines, but it is a crude predictor of human suffering. A shallow M6.9 under a dense city at dinner time — when gas stoves are lit — can kill far more people than a deep M8.0 offshore. Building codes, soil type, distance from population centers, and time of day all shape whether an earthquake becomes a disaster or a statistic.

Japan offers a compelling case study. Over the past century it has invested more than any other nation in earthquake engineering, early warning systems, and public preparedness. The result is visible in the data: modern earthquakes of similar magnitude kill dramatically fewer people than their early-twentieth-century counterparts. The 1923 Kantō earthquake (M7.9, roughly 105,000 dead) and the 1995 Kobe earthquake (M6.9, 6,434 dead) struck comparably dense areas, yet the death toll differed by an order of magnitude — a testament to decades of improved construction standards.

The glaring outlier is the 2011 Tōhoku earthquake. At M9.1 it was the most powerful earthquake ever recorded in Japan, but the shaking itself caused relatively few structural collapses thanks to modern engineering. The catastrophe came from the tsunami: waves over 10 meters high overwhelmed coastal defenses and killed roughly 19,700 people. Magnitude does matter for tsunamis — larger rupture areas displace more ocean — but even here, the critical variable was not the shaking but the wave.

Why magnitude alone fails

Five factors consistently decouple magnitude from damage:

Depth. Shallow earthquakes (<20 km) concentrate energy near the surface. The 1995 Kobe quake ruptured at just 16 km depth directly beneath the city, producing extreme ground acceleration despite its moderate magnitude.

Distance to population. The 1933 Sanriku earthquake (M8.4) struck offshore and killed roughly 3,000 people, mostly via tsunami. A quake of similar energy beneath central Tokyo would have been orders of magnitude worse.

Soil conditions. Soft alluvial soils amplify shaking. Much of Tokyo, Osaka, and Kobe sit on reclaimed land or river deltas — areas that liquefy during strong shaking, toppling buildings that might survive on bedrock.

Building codes and enforcement. Japan overhauled its seismic code after the 1923 Kantō disaster, again after 1968 Tokachi-Oki, and fundamentally in 1981. Each revision dramatically cut casualties in subsequent quakes of comparable magnitude.

Time of day and season. The 1923 Kantō quake struck at 11:58 AM on September 1st, when charcoal cooking fires were active across the city. The resulting firestorm killed more people than the shaking itself.

Reading the visualization

The map shows each earthquake as a circle. Size encodes magnitude; color encodes deaths — gray for zero, deepening red for larger tolls. The three charts below sort the same events by deaths, economic damage, and chronological position. Brush any view to cross-filter the others: selected earthquakes stay vivid while unselected ones fade to ghosts. This linked highlighting lets you ask questions the data cannot answer in a table — like whether the costliest earthquakes were also the deadliest (often not), or how the timeline of casualties relates to the adoption of modern building codes.
