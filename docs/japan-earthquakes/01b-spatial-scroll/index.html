A four-plate pileup

Japan straddles the boundary of four tectonic plates: the Pacific, Philippine Sea, Eurasian, and North American. No other major country sits at a junction like this.

Each dot on the map is an earthquake of magnitude 4.5 or greater, recorded by the USGS between 2000 and 2025. The pattern is not random. Quakes cluster along the edges where plates grind past, dive under, or collide with each other.

The Pacific Plate: deep and relentless

The Pacific Plate pushes westward at about 8 cm per year, diving beneath northern Honshu and Hokkaido along the Japan Trench. This subduction zone produces the deepest and most powerful quakes in the region.

Many of these events happen 40 to 100 km below the surface. Deep enough that you might feel a long, rolling motion in Tokyo rather than a sharp jolt. The highlighted quakes trace the trench from Hokkaido down to the Kanto region.

The Philippine Sea Plate: the Nankai threat

Southwest of the Kanto region, the Philippine Sea Plate slides beneath the Eurasian Plate along the Nankai Trough and extends south through the Izu-Bonin island arc.

This is where Japan's next anticipated megaquake will likely come from. Historical records show M8+ Nankai Trough earthquakes recurring every 100 to 150 years. The last was in 1946. Seismologists consider another one overdue.

Inland quakes: shallow and destructive

Not all dangerous earthquakes happen offshore. Shallow crustal quakes under 30 km depth strike directly beneath populated areas. Their shaking can be devastating even at moderate magnitudes.

The 2024 Noto Peninsula earthquake (M7.5, highlighted here), the 2004 Niigata quakes, the 2008 Iwate-Miyagi event: all ruptured along inland faults. These are harder to forecast because the faults are smaller and less well-mapped than the major plate boundaries.

2011 Tohoku: a seismic avalanche

On March 11, 2011, a M9.0 earthquake ruptured a 500-km stretch of the Japan Trench. The aftershock sequence was enormous: hundreds of M4.5+ events in the following months, a dense swarm across the offshore zone.

If you were in Japan that day, you remember the ground not stopping. Aftershocks came every few minutes for weeks. This cluster alone accounts for a quarter of all quakes in our dataset.

The full picture

All four plate boundaries shown together. The color coding reveals the structure: blue for the Pacific Plate's deep subduction, orange for the Philippine Sea system, green for shallow inland ruptures, red for the 2011 Tohoku swarm.

Living in Japan means living with this geology. The building codes, the emergency drills, the earthquake early warning on your phone: they exist because of these four colliding plates.

Why spatial clustering matters. Earthquake hazard is not evenly distributed. The clusters on this map directly inform where Japan invests in reinforced infrastructure, where tsunami walls go up, and which regions receive early warnings first. These patterns are the basis of preparedness.
