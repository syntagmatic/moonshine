Tsunami Propagation

How the 2011 Tohoku earthquake sent waves across an entire ocean

At 14:46 local time on March 11, 2011, a magnitude 9.0 earthquake ruptured a 500-kilometer stretch of the Japan Trench, roughly 70 km off the coast of Miyagi Prefecture. The quake itself was devastating. But the tsunami it generated killed more than 15,000 people and triggered the Fukushima Daiichi meltdown. What happened between the seafloor rupturing and 40-meter waves slamming into the Sanriku coast comes down to physics that, once you see it, is hard to forget.

How a Tsunami Forms

A tsunami is not a surface wave like wind-driven swells. It is a shallow water wave, a pulse that moves the entire water column from seafloor to surface. It starts when a large section of seafloor is abruptly displaced upward (or downward), shoving the water above it like a piston. The displaced water then radiates outward as a wave that carries huge energy because the entire depth of the ocean is in motion.

Cross-section of tsunami generation. A shallow thrust fault lifts the seafloor, displacing the water column above it, which then radiates outward as waves.

The key detail: the fault must be shallow (near the seafloor) and produce vertical displacement. The 2011 Tohoku rupture occurred at a depth of about 24 km on a thrust fault, the Pacific Plate diving beneath the Okhotsk Plate. Portions of the seafloor shifted upward by as much as 5 to 8 meters over an area roughly the size of Kyushu. That vertical displacement is what matters.

Speed Depends on Depth

Here is the single equation that governs tsunami propagation:

v = √(g × d)

where g is gravitational acceleration (9.8 m/s²) and d is ocean depth. In the open Pacific, with average depths around 4,000 meters, this gives roughly 200 m/s, about 700 km/h. That's the speed of a commercial jet. Near the coast, where depths drop to 10 meters, the wave slows to about 36 km/h, roughly the speed of a bicycle.

Why you can't see a tsunami in the open ocean. Out in deep water, a tsunami is perhaps 1 meter tall with a wavelength of 200 km or more. The surface rises and falls by one meter over a distance greater than the width of Tokyo. A ship in the open Pacific would barely notice it passing beneath. It only becomes a monster as it enters shallow water.

Propagation Across the Pacific

The 2011 tsunami radiated outward from the epicenter at 38.3°N, 142.4°E. Within 30 minutes, the first waves reached the nearest coastline, the Sanriku coast of northeastern Honshu. Over the following hours, the wavefront crossed the Pacific Basin, reaching Hawaii about 7 hours after the quake, the U.S. West Coast around 10 hours, and the coast of Chile roughly 22 hours later.

The wavefront is not a perfect circle. Bathymetry (the shape of the ocean floor) bends and distorts it. Waves travel faster over deep ocean trenches and slower over ridges and seamounts. The Emperor Seamount Chain and the Mid-Pacific Mountains acted as lenses, focusing energy toward Hawaii and deflecting it away from other areas. But the concentric-ring model captures the essential picture: outward propagation at jet speed.

Shoaling: The Wave Transforms

As a tsunami enters shallow water near the coast, it undergoes a transformation called shoaling. The front of the wave slows down (shallower water = lower speed), but the back of the wave is still in deeper water and moving faster. The wave compresses. Its wavelength shrinks from hundreds of kilometers to just a few. And all that energy, which was spread vertically through 4 km of water column, gets squeezed into a few meters of depth. The wave height can amplify by a factor of 10 to 40.

As the tsunami moves from deep ocean to shallow coast, it slows down, compresses, and grows dramatically in height. The deep-ocean wave is essentially invisible; the coastal wave is catastrophic.

On March 11, the Tohoku tsunami arrived at parts of the Sanriku coast as a wall of water exceeding 40 meters in height. At Miyako, the run-up reached 38.9 meters. These heights were not just the result of shoaling. They were amplified by the geography of the coast itself.

The Sanriku Coast: Geography as Amplifier

The Sanriku coast of northeastern Honshu has a distinctive ria topography: deep, narrow, V-shaped bays carved by rivers and then drowned by rising sea levels. When a tsunami enters one of these bays, the converging walls funnel and focus the wave energy into an increasingly narrow space. The wave height grows far beyond what open-coast shoaling alone would produce.

This is not new. The same geography amplified tsunamis in 1896 (Meiji Sanriku, 38.2 m) and 1933 (Showa Sanriku, 28.7 m). The Sanriku coast has the highest tsunami risk in Japan because it faces the Japan Trench directly and has ria bays that act as natural amplifiers.

The 30-minute window. The Japan Trench lies only 70 to 200 km off the Pacific coast of Tohoku. After a major trench earthquake, coastal communities may have as little as 15 to 30 minutes before the first tsunami arrives. Japan's early warning system issued an alert within 3 minutes of the 2011 quake, but the initial estimate severely underestimated the wave height (3 to 6 m predicted; 10 to 40 m actual). The system was later redesigned to better handle magnitude-9 events.

Why Not Every Earthquake Makes a Tsunami

Earthquakes happen constantly along plate boundaries. But only a specific type produces dangerous tsunamis. Three conditions matter: the fault must be shallow, it must be a thrust (or normal) fault that produces vertical motion, and the earthquake must be large enough to displace a significant area of seafloor.

Three fault types compared. Only shallow thrust faults produce the vertical seafloor displacement that generates major tsunamis.

The 2011 Tohoku earthquake checked every box: magnitude 9.0, shallow depth (24 km), thrust mechanism on the subduction interface, rupture area of roughly 500 × 200 km. It was, in seismological terms, the perfect tsunami generator.

Deep earthquakes, even large ones, pose less tsunami risk because the rupture occurs far below the seafloor and the deformation is absorbed by the rock above before it reaches the ocean floor. Strike-slip faults (like the San Andreas) move rock horizontally past each other, which barely disturbs the water column.

Japan's Position

Japan sits at the intersection of four tectonic plates, but the tsunami hazard is concentrated on the Pacific coast. The Japan Trench and the Nankai Trough are both subduction zones where oceanic plates dive beneath Japan, producing the shallow thrust fault geometry that generates tsunamis. The Pacific coast of Tohoku faces the trench at close range. The Sea of Japan side has no comparable subduction trench and faces much lower tsunami risk.

The threat is directional. It comes from the east, from the trench, and it arrives fast. The warning time is brutally short for the nearest coast, and the ria geography of Sanriku amplifies what arrives. The 2011 disaster was not a surprise in the geological sense. It was the latest in a pattern stretching back centuries. What was underestimated was the maximum size.

The physics, at least, is straightforward. A shallow thrust quake shoves the seafloor up. The ocean transmits that shove at jet speed. The coast compresses it into a wall. Everything else, the warning systems, the seawalls, the evacuation routes, is an attempt to work within the constraints of that physics.
