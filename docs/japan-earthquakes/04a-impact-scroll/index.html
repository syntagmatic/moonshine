Human Impact

A century of earthquakes in Japan, what happened, and what changed

Japan sits at the junction of four tectonic plates. Earthquakes are not rare here. They are constant. The country records roughly 1,500 perceptible tremors each year.

But certain quakes have been turning points. Each exposed weaknesses in building codes, emergency response, or hazard planning, and each drove concrete changes. This is the story of those turning points, told through the people they affected and the policies they produced.

A Century of Seismic Turning Points

The timeline shows seven earthquakes that each changed how Japan prepares for and responds to seismic disaster.

Every dot is lives lost. The size reflects the casualty count. Scroll through each one.

1923

Great Kanto Earthquake

At 11:58 a.m. on September 1, a magnitude 7.9 earthquake struck the Kanto plain just before lunch. Cooking fires across Tokyo and Yokohama ignited a firestorm that burned for two days. Roughly 105,000 people died, most from fire, not from buildings falling on them.

The destruction was so total that it reshaped Tokyo's physical layout. The city was rebuilt with wider roads and firebreaks.

This is where modern earthquake engineering in Japan begins. It produced the first seismic design requirements in building codes and established September 1 as national Disaster Prevention Day.
1948

Fukui Earthquake

A magnitude 7.1 earthquake struck Fukui Prefecture while the region was still rebuilding from wartime bombing. 3,769 people died. Newly built structures collapsed alongside old ones, making it obvious that informal rebuilding practices were not enough.

The double blow of war damage and earthquake damage made the human cost far worse than the magnitude alone would suggest.

Directly prompted the Building Standard Law of 1950, Japan's first comprehensive national building code. Every seismic regulation since then builds on this law.
1995

Great Hanshin Earthquake (Kobe)

At 5:46 a.m. on January 17, a magnitude 6.9 earthquake struck directly beneath Kobe. 6,434 people died, many in the collapse of older wooden buildings and mid-rise concrete structures built before the 1981 code revision.

The Hanshin Expressway, an elevated highway considered modern and safe, toppled sideways. It became the defining image of the disaster. The government's initial response was widely criticized as slow and confused.

Kobe exposed a dangerous gap: buildings from before the 1981 code revision were far more vulnerable than anyone had acted on. Massive retrofit programs followed, along with a complete overhaul of disaster response coordination.
2004

Chuetsu Earthquake

A magnitude 6.8 earthquake in Niigata Prefecture triggered landslides that buried roads and cut off mountain communities. 68 people died. A Joetsu Shinkansen bullet train derailed at speed, the first derailment in the system's 40-year history.

That no passengers died in the derailment came down to structural luck and good track design, not any detection system.

The derailment accelerated development of the early earthquake detection and automatic braking system now standard on all Shinkansen lines. Landslide hazard mapping was expanded too.
2011

Tohoku Earthquake and Tsunami

The magnitude 9.1 earthquake on March 11 was the most powerful ever recorded in Japan. But the tsunami killed far more people than the shaking did. Waves up to 40 meters high reached 10 kilometers inland. 19,759 people died.

The tsunami also triggered the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear disaster, the worst nuclear accident since Chernobyl. Entire coastal towns were erased. The scale exceeded every model and every seawall.

Tohoku broke a core assumption: that past earthquakes set the upper bound for future ones. Probabilistic hazard models were rebuilt from scratch. Nuclear safety regulations were rewritten, and massive seawall construction programs began.
2016

Kumamoto Earthquakes

On April 14, a magnitude 6.5 foreshock hit Kumamoto. Residents sheltered in buildings deemed safe. Then, 28 hours later, a magnitude 7.3 mainshock struck and collapsed structures that had been weakened but not visibly damaged. 273 people died.

Kumamoto Castle, a 400-year-old landmark, lost stone walls and turrets. It was a visible measure of the quake's force.

The double-mainshock pattern broke the standard assumption that the largest shock comes first. Shelter-in-place guidance and post-quake building assessment procedures were both revised.
2024

Noto Peninsula Earthquake

On New Year's Day 2024, a magnitude 7.6 earthquake struck the remote Noto Peninsula in Ishikawa Prefecture. At least 245 people died, with severe damage concentrated in older wooden buildings in rural towns.

The peninsula's narrow roads were blocked by landslides and collapsed buildings, isolating communities for days. The region's aging population made evacuation and rescue particularly difficult.

Noto put a sharp point on a growing problem: Japan's rural areas have aging infrastructure and shrinking populations. Retrofitting before a disaster and recovering after one are both harder in places where fewer people live and less money flows.

The Pattern

Each of these earthquakes revealed something the existing system had missed: firestorms, inadequate codes, pre-1981 buildings, landslide risk, tsunamis beyond any model, sequential shocks, rural vulnerability.

Japan's seismic safety system was never designed once and left in place. It was rebuilt, repeatedly, after failure. The building code has been significantly revised at least five times since 1923. Emergency response has been restructured after every major event.

Disaster, investigation, reform. No country has more experience learning from earthquakes. The cost of that education has been measured in lives.
