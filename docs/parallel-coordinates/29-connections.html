Connections

Twenty-eight explorations have passed through this representation. Some patterns emerge that weren't obvious at the start.

This series began with a single observation. Alfred Inselberg, in the 1980s, noticed that if you draw the axes of an N-dimensional coordinate system side by side instead of perpendicular, and connect the values with line segments, you obtain a representation where a point becomes a polyline and a line becomes a point. This is the duality explored in article 1. Everything that came after followed from it.

For the first four articles, we stayed close to the foundations: what crossings encode, how hyperplanes leave structured signatures, why axis permutations change what you can see. Then we moved outward: clusters, outliers, containment, brushing. Then into applied domains — process control, diagnosis, aircraft separation, configuration spaces, and multiobjective fronts.

Articles 14 through 17 pushed into the fourth dimension: regular polytopes, symmetry, topological invariants, and finally the uncomfortable question of what parallel coordinates can and cannot do as dimensions multiply. And then articles 18 through 28 pushed the representation into its strangest territory: E8 and the other exceptional root systems, the Leech lattice, Monstrous Moonshine, the Golay code, quasicrystals, the permutohedron and associahedron, Cayley graphs, knot invariants, error-correcting codes, and the Birkhoff polytope.

Along the way, I kept running into things that had nothing to do with Inselberg's original concern but could be restated, almost cleanly, as parallel coordinates. What follows is the set of such observations. Some are conjectural, some are precise, and one or two are playful. All of them emerged from the same quarter–turn that started the series: take the axes, lay them down side by side, and see what happens.

The generative nature of a mathematical idea is measured by how many places, not its own, it keeps showing up.

Seven Connections That Emerged

Connection 1

Brushing is Lattice Projection

The most surprising identity in the series. Brushing on a parallel coordinates display is the operation of constraining one axis to a sub-interval — a filter. The cut-and-project method for building quasicrystals is the operation of selecting lattice points whose perpendicular-space coordinates fall inside an acceptance window. These are the same thing.

In the cut-and-project view, you split the coordinates of an N-dimensional lattice into a "physical" part (the coordinates you will project onto) and a "perpendicular" part (the coordinates you will constrain). A lattice point is admitted if its perpendicular coordinates land inside a bounded window. If the projection direction is irrational relative to the lattice basis, the projected points form a quasicrystal. What looks aperiodic in the physical space is not aperiodic at all — it is a range constraint in the full space.

This is, precisely, axis brushing. Take a lattice and draw its points in 2-axis parallel coordinates. Brush the perpendicular axis to a narrow window. The surviving polylines correspond to lattice points whose perpendicular coordinate lies in the brushed range. Project their physical coordinate onto a line. You have just computed a Fibonacci chain by brushing.

The equivalence. In the cut-and-project formulation, the window is "the acceptance region in perpendicular space." In brushing, the window is "the selected range on an axis." The word changes, the operation does not. Mathematicians call one side of this filtering and the other side aperiodic tiling theory, but there is nothing between them.

Connection 2

Indexed Points Encode Combinatorial Structure

From article 1: the intersection points between adjacent-axis polyline segments — Inselberg's indexed points — encode lines in N-space. A set of indexed points characterizes a line completely. Articles 14 through 17 showed that for polytopes, the indexed points cluster, and the clusters are the polytope's combinatorial structure: edges, faces, symmetry orbits.

But the observation runs further. For any structured point set, the indexed points between adjacent axes form a distinctive texture. Random data produces a cloud. The tesseract produces a cross-pattern, because coordinates only take the values . The E8 root system produces dense concentric arcs, because every root has length and the pairwise angles take only a handful of values. A Hamming code's 16 codewords produce a tight lattice of isolated points, because the coordinates are binary and the constraints between axes are linear.

Each of these textures is effectively a fingerprint. You could, in principle, show someone the indexed-point distribution without telling them what the data is, and they could guess the structure. Not exactly — but at the level of type.

There is a sense in which this is the original duality wearing a second hat. The indexed-point locations are the coordinates of the dual object — of the line, or the combinatorial structure containing that line — in a derived space. And a structured set of lines has, correspondingly, a structured set of dual points.

Connection 4

Sphere Packing is Density in PC Space

The 240 roots of E8 are the kissing vectors of the densest 8-dimensional sphere packing. The 196,560 minimal vectors of the Leech lattice are the kissing vectors of the densest 24-dimensional packing. Both numbers are optimal; both were proved so by Maryna Viazovska and collaborators in 2016. These two lattices occupy their respective dimensions the way a crystal occupies space: as efficiently as possible.

In parallel coordinates, this optimality shows up as a density pattern. The coordinate values at each axis are not uniformly distributed — they concentrate on a finite set. For E8, the coordinates live in ; the marginal distribution on any axis is a handful of spikes. For the Leech lattice (scaled to have integer entries), the coordinates live in with particular frequencies imposed by the underlying Golay code structure.

The density of polylines passing through a value on an axis reflects how often that value appears among the kissing vectors. And the joint density between adjacent axes reflects the constraint structure of the lattice. Sphere packing is a density condition, and parallel coordinates displays density directly.

Connection 5

Group Structure Appears as Bundle Structure

Repeating through the later articles: the algebraic structure of a group shows up in parallel coordinates as visual bundles. The 24 elements of S4, drawn as polylines with each axis encoding a position of the permuted list, split into five bundles by conjugacy class — identity, transpositions, double transpositions, 3-cycles, 4-cycles. The 120 elements of split into seven bundles, one per partition of 5. Each bundle has the size predicted by the group-theoretic formula for conjugacy class size, and the polylines within a bundle share a characteristic pattern.

The same principle plays out in root systems. The 240 roots of E8 split into orbits under the Weyl group. Integer-coordinate and half-integer-coordinate roots form different bundles with different textures. For monstrous moonshine, the coefficients of the McKay-Thompson series bundle by Monster conjugacy class, each class contributing its own characteristic sequence of numbers.

A bundle in parallel coordinates is a cluster of polylines that track together. A conjugacy class in group theory is a set of elements that are related by an internal symmetry. The two things look the same on screen not by coincidence but because conjugate elements really are interchangeable from the inside, and that interchangeability translates cleanly to polyline similarity.

Connection 6

Duality Runs Everywhere

Inselberg's point-line duality from article 1 is one example of a much broader pattern. Every area this series touched has its own notion of dual, and every notion of dual leaves a visible relationship in parallel coordinates.

  • Polytopes. The tesseract and 16-cell are dual. The tesseract has 16 vertices, 32 edges, 24 faces, 8 cells; the 16-cell has 8, 24, 32, 16. The polyline structures are mirror images of each other.
  • Codes. A linear error-correcting code has a dual code defined by the parity-check matrix. The Hamming code's dual is the simplex code.
  • Lattices. The Leech lattice is self-dual. E8 is self-dual. Self-duality corresponds to a pattern invariant under the duality transformation.
  • Lie theory. The Dynkin diagram of a root system has an involution that sends simple roots to other simple roots. The involution is the Lie-theoretic duality.
  • Knots. Every knot has a mirror. Most invariants agree on a knot and its mirror; a few (the signature, the Jones polynomial under ) distinguish them.

The interesting thing is not that each subject has a duality. The interesting thing is that the parallel coordinates representation of each duality looks, in its own way, like the representation of every other. Each is a reflection or a permutation or an inversion of the polyline structure. You learn to see duality as a style of operation.

Connection 7

Parallel Coordinates is a Functor

A half-serious observation. The parallel coordinates transformation takes a category of geometric objects — points, lines, flats, polytopes, embedded structures — and produces a category of other geometric objects — polylines, indexed points, polyline families, dual patterns. The transformation preserves some things and breaks others.

What it preserves: incidence (two polylines that represent incident flats intersect at the predicted indexed point), dimension (a flat of dimension in N-space becomes a structure in parallel coordinates with the same intrinsic dimension), and — crucially — the relation "lies on the same line." What it breaks: the continuity of rotation (a rotation of the source becomes a nonlocal transformation of the target), and the simplicity of translation (a translation moves all indexed points in a correlated way).

If we were being strict about the category-theoretic sense of functor, we would have to state which morphisms are preserved. In Inselberg's setting, the answer is: projective transformations on the source side correspond, under a specific duality, to projective transformations on the target side. The parallel coordinates transformation intertwines the two. This is exactly the statement that there is a self-functor on the category of projective flats, and that parallel coordinates computes that functor.

Is this a useful way to think? I am not sure. But it suggests a research direction: look for other visualization primitives that behave as functors on geometric categories. Each one would give a different dual and a different way of seeing. The Radon transform is one. The stereographic projection is another. Parallel coordinates is a third. A systematic study of "functorial visualization" would catalog the class.

What Didn't Work

Not every structure fit. Writing the later articles, I kept running into walls. Some of them are worth naming, because each one suggests a limit of the representation and a direction for what to do next.

Density. The Leech lattice has 196,560 minimal vectors. You cannot draw them as polylines. We sampled 500 and showed the rest as statistical aggregates. For objects of this density, parallel coordinates reduces to a density texture — essentially a picture of the marginal distributions. Most of the combinatorial structure is invisible. We need a different representation for dense discrete sets.

Size of the data matrix. Monstrous moonshine involves a character table. Parallel coordinates with 194 axes is unreadable — beyond approximately 12 axes, individual polylines cease to be distinguishable. We showed fragments: low-order Fourier coefficients, selected conjugacy classes. The full object was always behind a wall.

High dimension. Even E8, at 8 dimensions, is near the limit of what the representation can show clearly. Beyond dimension 12 or so, polylines crowd together and the eye cannot follow individual lines. The curse of dimensionality is not specific to parallel coordinates — it is a general perceptual limit — but parallel coordinates reveals it early.

Complex values. Representation theory and modular-form theory use complex numbers as first-class citizens. A character value is a complex number; a Fourier coefficient is a complex number; a Hermitian inner product takes complex values. Parallel coordinates natively shows real values. Showing complex values requires a choice: modulus and phase as two separate axes, real and imaginary as two separate axes, or something more creative. None of the choices are canonical, and all of them double the axis count.

Continuous objects. Smooth manifolds, Lie groups as topological spaces, fundamental groups and their covering spaces — these are continuous or topological objects, not sets of discrete points with numerical coordinates. We can sample them, but the sample is not the object. For continuous structures, the representation is a lossy approximation.

What Could Come Next

The explorations in this series are not complete. Each of the limitations above suggests a direction — an extension or a replacement or a research question. I am going to list the ones I think are most tractable.

Scaling to hundreds of axes. The representation needs to degrade gracefully when dimensions grow. One approach is hierarchical grouping: axes are collected into named sub-blocks, and users can drill into a block to see its internal structure. Another is attention-based display: highlight the three or four axes most relevant to a selected polyline, and dim the rest. A third is the use of coupled mini-maps that show the full axis set at low resolution and a detail view at high resolution. None of these are new ideas in visualization generally, but they are not well-developed for parallel coordinates specifically.

Parallel complex coordinates. A natural extension. For each axis, instead of a real value, show a complex value — a point in the complex plane. Polylines become chains of 2D points. The duality has to be reworked: a point is no longer a polyline in the plane, it is a polyline in complex space. This may or may not be a useful representation for character tables and modular forms.

Group-equivariant parallel coordinates. If the underlying data has a known symmetry group, the visualization should respect it. An equivariant layout would arrange axes so that the group action becomes a visible cyclic, reflective, or swap symmetry in the polyline pattern. For root systems, this would mean arranging the axes in the order of a Coxeter element's action, so that the rotation of the Weyl group becomes a visible rotation in parallel coordinates.

Topological parallel coordinates. Move beyond points in to chains and cycles of a simplicial complex. A polyline would represent not a point but a path in the complex. Intersections would represent boundary relations. This would push parallel coordinates toward homology, and might give a visual language for persistent homology in applied topology.

Inverse problems. Given an unfamiliar polyline pattern, can we reconstruct the mathematical structure that produced it? The connections in this article suggest that such reconstruction is feasible for well-known structures — the textures of the tesseract and E8 and the Hamming code are distinguishable. Could we train a classifier on parallel coordinates images to recognize mathematical objects? Probably. Would it be useful? Maybe — for an applied mathematician encountering an unfamiliar dataset, a classifier that said "this looks like a root system of type " could be a real assist.

The Journey

One way to measure a mathematical idea is to look at how often it reappears in places far from where it started. By that measure, the Inselberg duality is an excellent idea. Started as a visualization trick for correlation analysis. Picked up a general theory of hyperdimensional geometry. Showed up in aircraft separation, robotics, process control, and diagnosis. Turned out to be the natural language for describing regular polytopes in the fourth dimension. Began to make friends with root systems, quasicrystals, codes, lattices, groups, and knots.

Figure 7. The series arc. Six phases, twenty-nine articles, one duality.

None of this required anything beyond the original observation. Inselberg saw, in the 1980s, that you could draw the axes side by side and connect the values with a line. He wrote down the convergence-point formula and worked out that projective transformations map cleanly through the duality. And that was enough.

The representation is generative. It keeps finding new uses. Not because parallel coordinates is a universal representation — it is not, as what-didn't-work shows — but because the underlying duality connects two families of geometric objects in a way that turns out to be useful almost everywhere. When a structure has a set of points or lines that obey certain constraints, parallel coordinates lets you see the constraints.

And when you can see the constraints, you can see the structure. That, in the end, is what this series has been about.

Twenty-nine articles. One duality. Everything else is consequence.
