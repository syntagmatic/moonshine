Diagnosis

Medical measurements form different profiles for different conditions. The classification boundary appears as the gap between polyline bundles.

A patient arrives with a set of numbers: blood pressure, heart rate, cholesterol, ST depression on the electrocardiogram. Each number alone is ambiguous. A resting blood pressure of 140 might be perfectly normal for one person and alarming for another. Diagnosis is inherently multidimensional: the doctor evaluates the entire profile, not any single measurement.

In parallel coordinates, each patient becomes a polyline that visits every measurement axis. A healthy patient traces one profile; a patient with heart disease traces a different one. When you plot hundreds of patients at once, the two groups form two bundles. The bundles overlap on some axes and separate on others. The gap between them is the classification boundary, the geometric trace of the decision surface that separates healthy from diseased.

This is the applied face of the geometric concepts from explainer 05 (clusters), explainer 06 (outliers), and explainer 07 (containment). Clusters are diagnoses. Outliers are unusual presentations. The classification boundary is a hyperplane.

Figure 1. Parallel coordinates plot of 303 heart disease patients. The two diagnostic classes form overlapping but distinguishable bundles. Notice where the bundles separate (max heart rate, oldpeak, vessels) and where they overlap (cholesterol).

The two bundles are not cleanly separated on every axis. Age and cholesterol show heavy overlap between the classes. But look at max heart rate: healthy patients tend to reach higher values, pulling their polylines upward on that axis. Oldpeak (ST depression) is higher in disease patients, pulling their lines upward there. And the number of major vessels shows a dramatic difference: healthy patients cluster at 0, while disease patients spread across 1, 2, and 3.

Discriminating Dimensions

Not all axes contribute equally to distinguishing the classes. We can quantify each axis's discriminating power using Cohen's d, the difference in class means normalized by the pooled standard deviation:

A Cohen's d near zero means the two classes overlap almost completely on that axis. A large absolute d means the axis separates the classes well. The bar chart below shows the separation score for each axis. Click "Sort by separation" to reorder the parallel coordinates axes so the most discriminating dimensions sit next to each other, concentrating the visual separation.

Figure 2. Parallel coordinates with a bar chart of Cohen's d separation scores. The axes with the highest bars are the most diagnostically useful. Sorting by separation groups the discriminating axes together, making the class boundary more visible.

Axis order matters for diagnosis. When the most discriminating axes are adjacent (as in explainer 04), the crossing patterns between classes become more pronounced. Reordering by separation score is a concrete application of the axis-ordering principle.

Classifying a New Patient

Given the two class bundles, a new patient can be classified by asking: which bundle is closer? Use the sliders below to set a new patient's measurements. The bold polyline shows the patient's profile against the faded background of both classes. A nearest-centroid classifier computes the Euclidean distance (on standardized axes) to each class centroid and assigns the patient to the closer group.

Figure 3. A new patient's polyline (bold black) against the faded class bundles. Adjust the sliders to move the polyline and watch the nearest-centroid prediction update in real time. The decision boundary is the hyperplane equidistant from the two centroids.

The classification boundary here is a hyperplane in six-dimensional space, equidistant from the two class centroids. From explainer 03, we know that hyperplanes leave a specific geometric signature in parallel coordinates. The decision surface is not visible as a single line; it is the implicit boundary embedded in the gap between the two bundles.

Brushing for Subgroups

Diagnosis is not uniform across all patients. Age modifies the clinical picture: the same cholesterol level carries different risk at 35 than at 70. Brushing the age axis, as introduced in explainer 08, lets us slice the data into age-based subgroups and observe how the diagnostic profiles shift.

Drag to create a brush on the Age axis. Drag the brush up and down to select different age ranges.

Figure 4. Brushing on the age axis selects a subgroup. Young patients (low age range) show different class separation patterns than old patients. The discriminating axes shift as the population changes.

Young patients who develop heart disease tend to have more extreme values on the discriminating axes, because disease at a young age is rarer and requires stronger risk factors. Older patients show more overlap between classes: age itself becomes a risk factor, blurring the distinction on other axes. This is the geometric expression of a well-known clinical principle: the diagnostic profile is age-dependent.

Subgroup analysis is hyperplane slicing. Brushing the age axis restricts the data to a slab in 6D space. The class separation within that slab may be stronger or weaker than in the full dataset. Different slabs reveal different diagnostic signatures, which is why clinical guidelines often stratify by age.
