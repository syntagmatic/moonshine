Cayley Graphs of Finite Groups

Each group element becomes a vertex. Each generator becomes an edge color. The algebra turns into a graph you can walk.

A finite group is an algebraic object: a set with a multiplication, an identity, and inverses. You can write down its Cayley table. You can enumerate its subgroups, its conjugacy classes, its representations. But you cannot, naively, see it. A Cayley graph changes that. Fix a generating set , pick the group elements as vertices, and draw an edge from to whenever . The whole group becomes a combinatorial object: a graph whose vertices are labelled by group elements and whose edges are colored by generators.

The graph is not a decoration. It is the group's geometry. Distance in the graph is the word metric: the minimum number of generators needed to spell out a group element. Conjugacy classes show up as clusters. Cycles in the graph are relations among the generators. Normal subgroups appear as quotient maps that collapse entire sheets of the graph. And for the classical families — symmetric groups, dihedral groups, quaternion groups, alternating groups — the Cayley graph is often a well-known polytope, a prism, or a near-icosahedron. The choice of generating set is a choice of presentation, and different presentations give different graphs, but the same underlying group.

This article walks through the Cayley graphs of six finite groups, from the hexagon of up to the 120-vertex graph of . Along the way the permutohedron reappears as the Cayley graph of under adjacent transpositions, and the regular polytopes re-emerge as Cayley graphs of their own symmetry groups. The last two figures connect the graph-theoretic picture to the parallel coordinates view: conjugacy classes appear as bundles of polylines, and the word metric appears as a histogram.

Figure 2 — S4 in Parallel Coordinates

Four objects have 24 permutations. With the three adjacent transpositions , the Cayley graph has 24 vertices and 36 edges. Each vertex has three neighbors, one for each generator. The graph is the edge skeleton of the truncated octahedron — the permutohedron from article 24.

In parallel coordinates, each of the 24 permutations becomes a polyline over four axes (the four positions). Two polylines represent adjacent vertices in the Cayley graph when they differ by a single adjacent transposition — that is, when they agree on all but two consecutive positions, and the two disagreeing positions are a swap of a pair whose values differ by one from each other's positions. Because each transposition is an odd permutation, a single edge flips the sign of the permutation. Color the polylines by sign and the graph becomes a bipartite structure: every edge connects an even (blue) vertex to an odd (red) vertex. This is the algebraic statement that is a subgroup of index 2.

Figure 7 — The Word Metric

The word metric on a Cayley graph counts the minimum number of edges needed to get from one vertex to another — equivalently, the minimum length of a word in the generators that equals . For the symmetric group under adjacent transpositions, the word metric from the identity has a name from statistics: the Kendall tau distance, also known as the inversion count. A permutation's distance from the identity is the number of pairs of positions that are out of sorted order.

The distance distribution — the number of group elements at each distance from the identity — is called the growth function of the group. The word metric is a real geometry. Finite Cayley graphs are rigid metric spaces in their own right, and many questions — diameter, girth, expansion — have deep connections to representation theory. The diameter of a Cayley graph is how far apart the two farthest elements can be. For under adjacent transpositions, the diameter is exactly , achieved by the reverse permutation. Beyond the classical families, the Weyl group of E8 has order 696,729,600 — its Cayley graph is 8-regular and has diameter 120, far beyond anything we can draw, but the same principles apply.

Where This Goes

A finite group is a finite combinatorial object. Its Cayley graph is a finite metric space. Many of the most striking facts in finite group theory can be phrased as statements about Cayley graphs: subgroups as subgraphs, cosets as sheets, normal subgroups as graph fiber-bundles over a quotient graph. Conjugacy classes organize the vertices; irreducible representations decompose the graph Laplacian; the number of conjugacy classes equals the number of irreducible representations.

Parallel coordinates are useful here for the same reason they are useful elsewhere: when the Cayley graph is a polytope in 4D or higher, you cannot draw it, but you can draw the polylines that its vertices produce, and you can see the clusters that the conjugacy classes make. For , with 120 polylines, the structure is already busy. For the Weyl groups of E8, the exceptional root systems in general, or sporadic simple groups, drawing every element is out of reach — but the conjugacy partition stays legible.
