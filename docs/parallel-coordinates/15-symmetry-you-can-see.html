Symmetry You Can See

Rotational symmetry groups of 4-polytopes appear as pattern invariances in parallel coordinates. The 24-cell, unique to four dimensions, is the star.

A symmetry is a transformation that leaves an object unchanged. Rotate a square by 90 degrees and it looks the same. Reflect it across a diagonal and it looks the same. The collection of all such transformations forms a group, and the structure of that group encodes the object's geometry.

In parallel coordinates, a 4-polytope becomes a bundle of polylines, one per vertex. A symmetry of the polytope permutes the vertices but preserves the edge structure, so it permutes the polylines while preserving their crossing pattern. The visual effect: the entire pattern maps to itself. You can see the symmetry as an invariance of the polyline bundle.

This explainer focuses on three polytopes that form a tightly linked family: the tesseract (the 4D hypercube), its dual the 16-cell, and the extraordinary 24-cell, which is its own dual and has no analogue in any other dimension. We saw how to read polytope structure from polylines in explainer 14. Now we ask: what happens when we rotate these objects, and when does the pattern snap back to itself?

The Symmetry Group in Action

Consider a polytope rotating continuously in a 4D rotation plane. At most orientations the polyline bundle looks different from its starting configuration. But at certain special angles the rotated polytope is indistinguishable from the original: every vertex has moved to a position previously occupied by some other vertex, and every edge maps to an edge. The polyline pattern is invariant.

Vertex Figures

The vertex figure of a polytope is the shape formed by the neighbors of a vertex. It encodes the local combinatorial structure: how many edges, faces, and cells meet at that point.

For the tesseract, each vertex connects to 4 others, and those 4 neighbors form a tetrahedron. For the 24-cell, each vertex connects to 8 others, and those 8 neighbors form a cube. This is one of the 24-cell's remarkable properties: its vertex figure is the 3D cube, making it a bridge between dimensions.

In parallel coordinates, selecting a vertex highlights one polyline. Its neighbors are the polylines connected to it by edges. The vertex figure is visible as the local crossing pattern of these neighbor polylines.

Self-Duality

The dual of a polytope is constructed by swapping vertices and cells: place a new vertex at the center of each cell, and connect two new vertices if their cells share a face. The tesseract (16 vertices, 32 edges, 24 faces, 8 cells) dualizes to the 16-cell (8 vertices, 24 edges, 32 faces, 16 cells). Vertices and cells swap counts; edges and faces swap counts.

The 24-cell is self-dual: it has 24 vertices and 24 cells, 96 edges and 96 faces. Dualizing it produces another 24-cell with the same structure. In parallel coordinates, this means the polyline bundle of the 24-cell and the polyline bundle of its dual are structurally identical. This is the deepest kind of symmetry: the object is its own mirror image under the vertex-cell exchange.

Compare with the tesseract/16-cell pair. The tesseract's 16 polylines and the 16-cell's 8 polylines look fundamentally different. Duality swaps them, but they are not the same. The 24-cell stands alone.

Why the 24-cell is unique. In 3D, every regular polyhedron has a distinct dual (the tetrahedron is self-dual, but that is the only case). In 4D, there are six regular polytopes, and the 24-cell is the only one besides the 5-cell that is self-dual. But unlike the 5-cell (the 4D simplex, which is self-dual in every dimension), the 24-cell has no analogue in any other dimension. It exists only in 4D, with its 1152 symmetries, its octahedral cells, and its cube vertex figure. Its parallel coordinates signature is uniquely dense and structured.

These four views, symmetry invariance, vertex figures, Schläfli recursion, and self-duality, converge on a single insight: the 24-cell's parallel coordinates pattern is not just a visualization but a direct encoding of the richest symmetry structure in four-dimensional geometry. The duality between points and polylines that opened this series finds its most intricate expression here.
