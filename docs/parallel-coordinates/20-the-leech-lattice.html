The Leech Lattice

196,560 kissing vectors in 24 dimensions. The densest sphere packing, the Conway groups, and the shadow of the Monster.

Beyond E8

In article 18, we explored the 240 roots of E8 in 8 dimensions. Those 240 vectors achieved the densest sphere packing in 8D and possessed a symmetry group of staggering order. The Leech lattice takes every one of those superlatives and multiplies it beyond recognition.

The Leech lattice lives in 24 dimensions. Its shortest nonzero vectors have squared norm 4 (not 2, as in ordinary root systems), and there are 196,560 of them—more than 800 times the count of E8's roots. These 196,560 vectors achieve the kissing number in 24 dimensions: the maximum number of non-overlapping unit spheres that can simultaneously touch a central sphere. In 2016, Maryna Viazovska proved that the E8 lattice gives the densest packing in 8D. In 2019, Cohn, Kumar, Miller, Radchenko, and Viazovska extended the result to 24D, confirming that the Leech lattice achieves the densest possible sphere packing in 24 dimensions.

The lattice is even (every vector has even squared norm) and unimodular (its fundamental domain has volume 1). It is the unique even unimodular lattice in 24 dimensions with no roots—no vectors of squared norm 2. This absence of short vectors is precisely what makes it so remarkable. It forces the packing density to concentrate at the next available shell, creating the extraordinary kissing configuration.

Its symmetry group, the Conway group Co₀, has order . Quotienting by the center gives the sporadic simple group Co₁, one of 26 exceptional finite groups that resist classification into infinite families. Co₁ sits inside a chain of sporadic groups that terminates at the Monster—the largest sporadic simple group, with an order of approximately . The Leech lattice is where the path to the Monster begins.

Scale Comparison: E8 and the Leech Lattice

Both E8 and the Leech lattice are even unimodular lattices. Both achieve the densest sphere packing in their respective dimensions. But the jump from 8 to 24 dimensions is not incremental—it is a phase transition in complexity. To see this, compare 240 randomly sampled Leech vectors against all 240 E8 roots. Same number of polylines, but three times the axes and a different coordinate vocabulary.

This visual density reflects real mathematical density: the Leech lattice is far more tightly packed.

The 196,560 Minimal Vectors

We cannot draw all 196,560 polylines at once—the result would be an opaque wall of ink. Instead, we show the structure statistically. The minimal vectors of the Leech lattice decompose into three types, classified by their coordinate patterns (using coordinates scaled so that the lattice vectors have integer entries):

Type A (shape 42022): Two coordinates are ±4, the remaining 22 are zero. There are such vectors: choose 2 positions from 24, then choose 2 signs independently.

Type B (shape 28016): Eight coordinates are ±2, the remaining 16 are zero. The eight nonzero positions must form an octad—one of the 759 weight-8 codewords of the extended binary Golay code. The signs must have an even number of minus signs. There are such vectors.

Type C (shape 31123): One coordinate is ±3, the remaining 23 are ±1. The sign pattern of the ±1 coordinates (flipped if the ±3 coordinate is −3) must form a Golay codeword. There are such vectors.

The Golay Code

The extended binary Golay code is a [24, 12, 8] binary linear code: 24-bit codewords, 12-dimensional subspace, minimum Hamming distance 8. It has codewords distributed by weight as follows:

The 759 octads form a Steiner system S(5,8,24): any 5 of the 24 positions lie in exactly one octad. This is an extraordinarily rigid combinatorial structure. The Golay code is generated by a 12×24 matrix , where is the identity and is a specific 12×12 matrix related to the icosahedron.

The Golay code enters the Leech lattice through Construction A: the positions where Type B vectors have their eight nonzero coordinates must form an octad. This is the constraint that gives the Leech lattice its structure. Without the Golay code, a lattice of vectors with eight ±2 entries would have far too many vectors and would fail to be even unimodular. The code selects exactly the right subset.

In the parallel coordinates view, each codeword is a polyline that visits either 0 or 1 at each of the 24 axes. The octads (weight 8) have exactly 8 peaks and 16 valleys. The dodecads (weight 12) are split evenly. Notice the regularity: every axis participates in the same number of octads (253 of the 759), reflecting the code's transitivity. The automorphism group of the Golay code is the Mathieu group M₂₄, one of the first sporadic simple groups to be discovered. We will return to the Golay code in depth in article 22.

The Sporadic Chain

The symmetry group of the Leech lattice is the starting point for a remarkable chain of sporadic simple groups. John Conway discovered three new sporadic groups—Co₁, Co₂, Co₃—by studying stabilizers of vectors in the Leech lattice. Four more sporadic groups (the McLaughlin group, the Higman-Sims group, the Suzuki sporadic group, and the Hall-Janko group) appear as stabilizers of other configurations. Together with the five Mathieu groups (which arise from the Golay code), these form the second generation of Robert Griess's Happy Family—the 20 sporadic groups that live inside the Monster.

Of the 26 sporadic simple groups, 20 live inside the Monster as subquotients. These 20 form the Happy Family, organized into three generations. The first generation is the five Mathieu groups (from the Golay code). The second generation is the seven groups arising from the Leech lattice (the three Conway groups plus McL, HS, Suz, and J₂). The remaining eight form the third generation, connected through the Fischer groups and the Baby Monster. The six sporadic groups outside the Monster—J₁, J₃, J₄, O'Nan, Rudvalis, and Lyons—are the pariahs.

The Leech lattice is the nexus where coding theory (the Golay code), lattice geometry (sphere packing), and group theory (sporadic groups) converge. We will follow the thread onward to the Monster in article 21, where the connection to modular forms and number theory completes the picture.

What Parallel Coordinates Reveal

At 24 axes, parallel coordinates are stretched to their limit. The polylines are dense, the axis labels crowd together, and individual vectors are hard to track. But the statistical views—heatmaps, histograms, type-colored samples—reveal structure that no other 2D projection can show. The three-type decomposition becomes visible as distinct coordinate signatures. The E8 block structure shows up as polylines that are silent outside their 8-axis block. The Golay code's combinatorial rigidity manifests as the precise pattern of which axes are active in Type B vectors.

These are not just visualization tricks. The coordinate structure of the Leech lattice—which values appear where, which patterns of nonzero entries are permitted—is the lattice's most fundamental description. Parallel coordinates display exactly this information. For the E8 lattice with 8 axes and 240 vectors, the display was manageable. For the Leech lattice, we need sampling and statistics. For the next lattice up the chain (the 48-dimensional lattice P₄₈h), even these strategies may fail. 24 dimensions is the sweet spot: rich enough to be extraordinary, constrained enough to be visualized.
