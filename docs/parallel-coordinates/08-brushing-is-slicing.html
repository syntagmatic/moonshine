Brushing Is Slicing

Axis brushing and angular brushing are geometric operations in the dual space. Each brush cuts a slice through the data, and the shape of that slice determines what you can select.

Brushing is the primary interaction technique for parallel coordinates. You drag a range on an axis to select polylines. It feels like highlighting, but what is actually happening is geometric: you are slicing the data space with hyperplanes and selecting the points that survive.

This explainer builds on the point-line duality from the first article and the crossing geometry from the second. A point in data space maps to a polyline in parallel coordinates. Brushing selects a subset of those polylines, which corresponds to selecting a region of data space. The shape of the region depends on the type of brush. Axis brushing gives rectangular regions. Angular brushing, which selects by the slope of line segments between axes, gives wedge-shaped regions. The difference matters.

Axis Brushing = Hyperplane Slice

Selecting a range on axis selects all data points where . Geometrically, this is slicing the data space with two parallel hyperplanes perpendicular to the axis. Every polyline that passes through the brushed range is a data point that lies between those two hyperplanes.

In two dimensions, the hyperplanes are just vertical or horizontal lines. In higher dimensions, each axis brush is a slab, an infinite region bounded by two parallel planes. The selected points are those inside the slab.

Multi-Axis Brushing = Intersection of Slices

Brushing on multiple axes simultaneously takes the intersection. Each brush adds a constraint. If you brush axis to select and axis to select , the selected points satisfy both conditions. The region in data space is a hyperrectangle, the Cartesian product of the individual intervals.

In the scatterplot view, this is immediately visible. A brush on one axis selects a band (horizontal or vertical). A brush on a second axis selects a perpendicular band. Their intersection is a rectangle. This is the fundamental limitation of axis brushing: no matter how many axes you brush, the selection region is always axis-aligned, always a box.

Angular Brushing = Slope Selection

Angular brushing takes a different approach. Instead of selecting by the value on a single axis, it selects by the relationship between two variables, the slope of the line segment connecting their values. Drawing a line between two axes (a "strum") selects all polylines whose segment intersects that strum line.

Recall from the duality that a line in Cartesian space maps to a convergence point in parallel coordinates. The slope of a PC segment encodes the relationship between two variables. A strum brush selects segments by asking: does this segment cross a particular line? This is a constraint on the slope, not on the individual values.

The segment-segment intersection test determines which polylines are selected. If the strum line and a polyline's segment between two axes cross each other, that data point is selected.

Click and drag between the two axes to draw a strum line. Click an existing strum line to remove it.

The Wedge: Two Strums Define a Region

A single strum line divides the segment space in two. Drawing a second strum line between the same pair of axes defines a wedge. The selected polylines are those whose segments pass between the two strum lines, whose slopes fall in a range.

In the dual Cartesian space (the scatterplot), the wedge selection corresponds to a wedge-shaped region, selecting points by angle rather than by axis-aligned range. This is something axis brushing cannot do.

Rectangles vs. wedges. Axis brushing selects by value range on individual axes, producing rectangular regions in any 2D projection of the data. Angular brushing selects by the ratio between variables, producing wedge-shaped regions. The wedge can isolate clusters that lie along diagonal directions, which rectangles cannot tightly bound.

Expressiveness. Any rectangular selection can be approximated by a pair of strum lines (by tilting them to near-vertical). But a diagonal wedge cannot be expressed as any combination of axis brushes. Angular brushing is strictly more expressive.

Combined Brushing

The real power comes from combining both brush types. Axis brushes constrain individual variables. Strum brushes constrain relationships between variables. Together, they can isolate complex regions in high-dimensional space that neither type alone could reach.

A strum brush between two other variables picks out the diagonal structure. The intersection captures exactly the target cluster.

Every brush, whether axis or angular, is a geometric operation on the data space. Axis brushes are hyperplane slices. Strum brushes are wedge slices. Each narrows the selection. Together they carve out polytope-shaped regions in high-dimensional space, all through direct manipulation of the parallel coordinates display. The duality makes the geometry tangible.
