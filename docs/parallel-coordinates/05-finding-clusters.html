Finding Clusters

Clusters in high-dimensional space appear as bundles of nearly parallel polylines. The tighter the cluster, the tighter the bundle. Inselberg's envelope construction explains exactly why.

In the duality, we saw that a single point in N-dimensional space maps to a polyline in parallel coordinates. A collection of similar points, then, maps to a collection of similar polylines. When data points cluster together in some region of the data space, their polylines travel together across the axes, forming a visible bundle. The width of the bundle reflects the spread of the cluster. The path of the bundle reflects the cluster's center.

This is not just a visual metaphor. Inselberg showed that the geometry of clusters in parallel coordinates follows precise rules. An elliptical cluster in a scatterplot between two variables produces a specific hyperbolic signature between the corresponding parallel axes. The shape of that signature, its width at the waist, its orientation, encodes the correlation structure of the cluster.

Real Data: The Wine Dataset

The UCI Wine dataset contains 178 samples of wine from three different grape cultivars grown in the same region of Italy. Each sample is measured across 13 chemical properties. The three cultivars produce three distinct bundles that separate across several dimensions, most clearly on flavanoids, color intensity, proline, and od280/od315 dilution.

This is the kind of structure that parallel coordinates were made for. In a scatterplot matrix, you would need 28 pairwise panels to show the same 8 variables. Here, you see all eight dimensions at once, and the three-class structure is immediately visible as three bundles of polylines traveling in different directions.

Figure 2. The UCI Wine dataset (178 rows, 8 most discriminating features). Three grape cultivars form three distinct bundles. Cultivar 1 (blue) runs high on proline and flavanoids. Cultivar 3 (orange) is high on color intensity and low on od280/od315. Cultivar 2 (green) occupies the middle ground.

The Bowtie Anatomy

Why do clusters produce bundles? The mechanism goes back to the duality and the crossing geometry. Consider data points clustered in an ellipse in the scatterplot between two adjacent variables. Each point maps to a line segment between the corresponding pair of parallel axes. The envelope of all those line segments, the curve tangent to every one, forms a hyperbolic "bowtie" pattern.

Inselberg showed that the boundary of an elliptical cluster maps to a generalized hyperbola (an h-star) via the envelope construction. To see why, consider a point on the boundary of the ellipse. In parallel coordinates, that point produces a line segment. As the point traces the ellipse boundary, its line segment sweeps out a family of lines. The envelope of that family, the curve that is tangent to each line in turn, is the bowtie.

Recall that for a line , the convergence point in parallel coordinates is at . When data points cluster, their pairwise slopes cluster too, producing a concentration of convergence points. That density of convergence points is what forms the bowtie waist. The tighter the correlation, the narrower the waist.

Bowtie width encodes correlation. A perfectly correlated cluster (r = 1) produces line segments that all pass through a single convergence point, collapsing the bowtie to a line. As correlation decreases, the convergence points spread, and the bowtie widens.

Bowtie orientation encodes sign. Positive correlation places the bowtie waist outside the axes (to the right of the right axis). Negative correlation places it between the axes, producing a crossing pattern.

Multiple clusters, multiple bowties. If the data contains several clusters, each contributes its own bowtie. Their separation in parallel coordinates reflects how well the clusters separate in data space.

Brushing Reveals Clusters

When the data has no color labels, the cluster structure is still present in the geometry of the polylines. You just need a way to pull it out. Axis brushing does this: drag a range on any axis to highlight the polylines that pass through it. The highlighted subset often reveals coherent structure across all the other axes, exposing a cluster you could not see in the full tangle.

This is the wine data again, but without the class colors. Try brushing different ranges on different axes. Selecting the high end of flavanoids picks out cultivar 1. Selecting the high end of color intensity picks out cultivar 3. The three-cluster structure that was obvious with color labels can be rediscovered purely through interaction. Finding clusters in parallel coordinates is a matter of reading bundles: tight groups of polylines that travel together, separate from other groups. The bowtie construction explains the local geometry between each pair of axes. Brushing provides the interactive tool to test hypotheses about cluster membership. Together, they turn an N-dimensional clustering problem into something you can see and explore directly.
