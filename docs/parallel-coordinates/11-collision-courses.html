Collision Courses

Two aircraft in flight define a point in 8-dimensional state space. The collision condition carves out a hypersurface. Points inside it are dangerous. Parallel coordinates make this visible.

An aircraft moving through a two-dimensional airspace has four numbers at any instant: position and velocity . Two aircraft together have eight. The pair's full state is a single point in 8-dimensional space, and the question "will they collide?" is a question about where that point falls relative to a surface in that space.

This is not a contrived example. Inselberg developed the geometric theory of parallel coordinates while working on air traffic control problems. The collision detection problem was the original motivation: given a pair of constant-velocity trajectories, determine whether the closest point of approach falls within a safety threshold. In Cartesian coordinates, that test requires computing a distance in high-dimensional space. In parallel coordinates, it becomes a visual pattern.

The Dangerous Region

For random pairs of trajectories, most are safe. The dangerous ones are not scattered uniformly through the state space; they cluster. The condition defines a region in the joint 8D space. In the reduced view of CPA time vs. CPA distance, the dangerous trajectories occupy a band near the bottom of the plot.

This is the key insight that connects collision detection to the envelope representation from explainer 03: the boundary of the dangerous region is a hypersurface. Its parallel coordinates signature is a structured pattern, not noise. That structure is what makes visual detection possible.

Pattern recognition over computation. A human controller cannot evaluate the CPA formula for every pair. But the clustering of dangerous polylines in parallel coordinates means that, with practice, the eye can pick out hazardous configurations from the visual pattern alone. This is the operational payoff of the geometric theory.

Time Evolution

A single pair of aircraft traces a path through the 4D position space as time advances. At each moment, their positions define a polyline in parallel coordinates. Over 60 time steps, these polylines sweep through the display. When the aircraft converge, the polylines converge toward a common region. When they diverge, the polylines fan out.

Color encodes time: light for early, dark for late. The convergence of the trajectory is visible as the darkening polylines funnel together, then separate after closest approach.

The Separation Envelope

The boundary between safe and dangerous states is a hypersurface in the 8D joint state space. In parallel coordinates, this hypersurface has an envelope representation: it appears as a bounding curve that separates the safe polylines from the dangerous ones.

This connects to the containment test from explainer 07. A new trajectory pair is safe if and only if its polyline lies outside the dangerous envelope. Testing "inside or outside" is the same geometric operation, whether the surface encloses a polytope or a collision region. The geometry does not care what the axes represent.

From application to theory. Inselberg did not develop the mathematics of parallel coordinates and then look for applications. He started with this problem -- detecting dangerous proximity in high-dimensional state spaces -- and worked backward to the geometry that makes it tractable. The hypersurface representation, the envelope construction, and the containment test all emerged from the need to see collision conditions that cannot be drawn in Cartesian space.
