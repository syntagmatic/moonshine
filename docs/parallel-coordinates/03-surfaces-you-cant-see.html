Surfaces You Can't See

A hyperplane in N dimensions is invisible. But its representation in parallel coordinates leaves a structured signature: an envelope that reveals the surface's geometry without ever showing the surface itself.

As we saw in The Duality, a point in Cartesian space maps to a polyline in parallel coordinates, and a line maps to a convergence point. But what about curves? Surfaces? Hyperplanes in dimensions we cannot visualize? The duality extends to all of these, and the key construction is the envelope.

A smooth curve is not just a set of points. It is also the object tangent to a family of lines. Each tangent line maps to a convergence point in parallel coordinates. As the tangent rolls along the curve, the convergence point traces a new curve: the envelope. This is how parallel coordinates represent continuous geometry, and it is how structures in arbitrary dimensions become visible.

When all coefficients are equal, the polylines form a symmetric fan. When one coefficient is much larger than the others, the corresponding axis dominates and the polylines pinch through a narrow band on that axis. When a coefficient is negative, the slope of the polyline segments reverses across that axis.

Why it works: The hyperplane equation is a single linear constraint on N variables. It removes one degree of freedom. In parallel coordinates, this constraint restricts where polylines can go, creating a one-parameter-less family. The result is visible structure where unconstrained data would produce visual noise.

Cusp-Inflection Duality

The envelope construction reveals one of Inselberg's most striking results: cusps and inflections exchange roles across the transformation. A cusp in Cartesian space (where the curve has a sharp point) corresponds to an inflection in the parallel coordinates envelope. Conversely, an inflection in Cartesian space (where curvature changes sign) produces a cusp in the envelope.

This duality is orientation-independent. It follows from the geometry of the tangent family: at a cusp, the tangent direction reverses, which creates a smooth turning in the convergence point trajectory. At an inflection, the tangent sweeps smoothly through a critical angle that creates a cusp in the dual.

Figure 3. Cusp-inflection duality. Left: a curve in Cartesian space. Right: its envelope representation in parallel coordinates. A cuspidal cubic (top) produces a smooth inflection in the envelope. A cubic with an inflection (bottom) produces a cusp in the envelope.

Cusp-inflection duality: A cusp in Cartesian space maps to an inflection in the envelope, and an inflection maps to a cusp. This is a theorem, not a visual coincidence. It holds for any smooth curve regardless of position or orientation.

Convexity Detection

This duality has a practical consequence. A convex curve has no inflection points. Therefore its envelope representation has no cusps. The envelope of a convex shape is a smooth, cusp-free curve. A non-convex shape, by contrast, must have inflection points somewhere, and these appear as cusps in the envelope.

Parallel coordinates can detect convexity visually: if the envelope between the axes is smooth, the curve is convex. If there are cusps, the curve is not.

From Curves to Hypersurfaces

Everything above generalizes. A smooth surface in has a tangent plane at each point. Each tangent plane is a hyperplane, and hyperplanes map to structured polyline families in parallel coordinates. The envelope of these families represents the surface.

A smooth hypersurface in has a tangent hyperplane at each point. The family of tangent hyperplanes, represented in parallel coordinates, produces an envelope that encodes the hypersurface's geometry. Curvature, convexity, and singularities all have signatures in this envelope.

The hierarchy: Points map to polylines. Lines map to convergence points. Curves map to envelopes of convergence points. Surfaces map to envelopes of polyline families. At every level, the duality preserves the geometric information while changing the representation. Objects that are invisible in Cartesian space because they live in too many dimensions become visible as structured patterns in parallel coordinates.

This is the machinery that makes parallel coordinates more than a display technique. The envelope construction means that continuous geometry, not just discrete data points, has a parallel coordinates representation.
