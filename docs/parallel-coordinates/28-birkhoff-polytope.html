The Birkhoff Polytope

Every doubly stochastic matrix is a convex combination of permutation matrices. The polytope of these matrices is where the assignment problem lives.

Take the set of all n×n matrices with non-negative entries whose rows and columns each sum to 1. This set, the doubly stochastic matrices, is a convex polytope living inside the n2-dimensional space of matrices. It is called the Birkhoff polytope Bn, and it is one of the most studied polytopes in combinatorial optimization.

The Birkhoff polytope sits at a crossroads. Linear programming sees it as the feasible region of the assignment problem. Combinatorics sees it as the convex hull of the n! permutation matrices. Measure theory sees it as the space of couplings between two uniform distributions. Majorization theory sees it as the operator that smooths one vector into another. Optimal transport sees it as the relaxation that becomes tractable after entropy regularization. The same polytope wears all of these hats.

The central theorem — the Birkhoff–von Neumann theorem — says that all of these perspectives agree on the corners. The extreme points of Bn are exactly the permutation matrices. Every doubly stochastic matrix is a weighted average of permutations.

The Shape of Bn

Although Bn lives in an ambient space of dimension n2, the sum-to-one constraints force it onto a lower-dimensional slice. The row sums give n equations; the column sums give another n; but one of these is redundant (they both imply the total equals n). That leaves 2n−1 independent constraints and so

For n=3, B3 has dimension 4 and lives in ℝ9. It has 6 vertices (the 3! permutations) and is combinatorially equivalent to the cyclic polytope with 6 vertices in dimension 4. For n=4, B4 has dimension 9 and 24 vertices. For n=5, it jumps to dimension 16 with 120 vertices. The facets are easy: they come from the non-negativity constraints xij ≥ 0, giving n2 facets for n≥3. The vertex count explodes factorially; the facet count grows only quadratically. This asymmetry is why the polytope is so dense with faces of intermediate dimension.

Exact volumes are known only for n up to 10. The computation is already gruesome by n=9, where the Ehrhart polynomial has degree 64. The volume problem for Bn is one of the famous open problems in polyhedral combinatorics.

Random Doubly Stochastic Matrices in Parallel Coordinates

The sixteen entries of a 4×4 matrix give us sixteen axes. A doubly stochastic matrix becomes a polyline visiting each entry in a fixed reading order: row 1 left-to-right, then row 2, and so on. The sum constraints are visible as a structural signature. Within each block of four adjacent axes, the row sums to 1; every four axes apart, the column sums to 1. A cloud of random doubly stochastic matrices is not just a cloud — it is a cloud carved out by these hidden equalities.

Figure 1. 4×4 doubly stochastic matrices generated by Sinkhorn-normalizing random positive matrices. Each polyline visits the 16 entries in reading order. Each row of four axes must sum to 1; each column of four axes separated by four positions must also sum to 1. The toggle shades those groupings.

All the polylines pass through the same nine-dimensional slice of the 16-cube — that is what dimension 9 means. The polylines look busy but they are constrained. Pick any three entries in a row; the fourth is forced. Pick three entries in a column; the fourth is forced. Six constraints, two of them redundant, which is why 16 − 7 = 9.

Permutation Matrices: The Vertices

The 24 permutation matrices of size 4 are the 24 ways to place four non-attacking rooks on a 4×4 board. Each row has exactly one 1, each column has exactly one 1, and everything else is 0. Their polylines are the most constrained objects possible: each one consists entirely of 0-values and 1-values, and the pattern of which entries take the value 1 is a permutation.

Figure 2. All 24 permutation matrices of size 4 as polylines over the 16 matrix entries. Even permutations (blue) and odd permutations (red) split the vertex set into the alternating group A4 and its coset. Every polyline has exactly four 1-valued axes and twelve 0-valued axes.

Every doubly stochastic matrix is a convex combination of these 24 polylines. The interior of B4 is populated by polylines whose height on each axis is a weighted average of the heights of the vertices at that axis. If you imagine mixing all 24 vertices with weight 1/24 each, you get the uniform doubly stochastic matrix: every entry equals 1/4, so every axis sits at height 1/4.

Convex Combinations: Living Inside the Polytope

Pick two, three, or four permutation matrices, assign weights that sum to 1, and the result is a doubly stochastic matrix lying on the convex hull of those vertices. If you pick just two vertices you get a point on an edge. Three give a point on a face. Four give a point in a tetrahedral face (or the interior, if the four vertices are in general position).

Figure 3. Pick up to four permutation matrices (top controls) and assign weights with the sliders. The selected vertices appear as faded polylines; the convex combination appears in solid color and as a heatmap. Every doubly stochastic matrix in B4 can be written this way — often with at most (n−1)2+1 = 10 vertices.

Carathéodory's theorem says any point in a d-dimensional polytope can be written as a combination of at most d+1 vertices. For B4, that is ten permutation matrices — so although a doubly stochastic 4×4 matrix might be a mixture of many permutations, it always has a decomposition using at most ten. The decomposition is rarely unique. This is the content of Birkhoff's algorithm: repeatedly pull out the permutation matrix that supports the largest weight until nothing remains.

The Assignment Problem

Given a cost matrix C, we want the permutation σ that minimizes Σi C[i, σ(i)]. Four workers, four jobs, and a 4×4 table of costs: which worker gets which job?

The feasible region of this problem, written as a linear program, is the Birkhoff polytope. We minimize a linear function C·X over X ∈ B4. A linear function over a polytope attains its minimum at a vertex. By Birkhoff–von Neumann, the vertices are permutation matrices. So even though we relaxed the problem to allow any doubly stochastic matrix, the optimum is always a permutation. The linear program's optimum is automatically integer. This is what makes the assignment problem so tractable, and this is the geometric content of the Hungarian algorithm.

Sinkhorn Normalization

Sinkhorn's theorem (1964) gives a constructive way to map a positive matrix onto the Birkhoff polytope. Alternately normalize the rows so each row sums to 1, then normalize the columns so each column sums to 1, then rows, then columns. The process converges to a doubly stochastic matrix that is the unique Sinkhorn projection of the starting matrix. The limiting matrix has the form D1AD2 for diagonal matrices D1 and D2 with positive entries.

Figure 5. Sinkhorn iteration on a random 4×4 positive matrix. Each “step” normalizes rows (odd) or columns (even). The row-sum and column-sum deviations shrink exponentially. After a handful of iterations the matrix is indistinguishable from doubly stochastic. The limiting matrix is the Sinkhorn projection — a specific point inside B4.

The Sinkhorn projection is central to modern optimal transport. The original transport problem asks: given supply masses and demand masses (here all equal to 1), find the transport plan (a doubly stochastic matrix) that minimizes total cost. Add an entropy-regularization term and the optimum becomes the Sinkhorn projection of the Gibbs kernel exp(−C/ϵ). That projection is computed by alternating row and column normalization — the same algorithm Birkhoff-normalizers discovered half a century earlier.

Why Sinkhorn converges. Each row normalization multiplies rows by positive scalars; each column normalization multiplies columns by positive scalars. The two operations contract a projective metric on positive matrices (the Hilbert metric). They commute with the Birkhoff–von Neumann structure in a way that makes the fixed point unique up to scale, and the convergence rate is governed by the spectral gap of a doubly stochastic operator.

Majorization and the Permutation Cloud

A vector y majorizes a vector x, written x ≺ y, if the two have the same total and, in sorted order, the partial sums of y dominate those of x. Intuitively, y is more unequal than x: more spread out. The Hardy–Littlewood–Pólya theorem says this is equivalent to the existence of a doubly stochastic matrix D with x = Dy.

In other words, y can be smoothed into x by applying a doubly stochastic averaging operator. Since every doubly stochastic matrix is a convex combination of permutations, smoothing is the same as averaging over permuted copies. The set of vectors majorized by y is exactly the permutohedron of y: the convex hull of all n! vectors obtained by permuting y's entries. This polytope is the image of Bn under the linear map D ↦ Dy.

When y's entries are all equal, the six permutations collapse onto a single point and the permutohedron degenerates. The only vector majorized by (c, c, c) is (c, c, c) itself. When y is maximally unequal, the hexagon grows to its largest size. Inequality and majorization-depth go together.

Connections

The Birkhoff polytope touches so many corners of mathematics that it serves as a hub. It is the assignment polytope of linear programming. Its vertices are the elements of the symmetric group Sn, so its graph is a Cayley graph (with transposition generators). Its projection under D ↦ Dy gives the permutohedron. Its integer points are doubly stochastic matrices with integer entries — magic squares. Its real points are transport plans in discrete optimal transport. Its rational points are probability distributions over permutations.

A striking connection: the permutation matrices are vertices of Bn, but they are also vertices of the permutohedron under a different embedding. In Bn, a permutation σ maps to a 0/1 matrix of size n×n. In the permutohedron, the same permutation maps to the vector (σ(1), σ(2), …, σ(n)) in ℝn. One embedding lives in n2 dimensions and is a polytope of dimension (n−1)2. The other lives in n dimensions and is a polytope of dimension n−1. The symmetric group has two very different geometric lives.

And the Hungarian algorithm, at the other end of the scale, is an edge-walking procedure. It starts at some permutation (a vertex of Bn) and at each step pivots along an edge — which corresponds to composing with a transposition. The algorithm's correctness comes from the fact that linear functions attain their minimum on polytope vertices, and its efficiency from the fact that the polytope has only O(n3)-length shortest paths between vertices. Everything lives on the polytope.

The one-line summary. The Birkhoff polytope Bn is the convex hull of the n! permutation matrices. It has dimension (n−1)2 and n2 facets. Birkhoff–von Neumann says every doubly stochastic matrix is a convex combination of permutation matrices. Hardy–Littlewood–Pólya says applying a doubly stochastic matrix is smoothing in the majorization order. Sinkhorn says you can project any positive matrix onto Bn by alternating row and column normalization. The assignment problem says linear optimization over Bn is the Hungarian algorithm. Five fields, one polytope.
