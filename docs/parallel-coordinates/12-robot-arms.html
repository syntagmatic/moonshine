Robot Arms

Joint angles define a point in configuration space. The reachable workspace traces an envelope. Inverse kinematics becomes a visual search problem in parallel coordinates.

A robot arm with N revolute joints has N joint angles. Each configuration of the arm is a point in N-dimensional configuration space. The forward kinematics function maps this point to the position of the end-effector: the tip of the arm, the point that does useful work.

In parallel coordinates, each configuration becomes a polyline across N joint-angle axes. Add axes for the end-effector position coordinates, and the polyline extends to encode both input (joint angles) and output (workspace position) of the forward kinematics function. The entire mapping becomes visible.

Each choice of joint angles determines a unique end-effector position. But the inverse problem, finding joint angles that reach a desired position, generally has multiple solutions. This asymmetry is where parallel coordinates become revealing.

Inverse Kinematics as Visual Search

The fundamental problem in robotics is inverse kinematics: given a target position for the end-effector, find the joint angles that achieve it. In parallel coordinates, this becomes a search problem. Sample many configurations, plot each as a polyline across the joint-angle axes and end-effector axes, and then select the polylines that pass through the desired end-effector region.

Drag the target (red circle) in the arm view. Valid configurations update in the parallel coordinates view.

Figure 1. A planar 3-link arm. Drag the target to set a desired end-effector position. All configurations that reach within tolerance of the target are shown as polylines on the three joint-angle axes. The manifold of inverse kinematics solutions is the family of polylines.

When the target is well inside the reachable workspace, many configurations achieve it, and the parallel coordinates view fills with a structured family of polylines. As the target moves toward the boundary of the workspace, the family thins. Beyond the boundary, no polylines survive: the position is unreachable.

Configuration Space Sampling

To see the full structure of the forward kinematics mapping, sample configurations uniformly at random and plot each as a polyline across all five axes: three joint angles and two end-effector coordinates. The result is a dense picture of how joint space maps to workspace.

Brush on the x or y axes to select configurations that reach a region of workspace. The selected polylines show which joint angle combinations achieve that reach.

Figure 2. 500 random configurations on 5 parallel axes. Brush on the end-effector axes (x, y) to isolate configurations that reach a particular workspace region. The remaining polyline segments reveal the joint-angle structure.

Brushing is inverse kinematics. Selecting a region on the end-effector axes and reading off the joint-angle values on the remaining axes is exactly the inverse kinematics problem. Parallel coordinates turn it from an algebraic inversion into a geometric selection.

This connects directly to the geometric theory. The reachable workspace is a region whose boundary is an envelope (explainer 03), and selecting configurations that reach a target is a containment query (explainer 07). The parallel coordinates framework unifies these ideas.

Joint Limits

Physical robot joints have limits. An elbow cannot rotate a full 360 degrees. A shoulder has a restricted range. In parallel coordinates, joint limits appear as bounded ranges on each axis, with forbidden regions shaded.

When limits are imposed, the set of valid configurations shrinks. Polylines whose segments enter the forbidden region represent configurations that violate a joint constraint. The reachable workspace shrinks correspondingly, sometimes dramatically.

Figure 3. Joint limits constrain the reachable workspace. Toggle limits on/off to see how physical constraints shrink the solution space. Forbidden regions are shaded on each axis.

Joint limits also break symmetry. Without limits, every reachable target has solution families related by full rotations. With limits, some solutions become physically impossible even though the kinematics are valid. The parallel coordinates view shows this directly: the forbidden shading clips the polyline family.

Redundancy: 2-Link vs. 3-Link

A 2-link arm reaching a point in 2D has exactly two degrees of freedom for a two-dimensional target. For most reachable positions, there are exactly two solutions: "elbow up" and "elbow down." The inverse kinematics problem is exactly determined.

A 3-link arm has three degrees of freedom for a two-dimensional target. There is one extra degree of freedom, and the arm is redundant. For each reachable target, there is a continuous family of solutions, not just two. The extra joint angle varies freely while the end-effector stays fixed.

Drag the target in either panel. The 2-link arm snaps to its two solutions. The 3-link arm shows a manifold of solutions.

Figure 4. Redundancy comparison. Left: a 2-link arm produces two polylines (elbow up, elbow down) for most targets. Right: a 3-link arm produces a continuous family. The extra joint angle is the null-space degree of freedom.

Redundancy in parallel coordinates. For the 2-link arm, inverse kinematics gives two discrete polylines. For the 3-link arm, it gives a one-dimensional manifold: a band of polylines that share the same end-effector values but vary freely in the third joint angle. The null-space dimension is visible as the width of this band.

This is the geometric signature of redundancy. In N-dimensional configuration space reaching a K-dimensional target, the solution manifold has dimension N - K. In parallel coordinates, this manifold appears as a structured family of polylines whose spread on each axis corresponds to the freedom in the null space. More joints, wider band, more choices for the motion planner.
