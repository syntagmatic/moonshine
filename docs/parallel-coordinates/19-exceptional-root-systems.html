The Exceptional Root Systems

From G₂'s 12 roots to E₈'s 240. Five structures that don't fit any infinite family, each with its own geometry.

The ADE Classification

The classification of simple Lie algebras is one of mathematics' most satisfying results. Every simple Lie algebra belongs to one of four infinite families——or to exactly five exceptions: . The infinite families are the classical root systems, each parameterized by a rank that can grow without limit. The five exceptions are fixed, finite, and irreducible. They exist because the constraints on root systems are tight enough to force specific structures but loose enough to allow a handful of anomalies.

We encountered the largest exception, , in article 18. Its 240 roots in 8 dimensions sit at the apex of a chain: . This article covers the other four exceptional systems: . Each has its own character. G₂ lives in just 2 dimensions but packs 12 roots into a hexagonal star with an unusual triple bond in its Dynkin diagram. F₄ lives in 4 dimensions and its 48 roots encompass the 24-cell, one of the remarkable regular polytopes we met in article 14. E₆ and E₇ bridge the gap to E₈, carrying 72 and 126 roots respectively, each visible as a subsystem of the larger one.

The tool we bring to each system is the same: parallel coordinates on one side, the Petrie projection (onto the Coxeter plane) on the other. The Petrie projection compresses all the dimensions into a 2D mandala with -fold symmetry, where is the Coxeter number. Parallel coordinates preserve the full coordinate structure as polylines. Together, these two views reveal both the global symmetry and the dimensional detail of each root system.

The Classification

Every root system has a set of invariants that characterize it: rank (the dimension of the ambient space), the number of roots, the Coxeter number (the order of the Coxeter element), and the Weyl group order (the number of symmetries). G₂ has a deep connection to the octonions. The compact form of the Lie group G₂ is the automorphism group of the octonion algebra—it consists of all linear transformations of the 8-dimensional octonions that preserve the multiplication table. Since the octonions are the last of the four normed division algebras (after the reals, complex numbers, and quaternions), G₂ guards the door to non-associativity.

E₆ is the first of the E-series, with rank 6 and 72 roots. Its Dynkin diagram is a chain of five nodes with a branch at the third node: . This branching is what makes the E-series exceptional—the classical D-series also branches, but only at the end, producing a less constrained geometry.

Because we embed E₆'s 6-dimensional root space inside the 8-dimensional E₈ coordinate system, a few roots project to the center—they differ only in the two "dead" dimensions orthogonal to the Coxeter plane.

E₆ has a celebrated connection to algebraic geometry: the symmetry group of the 27 lines on a smooth cubic surface is the Weyl group of E₆. This means that the combinatorial structure governing which of the 27 lines intersect which others is encoded in the root system.

All Five Together

Placing the five exceptional systems side by side reveals the escalation from 2 axes to 8. G₂ has just 12 polylines threading through 3 axes (with a zero-sum constraint reducing the effective dimension to 2). F₄ has 48 polylines on 4 axes. E₆, E₇, and E₈ use 8 axes with increasing numbers of polylines as fewer coordinates are constrained to be equal.

Classical vs Exceptional

What makes the exceptional systems exceptional? One way to see it: compare root systems of the same rank. In 4 dimensions, the classical systems A₄ and D₄ coexist with the exceptional F₄. All three have rank 4, but they differ in root count, symmetry, and internal structure.

Its Weyl group has order 192. F₄ has 48 roots, with a Weyl group of order 1,152—six times larger than A₄'s and six times larger than D₄'s.

The parallel coordinates view makes the structural difference visible. A₄'s polylines are sparse with many zeros. D₄'s polylines hit only ±1 with exactly two non-zero coordinates. F₄'s polylines include both patterns plus the half-integer family, creating a denser web with more crossing structure.

The exceptional root systems are not just larger; they are more connected. Each root in F₄ has more neighbors, the angle structure is richer, and the Weyl group acts more transitively. The classical systems tile space in predictable patterns. The exceptional ones fill their dimensions with a tighter, less reducible geometry that resists being parameterized by a single integer.
