E8: Geometry of Exceptional Symmetry

240 roots in 8 dimensions. The largest exceptional root system, visualized through the Petrie mandala and parallel coordinates.

What Is E8?

The classification of simple Lie groups is one of the great achievements of 19th-century mathematics. Most simple Lie groups fall into infinite families: the classical groups . But five exceptional groups sit outside every family, named . Of these, is the largest.

Its root system consists of 240 vectors in , arranged with a symmetry so precise that the structure is unique up to rotation. The Weyl group of E8, the group of symmetries preserving this arrangement, has order . E8 appears in string theory, where it governs the gauge symmetry of heterotic string models. It appears in number theory, lattice theory, and discrete geometry. In 2016, Maryna Viazovska proved that the E8 lattice achieves the densest possible sphere packing in 8 dimensions, resolving a problem that had been open for decades.

We encountered polytopes in article 14 and symmetry groups in article 15. E8 sits at the apex of those ideas. Its root system is not merely a polytope in 8D; it is one whose combinatorial and geometric properties constrain each other so tightly that the entire structure is forced. To visualize it, we will use two complementary views: the famous Petrie projection, which compresses 8 dimensions into a mandala of concentric rings, and parallel coordinates, which preserve the full 8-dimensional coordinate information as polylines.

The Dynkin Diagram

The combinatorial skeleton of E8 is encoded in its Dynkin diagram: 8 nodes, connected by 7 edges. Each node represents a simple root . An edge between nodes means the corresponding simple roots meet at an angle of 120 degrees, so their inner product is −1. The E8 diagram has a single branch point where one node connects to three others, forming a T-shape that distinguishes E8 from the classical D-series diagrams (which also branch, but with shorter arms).

Edge Structure

Two roots are connected by an edge when their inner product is 1, equivalently when their Euclidean distance is . The E8 root system has 6,720 edges. Each vertex has degree 56: every root has exactly 56 neighbors at distance . The resulting graph is the E8 root polytope skeleton, sometimes called the Gosset graph.

In the Petrie projection, the edges weave a dense web between the 240 vertices. Drawn with very low opacity, the edge density creates a visual texture that reveals the system's internal geometry. In parallel coordinates, each edge appears as an indexed point between each pair of adjacent axes, following the line-to-point duality. With 6,720 edges and 7 axis gaps, that produces 47,040 indexed points, which we render as a density field.

Sphere Packing

The 240 roots of E8 are also the kissing vectors of the E8 lattice packing. In 8 dimensions, if you center a unit sphere at the origin, exactly 240 non-overlapping unit spheres can touch it. The centers of those touching spheres are the root vectors (scaled appropriately). This kissing number, 240, is the maximum possible in 8 dimensions.

This is remarkable. In low dimensions, the kissing number problem is already difficult: it took until 1953 to prove that 12 is the answer in 3D (settling a debate between Newton and Gregory). E8 is one of only three dimensions where the exact kissing number is both known and achieved by a lattice packing. The other is dimension 24, where the Leech lattice achieves kissing number 196,560.

Figure 6. Kissing numbers by dimension. Dimensions 8 and 24 stand out dramatically. The E8 and Leech lattice packings achieve these exact bounds.

Viazovska's breakthrough. In 2016, Maryna Viazovska proved that E8 achieves the densest possible sphere packing in 8 dimensions, using a novel method based on modular forms. A week later, she and collaborators extended the result to dimension 24. The 240 kissing vectors are exactly the 240 roots of E8.

The exponents reveal structure. The exponents of E8 are {1, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23, 29}. These are exactly the integers less than 30 that are coprime to 30. The 8 concentric rings in the Petrie projection correspond to these exponents: ring k rotates at angular frequency proportional to the k-th exponent. The outermost ring moves at frequency 1 (slowest), the innermost at frequency 29 (fastest).

What we have seen in this article is a single mathematical object examined from multiple directions. The Dynkin diagram encodes the local angle structure. Parallel coordinates preserve the full 8-dimensional coordinate data. The Petrie projection reveals the global rotational symmetry. Each view shows something the others hide. Together, they give us a way to see, if not fully comprehend, the geometry of E8.
