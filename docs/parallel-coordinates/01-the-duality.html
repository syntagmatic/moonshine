The Duality

A point in Cartesian space becomes a polyline in parallel coordinates. A line becomes a point. This inversion is the foundation of everything that follows.

In a Cartesian plot, a point sits at the intersection of its coordinate values. Simple, familiar, and limited to two or three dimensions. Parallel coordinates take a different approach: draw the axes side by side instead of perpendicular, and connect the values with line segments. A two-dimensional point becomes a line. A three-dimensional point becomes a polyline with two segments. An N-dimensional point becomes a polyline with N-1 segments.

This is not just a display trick. There is a precise mathematical duality between Cartesian and parallel coordinate representations, discovered by Alfred Inselberg in the 1980s. Points map to lines, and lines map to points. The mapping preserves geometric structure in ways that make high-dimensional relationships visible.

Line to Point

The duality runs deeper than point-to-polyline. Consider a set of points that all lie on a single line in Cartesian space. Each point maps to a line segment in parallel coordinates. These segments all pass through a single point, called the convergence point or indexed point.

Negative correlation (m < 0): convergence falls between the axes. The line segments cross, forming an X-pattern. At m = −1, the convergence point sits exactly at the midpoint.

No correlation (m = 0): convergence is on the right axis. All segments fan from different heights on the left axis to the same height on the right.

Positive correlation (0 < m < 1): convergence is to the right of the right axis. Segments run roughly parallel.

Perfect positive (m = 1): segments are exactly parallel. The convergence point is at infinity. This is the projective case.

Steep positive (m > 1): convergence is to the left of the left axis. Segments cross again, but the crossing pattern differs from the negative-slope case.

The Projective Perspective

The case m = 1 is special. When the slope is exactly 1, the formula gives a division by zero. Geometrically, all the line segments in parallel coordinates become exactly parallel, and the convergence point is at infinity. This is not a singularity but a feature: the duality naturally lives in projective space, where points at infinity are first-class citizens.

As the slope approaches 1 from either side, the convergence point races off to the left or right. At m = 1, it wraps around to the point at infinity on the horizontal line. Projective geometry handles this seamlessly. The parallel case is not degenerate; it is the representation of a specific, well-defined relationship.

Multiple Points, Multiple Lines

When a dataset contains many points, the parallel coordinates view fills with polylines. The structure of these polylines encodes the structure of the data. Points that are close in Cartesian space produce polylines that are close in parallel coordinates. Linear relationships produce convergence patterns. Clusters produce bundles.

Figure 5. Different data structures produce different polyline patterns. Random data fills the space. Correlated data produces crossing patterns. Clusters produce bundles. Points on a line converge.

From Two Dimensions to N

In N dimensions, a line is determined by two points. In parallel coordinates with N axes, those two points produce two polylines. The polylines intersect at exactly N−1 points, one between each pair of adjacent axes. These N−1 intersection points are the indexed points that fully characterize the line.

A line in N-dimensional space has 2(N−1) degrees of freedom. The N−1 indexed points each have 2 coordinates, giving 2(N−1) parameters. The encoding is exact. No information is lost.

This is the foundation. Everything that follows in this series, from cluster detection to polytope visualization, builds on this duality between points and polylines, lines and convergence points. The geometry of parallel coordinates is not a metaphor. It is a precise, invertible mathematical transformation.
