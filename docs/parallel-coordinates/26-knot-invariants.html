Knot Invariants as High-Dimensional Signatures

Each knot leaves a fingerprint across a dozen invariant axes. Parallel coordinates reveal which invariants correlate, which are independent, and how the space of knots is shaped.

Two knots are the same if one can be continuously deformed into the other without cutting the string. That is a topologist's definition, and it is a hard one: the deformation is ambient isotopy in three-space, and there is no general algorithm that tells you whether two pictures of tangled string represent the same knot. The alternative strategy is to compute quantities that stay fixed under any deformation. These are knot invariants. If two knots produce different values of an invariant, they are definitely different. If they produce the same values, you still do not know.

Invariants come in many flavors. The crossing number counts the minimum number of crossings in any planar diagram. The unknotting number counts the minimum crossing changes needed to untie the knot. The genus is the smallest genus of a Seifert surface bounded by the knot. The bridge number counts maxima over all projections. The Alexander polynomial and Jones polynomial assign Laurent polynomials in one variable. The determinant is the Alexander polynomial evaluated at . Each invariant captures a different aspect of the knot's geometry, and none is complete: there exist pairs of distinct knots that share every invariant on this list.

If you collect all these numbers for a single knot, you get a vector in a high-dimensional invariant space. The trefoil is one point. The figure-eight is another. The space of all prime knots up to 12 crossings is roughly 2,977 such points, sitting in whatever ambient space your feature vector spans. This is exactly the setting parallel coordinates was designed for: a point per knot, an axis per invariant, a polyline per row. We cannot draw 12-dimensional space directly, but we can draw its shadow on twelve parallel axes and read the bundle structure to see which invariants move together, which are uncorrelated, and which knots sit in anomalous positions.

The Invariants We Will Use

This article uses ten common invariants for each knot. Nine of them are non-negative integers; the last is a signed integer. Every one is computed from the topology of the knot itself, not from any particular diagram. Where a particular value depends on the orientation convention (signature, for example), we use the Knot Atlas values directly.

Figure 3 — Which Invariants Say the Same Thing?

A ten-dimensional plot has pairs of coordinates. Between each pair there is a joint scatter that you can summarize with a single number: the Pearson correlation. Stack all 45 numbers into a 10×10 symmetric matrix with ones on the diagonal, and you get the correlation heatmap of the invariants. Dark red cells are strongly positive: when one invariant grows, the other grows. Dark blue cells are strongly negative. Cells near zero are near white: the two invariants essentially float independently across the sample of knots.

Hover any cell to read the correlation and see the invariants' names. The diagonal is always 1.

Figure 3. Pearson correlation matrix of the ten invariants computed over 31 knots. Red = positive, blue = negative, white = zero. The strongest positive correlations are the expected ones: crossing number with Jones breadth (~1.00, they nearly coincide for alternating knots), smooth 4-genus with unknotting number (~0.93), and genus with Alexander degree (~0.80). The weakest are determinant against unknotting, signature against crossings, and bridge against braid index — near zero, evidence that these invariants carry independent information. The more blue or red the cell, the more redundant the axis pair.

Read Figure 3 as a guide to which axes contribute independent signal. A pair of invariants with correlation near 1 are almost redundant: you could drop one without losing much. A pair with correlation near 0 are nearly orthogonal: each one tells you something the other does not. The dimensionality of the invariant space is not really ten, but more like the number of independent directions in this correlation matrix. Principal component analysis on this matrix would show that the first two or three components already explain most of the variance, but the tails — the weakly correlated pairs — are where the most discriminating signal lives.

Figure 4 — Knots That Trip Up the Alexander Polynomial

The unknot has a dull signature. Every invariant in Table 1 collapses to 0 or 1. That is the whole point of an invariant: a non-trivial knot should be visibly different. And for almost every knot in the atlas, the Alexander polynomial is different from the unknot's. Almost.

In 1957, Shinichi Kinoshita and Hidetaka Terasaka wrote down an eleven-crossing knot — now called the Kinoshita-Terasaka knot — that is demonstrably not the unknot, but whose Alexander polynomial is exactly . John Conway later found a second eleven-crossing knot, the Conway knot , related to by mutation (cutting a tangle out, rotating it 180°, and gluing it back). The two knots share not only the trivial Alexander polynomial, but also the same Jones polynomial. They are the most famous Alexander-polynomial conspirators in the tables.

Figure 4 plots the same polyline layout as Figure 2 but now with just three knots highlighted: the unknot, the Kinoshita-Terasaka knot, and the Conway knot. All other 27 rows are dimmed. Notice where the three polylines agree: determinant (all 1), Alexander degree (all 0), and on those two alone they are indistinguishable from the unknot.

The unknot (blue), Kinoshita-Terasaka (orange), and Conway (red) agree on determinant = 1 and Alexander degree = 0. They disagree elsewhere: bridge, braid, Jones breadth, and the smooth 4-genus separate them.

Figure 4. Three conspirators against the Alexander polynomial. The unknot, Kinoshita-Terasaka (), and Conway () coincide on determinant and Alexander degree — invariants computed from . On every other axis they separate. In particular, the Conway knot's smooth 4-genus is 1, while the Kinoshita-Terasaka knot is smoothly slice (). This is Lisa Piccirillo's 2020 result: the two knots are distinguished not by any classical polynomial but by the smooth slice genus.

Mutation is subtle. Two knots that are mutants of each other share their Alexander, Jones, and HOMFLY polynomials; their signature; and their hyperbolic volume. All the classical invariants miss the distinction. Piccirillo's 2020 proof that the Conway knot is not smoothly slice used an elaborate construction involving a different knot whose 4-genus could be computed, transferring the result via concordance. The parallel-coordinates version of the story: even with 10 axes, two distinct knots can overlap on 8 of them. The whole point of adding more invariants is to find an axis where they diverge.

Figure 6 — Invariant Bounds as Axis Orderings

Invariants do not float freely. There are classical inequalities between them that act as lower and upper bounds, and those bounds show up in parallel coordinates as ordering constraints: if invariant is always at most invariant , then on adjacent axes no polyline can jump upward from the -axis to the -axis by more than the gap allows — and more importantly, on a normalized scale every polyline must be monotone non-increasing or non-decreasing in that pair. Three classical bounds appear in our data:

The first is the genus bound on the unknotting number. The second is Bennequin's inequality relating crossings and genus. The third is Milnor's inequality relating smooth 4-genus to unknotting number. Figure 6 overlays these bounds as shaded envelope regions against the polylines that satisfy them. Below each pair of axes, the readout reports how many of our knots saturate the inequality (achieve equality) and how many sit strictly inside.

Toggle between the classical inequalities. The shaded region is the forbidden zone; no polyline can enter it. Every polyline slope in the pair must lie inside the envelope.

Figure 6. Knot invariant bounds visualized as envelopes between adjacent parallel-coordinates axes. The smooth 4-genus is at most the unknotting number (each crossing change cobounds a disk in ) and at most the 3-genus (push the slice surface to the boundary). The Alexander polynomial degree is bounded above by twice the 3-genus. And for every knot in our sample, twice the genus is at most the crossing number. These four inequalities shape the "orthant" where valid knot invariant vectors live — not every row of non-negative integers is realized.

Invariant space is not a box. If the ten invariants were independent, any row of integers with non-negative entries could correspond to a knot. They don't. Genus, bridge, unknotting, and 4-genus sit inside a tight web of inequalities. The braid index, the crossing number, and the Seifert form are all coupled. The "space of knots" is a thin, curved subset of , and the parallel-coordinates view shows the constraint envelopes directly as the outline of the polyline bundle.

This is the same pattern as the outlier signature. In article 6 a single row that sits outside the bundle is flagged as anomalous. For knots, "outside the bundle" would mean violating an inequality — which cannot happen if the values come from a real knot. So the knot atlas sits neatly inside its constraint envelope. When someone discovers a knot with genus 1 but unknotting number 2, that knot redefines the envelope.

Where This Goes

Parallel coordinates for knot invariants turn the classical tables into an atlas you can read. The same structure applies to any catalog of mathematical objects characterized by a list of invariants: exceptional root systems with their ranks, orders, and numbers of roots; finite groups with their orders, conjugacy class counts, and Cayley graph diameters; polytopes with their face numbers, symmetry group orders, and dual-polytope pairings. In each case the invariants land as points in a high-dimensional orthant, sit on a curved constraint surface, and cluster into families that the parallel-coordinates view makes visible.

Knot theory is a particularly sharp example because the constraint surface is almost all the structure. The classical invariants do not distinguish every knot, the space of accessible invariant vectors is thin, and the discovery of new knots or new invariants reshapes the boundary. The next wave of invariants — Khovanov homology, Heegaard Floer, the Witten-Reshetikhin-Turaev-style quantum invariants — add more axes, and the bundle of polylines for known knots spreads out along those axes as the new coordinates catch distinctions the old ones missed. The Conway and Kinoshita-Terasaka knots, which coincided on every pre-1980 invariant, separate once you add the smooth 4-genus. The next pair of mutant twins will separate once we add the next axis.
